# Mercury Injection Capillary Pressure

**Summary**: A method used to determine pore throat size distribution and capillary pressure in rock samples.

## Key Results and Findings

- Mercury injection data were converted into J-space to allow for the averaging of capillary pressure curves across different hydraulic units. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 164]])

- Pore throat size distributions were used to categorize rocks into micro, meso, macro, and super-macro pores. ([[2011-al-nasser|Al-Nasser, 2011, p. 40]])

- The data showed that the degree of dispersion in numerical derivatives provided a clear indication of measurement accuracy. ([[2013-nooruddin|Nooruddin, 2013, p. 34]])

- Revealed a bimodal distribution of pore-throat sizes in several samples. ([[2014-el-deek|El-Deek, 2014, p. 132]])

- MICP data was used to calculate J-function parameters (a and b) for all lithofacies. ([[2015-al-amri|Al-Amri, 2015, p. 27]])

- The study utilized 202 core samples obtained via mercury injection test, each with at least 50 data points. ([[2017-busaleh|BuSaleh, 2017, p. 47]])

- MICP showed that pore-throat sizes were less than 10 µm for all lithofacies, with multi-modal distributions (unimodal, bimodal, and trimodal). ([[2020-al-zoukani|Al-Zoukani, 2020, p. 123]])

- Capillary pressure curves showed that 20 psi was sufficient to displace brine from macropores in carbonate, but not all brine from smaller pores. ([[2024-suleiman-2|Suleiman, 2024, p. 63]])

- Capillary pressure curves were generated by desaturating samples from 20 psi to 200 psi in 20 psi steps. ([[2024-suleiman-3|Suleiman, 2024, p. 33]])

- Grain-dominated facies exhibited a bimodal pore network of micropores and macropores, while mud-dominated facies were associated with micropores. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 117]])

- The study found that for coated carbonate rocks, injection curves were dependent on sample size, with larger samples showing lower displacement pressures. ([[1991-ali|Ali, 1991, p. 22]])

- The technique provided a means to evaluate how sample size, coating, and time affected the resulting capillary pressure curves. ([[1991-ali-2|Ali, 1991, p. 25]])
