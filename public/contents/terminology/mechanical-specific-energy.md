# Mechanical Specific Energy

**Summary**: A measure of the energy required to remove a unit volume of rock during drilling.

---

## How It Appears in the Thesis Collection

- [[2013-khamis|Khamis, 2013]] used mechanical-specific-energy (MSE) as a baseline for evaluating bit efficiency.

- [[2020-al-abduljabbar|Al-AbdulJabbar, 2020]] used MSE as an input parameter for several ROP prediction models.

- [[2022-al-dahmoush|Al-Dahmoush, 2022]] used MSE analysis to evaluate drilling efficiency and detect bit deterioration in abrasive sandstone.

## Scientific Context

- [[2013-khamis|Khamis, 2013]] Used to identify 'founder' points where energy is wasted on non-productive activities like bit balling or vibration.

- [[2020-al-abduljabbar|Al-AbdulJabbar, 2020]] used MSE as an input parameter for several ROP prediction models.

- [[2022-al-dahmoush|Al-Dahmoush, 2022]] used MSE analysis to evaluate drilling efficiency and detect bit deterioration in abrasive sandstone.

## Measurement and Characterization Methods

- [[2013-khamis|Khamis, 2013]] Ratio of input energy (WOB, Torque) to ROP.

## Key Findings Across Sources

- MSE is useful because it provides a reference point; if MSE matches rock compressive strength, the bit is operating efficiently. ([[2013-khamis|Khamis, 2013, p. 30]])


- If MSE is significantly higher than the formation's UCS, it indicates that much of the energy is being wasted on vibrations or shocks. ([[2020-al-abduljabbar|Al-AbdulJabbar, 2020, p. 58]])

- MSE trends showed sudden increases indicating potential shock/vibration or bit wear; specifically, a jump in SMSE indicates energy waste. ([[2022-al-dahmoush|Al-Dahmoush, 2022, p. 44]])
