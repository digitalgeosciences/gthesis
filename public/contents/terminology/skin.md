# Skin

**Summary**: A measure of the extra pressure drop caused by formation damage near the wellbore.

---

## How It Appears in the Thesis Collection

- [[2001-mohammed|Mohammed, 2001]] measured skin factor to quantify the impact of drilling fluid on well productivity.

- [[2011-alburaikan|AlBuraikan, 2011]] calculated the skin factor for well X-1 as -2.89.

- [[2013-khan-2|Khan, 2013]] included as a skin effect in the final calculation for both standard and fractional models.

- [[2013-al-nemer|Al-Nemer, 2013]] used skin factor as a key parameter in both horizontal and dual porosity models.

- [[2014-al-gahtani|Al-Gahtani, 2014]] investigated the impact of potential skin damage and variation along the horizontal drain-hole.

- [[2022-alkathim|Alkathim, 2022]] used skin factor as an indicator of formation damage and its removal via acidizing.

- [[1999-hasan|Hasan, 1999]] evaluated skin factors to determine the impact of drilling-induced damage on horizontal wells.

## Key Findings Across Sources

- Skin factor decreased significantly as particle size increased from 8 to 12 microns. ([[2001-mohammed|Mohammed, 2001, p. 111]])


- The negative skin of -2.89 was potentially attributed to georeserves or fractures. ([[2011-alburaikan|AlBuraikan, 2011, p. 65]])

- Skin effects are always present in oil/gas reservoirs, typically caused by drilling and completion procedures. ([[2013-khan-2|Khan, 2013, p. 54]])

- The actual skin value was found to be 5 in several test cases. ([[2013-al-nemer|Al-Nemer, 2013, p. 22]])

- The model allows for the consideration of formation damage in different sections of the wellbore. ([[2014-al-gahtani|Al-Gahtani, 2014, p. 24]])

- A positive skin value indicates a damaged well, while a negative value implies enhanced permeability from stimulation. ([[2022-alkathim|Alkathim, 2022, p. 22]])

- A skin factor of S=20 resulted in a 58% reduction in production rate for a specific geometry. ([[1999-hasan|Hasan, 1999, p. 60]])
