# Mechanical Stratigraphy

**Summary**: The ability of a rock to fracture and maintain open fractures.

---

## Key Findings Across Sources

- Lithofacies-III was identified as the most brittle due to its high quartz content (78.1%). ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 48]])


- Brittleness is often calculated using mineralogical content (quartz vs. clay) or elastic parameters like Young's modulus and Poisson's ratio. ([[2016-lawal|Lawal, 2016, p. 42]])

- Identified high-brittleness layers (A1, A3, B2, C2, and C4) as the most susceptible to fracturing. ([[2016-saleh|Saleh, 2016, p. 109]])

- Not stated in source ([[2017-khan|Khan, 2017]])

- The study identified toughness/modulus and interface barriers that affect hydraulic fracture growth. ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 138]])

- High-resolution profiles were achieved using micro-rebound testing, which provided better definition of ductile and brittle zones than log-based methods. ([[2021-albensaad|Albensaad, 2021, p. 14]])

- The directional dependence of brittleness on crack orientation is a key guideline for fracking indices. ([[2021-lawal|LAWAL, 2021, p. 15]])

- DAP treatment resulted in the most significant improvement in rock's hardness, reaching up to 30% for chalk. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 67]])
