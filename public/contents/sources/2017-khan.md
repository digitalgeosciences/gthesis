# Numerical modeling of the geomechanical behavior of a carbonate petroleum reservoir undergoing CO2 injection

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the geomechanical behavior of carbonate reservoirs, specifically focusing on the impact of CO2 injection on pore pressure and reservoir stability. It utilizes a fully coupled numerical modeling approach using COMSOL multiphysics to evaluate these effects in both single-porosity and naturally fractured systems.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the challenge of predicting how carbon dioxide injection affects the geomechanical integrity, stress distribution, and pore pressure of carbonate reservoirs (specifically the Biyadh sandstone and Arab-D), which are prone to deformation and potential failure due to high injection pressures.

## Objectives

- Determine the change in the pore pressure of the reservoir during the process of carbon dioxide injection.
- Calculate the changes in the effective stresses and ground uplift during carbon dioxide injection.
- Perform stability analysis for the reservoir and predict safe values of the injection parameters.
- Estimate the safe value of the carbon dioxide occupancy limit for the reservoir.
- Develop a coupled numerical modeling methodology for CO2 flow and geomechanical behavior in carbonate reservoirs.
- Validate the COMSOL multiphysics model against existing literature data (e.g., Rudnicki, Haidary et al.).

## Methods

The study employs a coupled geomechanical and fluid flow modeling approach. COMSOL Multiphysics is used to solve non-linear field equations for displacement, matrix pressure, and fracture pressure. The model incorporates dual-porosity (Warren and Root) concepts where the reservoir is divided into matrix blocks and fractures. It accounts for sorption-based deformation using Langmuir constants. The Mohr-Coulomb failure criterion is applied to assess stability. A 3D simulation of the Arab Jubaila limestone reservoir (6548 elements, 53,060 degrees of freedom) was modeled with a 10-year injection period at varying pressures (23, 25, and 27 MPa).

## Data and Materials

The study models a single-porosity Biyadh sandstone reservoir (for comparative purposes) and a naturally fractured Ghawar carbonate reservoir. Scenarios included varying numbers of injection wells (1 to 4), different well spacings, and both non-fractured and fractured caprock conditions over a 5-year injection period.

## Key Findings

- The inclusion of a production well at the sequestration site tends to decrease the overall pore pressure compared to an injection-only scenario. ([[2017-khan|Khan, 2017, p. 21]])
- Carbon dioxide leakage into fractured caprock causes increased pore pressure in overburden layers, resulting in ground uplift that can be used to identify fracture locations. ([[2017-khan|Khan, 2017, p. 21]])
- The study determined that the current CO2 injection scenarios are at the safe side of the maximum occupancy limit based on formation volume factors. ([[2017-khan|Khan, 2017, p. 21]])
- Pore pressure increases during CO2 injection, while effective stresses decrease; however, in coupled models, increased pore pressure leads to an increase in horizontal stresses. ([[2017-khan|Khan, 2017, p. 36]])
- The study identifies that the optimal number and arrangement of injection wells can reduce pore pressure build-up and increase reservoir storage capacity. ([[2017-khan|Khan, 2017, p. 21]])
- The COMSOL numerical model for pore pressure variation in a single-layer system showed excellent agreement with the analytical solution by Rudnicki (Ref 79). ([[2017-khan|Khan, 2017, p. 39]])
- In the multi-layered model, the reservoir's stability was confirmed to be consistent with results from Haidary et al., where Mohr's circle analysis showed that shear failure occurs at specific critical pore pressure thresholds. ([[2017-khan|Khan, 2017, p. 40]])
- The study determined that for a single-porosity reservoir, the geomechanical behavior is governed by coupled non-linear field equations including stress, strain, and fluid flow. ([[2017-khan|Khan, 2017, p. 29]])
- A 3D model of the Biyadh sandstone reservoir included an overburden system with varying properties (e.g., Young's Modulus ranging from 15 to 20 GPa). ([[2017-khan|Khan, 2017, p. 35]])
- The study identified that dual-porosity modeling is necessary for accurately estimating the strength of the reservoir matrix and its deformation in carbonate reservoirs like Arab-D. ([[2017-khan|Khan, 2017, p. 25]])
- The critical pore pressure for the intact reservoir to avoid new shear fractures is 33.71 MPa (at 26 MPa injection). ([[2017-khan|Khan, 2017, p. 45]])
- The critical pore pressure for reactivation of existing fractures in a fractured system is 25.05 MPa. ([[2017-khan|Khan, 2017, p. 46]])
- The critical pore pressure for tensile failure of the reservoir is 31.10 MPa. ([[2017-khan|Khan, 2017, p. 46]])
- The initial pore volume for the Biyadh sandstone reservoir is 3.072 x 10^8 m^3, with a CO2 injection volume of 2.02 x 10^9 m^3 over 10 years. ([[2017-khan|Khan, 2017, p. 55]])
- The occupancy of CO2 in the current scenario is 2.4% of the available pore volume, which is within the safe limit of 3%. ([[2017-khan|Khan, 2017, p. 56]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Incorporate normal stresses on faults into models to avoid induced seismicity from high-pressure CO2 injection.
- Extend the model to include Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR) during the sequestration process to offset costs.
- Include post-injection geochemical modeling to study interactions between stored CO2 and the reservoir rock.
