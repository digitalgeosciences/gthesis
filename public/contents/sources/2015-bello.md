# INVESTIGATING THE PHYTOREMEDIATION POTENTIAL OF SELECTED SAUDI PLANTS IN REMOVING POLLUTANTS FROM CONTAMINATED WATERS

**Summary**: The thesis investigated the phytoremediation potential of two Saudi plant species, Bolboschoenus maritimus and Phragmites australis, in removing organic (naphthalene) and inorganic (cadmium, lead, and nickel) pollutants from contaminated water. The study utilized a hydroponic system to evaluate removal rates over 6 weeks and concluded that both plants were highly effective for cadmium and lead remediation.

---

## Research Problem

Pollution by polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) and heavy metals poses significant environmental risks due to their persistence and toxicity. Existing physicochemical treatment methods are often costly or energy-intensive, necessitating the development of more cost-effective, eco-friendly biological alternatives like phytoremediation using locally adapted plants.

## Objectives

- Demonstrate the phytoremediation potential of Bolboschoenus maritimus and Phragmites australis in removing selected organic and inorganic pollutants in water.
- Investigate the mechanisms employed by each plant to carry out phytoremediation.
- Investigate the microbial community present in the spiked water and rhizosphere of the plants.
- To identify and evaluate the phytoremediation potential of indigenous plants that are well adapted to the location
- To identify the patterns of accumulation of contaminants in the organs (namely: roots, stems and leaves) of selected plants
- To identify the mechanisms of contaminant uptake in the tested plants
- To identify the microbial communities in each of the tested plants and their roles in the phytoremediation of the contaminants
- To investigate the phytoremediation potential of Bolboschoenus maritimus and Phragmites australis in removing selected organic and inorganic pollutants in water.
- To evaluate the removal efficiency of cadmium (Cd), lead (Pb), and nickel (Ni) by these plants.
- To assess the impact of hydroponic conditions on the remediation performance of the chosen species.
- Determine the phytoremediation potential of Bolboschoenus maritimus and Phragmites australis for removing cadmium, lead, and nickel.
- Evaluate the effect of pH on the remediation of heavy metals by both plant species.
- Assess the impact of salinity (TDS) on the removal of heavy metals and naphthalene.
- Investigate the role of bacteria in the phytoremediation process of naphthalene.
- Identify specific bacterial colonies involved in the degradation of organic pollutants.
- Investigate the phytoremediation potential of Bolboschoenus maritimus and Phragmites australis.
- Determine the removal efficiency of cadmium, lead, and nickel from spiked water.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of these plants in removing naphthalene (a target organic pollutant).
- Assess the impact of environmental factors like pH and salinity on remediation performance.
- To demonstrate the phytoremediation potential of Bolboschoenus maritimus
- To demonstrate the phytoremediation potential of Phragmites australis
- To evaluate the removal of naphthalene (organic pollutant)
- To evaluate the removal of cadmium, lead, and nickel (inorganic pollutants)

## Methods

The study used a hydroponic system consisting of a 1L nutrient solution, air pump for aeration, and gravel as growing medium. Plants were collected from Al-Asfar Lake and established in the system for two weeks before being exposed to contaminants. Naphthalene was prepared by dissolving crystals in n-hexane (10 ppm). Heavy metals (Cd, Pb, Ni) were added at 5 ppm concentrations. Microbiological analysis included cultivation on Nutrient Agar (NA), MacConkey, and EMB agar plates followed by biochemical tests (Indole, Methyl Red, Voges-Proskauer, etc.). Naphthalene was extracted using liquid-liquid extraction (n-hexane/DCM) and analyzed via GC/MS (Thermo Scientific Trace GC Ultra). Heavy metals were analyzed using ICP-OES (Perkin Elmer Optima 8000). Statistical analysis was performed using ANOVA to determine significant differences between plant species and contaminants.

## Data and Materials

Two indigenous plant species: Phragmites australis and Bolboschoenus maritimus. Samples were collected from Al-Asfar Lake, Al-Hassa. Experimental setup included 2.25L containers for hydroponic growth. Naphthalene concentration was set at 10 ppm; heavy metals (Cd, Pb, Ni) were set at 5 ppm.

## Key Findings

- B. maritimus showed a removal of 99% for cadmium, 94% for lead, and 83% for nickel over a 6-week period. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 14]])
- P. australis showed a removal of 93% for cadmium, 95% for lead, and 84% for nickel over a 6-week period. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 14]])
- Phytoremediation of naphthalene by B. maritimus resulted in a removal of 96% (residual of 4%) over 6 weeks. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 14]])
- Phytoremediation of naphthalene by P. australis resulted in a removal of 91% (residual of 9%) over 6 weeks. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 14]])
- Four distinct bacteria colonies were identified: Enterobacter spp., Staphylococcus spp., Pseudomonas spp., and Bacillus spp. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 14]])
- Naphthalene is a target organic pollutant with a molecular weight of 128.19 g/mol and an octanol-water partition coefficient (LK) of 3.37. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 22]])
- The study identifies several mechanisms for phytoremediation: phytodegradation, phytoextraction, rhizodegradation, phytovolatilization, phytostabilization, and rhizofiltration. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 24]])
- Hydroponics is preferred for studying phytoremediation of contaminated water because it eliminates or minimizes interactions between the growth medium and the contaminants. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 28]])
- The uptake of organic contaminants by plants depends on the log of octanol-water partition coefficient (log Kow), with an optimal range of 0.5 - 3.0 for transpiration. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 32]])
- Plants are categorized into excluders, indicators, and accumulators/hyper-accumulators based on their ability to manage heavy metal stress. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 34]])
- Bolboschoenus maritimus showed a 94% removal of cadmium (Cd) within 2 weeks, reducing to 99% by the end of the 6-week experiment. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 58]])
- Phragmites australis achieved 92% removal of cadmium (Cd) in 2 weeks and maintained a 93% removal rate over 6 weeks. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 58]])
- Both Bolboschoenus maritimus and Phragmites australis were effective for the remediation of lead (Pb), with both showing 94% removal in 2 weeks. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 59]])
- Phragmites australis reduced lead (Pb) concentration to a 5% residual by the end of 6 weeks. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 59]])
- The removal of nickel (Ni) was less efficient than other metals, with Bolboschoenus maritimus reaching 83% removal and Phragmites australis reaching 83% removal by the end of 6 weeks. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 59]])

## Limitations

Phytoremediation is limited by the depth of plant roots (suitable only for shallow soil/water), the required surface area, and the duration of treatment compared to physicochemical methods. It is also affected by seasonal changes.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Test different concentrations of cadmium and lead.
- Evaluate the plants for other heavy metals.
- Evaluate the potential for removing mixtures (composites) of lead and cadmium at varying concentrations.
- Evaluate the performance of different types of hydroponic systems.
