# INVESTIGATING THE PHYTOREMEDIATION POTENTIAL OF SELECTED SAUDI PLANTS IN REMOVING POLLUTANTS FROM CONTAMINATED WATERS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the phytoremediation potential of two Saudi plant species, Bolboschoenus maritimus and Phragmites australis, in removing heavy metals (Cd, Pb, Ni) and a polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon (naphthalene) from contaminated water. The study concludes that both plants are highly effective for heavy metal removal but less so for naphthalene, with the presence of specific bacteria contributing to the remediation process.

---

## Research Problem

The accumulation of heavy metals and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) in water bodies poses significant risks to human health and the environment, requiring cost-effective and sustainable treatment methods like phytoremediation using local species adapted to arid conditions.

## Objectives

- To identify and evaluate the phytoremediation potential of indigenous plants that are well adapted to the location
- To identify the patterns of accumulation of contaminants in the organs (namely: roots, stems and leaves) of selected plants
- To identify the mechanisms of contaminant uptake in the tested plants
- To identify the microbial communities in each of the tested plants and their roles in the phytoremediation of the contaminants
- To determine the ability of B. maritimus and P. australis to remove cadmium, lead, and nickel from spiked distilled water.
- To evaluate the effect of pH on the remediation of heavy metals by both plants.

## Methods

The study utilized a hydroponic system (Deep Water Culture) to grow Bolboschoenus maritimus and Phragmites australis. Naphthalene was added at 10 ppm, while cadmium, lead, and nickel were added at 5 ppm. Samples were collected every two weeks over a 6-week period. Microbial analysis involved culturing bacteria on Nutrient Agar (NA) and performing biochemical tests (Indole, Methyl Red, Voges-Proskauer, etc.). Naphthalene was extracted from water using liquid-liquid extraction (n-hexane/dichloromethane) and from plant tissues using an Accelerated Solvent Extractor (ASE 200). Analysis of naphthalene was performed via GC/MS. Heavy metals were analyzed after acid digestion of plant tissue.

## Data and Materials

Two plant species (B. maritimus, P. australis), three heavy metal contaminants (Cd, Pb, Ni) at 5ppm each, one organic pollutant (naphthalene) at 10ppm, and various water conditions (distilled, groundwater, different pH levels of 4, 7, 10, and varying TDS levels).

## Key Findings

- B. maritimus showed a removal efficiency of 99% for cadmium (1% residual), 94% for lead (6% residual), and 83% for nickel (17% residual) over 6 weeks. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 14]])
- The phytoremediation of naphthalene by B. maritimus resulted in a removal efficiency of 96% (4% residual). ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 14]])
- Four distinct bacterial colonies were identified in the planted media: Enterobacter spp., Staphylococcus spp., Pseudomonas spp., and Bacillus spp. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 14]])
- The primary mechanisms for phytoremediation by both plants were likely phytostabilization and rhizodegradation. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 14]])
- B. maritimus achieved a 94% removal of cadmium in 2 weeks and 99% by the end of 6 weeks. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 58]])
- Both plants were effective for lead remediation, with B. maritimus reaching 94% removal in 2 weeks and P. australis reaching 93% removal in 2 weeks (initially). ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 58]])
- The two plants were less effective for nickel, achieving only 57% removal in 2 weeks and 83% removal in 6 weeks. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 59]])
- pH did not significantly enhance the remediation ability of either plant for any of the three metals. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 97]])
- B. maritimus showed a higher removal rate for nickel in groundwater samples (TDS 3645ppm) after 6 weeks, reaching a residual of only 1%. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 80]])
- P. australis was found to be more effective at removing nickel at pH 10 compared to other pH levels. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 70]])
- Naphthalene removal by both plants improved significantly after 6 weeks, with B. maritimus reaching a 4% residual and P. australis reaching an 8% residual. ([[2015-bello|Bello, 2015, p. 96]])
