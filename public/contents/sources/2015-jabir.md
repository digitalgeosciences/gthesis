# HIGH-RESOLUTION 3D GEOCELLULAR FACIES AND PROPERTY MODELING OF BENTIU-1 RESERVOIR INTERVAL, JACK OILFIELD, FULA SUB-BASIN, SUDAN

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the Bentiu-1 reservoir interval in the Jack Oilfield, Sudan, by developing a 3D geocellular model to characterize lithofacies heterogeneity and petrophysical properties. Using a combination of well logs, core data, and geostatistical workflows (SIS and SGS), the study identifies distinct lithofacies and correlates them with porosity, permeability, and water saturation.

---

## Research Problem

The Bentiu-1 Reservoir Interval in the Muglad Rift Basin is characterized by significant lithofacies heterogeneity and complex structural settings, requiring high-resolution 3D modeling to accurately characterize its petrophysical properties and spatial distribution.

## Objectives

- Establish a comprehensive structural framework to determine major reservoir architectures and geometries in terms of faults and horizons.
- Build a 3-D litho-stratigraphic grid populated with lithofacies determined from well-log interpretation, core samples, and previous studies.
- Build 3-D property models for porosity, permeability, and water saturation based on well-log interpretation and well reports.
- Map the petrophysical models back to the structurally controlled 3-D litho-stratigraphic model using lithofacies as geological constraints.
- Establish final high-resolution 3-D geocellular models for the reservoir.
- Perform multiple realizations of the models to quantify uncertainty and rank them.

## Methods

The study employs a three-stage workflow: (1) Structural Analysis, where a 3-D model was built from 3-D seismic data integrated with well logs to define faults and horizons; (2) Facies Classification and Petrophysical Analysis, using 'cluster analysis' in Interactive Petrophysics software for electro-facies identification, followed by calculation of shale volume (Larionov equations), porosity (density/sonic/neutron logs), water saturation (Indonesia Equation), and permeability (porosity-permeability relationship); (3) Geostatistics Approach, involving descriptive statistics, normal score transformations, log transformations, and a hierarchical modeling workflow including 3D grid construction, upscaling, variogram analysis, ordinary kriging for property estimation, and Sequential Indicator Simulation (SIS) for lithofacies followed by Sequential Gaussian Simulation (SGS) for petrophysical properties.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized: (1) a 3-D cropped seismic cube of 20 km² covering the Jack Oilfield; (2) well-logging data from 20 wells in the field; (3) selected cutting samples of the Bentiu-1 interval; and (4) core analysis reports, well completion reports, and master logs.

## Key Findings

- The lithofacies classification revealed three distinct types: sand, shaly-sand, and shale. ([[2015-jabir|Jabir, 2015, p. 13]])
- Petrophysical analysis of porosity, permeability, and water saturation showed these properties occur over specific zones correlated with identified lithofacies. ([[2015-jabir|Jabir, 2015, p. 13]])
- Four locally fining upward lithological cycles were identified from the 3-D stochastic simulation, each dominated by sand at the bottom and capped with shale at the top. ([[2015-jabir|Jabir, 2015, p. 13]])
- Four petrophysical units were firmly identified by the presence of three marker-beds characterized by low porosity, low permeability, and high water saturation (corresponding to the shale cap). ([[2015-jabir|Jabir, 2015, p. 13]])
- The Bentiu-1 reservoir interval thickness ranges between 90 m and 250 m and contains over ten productive zones. ([[2015-jabir|Jabir, 2015, p. 21]])
- Porosity values in the first reservoir interval (Abo Gabra) range from 10 to 28% with permeability of 2.325 mD to 3 Darcy. ([[2015-jabir|Jabir, 2015, p. 44]])
- The second reservoir interval (Bentiu Formation) has porosity values ranging from 20 to 35% and permeability values between 100 mD and 5 Darcy. ([[2015-jabir|Jabir, 2015, p. 44]])
- Three lithofacies were identified: medium to coarse-grained sandstone (sub-lithic arenite), fine to silty sandstone (sub-felsic arenite to arenite), and a Shale/Claystone lithofacies. ([[2015-jabir|Jabir, 2015, p. 175]])
- The average porosity calculation showed a high correlation with core data, yielding R2 values of 0.85 for Well X-2 and 0.79 for Well X-3. ([[2015-jabir|Jabir, 2015, p. 77]])
- Permeability was modeled using the exponential relationship: K (mD) = 0.1845 * e^(0.3568 * phi). ([[2015-jabir|Jabir, 2015, p. 95]])
- The cementation index (m) for the study interval was determined to be 1.9, and the saturation exponent (n) was found to be 1.77. ([[2015-jabir|Jabir, 2015, p. 98]])
- Clay volume increased from north to south towards the central parts of the area and from the western rims toward the central highs. ([[2015-jabir|Jabir, 2015, p. 76]])
- The study interval is characterized as a dome-like structure with an average thickness of approximately 170m. ([[2015-jabir|Jabir, 2015, p. 109]])
- Six faults were identified: Fault_Major (a deep-seated normal fault) and five minor faults (Fault-102 to Fault-106). ([[2015-jabir|Jabir, 2015, p. 114]])
- Lithofacies distribution in the reservoir shows that sand has the highest percentage (44.6%), while shale has the lowest. ([[2015-jabir|Jabir, 2015, p. 123]])

## Limitations

The permeability data was calculated based on a pre-defined relationship with porosity, which may over/underestimate values due to being only a function of porosity. The vertical semivariogram fit could be improved by subdividing the interval into stratigraphic subzones.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Acquire more core data to reduce uncertainty in permeability calculations.
- Subdivide vertical intervals into stratigraphic subzones to better account for periodic behavior in semivariograms.
- Utilize co-kriging to link lithofacies and petrophysical parameters to seismic attributes like acoustic impedance.
- Implement Object-Based Modeling (OBM) or Multi-Point Geostatistics (MPS) with training images from similar depositional environments.
