# STUDYING LONG AND SHORT-TERM AIRBORNE PARTICULATE MATTER OVER THE ARABIAN PENINSULA

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the distribution and characteristics of long and short-term airborne particulate matter (PM10) over the Arabian Peninsula using ground-based samples from Jubail and Ras Tanura, along with satellite data. It utilizes various analytical techniques including FESEM, XRD, XRF, and ICP-MS to characterize sample morphology, mineralogy, and trace metals, while employing statistical models like ARIMA and NNAR to predict future trends.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the impact of air pollution on the environment by analyzing the distribution and characteristics of particulate matter (PM10) over the Arabian Peninsula, specifically focusing on distinguishing between natural and anthropogenic sources through long-term satellite data and short-term ground-based sampling.

## Objectives

- Determine physical, optical, and seasonal Airborne Particulate Matter characteristics over several geographical locations in the Arabian Peninsula based on long-term space-borne and ground-based observations.
- Assess the morphological and mineralogical characteristics of the Particulate Matter over two different cities within the Arabian Peninsula.
- Predict the future trend of the Particulate Matter using historical data gathered from space-borne and ground-based stations.
- Determine mineral composition of the PM samples collected from Jubail and Ras Tanura.
- Determine trace metal levels in the PM samples.
- Forecast future trends for particulate matter using ARIMA and NNAR models.

## Methods

The study utilized satellite data from the Terra Satellite (MODIS Sensor) for large-scale analysis of AOD, Angstrom exponent ($\alpha$), and size distribution. Ground-based measurements were collected via AERONET stations (e.g., Mezaira, UAE; KAUST_Campus). Localized sampling in Jubail and Ras Tanura involved a Synchronized Hybrid Ambient Real-time Particulate Monitor (Thermo Scientific 5030i) for PM10 concentration. Mineralogical characterization was performed using X-ray Diffraction (XRD), and chemical composition was determined via X-ray Fluorescence (XRF). Morphological analysis of particles was conducted using Field-Emission Scanning Electron Microscopy (FESEM).

## Data and Materials

Data included: 1) AERONET ground-based data from Solar Village (2001-2013), Mezaira (2004-2017), and KAUST-Campus (2012-2018). 2) Satellite data from Terra/Aqua (MODIS, MISR) for the North and South regions. 3) Physical samples of PM10 collected over a two-week period in Jubail and Ras Tanura.

## Key Findings

- Analysis showed that increased particulate matter concentration in the East and Center of the Arabian Peninsula is attributed to natural particles, while increases in the West and South are linked to man-made particles. ([[2019-alotaibi|Alotaibi, 2019, p. 21]])
- Analysis showed particulate matter concentration increased over time in all five regions (Central: 58%, Western: 15%, Southern: 13%, Northern: 8%, Eastern: 6%). ([[2019-alotaibi|Alotaibi, 2019, p. 187]])
- Man-made particulate matter showed a decrease over time in the Northern (29%), Central (28%), and Eastern (21%) regions, but increased in Western (72%) and Southern (18%) regions. ([[2019-alotaibi|Alotaibi, 2019, p. 187]])
- Man-made particulate matter increased significantly in the Western (72%) and Southern (18%) regions. ([[2019-alotaibi|Alotaibi, 2019, p. 21]])
- SEM analysis showed average particle sizes of 1.4 - 2.8 µm in Jubail, which are smaller than those in Ras Tanura (5 - 7 µm). ([[2019-alotaibi|Alotaibi, 2019, p. 22]])
- XRF results for Jubail Industrial City showed higher concentrations of zinc, barium, sulfur, sodium, and potassium compared to Ras Tanura. ([[2019-alotaibi|Alotaibi, 2019, p. 22]])
- Ras Tanura samples exhibited higher concentrations of calcium, chlorine, aluminum, iron, and magnesium than Jubail samples. ([[2019-alotaibi|Alotaibi, 2019, p. 22]])
- XRD analysis identified the particulate matter as being composed of silica, koktaite, mascagnite, and calcium carbonate. ([[2019-alotaibi|Alotaibi, 2019, p. 22]])
- ICP-MS analysis confirmed that trace metals (lithium, cobalt, vanadium, arsenic, silver, manganese, nickel, molybdenum, copper, chromium, cadmium, and lead) were within regulatory limits. ([[2019-alotaibi|Alotaibi, 2019, p. 22]])
- AOD values greater than one indicate high aerosol loadings and severe pollution; values less than one indicate lower/moderate levels. ([[2019-alotaibi|Alotaibi, 2019, p. 38]])
- An Angstrom Exponent (AE) of $\ge$ 1 indicates fine mode aerosols (<2.5 µm), while an AE < 1 indicates coarse mode aerosols (>2.5 µm). ([[2019-alotaibi|Alotaibi, 2019, p. 40]])
- In the Central Region (Solar Village), annual trends showed an increase in PM10 concentration by 37% while anthropogenic emissions decreased by 35%, suggesting a dominance of natural sources. ([[2019-alotaibi|Alotaibi, 2019, p. 78]])
- Monthly analysis for fine particles (α380-500) showed a decrease of 92% over the study period in the Central Region. ([[2019-alotaibi|Alotaibi, 2019, p. 81]])
- In the Eastern Region (Mezaira), annual trends showed an increase in PM10 concentration by 9% and a decrease in anthropogenic emissions by 35%. ([[2019-alotaibi|Alotaibi, 2019, p. 97]])
- The study identified that natural emission is higher than anthropogenic in all seasons except the fall season. ([[2019-alotaibi|Alotaibi, 2019, p. 93]])

## Limitations

Data from Mezaira and KAUST_Campus were excluded from some models due to numerous missing values. Results for Western, Northern, and Southern regions were not statistically significant in certain probability tests.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Study size distribution over the south and north of the Arabian Peninsula.
- Establish GCC-level cooperation to reduce man-made particle emissions.
- Conduct more research in West and South areas to understand reasons for increased man-made particles.
- Extend chemical characterization of long-term particulate matter across more areas.
