# Adsorption of Surfactants on Saudi Arabian Limestone

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the adsorption and precipitation behavior of petroleum sulfonates and ethoxylated sulfonates on Saudi Arabian limestone under varying conditions of pH, salinity, temperature, and the presence of additives like n-butanol and sacrificial agents. It concludes that while ethoxylated sulfonates offer better salt tolerance than petroleum sulfonates, both are subject to significant loss through adsorption and precipitation in high-salinity, high-temperature environments.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the challenge of surfactant adsorption and precipitation in Saudi Arabian limestone reservoirs, which can significantly impact the effectiveness of chemical flooding in high salinity (up to 20%) and high temperature (90 °C) environments.

## Objectives

- Investigate the effect of parameters such as solution salinity, pH, temperature, solid-liquid ratio, surfactant type, and ethoxylation number on surfactant adsorption.
- Determine the impact of sacrificial agents (e.g., sodium metasilicate) and co-surfactants (n-butanol) on reducing surfactant loss.
- Study the effect of crude oil presence on the adsorption behavior of ethoxylated sulfonates.
- Determine the salt tolerance and precipitation behavior of various surfactants in the presence of monovalent and multivalent ions.
- Formulate conditions to minimize surfactant adsorption for Saudi Arabian limestone reservoirs.
- Evaluate the adsorption behavior of petroleum sulfonate (TRS10-410) as a reference on limestone.

## Methods

The study utilized ground limestone (BET surface area 13.2 m2/g) and several surfactants: TRS10-410, V2880, V3348, B1083, B1139, and T150. Surface area was measured using a SORPTOMATIC 1800 apparatus (BET method). Adsorption of anionic surfactants was determined via two-phase titration with HTAB as the titrant. UV/Visible spectrophotometry was used for nonionic surfactant analysis. Precipitation behavior was assessed through turbidity measurements and cloud point determination. CMC was determined using surface tension measurements with a Kruss K10 digital tensiumeter.

## Data and Materials

The study used Saudi Arabian limestone samples (crushed core), pure crystalline calcite as a reference, petroleum sulfonate (TRS10-410), several ethoxylated sulfonates (V2880, B1083, B1139), n-butanol, and sodium metasilicate. Experimental conditions included temperatures of 25°C, 50°C, and 90°C; salinity levels up to 20% NaCl; and various pH values.

## Key Findings

- The surface area of the ground Saudi Arabian limestone was determined to be 13.2 m2/g using the BET method. ([[1987-ahmed|Ahmed, 1987, p. 44]])
- Adsorption of surfactants on limestone increased significantly with increasing temperature at three levels (25, 50, and 90 °C). ([[1987-ahmed|Ahmed, 1987, p. 22]])
- Increasing the ethoxylation number of surfactants decreased adsorption but concurrently increased the critical micelle concentration (CMC). ([[1987-ahmed|Ahmed, 1987, p. 123]])
- Adsorption of surfactants on limestone could be reduced with the addition of silicates as sacrificial agents and n-butanol as a co-surfactant. ([[1987-ahmed|Ahmed, 1987, p. 22]])
- Nonionic surfactant (T150) showed approximately five-fold higher adsorption than the anionic surfactant (B1083) of the same ethoxylation number under identical conditions. ([[1987-ahmed|Ahmed, 1987, p. 23]])
- Petroleum sulfonate (TRS10-410) was not tolerant to even as low as 1% NaCl and 0.15% CaCl at room temperature, showing precipitation behavior. ([[1987-ahmed|Ahmed, 1987, p. 14]])
- Ethoxylated sulfonates were found to be highly tolerant to both mono and multivalent salts at 20% NaCl and 90 °C. ([[1987-ahmed|Ahmed, 1987, p. 124]])
- Adsorption of sulfonates increased significantly in the presence of crude oil, with middle phase microemulsions showing more adsorption than oleic phase microemulsions. ([[1987-ahmed|Ahmed, 1987, p. 124]])
- The adsorption of surfactants on limestone showed marginal change above ZPC (pH=8.3) and extremely high adsorption below pH 8.3 where the surface becomes positively charged. ([[1987-ahmed|Ahmed, 1987, p. 22]])
- The zero point of charge (ZPC) for limestone was determined to be 8.3, consistent with literature values for pure calcite (8-9). ([[1987-ahmed|Ahmed, 1987, p. 57]])
- At pH levels below 9.0, where the surface is positively charged, adsorption of sulfonate is high; above pH 9.0, adsorption becomes relatively insensitive to pH as the surface becomes negatively charged. ([[1987-ahmed|Ahmed, 1987, p. 66]])
- The addition of 1% NaCl caused precipitation of TRS10-410 at approximately 1 g/l concentration; 5% NaCl caused severe precipitation starting at 0.01 g/l. ([[1987-ahmed|Ahmed, 1987, p. 104]])
- The solubility product (Ks) for calcium disulfonate was calculated as 1.04 x 10^7 at 25°C and 6.94 x 10^8 at 90°C. ([[1987-ahmed|Ahmed, 1987, p. 112]])
- Ethoxylated sulfonates (e.g., V2880) showed higher salt tolerance than petroleum sulfonates, but still exhibited significant precipitation at high temperatures and salinities. ([[1987-ahmed|Ahmed, 1987, p. 115]])
- The addition of n-butanol was found to reduce the phase separation temperature (PST) in some systems, though its effect on adsorption was complex due to competing mechanisms of solubility enhancement and site competition. ([[1987-ahmed|Ahmed, 1987, p. 118]])

## Limitations

The study notes that some results (e.g., those involving complex mixtures or specific high-temperature conditions) are limited by the lack of perfectly defined systems, such as pure calcite and single surfactants at temperatures below 90°C.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Mix petroleum sulfonates with ethoxylated sulfonates to incorporate the redissolution feature of petroleum sulfonates into ethoxylated sulfonates.
- Prioritize adsorption studies on higher ethoxylation number sulfonates along with phase behavior studies.
- Test other sacrificial agents like tripolyphosphate, tetrasodium pyrophosphate, and sodium carbonate.
- Use other alcohols of varying chain lengths to evaluate their effect on adsorption.
- Conduct experiments on a well-defined system (pure calcite and pure anionic surfactant) at temperatures below 90 °C and salinities below 20% to clarify mechanisms.
