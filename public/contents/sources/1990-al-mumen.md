# The Effect of Injected Water Salinity on Oil Recovery

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of injected water salinity on oil recovery using Berea sandstone cores and Arabian Light crude oil. It concludes that oil recovery increases with salinity up to 100,000 mg/l, supported by a decrease in interfacial tension (IFT) and an increase in the relative permeability ratio.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the challenge of selecting optimal injection water quality (specifically salinity) for waterflooding in the Arab-D reservoir to maximize oil recovery while avoiding chemical incompatibilities between injected waters (Sea, Wasia) and formation water.

## Objectives

- Investigate the effect of injected water salinity on oil recovery.
- Investigate the effect of incompatibility of sea water and Wasia water with formation Arab-D water on oil recovery
- Evaluate the effect of increasing NaCl brine salinity on oil recovery.
- Compare the performance of actual water samples (Sea, Wasia, and Arab-D) against standard NaCl brines.
- Analyze the influence of interfacial tension (IFT) and viscosity ratio on displacement efficiency.
- Investigate the impact of specific ions (Ca2+, Mg2+, etc.) on oil recovery and compatibility.

## Methods

Coreflooding experiments were conducted at 90°C and 1500 psi using Berea sandstone cores. Five types of brines (NaCl concentrations from 0.55% to 20%, Wasia water, Sea water, and 100,000 mg/l synthetic Arab-D water) were used as displacement fluids. Analytical techniques included ICP for ion analysis, pendant drop method for IFT measurement, and core flooding in a Hassler-type holder.

## Data and Materials

Berea sandstone cores (1 inch diameter, 1 inch length), multiple brine runs with varying NaCl concentrations (5,500 to 200,000 mg/l), three actual water samples (Wasia, Sea, and Arab-D), and various ion concentration measurements.

## Key Findings

- Oil recovery increases as the injected water salinity increases up to 100,000 mg/l. ([[1990-al-mumen|Al-Mumen, 1990, p. 22]])
- Increasing the injected water salinity beyond 10% did not give a significant increase in oil recovery. ([[1990-al-mumen|Al-Mumen, 1990, p. 152]])
- The increase in oil recovery was supported by a decrease in IFT as a function of water salinity up to 100,000 mg/l. ([[1990-al-mumen|Al-Mumen, 1990, p. 22]])
- The relative permeability ratio (Kro/Krw) was found to increase as the injected water salinity increases. ([[1990-al-mumen|Al-Mumen, 1990, p. 22]])
- Compatibility tests showed that when connate Arab-D water was mixed with sea water at reservoir temperature, calcium sulphate precipitated. ([[1990-al-mumen|Al-Mumen, 1990, p. 153]])
- Analysis of the coreflooding effluents did not show any incompatibility problems due to injection of Wasia, sea, and 100,000 mg/l Arab-D waters. ([[1990-al-mumen|Al-Mumen, 1990, p. 153]])
- Berea cores were fired at 900°C for 18 hours to stabilize clay content. ([[1990-al-mumen|Al-Mumen, 1990, p. 37]])
- The core samples showed a porosity of 22.97% to 24.64% and permeability of 565 to 728 md. ([[1990-al-mumen|Al-Mumen, 1990, p. 37]])
- IFT values for various brines were measured using the pendant drop method at 90°C. ([[1990-al-mumen|Al-Mumen, 1990, p. 43]])
- Viscosity of brines increased linearly with salinity from approximately 0.3 to 0.6 cP. ([[1990-al-mumen|Al-Mumen, 1990, p. 44]])
- Oil recovery increased with increasing injected water salinity up to 60,000 mg/l; beyond this point, the increase in breakthrough recovery was not significant. ([[1990-al-mumen|Al-Mumen, 1990, p. 64]])
- The difference in oil recovery between runs decreased as the injected water salinity increased (Fig. 4.3). ([[1990-al-mumen|Al-Mumen, 1990, p. 64]])
- Oil recovery from actual water runs was similar across different types, with a very small difference of 2.46% IOIP between Run #7 (sea water) and Run # 9 (100,000 mg/l Arab-D). ([[1990-al-mumen|Al-Mumen, 1990, p. 70]])
- The reduction in IFT from 16.5 to 6.5 dyne/cm was identified as a primary driver for increased oil recovery. ([[1990-al-mumen|Al-Mumen, 1990, p. 75]])
- Viscosity ratio decreased with increasing water salinity, which contributed to improved mobility of the oil phase. ([[1990-al-mumen|Al-Mumen, 1990, p. 75]])

## Limitations

The study notes that while IFT is a key factor, its influence is only clearly observed when values are below certain thresholds; however, the specific threshold for this system was not explicitly quantified beyond the observation of trends.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The effect of injected water salinity on oil recovery under different injection rates using actual limestone core samples should be investigated.
