# Improved CO2 Flooding for Enhanced Heavy Oil Recovery

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the use of ionic liquids (specifically Sx1 and Sx4) to reduce Minimum Miscibility Pressure (MMP) and Flow Control Mobility Pressure (FCMP) in CO2-oil systems for heavy oil recovery. The study concludes that ionic liquids are more effective than commercial chemicals at lower concentrations, particularly for crudes with high asphaltene content at elevated temperatures.

---

## Research Problem

Heavy oil reservoirs at great depths (e.g., Burgan field in Kuwait) are difficult to treat with thermal EOR methods due to heat loss and complexity. There is a need for an alternative method like CO2 flooding, which requires lowering the MMP to avoid fracturing the formation while achieving miscibility.

## Objectives

- Perform screening on different substances (micro-emulsions, non-ionic surfactants, hydrophilic silica nanoparticles, n-butanol, xylene, di-limonene, 1-methyl-3-octylimidazolium chloride, and 1-decyl-3-methylimidazolium chloride) to assess their ability to improve CO2 miscibility in crude oil.
- Evaluate the capability of several chemicals to reduce MMP in CO2-oil systems to enhance heavy oil recovery, along with their effect on crude oil viscosity.
- Determine the MMP between Saudi crude oils and CO2 before and after chemical addition using the vanishing interfacial tension (VIT) technique.
- Identify an optimum chemical and concentration to reduce both MMP and first-contact miscibility pressure (FCMP).
- Investigate the effect of crude oil composition, temperature, and chemical concentrations on the CO2 miscibility.
- Evaluate the impact of different chemicals (BL, NIS, XY, DL) on MMP and FCMP.

## Methods

The study utilized a screening process involving several chemicals (NIS, Silica NPs, n-butanol, Xylene, D-limonene, and Ionic Liquids Sx1 and Sx4) to determine their impact on MMP and viscosity. Techniques included: 1) Density measurement using an Anton Paar DMA 4500M; 2) Viscosity measurement using a Modular Compact Rheometer (MCR 702); 3) SARA analysis via TLC and FID (Iatroscan MK-7); 4) Total Acid Number (TAN) determination via potentiometric titration (Metrohm Ti-touch 916); 5) Vanishing Interfacial Tension (VIT) and pendant drop methods using a Drop Shape Analyzer (KRÜSS DSA 100 HP) to determine MMP and First Contact Miscibility Pressure (FCMP).

## Data and Materials

The study evaluates five different Saudi crude oils. Chemical additives tested include micro-emulsions, non-ionic surfactants, hydrophilic silica nanoparticles, n-butanol, xylene, di-limonene, 1-methyl-3-octylimidazolium chloride (an ionic liquid), and 1-decyl-3-methylimidazolium chloride.

## Key Findings

- Ionic liquids were identified as the optimum chemicals to reduce both MMP and FCMP in the CO2-crude oil system for three of the five Saudi crudes. ([[2023-al-hakami|Al-Hakami, 2023, p. 20]])
- The addition of ionic liquids resulted in a reduction of 15.5% in MMP in the extra heavy crude oil system. ([[2023-al-hakami|Al-Hakami, 2023, p. 22]])
- Non-ionic surfactants were found to be more effective than fatty acids or alcohols for reducing MMP; specifically, propoxylated surfactants reduced MMP from 19.1 to 13.8 MPa (a 27.7% reduction). ([[2023-al-hakami|Al-Hakami, 2023, p. 56]])
- Alcohols were shown to reduce the interfacial tension between CO2 and crude oil, with branched alcohols providing a greater reduction than linear ones. ([[2023-al-hakami|Al-Hakami, 2023, p. 53]])
- The inclusion of chemicals as a pre-slug resulted in a larger recovery factor compared to incorporating them within the CO2 injection stream. ([[2023-al-hakami|Al-Hakami, 2023, p. 56]])
- Crude 3 was identified as the median crude for screening, with a viscosity of 23.5 cP at 50°C. ([[2023-al-hakami|Al-Hakami, 2023, p. 96]])
- Addition of 0.75 wt.% NIS to Crude 3 increased its viscosity from 23.49 cP to 29.09 cP at 50°C, showing a negative impact on viscosity. ([[2023-al-hakami|Al-Hakami, 2023, p. 98]])
- Addition of 10 wt.% BL (n-butanol) reduced Crude 3 viscosity by 44.9% at 50°C compared to the base case. ([[2023-al-hakami|Al-Hakami, 2023, p. 100]])
- The optimal concentration for reducing both viscosity and ensuring low asphaltene precipitation (low CII) was determined to be 2.5 wt.% of BL. ([[2023-al-hakami|Al-Hakami, 2023, p. 103]])
- Addition of 0.6 wt.% NIS resulted in an increase of MMP by 24.9% for the system including nanoparticles, making it unsuitable for industrial use. ([[2023-al-hakami|Al-Hakami, 2023, p. 105]])
- The addition of 10 wt.% DL (d-limonene) reduced MMP from 1784.5 psi to 1421.8 psi (20.34% reduction). ([[2023-al-hakami|Al-Hakami, 2023, p. 119]])
- Ionic liquid Sx1 at 1 wt.% concentration reduced MMP by 17.27% and FCMP by 30.14% in Crude 3. ([[2023-al-hakami|Al-Hakami, 2023, p. 123]])
- For Crude 2, the addition of 0.5 wt.% Sx1 at 90°C reduced FCMP from 2973.5 to 2674.9 psi (10.04% reduction). ([[2023-al-hakami|Al-Hakami, 2023, p. 130]])
- Ionic liquids (Sx1 and Sx4) were identified as the most effective chemicals for reducing MMP and FCMP with the lowest required concentrations compared to commercial chemicals. ([[2023-al-hakami|Al-Hakami, 2023, p. 138]])
- Sx1 showed a superior reduction in MMP compared to Sx4. ([[2023-al-hakami|Al-Hakami, 2023, p. 138]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Solubility of chemicals in scCO2 should be tested using a PVT system.
- MMP and FCMP should be measured between crude oil and CO2 where the chemicals are actually solubilized.
- CO2 flooding should be implemented in the early stages of production for deep heavy oil reservoirs.
