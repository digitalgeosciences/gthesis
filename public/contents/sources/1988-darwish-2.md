# Pleistocene-holocene sedimentation along King fahd Causeway between Saudi Arabia and Bahrain.

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the sedimentary history and diagenetic processes of Pleistocene-Holocene rocks along the King Fahd Causeway. It identifies two main mineralogical units: a lower dolomite interval (up to 40m thick) and an upper non-dolomitic interval, correlating these to specific sea level changes in the Arabian Gulf.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the lack of detailed research on Pleistocene-Holocene sediments in the Saudi Arabian shallow shelf area, specifically focusing on the complex mineralogical units (dolomite vs. non-dolomite) and their corresponding depositional environments at the entrance of the Gulf of Salwa.

## Objectives

- To determine the compositional and textural characteristics of the Pleistocene-Holocene sediments deposited between Saudi Arabia and Bahrain.
- To understand the nature and distribution of these sediments.
- To reveal the diagenetic processes in the carbonate sequences of these sediments.
- To integrate and use the obtained data in interpretation of the depositional and diagenetic history of these sediments, which will in turn contribute to an understanding of Pleistocene-Holocene sea level changes in this part of the Arabian Gulf.
- Identify mineralogical units within the core samples along the King Fahd Causeway.
- Determine the depositional environment for both dolomitic and non-dolomitic lithofacies.

## Methods

The study utilized 10 rock cores (up to 44m long) from the King Fahd Causeway project. Petrographic analysis was performed on 100 samples prepared as thin sections and stained with Alizarin Red "S". Mineralogical composition was determined using X-ray Powder Diffraction (XRD) on 50 samples, including glycolation and heating to identify clay minerals. Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) and Energy Dispersive X-Ray Fluorescence (EDS) were used for detailed textural and compositional data of 16 selected samples. Sieve analysis was conducted to determine grain size distribution.

## Data and Materials

10 rock cores up to 44 meters long; 100 rock samples for petrographic analysis; 50 samples for XRD mineralogical analysis; 16 samples for SEM-EDS analysis; core data from 3 boreholes (under bridges 1, 2, and 3).

## Key Findings

- The study identified two main mineralogical units: a lower dolomite interval (~40m thick) and an upper non-dolomite interval (3-9m thick) consisting of quartz sandstone, calcitic skeletal grainstone, and aragonitic oolitic/skeletal grains. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 19]])
- The dolomite lithofacies are characterized by several depositional cycles with upward shallowing sequences (skeletal packstone to green mudstone) and evidence of subaerial exposure (desiccation breccias). ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 19]])
- Aragonitic oolitic sand/grainstone contains approximately 60% aragonite, 20% calcite, and 20% quartz. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 48]])
- Quartz sandstone lithofacies contains approximately 75% quartz and 15% feldspar with a minor amount of aragonite. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 52]])
- Calcitic skeletal grainstone is characterized by high leaching, where molluscan shells are replaced by bladed sparry calcite cement (80% calcite, 20% quartz). ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 55]])
- Green mudstone contains 4-15% clay minerals, primarily palygorskite and illite, with the palygorskite being determined as authigenic. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 68]])
- Dolomitic oolitic grainstone shows evidence of intense diagenesis including dissolution-precipitation and calcitization (blocky calcite spar 20-40 um). ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 81]])
- The non-dolomite interval includes a transgression period (90,000-75,000 y.b.p.) and a major late Pleistocene regression. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 19]])
- The lower dolomite interval is approximately 40 meters thick and consists of several upward-shallowing cycles. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 122]])
- The upper non-dolomitic interval is 3 to 9 meters thick, consisting of interbedded quartz sandstone, calcitic skeletal grainstone, quartz sand, and aragonitic skeletal and oolitic sand. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 122]])
- Dolomitization was likely caused by seepage reflux of Mg-rich brines from coastal sabkhas. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 88]])
- Calcitization of dolomite occurred due to the seepage of Ca-rich fresh water during pluvial periods. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 98]])
- The green mudstone lithofacies are interpreted as being deposited in a restricted lagoonal environment with high salinity and potentially reducing conditions. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 111]])
- A quartz sandstone layer (up to 3m thick) represents an episode of sand dune migration during the brief Pleistocene regression (110,000-90,000 y.b.p.). ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 112]])
- The calcitic skeletal grainstone was originally aragonitic and deposited during a minor transgression between 90,000 and 70,000 y.b.p. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 121]])
