# Performance of Fluoro-Surfactant with Supercritical CO2 Flooding in High Salinity Carbonate Reservoirs

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the performance of an amine oxide-based amphoteric fluoro-surfactant combined with supercritical CO2 (sc-CO2) for enhanced oil recovery in high-salinity carbonate reservoirs. Using core-flood experiments on Indiana Limestone cores, it evaluates three injection strategies to determine optimal recovery and the role of surfactant in reducing interfacial tension.

---

## Research Problem

The primary challenge is the instability of standard surfactants under extreme reservoir conditions (high temperature and high salinity) and the poor mobility control of supercritical CO2 (leading to gravity override and viscous fingering). This study addresses these issues by testing a novel fluoro-surfactant specifically designed for stability in harsh environments.

## Objectives

- Evaluate sc-CO2/Fluorosurfactant as an EOR solution applicable to Saudi Reservoirs.
- Conduct core-flooding experiments to study oil recovery from limestone rock using sc-CO2/Fluorosurfactant system.
- Determine the optimum injection strategy (continuous, alternating, or co-injection).
- Perform Interfacial Tension tests to specifically ascertain the role of the surfactant in the increment of oil recovery.
- Evaluate the performance of fluoro-surfactant with supercritical CO2 flooding in high salinity conditions.
- Compare different injection strategies: base case (CO2 only), alternating surfactant and CO2, and co-injection.

## Methods

The study utilized a modified coreflooding rig designed for sc-CO2 and surfactants. Core samples were Indiana Limestone (19% porosity, 70 mD permeability). Two types of brines (Formation/Connate and Sea Water) were prepared with high TDS (up to 213,734 ppm). Uthmaniya Dead Oil was used as the reservoir fluid. The experimental setup included a Quizix pumping system for precise flow control, an oven to maintain temperature/pressure, and several high-pressure transfer cells. Three injection strategies were tested: continuous sc-CO2 flooding, alternating injection of sc-CO2 and surfactant solution, and co-injection of sc-CO2 and surfactant solution.

## Data and Materials

The study used a core-flooding system, a fluoro-surfactant at a concentration of 0.15% (vol.) in injection brine (sea water), and 'dead oil' for IFT measurements. Data points were collected across pressure ranges from 500 to 4000 psi.

## Key Findings

- The core samples (Indiana Limestone) exhibited a porosity of 19% and permeability of 70 mD. ([[2014-jangda|Jangda, 2014, p. 36]])
- Uthmaniya Dead Oil had an API gravity of 30.1° at room conditions. ([[2014-jangda|Jangda, 2014, p. 39]])
- The core-flood study showed an increase in oil recovery for the novel surfactant-CO2 system compared to standard methods. ([[2014-jangda|Jangda, 2014, p. 13]])
- Interfacial tension (IFT) was found to decrease as the concentration of surfactant increased for both tested systems. ([[2014-jangda|Jangda, 2014, p. 27]])
- Supercritical CO2 at experimental conditions (1800 psi and 90°C) had a density of 0.3 g/cc and viscosity of 0.025 cp. ([[2014-jangda|Jangda, 2014, p. 22]])
- Residual oil presence increased the adsorption of CD 1045 by about 32% in previous studies. ([[2014-jangda|Jangda, 2014, p. 31]])
- A decrease in foam mobility was observed with increasing foam quality between ranges of 33.3% to 80%. ([[2014-jangda|Jangda, 2014, p. 31]])
- The study confirmed that a novel surfactant-CO2 system can mitigate the high cost of surfactants by allowing for use at very low concentrations. ([[2014-jangda|Jangda, 2014, p. 13]])
- The base case of CO2 flooding alone resulted in a significant increase in oil recovery compared to initial waterflooding. ([[2014-jangda|Jangda, 2014, p. 78]])
- In the alternating strategy, Experiment 3 (larger CO2 slug) showed a higher increment in recovery factor (~34%) than Experiment 2 (equal slugs). ([[2014-jangda|Jangda, 2014, p. 79]])
- Experiment 4 (larger surfactant slug before CO2) achieved the highest total oil recovery of 69.7% and showed rapid recovery in early cycles. ([[2014-jangda|Jangda, 2014, p. 73]])
- Co-injection with fluoro-surfactant (Experiment 5) yielded a higher recovery (66.4%) than co-injection with ethomeen surfactant (Experiment 6, 58%). ([[2014-jangda|Jangda, 2014, p. 81]])
- IFT values for sea water + oil were significantly reduced from ~19 mN/m to ~5 mN/m when fluoro-surfactant was added. ([[2014-jangda|Jangda, 2014, p. 88]])
- A stable foam was observed in the continuous fluoro-surfactant and CO2 flooding experiment, indicated by a sustained high pressure drop (delta P) for over 1.2 PV. ([[2014-jangda|Jangda, 2014, p. 83]])
- The ethomeen surfactant showed no signs of stable foam and was less effective in high salinity/temperature conditions. ([[2014-jangda|Jangda, 2014, p. 83]])

## Limitations

The study notes that the mechanism of foam generation and propagation inside the reservoir remains an ambiguous subject; also, surfactant adsorption on rock was not fully addressed in this specific scope.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Conduct visual foam tests at reservoir conditions with various surfactants to find a stable foam system.
- Perform adsorption tests between selected surfactants and rock to address the reduction of surfactant effectiveness due to adsorption.
- Conduct IFT experiments using different concentrations and multiple surfactants at variable salinities.
