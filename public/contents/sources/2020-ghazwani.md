# FENTON-BASED ELECTROCHEMICAL REMOVAL OF AMMONIA FROM HIGHLY SALINE WATER

**Summary**: The thesis investigated the removal of ammonia from saline water using electrochemical advanced oxidation processes (EAOP) with BDD and DSA anodes. It concluded that high electrical conductivity significantly enhances ammonia removal through indirect oxidation via chlorine species, with both BDD and DSA electrodes achieving 100% removal in seawater-like conditions.

---

## Research Problem

Ammonia is a significant water pollutant in aquatic environments, leading to the formation of nitrite and nitrate. Traditional treatment methods often struggle with these pollutants in high salinity contexts where ammonia volatilization can also complicate measurement and treatment efficiency.

## Objectives

- Evaluate the effects of various experimental conditions (current density, feed solution conductivity, feed flow rate, Fe2+ dosage) on ammonia degradation.
- Compare the performance of BDD and DSA anodes in removing ammonia from saline water.
- Identify the optimal operating conditions for complete ammonia degradation using F-EAOP.
- Quantify the formation of ammonia degradation by-products (nitrate and nitrite).
- To investigate the efficiency of ammonia removal from saline water using Fenton-based electrochemical advanced oxidation processes using BDD anode and GDE cathode.
- To investigate the effect of water chemistry on the efficiency of ammonia removal (i.e. conductivity, pH, initial ammonia concentration).
- To determine the optimum treatment conditions using Fe-EAOPs process (i.e. current density, Fe2+ dosage, gas (air) flow rate, feed water circulation rate and treatment time).
- To identify the fate of ammonia degradation by-products (i.e. nitrite, nitrate and nitrogen gas).
- To conduct a kinetic study of ammonia removal under the optimum conditions.
- Evaluate the removal efficiency of ammonia using BDD and DSA anodes in various saline water matrices.
- Investigate the effect of electric current on ammonia oxidation rates.
- Assess the impact of feed water conductivity on the electrochemical removal of ammonia.
- Identify and quantify ammonia degradation by-products (nitrate, nitrogen).
- Perform kinetic studies to determine the rate constants for ammonia removal under optimal conditions.
- Evaluate Fenton-based electrochemical removal of ammonia from highly saline water
- Investigate advanced oxidation processes (AOPs) for wastewater treatment

## Methods

The study utilized an Electrochemical Treatment System (ETS) pilot plant featuring a multi-purpose plate-and-frame electrochemical cell with BDD anode and carbon-PTFE GDE cathode. Experimental procedures included preliminary investigations in open tanks to assess effects of current, conductivity, and iron concentration, followed by sealed tank experiments to determine optimal conditions and minimize ammonia loss due to volatilization. Analytical measurements for nitrate, nitrite, and ammonium were performed using a dual column ion chromatograph (Metrohm 850 Professional IC).

## Data and Materials

The study included 60 experimental runs (labeled #1 to #60). Feed water matrices included fresh water (FW), brackish water (BW), brackish water with added NaCl (BW+NaCl), and sea water (SW). Initial ammonia concentrations ranged from approximately 180 mg/L to 301 mg/L. Conductivity values tested were 400, 3250, 20700, and 43300 μS/cm.

## Key Findings

- The optimum operating conditions for BDD anode were found to be 60 mA/cm2, pH 7.5, 0.4 m3/h water circulation rate, and feed water electrical conductivity of 43300 ± µS/cm within 2 hours. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 15]])
- Similar results for DSA anode were achieved at 60 mA/cm2, pH 7.5, 0.4 m3/h water circulation rate, and feed water electrical conductivity of 41500 ± µS/cm within 2 hours. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 15]])
- An applied current of 120 mA/cm2 showed the greatest effect on ammonia removal when using the BDD anode, whereas the DSA anode did not show a similar increase in efficiency. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 15]])
- Nitrate (NO3) and nitrite (NO2) were identified as degradation by-products; NO2 ions were not produced while NO3 ions reached a concentration of 73 mg/L under optimum conditions. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 16]])
- The selectivity of N2 was measured and found to be 81.5% at the optimum operating conditions. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 16]])
- Blank runs showed that ammonia volatilization accounted for approximately 18-19% loss after 3 hours in an open tank setup without applied current. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 23]])
- Ammonia removal increased with higher electric currents, with 4.0 A and 6.0 A achieving approximately 50% removal after 3 hours of treatment. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 26]])
- Note: The text mentions current densities as 40 mA/cm^2 in the description but uses Amperes (A) in the results tables/figures. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020]])
- Current density of 40 mA/cm^2 was identified as providing optimal results for ammonia oxidation and electric charge consumption. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 26]])
- Feed water conductivity influenced ammonia removal more significantly than electric current; at 23,000 µS/cm, ammonia was reduced by nearly 70% after 3 hours. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 29]])
- At 9,800 µS/cm conductivity, ammonia removal reached approximately 74% after 180 minutes of electrolysis. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 29]])
- The addition of iron ions (Fe2+) did not significantly improve ammonia removal in the open tank system because the absence of compressed air limited H2O2 production for Fenton oxidation. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 31]])
- Ammonia removal increased with current intensity; for the BDD anode, removal reached 91% at 12.0 A in brackish water. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 52]])
- High electrical conductivity (20700 μS/cm and 43300 μS/cm) resulted in 100% ammonia removal within 2 hours for both BDD and DSA anodes. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 55]])
- The study determined that indirect oxidation via chlorine species is the primary mechanism for ammonia removal in high-conductivity water. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 56]])

## Limitations

The study noted that high current density (e.g., 12A) leads to higher energy consumption and potential issues with lower current efficiency due to secondary reactions like polarization and oxygen evolution.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Study the removal of other pollutants, such as total organic carbon (TOC), in the presence of ammonia.
- Conduct an economic study on different power sources for treatment.
- Evaluate EAOPs as a post-treatment unit in various water treatment processes.
- Select anodes based on specific water conditions; DSA is preferred for high conductivity due to lower costs compared to BDD.
