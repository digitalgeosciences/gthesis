# FENTON-BASED ELECTROCHEMICAL REMOVAL OF AMMONIA FROM HIGHLY SALINE WATER

**Summary**: The thesis investigated the removal of ammonia from highly saline water using electrochemical advanced oxidation processes (EAOP) with BDD and DSA anodes. It concluded that high electrical conductivity in seawater significantly enhances ammonia degradation through hydroxyl radicals and oxidizing chlorine radicals, achieving 100% removal within 2 hours.

---

## Research Problem

High ammonia contamination in shallow and saline groundwater in the Eastern Province of Saudi Arabia poses significant environmental risks. Existing treatment methods often suffer from high costs or poor efficiency in high- salinity environments, necessitating more effective electrochemical technologies.

## Objectives

- To investigate the efficiency of ammonia removal from saline water using Fenton-based electrochemical advanced oxidation processes using BDD anode and GDE cathode.
- To investigate the effect of water chemistry on the efficiency of ammonia removal (i.e. conductivity, pH, initial ammonia concentration).
- To determine the optimum treatment conditions using Fe-EAOPs process (i.e. current density, Fe2+ dosage, gas (air) flow rate, feed water circulation rate and treatment time).
- To identify the fate of ammonia degradation by-products (i.e. nitrite, nitrate and nitrogen gas).
- To conduct a kinetic study of ammonia study of ammonia removal under the optimum conditions.

## Methods

The research utilized an electrochemical pilot-scale plant with an undivided plate-and-frame cell. Two types of anodes were tested: Boron-doped Diamond (BDD) and Titanium-based Dimensional Stable Anode (Ti-DSA), paired with a carbon-PTFE gas diffusion electrode (GDE) as the cathode. Experiments were conducted in both open tank (preliminary investigation) and sealed tank setups to mitigate ammonia loss due to volatilization. Analytical measurements of nitrate, nitrite, and ammonium were performed using a dual column ion chromatograph (Metrohm 850 Professional IC). Mass balance calculations were used to determine nitrogen gas production.

## Data and Materials

The study included experiments with various water matrices (BW, BW+NaCl, SW) and different anode types (BDD, DSA). Data points included 16 specific experimental runs (#1-16 for BDD/DSA comparison) and kinetic studies involving ammonia concentrations ranging from 201 to 301 mg/L.

## Key Findings

- In open tank systems, ammonia loss due to volatilization was observed as high as 18-19% after 3 hours without applied current. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 23]])
- For the BDD anode in an open system, higher electric currents (4.0 A and 6.0 A) resulted in approximately 50% ammonia removal after 3 hours. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 26]])
- Feed water conductivity was found to be a more significant factor than current density; at 21,700 μS/cm and 43,300 μS/cm (seawater), 100% ammonia removal was achieved within 2 hours using the BDD anode. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 37]])
- The addition of iron ions (Fe2+) did not significantly improve ammonia removal in the open tank system because the absence of compressed air limited hydrogen peroxide production. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 31]])
- In a sealed tank setup with BDD anodes, current density of 12.0 A resulted in 91% ammonia removal after 3 hours. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 34]])
- The study identified that the primary mechanism for ammonia removal using BDD anodes was indirect oxidation via chlorine/hypochlorite species rather than direct electrochemical oxidation. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 37]])
- Under optimal conditions, nitrogen gas (N2) selection was found to be 81.5% at the optimum operating conditions. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 16]])
- Nitrite (NO2-) ions were not produced during treatment under optimized conditions. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 16]])
- High electrical conductivities (23,000 ϊ/cm and 41,500 ϊ/cm) resulted in 100% ammonia removal within 2 hours of electrolysis using a DSA anode. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 59]])
- At a moderate conductivity of 3050 ϊ/cm, approximately 24% of ammonia was removed after 30 minutes, increasing by only 10% over the next 150 minutes. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 59]])
- Nitrite was not produced as a byproduct; it was assumed to be rapidly oxidized to nitrate due to high chloride concentrations. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 65]])
- A nitrate concentration of approximately 42.7 mg/L was measured at 10 minutes, reaching 73.3 mg/L after a 3-hour run. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 65]])
- The selectivity of ammonia oxidation to nitrogen (N2) was calculated as 81.50% under optimum conditions. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 50]])
- First-order kinetic models provided a good fit for ammonia concentrations below 250 ppm, while second-order kinetics were more appropriate for concentrations above 250 ppm. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 70]])
- BDD anodes showed better results at high currents compared to DSA due to higher current efficiency and lower adsorption properties. ([[2020-ghazwani|Ghazwani, 2020, p. 55]])

## Limitations

The absence of compressed air in the open tank setup limited the formation of hydrogen peroxide, thereby preventing the observation of enhanced removal via Fenton-generated hydroxyl radicals.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future work should include studies on other pollutants (TOC), economic analyses of different energy sources, and testing with actual groundwater contaminated with high ammonia content. DSA anodes are recommended for high-conductivity water due to lower costs compared to BDD.
