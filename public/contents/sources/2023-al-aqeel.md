# USING THE ELECTROCHEMICAL TREATMENT OF A WET-AIR OXIDIZED SPENT CAUSTIC WASTE STREAMS FOR POTENTIAL REUSE OR SAFE DISPOSAL

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the electrochemical treatment of spent caustic waste streams from ethylene plants and oil refineries to reduce Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD) for safe disposal or reuse. The study utilizes a combination of anodic oxidation and electro-Fenton processes using boron-doped diamond (BDD) electrodes, concluding that optimal conditions (pH 4, 150 mA/cm2 current density) can achieve over 90% COD removal.

---

## Research Problem

Spent caustic waste from oil refining and petrochemical facilities is hazardous due to its high COD, toxicity, and corrosive nature. While wet air oxidation (WAO) is used as a pre-treatment, the resulting effluent often fails to meet strict environmental discharge limits (COD < 50 mg/L), necessitating an advanced post-treatment step.

## Objectives

- Identify the optimum operational parameters of authentic spent caustic treatment including initial COD concentration, pH, conductivity, electric current, circulation flow rate, airflow rate, addition of iron, and electrolysis time.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of a Fenton-based Electrochemical Advanced Oxidation Process (F-EAOP) in reducing COD levels.
- Collection and characterization of batches of wet air oxidized ethylene plant spent caustic effluents.
- Evaluation of the efficiency of COD removal under various conditions of current, circulation rate, air flow rates, pH, and Fe content with air.
- Identification of the optimum treatment conditions.
- Treat undiluted spent caustic (WAO-treated effluent) from an ethylene production plant to achieve maximum COD reduction.
- Identify optimum operating conditions for the electrochemical treatment system (pH, current density, circulation rate, Fe dosage, and air flow).
- Evaluate the effectiveness of different anode types (BDD vs. DSA) on COD removal.
- Assess the economic feasibility of the electrochemical treatment process.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of electrochemical treatment on spent caustic waste streams.
- Optimize operational parameters including pH, current density, iron dosage, and air rate for maximum COD removal.
- Assess the energy consumption and cost-effectiveness of different electrochemical processes.
- Determine the potential for reusing treated spent caustic waste.

## Methods

The study utilized a two-stage treatment process: first, wet air oxidation (WAO) to pre-treat spent caustic, followed by an electrochemical treatment pilot system (ETS). The ETS included an Electro MP Cell with carbon-PTFE GDE as the cathode and BDD as the anode. Analytical measurements were performed using a Metrohm 850 Professional IC for ions, HANNA HI 83224 photometer for COD, Analytikjena multi N/C analyzer for TOC, and a PCD 650 conductivity meter. A SCADA system with PLC and HMI was used to monitor parameters like pH, temperature, pressure, and ORP.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized 'authentic' spent caustic waste streams from an ethylene plant that had been pre-treated by wet air oxidation (WAO). The pilot system was equipped with BDD and GDE electrodes. Experimental parameters included varying pH, current density (up to 150 mA/cm2), circulation rates (e.g., 0.2 m3/h), and iron dosages.

## Key Findings

- Under near-optimal conditions (pH 4, current density of 150 mA/cm2, and a circulation rate of 0.2 m3/h), COD was reduced from 860 mg/L to 57 mg/L in 4 hours of electrolysis. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 14]])
- The addition of iron (II) and air did not significantly affect the COD abatement during the treatment process. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 16]])
- Spent caustic from ethylene plants typically contains high concentrations of sodium hydrosulfide, sodium bicarbonate, and sodium hydroxide, with COD levels often in the tens of thousands ppm. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 20]])
- Wet air oxidation (WAO) can convert sulfur species to more benign forms like sodium sulfate and oxidize complex chemical molecules into carbon dioxide and biodegradable short-chain organic compounds. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 23]])
- The study identified that a post-treatment step is necessary because WAO alone may not meet the strict environmental discharge limit of COD 50 mg/L. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 13]])
- The initial concentration of COD in the WAO-treated spent caustic was 670 mg/L. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 39]])
- The initial TOC concentration in the WAO-treated sample was 251 mg/L. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 39]])
- The initial pH of the spent caustic was 10. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 39]])
- Note: The thesis evaluates the feasibility of using BDD electrodes due to their high resistance to corrosion and ability to produce strong oxidizing species (O3, H2O2, and \u00b7OH). ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023]])
- The study confirms that electrochemical oxidation can be a cost-effective alternative to biological treatment for removing organic contaminants. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 24]])
- WAO process typically treats spent caustic at temperatures of 120-220 C in ethylene plants. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 25]])
- The highest COD removal was achieved at pH 4 (approx. 91%) compared to pH 7 and 10, attributed to the speciation of organics and formation of sulfate radicals. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 60]])
- Current density reached a plateau in oxidation power at approximately 15 A (150 mA/cm2); higher currents did not significantly improve COD removal but increased costs. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 63]])
- Circulation rates of 0.1, 0.2, and 0.3 m3/h showed that 0.2 m3/h was the optimal balance for mass transfer and residence time. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 65]])
- Fe(II) dosage did not improve COD removal at pH 4; in some cases, it decreased the reaction rate due to scavenging effects. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 67]])

## Limitations

The study noted that high current values can lead to parasitic reactions like oxygen evolution, which reduces current efficiency. Additionally, the use of certain anodes (DSA) was less effective than BDD for this specific waste stream.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Conduct experiments at smaller intervals of pH to reduce chemical usage and improve safety.
- Increase treatment time beyond 4 hours.
- Implement a hybrid process combining EO with activated carbon adsorption to remove remaining COD traces below 50 mg/L.
- Dilute EO-treated effluent with low-COD waste streams if available.
