# USING THE ELECTROCHEMICAL TREATMENT OF A WET-AIR OXIDIZED SPENT CAUSTIC WASTE STREAMS FOR POTENTIAL REUSE OR SAFE DISPOSAL

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the treatment of spent caustic waste streams from ethylene plants, which are hazardous and high in chemical oxygen demand (COD). It evaluates a two-stage process involving wet air oxidation (WAO) followed by a Fenton-based Electrochemical Advanced Oxidation Process (F-EAOP) using Boron Doped Diamond (BDD) and Gas Diffusion Electrodes (GDE) to reduce COD levels for potential reuse or safe disposal.

---

## Research Problem

Spent caustic waste streams from oil refining and petrochemical facilities are hazardous due to high concentrations of sulfides, mercaptans, and other organic compounds. Existing pre-treatments like wet air oxidation (WAO) often fail to meet strict environmental discharge limits (COD < 50 mg/L), necessitating a secondary treatment step.

## Objectives

- Collection and characterization of batches of wet air oxidized ethylene plant spent caustic effluents.
- Evaluation of the efficiency of COD removal under various conditions of current, circulation rate, air flow rates, pH, and Fe content with air.
- Identification of the optimum treatment conditions.
- Reduce COD to a level that meets restricted regulations for safe discharge
- Determine if the electrochemical approach can be used for spent caustic treatment
- Identify optimum operation conditions (pH, current, flow, Fe dosage, air) for the EO process

## Methods

The study utilized a pilot-scale Electrochemical Treatment System (ETS) equipped with Boron Doped Diamond (BDD) and Gas Diffusion Electrodes (GDE). Analytical instruments included a Metrohm 850 Professional IC for ion analysis, an Analytikjena multi N/C analyzer for TOC measurement, and a HANNA photometer for COD determination. The experimental design followed a sequential approach to optimize parameters: pH (4, 7, 10), current density (up to 200 mA/cm2), circulation rate (0.1-0.3 m3/h), Fe(II) dosage (0-1.5 mM), and air flow rates.

## Data and Materials

A 40 L sample of spent caustic from an ethylene plant in Jubail Industrial City, Saudi Arabia, pre-treated by WAO. Chemical reagents included Mercury (II) sulfate, Sulfuric acid, Potassium hydrogen phthalate, and Iron (II) sulfate.

## Key Findings

- The initial COD of the WAO-treated spent caustic was 670 mg/L with a TOC of 251 mg/L. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 39]])
- Under optimal conditions (pH 4, current density 150 mA/cm2, circulation rate 0.2 m3/h), COD was reduced from 860 mg/L to 57 mg/L in 4 hours of electrolysis. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 14]])
- The addition of iron (II) and air did not improve COD removal at pH 4, as the reaction rate decreased due to scavenging effects. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 65]])
- At a flow rate of 0.2 m3/h, the system achieved high enough circulation to ensure rapid homogenization and optimal mass transfer to electrodes. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 64]])
- The highest COD removal (approx. 91%) was observed at pH 4 compared to pH 7 and 10. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 60]])
- Current values of 15 A (150 mA/cm2) were found sufficient for maximum oxidation, as higher currents led to parasitic reactions like oxygen evolution. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 62]])
- WAO can reduce the initial COD of spent caustic effluent (approx. 2,500 mg/L) by 70%, down to approx. 700 mg/L. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 81]])
- The best results obtained were a residual COD of approx. 67 mg/L (91% COD removal) at pH 4, 150 mA/cm2 current density, and 0.2 m3/h circulation flow after 3 hours. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 81]])
- After 4 hours of treatment under optimal conditions, the treated effluent had a residual COD of 57 mg/L (93% COD removal). ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 81]])
- The reaction behaves as a first-order reaction with an R² value of 0.9855. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 76]])
- The anode type had a dramatic effect; the less active DSA anode did not remove COD effectively compared to the BDD electrode. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 81]])
- Fe(II) dosage and air flow rate did not significantly affect the process efficiency. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 81]])
- The cost of electrical energy (CE) for treatment ranged from 0.41 to 1.14 USD/m3, with optimal conditions costing 0.81-0.89 USD/m3. ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 77]])
- The operational cost of EO (approx. 0.88-0.96 USD/m3) is significantly less expensive than the cost of transporting waste to the USA for treatment (1.23 $/gal). ([[2023-al-aqeel|Al-Aqeel, 2023, p. 81]])

## Limitations

The study notes that if the electrode is unable to tolerate the harsh environment of spent caustic (corrosion resistance and lifetime), it will be commercially impractical.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Conduct experiments at smaller intervals of pH to reduce chemical use and improve safety.
- Increase the electrolysis time beyond 4 hours.
- Implement a hybrid process combining EO with activated carbon adsorption for removing COD traces below 50 mg/L.
- Dilute EO-treated effluent with low-COD waste streams if necessary.
