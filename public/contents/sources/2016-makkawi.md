# 3D GEOSTATISTICAL MODELING OF FACIES AND PETROPHYSICAL PROPERTIES OF THE UPPER KHARTAM OUTCROP OF KHUFF FORMATION, CENTRAL SAUDI ARABIA

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the 3D geostatistical modeling of facies and petrophysical properties (porosity and permeability) in the Upper Khartam Member of the Khuff Formation. It utilizes outcrop data from the AtTraffiyah area to develop a 3D model using various geostatistical algorithms like IK, SIS, and TGS to characterize carbonate reservoir heterogeneity.

---

## Research Problem

The high degree of heterogeneity in carbonate reservoirs (specifically the Upper Khartam Member) due to depositional and diagenetic processes makes accurate petrophysical characterization difficult. The study aims to use outcrop data as a proxy for understanding these complexities through 3D geostatistical modeling.

## Objectives

- Analyze and group lithofacies into depositional sub-environments
- Measure porosity and permeability from core plug samples of outcrop sections
- Perform statistical analysis on porosity and permeability data
- Construct 3D geostatistical models for facies, porosity, and permeability
- Evaluate the impact of diagenesis and stratigraphic framework on reservoir quality
- To improve the understanding of spatial facies distribution by constructing 3D geostatistical models for lithofacies of the Upper Khartam member.
- To predict the petrophysical properties through constructing 3D geostatistical models for the porosity and permeability of the Upper Khartam member outcrop.
- To characterize reservoir quality and architecture.
- Analyze and group lithofacies into depositional sub-environments within the Upper Khartam Member.
- Measure and statistically analyze porosity and permeability from core plugs of outcrop samples.
- Perform mineralogical characterization using XRD and SEM to identify minerals and pore types.
- Develop a 3D geostatistical model for facies and petrophysical properties using Petrel software.
- Analyze and group lithofacies into distinct depositional sub-environments.
- Determine the distribution types and zonation of reservoir rocks using integrated lithofacies and petrophysical properties.
- Construct a 3D geostatistical model of facies to capture spatial continuity and heterogeneity.
- Model the 3D distribution of porosity and permeability based on lithofacies and diagenetic scenarios.
- To model the 3D geostatistical facies and petrophysical properties of the Upper Khartam outcrop.
- To analyze and categorize lithofacies into depositional sub-environments.
- To evaluate the impact of diagenetic stages on porosity evolution in oolitic grainstones.
- To provide predictive scenarios for subsurface reservoir characterization based on outcrop analogs.

## Methods

The study employed a scale-dependent approach, modeling from large-scale (3rd order) sequences down to lithofacies (4th order). Lithofacies were categorized into four groups: foreshoal, shoal complex, backshoal, and tidal flat. Geostatistical techniques included Indicator Kriging for deterministic modeling of lithofacies and Stochastic Simulation (Truncated Gaussian Simulation and Sequential Indicator Simulation) for stochastic modeling. Variogram analysis was used to determine the spatial variability of facies. Porosity and permeability were statistically analyzed and correlated against rock types (grainy, muddy-grainy, muddy-dominated).

## Data and Materials

Data includes outcrop samples from the Upper Khartam Member in central Saudi Arabia, including 11 measured sections. Petrophysical data consists of porosity and permeability measurements from core plugs. The study also incorporates comparison data from the South Pars Field of Iran for modeling scenarios.

## Key Findings

- Identified 11 distinct lithofacies (LF1-LF11) within the Upper Khartam Member, categorized into five cycle sets (4th order sequences). ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 30]])
- Porosity and permeability values showed specific patterns of distribution related to different rock types: grainy-dominated, muddy-grainy-dominated, and muddy-dominated. ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 60]])
- Diagenesis significantly impacted the stratigraphic sequences, porosity, and permeability, with varying degrees of impact (abundant, fair, or less) across different lithofacies. ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 51]])
- The study identified specific petrophysical properties for rock types: grainy-dominated, muddy-grainy-dominated, and muddy-dominated. ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 62]])
- Three different algorithms were used to construct 3D outcrop models, with results compared against input data for facies proportions. ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 85]])
- Facies were analyzed and grouped into four depositional sub-environments which stacked in five cycle sets (4th order sequences). ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 15]])
- The study area was divided into three broad intervals: lower (zones 1 and 2), middle (zones 3 and 4) and the upper interval (zone 5). ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 16]])
- Zone 1 and 2 represent foreshoal and shoal; zones 3 and 4 represent shoal and backshoal; zone 5 represents tidal flat deposits. ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 16]])

- The Upper Khartam Member is characterized by five cycle sets (4th order sequences) with an average thickness of approximately 6.2m. ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 57]])
- Lithofacies were categorized into 11 distinct types, including oolitic grainstones and microbial laminates, representing a carbonate ramp setting. ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 47]])
- Porosity and permeability measurements showed specific patterns of distribution that were analyzed to characterize the heterogeneity within potential reservoir units. ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 25]])
- Meteoric diagenesis significantly affected the boundaries between cycle sets, leading to the dissolution of aragonitic particles and the creation of moldic and intraparticle pores. ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 46]])
- Dolomitization was extensively developed in the supratidal flat sub-environment (Cycle set 5), enhancing porosity values through intercrystalline pore types. ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 48]])
- Lithofacies LF3 (Massive oolitic grainstone) showed a thickness of 10 to 50 cm and was interpreted as being deposited in a stabilized shoal sub-environment. ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 48]])

## Limitations

The small horizontal area of the model made it difficult to obtain accurate spatial correlation relationships, leading to uncertainty in the constructed semivariograms; however, this was mitigated by using a common variogram for all facies to reduce complexity.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future research should address the degree of variability of porosity evolution if ooids with three different original compositions (aragonite, calcite, and bimineralic) were present in a single reservoir.
