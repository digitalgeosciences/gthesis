# 3D GEOSTATISTICAL MODELING OF FACIES AND PETROPHYSICAL PROPERTIES OF THE UPPER KHARTAM OUTCROP OF KHUFF FORMATION, CENTRAL SAUDI ARABIA

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the 3D geostatistical modeling of lithofacies and petrophysical properties (porosity and permeability) in the Upper Khartam Member of the Khuff Formation. By integrating outcrop data, variogram analysis, and multiple geostatistical algorithms (IK, SIS, TGS), the study characterizes reservoir heterogeneity and provides predictive scenarios for porosity evolution based on ooid mineralogy.

---

## Research Problem

The high heterogeneity in carbonate reservoirs like the Upper Khartam Member of the Khuff Formation is driven by complex depositional and diagenetic processes. Accurately modeling these properties in 3D is essential to understand reservoir architecture and predict porosity distribution across different lithofacies and mineralogical types.

## Objectives

- To improve the understanding of spatial facies distribution by constructing 3D geostatistical models for lithofacies of the Upper Khartam member.
- To predict the petrophysical properties through constructing 3D geostatistical models for the porosity and permeability of the Upper Khartam member outcrop.
- To characterize reservoir quality and architecture.
- Analyze and group lithofacies into four depositional sub-environments within the Upper Khartam Member.
- Perform statistical analysis on porosity and permeability to identify distribution patterns and outliers.
- Construct variograms for different lithofacies to determine spatial continuity and heterogeneity.

## Methods

The study utilized 11 physical logs of outcrop sections across a 2km x 1km area. Petrophysical data from 244 samples were statistically analyzed to determine mean, variance, and distribution types. Variogram analysis was performed for each lithofacies to define spatial continuity (nugget, sill, range) in horizontal and vertical directions. A 3D grid of 1,555,500 cells (5x5m dimensions) was constructed using Petrel software. Three geostatistical algorithms—Indicator Kriging (IK), Sequential Indicator Simulation (SIS), and Truncated Gaussian Simulation (TGS)—were applied to model lithofacies in different zones based on their specific geological characteristics. Porosity evolution scenarios were modeled by integrating diagenetic paragenesis with ooid mineralogy.

## Data and Materials

11 outcrop sections sampled from the Upper Khartam Member; 23 stratigraphic sections used for geostatistical modeling; core plugs prepared from collected samples with dimensions of 2.5 cm diameter and 5.1 cm length; 3D model volume of 2km x 1.5km x 32m.

## Key Findings

- The Upper Khartam Member is characterized by five cycle sets (4th order sequences) with an average thickness of approximately 6.2m, representing a variety of depositional environments from subtidal to tidal flats. ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 57]])
- Eleven lithofacies were identified, including oolitic grainstones (LF3-LF5), packstones, and microbial laminates (LF11). ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 47]])
- Meteoric diagenesis extensively affected the boundaries between cycle sets, where aragonitic particles were dissolved to create moldic and intraparticle pore types. ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 64]])
- Dolomitization in the tidal flat subenvironment (Cycle set 5) led to intercrystalline pore types that enhanced porosity values. ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 65]])
- Porosity and permeability measurements from core plugs showed distinct patterns of distribution when analyzed based on rock typing and stratigraphic intervals. ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 15]])
- The lithofacies were grouped into four depositional sub-environments: foreshoal, shoal complex, backshoal, and tidal flat. ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 123]])
- Porosity measurements (n=244) showed a mean of 10.88% and permeability (n=233) showed a mean of 1.18 md, with both following log-normal distributions when categorized by rock texture. ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 74]])
- Correlation between porosity and permeability was significantly higher for grainy-dominated (0.612) and muddy-dominated (0.735) rocks compared to muddy-grainy types (0.311). ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 78]])
- The major range of the lithofacies was found to be oriented approximately north–south, varying between 500 and 1000 m. ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 85]])
- The TGS algorithm provided the best representation for zones one and two due to their high lateral facies transition. ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 100]])
- The IK model showed the best representation of vertical stacking patterns in zone three. ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 100]])
- SIS was identified as a robust method for modeling facies in zones four and five due to low geological variability. ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 100]])
- The study identified three distinct porosity evolution scenarios based on ooid mineralogy: aragonitic, bimineralic, and dolomitized. ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 106]])
- Average porosity in the upper interval (Zone 5) was found to be 28% for the dolomitic skeletal oolitic grainstone. ([[2016-makkawi|Makkawi, 2016, p. 112]])

## Limitations

The study noted that small horizontal areas of the model can lead to uncertainty in constructed semivariograms due to limited sample points. Additionally, some geostatistical models (IK, TGS) introduced unrealistic features not present in the true stratigraphic order.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future research should address the impact of having three different types of ooids (aragonite, calcite, and bimineralic) within a single reservoir to understand the resulting complexity in porosity evolution.
