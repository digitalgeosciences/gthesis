# DEPOSITIONAL AND DIAGENETIC CONTROLS ON POROSITY EVOLUTION OF THE MIOCENE DAM FORMATION CARBONATES; AL-LIDAM AREA, EASTERN SAUDI ARABIA

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the depositional and diagenetic controls on porosity evolution in the Miocene Dam Formation carbonates of the Al-Lidam area. It utilizes a combination of thin-section petrography, SEM, and XRD to characterize 15 lithofacies across three sequence stratigraphic units (DLS 1-3) to determine how reservoir quality is influenced by both primary depositional settings and subsequent diagenetic processes like dolomitization and meteoric dissolution.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the challenge of predicting reservoir quality in carbonate rocks, which are often highly heterogeneous due. The specific problem is determining the extent to which porosity evolution in the Dam Formation (Miocene) is driven by primary depositional environments versus post-depositional diagenetic processes like dolomitization and dissolution.

## Objectives

- Investigate porosity and permeability evolution as a response to diagenetic processes and alterations of the Miocene Dam Formation carbonates.
- Establish a sequence stratigraphic framework to link diagenetic patterns with sea-level changes.
- Identify and characterize 15 distinct lithofacies within the Dam Formation based on petrographic and sedimentological features.
- Determine the depositional environments for each identified lithofacies using a combination of sedimentary structures, grain types, and ichnofacies.
- Investigate the impact of marine, meteoric (vadose and phreatic), and burial diagenesis on carbonate porosity.
- Identify lithofacies and characterize the sedimentary environment of the Dam Formation.

## Methods

The study employs a multi-scale approach including thin-section petrography for mineralogy and texture analysis, SEM imaging to identify micro-scale features like aragonite needles and clay minerals (palygorskite), and XRD to quantify mineral phases such as dolomite and gypsum. Petrographic point counting was used to quantify seven types of porosity: interparticle, intraparticle, moldic, vuggy, intercrystalline, shelter, and fracture. These data were then integrated into a sequence stratigraphic framework consisting of three 4th-order sequences (DLS 1, DLS 2, DLS 3) divided into Highstand (HST), Transgressive (TST), and Lowstand (LST) systems tracts.

## Data and Materials

The study analyzed over 400 thin-section photomicrographs from the Dam Formation in the Al-Lidam area. It identified 15 distinct lithofacies based on mineralogy, structure, fossils, and morphology. Data included XRD results for dolomite content (MgO vs CaO), SEM images of pore features, and petrophysical measurements of porosity and permeability across different stratigraphic units.

## Key Findings

- Fifteen lithofacies were identified within the Miocene Dam Formation, deposited in a carbonate ramp under supratidal, beach, intertidal, and shallow subtidal conditions. ([[2016-salih|Salih, 2016, p. 16]])
- Meteoric diagenesis led to the formation of moldic and vuggy porosity, particularly in intertidal grainstones and packstones. ([[2016-salih|Salih, 2016, p. 17]])
- Marine diagenesis was restricted to beach grainstone and subtidal quartz wacke-packstone lithofacies, appearing as aragonite and high magnesium calcite cement on grain margins. ([[2016-salih|Salih, 2016, p. 17]])
- mitigated_diagenesis_note ([[2016-salih|Salih, 2016]])
- Mimetic dolomitization was observed across the entire succession; its association with evaporites led to a sabkha-evaporative dolomitization model. ([[2016-salih|Salih, 2016, p. 17]])
- Three fourth-order, shallowing-upward sequences were identified, separated by two sequence boundaries. ([[2016-salih|Salih, 2016, p. 17]])
- The highest porosity and permeability values are associated with the high stand systems tracts (HST) of each sequence, followed by transgressive (TST) and lowstand (TST) systems tracts. ([[2016-salih|Salih, 2016, p. 17]])
- High-stand system tracts (HST) were characterized by more grainy sediments in association with dissolution-related porosities, while TST and LST were dominated by muddier sediments with less meteoric influence. ([[2016-salih|Salih, 2016, p. 17]])
- Lithofacies f2 (interbedded mudstone and evaporites) was interpreted as sabkha deposits due to the presence of mud cracks, red color, and associated minerals. ([[2016-salih|Salih, 2016, p. 54]])
- Lithofacies f8 (herringbone cross-bedded grainstone) showed evidence of tidal influence including mud drapes and reactivation surfaces. ([[2016-salih|Salih, 2016, p. 62]])
- The Dam Formation contains 15 distinct lithofacies characterized by varying grain sizes, sedimentary structures, and fossil content. ([[2016-salih|Salih, 2016, p. 77]])
- Micrite envelopes were found in several facies (f 8, f 9, f 10, f 11, f 12, and f 15) and served to preserve grain shapes and porosity during diagenesis. ([[2016-salih|Salih, 2016, p. 86]])
- Marine cementation (high-Mg calcite and aragonite) was primarily restricted to high-energy grainstone facies (f 8, f 15). ([[2016-salih|Salih, 2016, p. 90]])
- Meteoric phreatic diagenesis resulted in extensive dissolution of skeletal grains (bivalves and gastropods) due to their original aragonite mineralogy. ([[2016-salih|Salih, 2016, p. 98]])
- Vadose diagenesis was characterized by meniscus cementation, which significantly reduced permeability at the pore throats. ([[2016-salih|Salih, 2016, p. 95]])

## Limitations

The study notes that lowstand system tracts (LST) are dominated by mudstone facies which could not be prepared for petrographic analysis, limiting the data on those specific units.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Apply paleontology and biostratigraphy to better understand depositional environments.
- Use isotope geochemistry (Oxygen, Carbon, and Strontium) to delineate sequences and define diagenetic processes more precisely.
- Correlate current findings with subsurface data.
