# Integrated deterministic/probabilistic petroleum volume evaluation for a carbonate reservoir

**Summary**: The thesis evaluates the impact of integrating deterministic and probabilistic methods to quantify uncertainty in hydrocarbon volume estimates for a carbonate reservoir (Arab-D). It compares two simulation strategies—one using three distinct zones and one using a single unified zone—to determine how accounting for heterogeneity and dependency affects the accuracy of pore-volume calculations.

---

## Research Problem

Hydrocarbon volume estimation is subject to significant uncertainty due to geological, geophysical, and petrophysical factors. Standard deterministic methods provide only a single point value, failing to capture the range of possible outcomes or the risks associated with data limitations in complex carbonate reservoirs like the Arab-D.

## Objectives

- Assess the utility of probabilistic methodology as an aid in quantifying uncertainty and in pore-volume estimation.
- Integrate deterministic and probabilistic estimations to yield a better result for decision-making in exploration, drilling planning, field development, production planning, and economic evaluation.
- Determine how different simulation strategies (zoned vs. non-zoned) affect the accuracy of hydrocarbon volume estimates.
- Develop a 3D geocellular model to calculate deterministic pore volumes.
- Perform statistical analysis on porosity and net pay distributions.
- Conduct Monte Carlo simulations to evaluate the impact of uncertainty on volume estimates.

## Methods

The study utilizes both deterministic and probabilistic workflows. Deterministic methods are used to establish base values for area, net pay, and porosity from well logs and core data. Probabilistic analysis is conducted using Monte Carlo simulations (specifically utilizing Latin Hypercube sampling) to generate a distribution of possible outcomes. Two simulation strategies were compared: one where the reservoir was divided into three zones with individual distributions aggregated via Monte Carlo, and another where no zonation was performed. Data integration includes well log analysis (sonic, neutron, density), core analysis for petrophysical calibration, and lithofacies analysis to characterize the carbonate system.

## Data and Materials

The study area is an onshore field in the Eastern Province of Saudi Arabia featuring the Arab-D Reservoir (Upper Jurassic). The dataset includes 19 vertical wells with porosity logs (sonic, neutron, density) and core data from 7 wells analyzed by Saudi Aramco geologists.

## Key Findings

- The study found that applying aggregation and dependency in the probabilistic model leads to better results by providing a larger range of potential volumes and quantifying parameter uncertainties. ([[2003-al-shridi|Al-Shridi, 2003, p. 12]])
- In the correlated (dependent) case, simulation resulted in a tighter hydrocarbon volume distribution with a smaller standard deviation. ([[2003-al-shridi|Al-Shridi, 2003, p. 12]])
- Hydrocarbon volumes calculated in both cases were significantly different when comparing zoned vs. non-zoned models. ([[2003-al-shridi|Al-Shridi, 2003, p. 12]])
- Grainstones, the most important lithofacies in the Arab-D reservoir, can have porosities up to 30% and permeabilities exceeding 2000 mD. ([[2003-al-shridi|Al-Shridi, 2003, p. 48]])
- porosity values for wackestones and packstones are generally in the range of 5-15%, with permeabilities between 1 to 10 mD. ([[2003-al-shridi|Al-Shridi, 2003]])

- The study area is an asymmetrical anticline in a carbonate reservoir of the upper Jurassic, measuring 15,500 meters long and 10,500 meters wide. ([[2003-al-shridi|Al-Shridi, 2003, p. 54]])
- The geocellular model was composed of 320,292 cells based on a grid size of 250x250m. ([[2003-al-shridi|Al-Shridi, 2003, p. 54]])
- Porosity distribution in the upper zones (1 and 2) is normally distributed with high mean values due to grainstone facies, while lower zone-3 shows a positively skewed distribution with low mean porosity due to mudstone dominance. ([[2003-al-shridi|Al-Shridi, 2003, p. 64]])
- The determination of net pay was based on a porosity cutoff of 5%. ([[2003-al-shridi|Al-Shridi, 2003, p. 67]])
- Monte Carlo simulations with 10,000 trials were used to evaluate the impact of uncertainty in area, net pay, and porosity. ([[2003-al-shridi|Al-Shridi, 2003, p. 73]])
- The average pore-volume for the aggregated case (dependent variables) was calculated as 1621.24 MMbbis with a standard deviation of 86.26. ([[2003-al-shridi|Al-Shridi, 2003, p. 92]])
- The mean pore-volume is considered a conservative estimate, as the mode is nearly 5% larger than the mean due to the distribution shape. ([[2003-al-shridi|Al-Shridi, 2003, p. 93]])
- Sensitivity analysis identified porosity in zone-1 and net pay in zone-2 as the most significant factors influencing pore volume. ([[2003-al-shridi|Al-Shridi, 2003, p. 97]])
- The deterministic pore-volume derived from the 3D model closely matches the mean pore-volume of the Monte Carlo simulation, indicating that the input distributions were representative of the geology. ([[2003-al-shridi|Al-Shridi, 2003, p. 101]])

## Limitations

Deterministic estimates are limited as they provide only a single point value and do not show uncertainty or potential. The study notes that the accuracy of each parameter in calculations depends on the precision of measurement.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Run 3D seismic surveys early in field development to improve petrophysical parameter interpolation.
- Use probabilistic tools to better capture expert judgment and characterize uncertainty.
- Integrate logs, cores, and thin-sections at well locations to improve porosity models.
- Apply integrated deterministic and probabilistic methods in other fields/reservoirs for pore-volume and oil-in-place calculations.
