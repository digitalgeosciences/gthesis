# IMPROVING OIL-WELL CEMENT INTEGRITY USING A SYNTHETIC FIBER

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of adding polypropylene fiber to Portland Saudi cement class G on critical properties such as thickening time, fluid loss, free water separation, density, and compressive strength under high pressure and temperature (HPHT) conditions. The study concludes that 0.5% BWOC of polypropylene fiber provides an optimal balance between accelerated strength development and sufficient thickening time for deep well applications.

---

## Research Problem

The degradation of cement integrity in deep wells due to extreme high-pressure and high-temperature (HPHT) conditions leads to issues like gas migration and poor zonal isolation. There is a need for more durable, cost-effective additives like synthetic fibers to improve the mechanical properties of cement slurries.

## Objectives

- Investigate the effect of polymer fiber usage on the cement integrity.
- Evaluate the usage of fiber with and without additives to enhance the cement integrity.
- Identify the applicable and optimum cement mixture with fibers for the well cementing application.
- Study the microstructural characterization of the cement with fiber.
- Evaluate the effect of polypropylene fiber on various physical and chemical properties of Class G cement.
- Assess the performance of cement slurries under HPHT conditions.

## Methods

The study utilized four cement systems with varying concentrations of polypropylene fiber (0%, 0.25%, 0.5%, and 0.75% BWOC). Samples were cured for 24 hours at 292°F and 3000 psi in an HPHT chamber. Compressive strength was measured using both sonic and crushing methods. Tensile strength was determined via the Brazilian tensile test on core plugs. Rheological properties (plastic viscosity, yield point, gel strength) were measured using a Fann Viscometer. Porosity and permeability were measured using an automated porosity/permeability meter under 100 psi of confining pressure. Microstructural analysis included XRD to identify hydration products (C-S-H, quartz, etc.) and SEM for morphology and elemental analysis (SEM-EDS).

## Data and Materials

The study used Class G cement (specific gravity 3.14) mixed with silica flour, expanding agents, dispersants, fluid loss control agents, retarders, and defoamers. Samples were tested in a high-pressure environment simulating a well depth of 14,000 ft, BHP of 8265 psi, and BHST of 292°F.

## Key Findings

- Addition of fibers improves mechanical properties, accelerates thickening time, reduces free water content, and reduces density. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 17]])
- Polypropylene fiber (PPF) with a triangular cross-section provides higher surface area and higher flexural strength than ordinary rounded shapes. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 22]])
- The inclusion of fibers creates a mesh network that improves tensile strength and helps control lost circulation. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 23]])
- Adding 0.55% of organic fiber increased cement toughness by 10%, while adding up to 1.25% increased it by 40%. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 24]])
- A reduction in CO2 emissions of up to 51% was observed when using geopolymer-cement slurries with only 40% Portland cement content. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 47]])
- Adding silica flour (35-40%) is necessary for maintaining compressive strength in temperatures exceeding 230°F. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 40]])
- The base case cement slurry (without fiber) achieved an expected density of 125 PCF and a thickening time of 4-5 hours. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 38]])
- A study by Yalcinkaya et al. (2011) showed that total porosity in cement decreased from 26% to 22% after one month of exposure to acidic brine in a low-pressure system. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 49]])
- Modified cellulose-based polymers were found effective for fluid loss control at temperatures up to 170°F, but performance significantly decreased above this temperature due to poor water solubility. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 51]])
- A study by Arshad et al. (2014) showed that engineered fiber-based pills could increase mud weight up to 17 lbm/gal while reducing non-productive time (NPT). ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 53]])
- The addition of 3mm glass fibers was shown to increase tensile strength and enhance cement thixotropy without significantly decreasing compressive strength. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 54]])
- The addition of polypropylene fiber reduced the thickening time from approximately 5 hours (base mix) down to 1.5 hours for the 0.75% PPF mixture. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 66]])
- Adding 0.5% polypropylene fiber was identified as a potentially optimal practice for deep wells, providing sufficient thickening time (over 3 hours) while accelerating strength development. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 72]])
- Fluid loss values were observed to be between 50 and 65 ml, all within the acceptable industry range of 100 ml/30 min. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 73]])
- The addition of polypropylene fiber resulted in zero free water separation at the top of the cement slurry for all tested concentrations (0%, 0.25%, 0.5%, and 0.75%). ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 75]])

## Limitations

The study was conducted over short-term intervals; therefore, the long-term effects of polypropylene fiber on cement integrity were not evaluated.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Investigate the impact of polypropylene fiber in shallow applications with low pressure and temperature conditions.
- Test different water-to-cement ratios for various fiber concentrations.
- Investigate other types of fibers to compare performance.
- Evaluate the long-term effects of polypropylene fiber on cement integrity.
- Explore the use of polypropylene fiber as a lost circulation agent or fluid loss agent in drilling fluids.
