# Stability and Texture of CO2/N2 Foam in Sandstone

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the stability and texture of CO2/N2 foam in Berea sandstone using three different surfactants (FS-51, AOS, and Witcolate). It evaluates how varying foam quality and N2/CO2 ratios affect the apparent viscosity and stability of the foam under high-pressure and temperature conditions.

---

## Research Problem

The primary problem is that CO2-foam becomes weaker above its supercritical conditions (1100 psi and 31°C), leading to poor sweep efficiency in EOR. While N2 forms stronger foam, the specific stability and texture of mixed CO2/N2 foams in porous media have not been fully investigated.

## Objectives

- Evaluate mixture of sc-CO2/N2-surfactant as a foam EOR solution.
- Conduct steady-state foam flooding experiments to study stability and texture of foam being injected in the sandstone rock using mixture of sc-CO2 and N2 with three different surfactant systems.
- Determine the optimum injection strategy based on optimum individual gas/liquid injection rates, CO2/N2 ratio and foam quality.
- Determine the critical micelle concentration (CMC) for three different surfactants: FS-51, AOS, and Witcolate.
- Measure the interfacial tension (IFT) between surfactant solutions and sc-CO2.
- Evaluate the effect of N2/CO2 ratios on foam stability and texture in a Berea sandstone core.

## Methods

The study utilized a core-flooding experimental setup consisting of fluid injection pumps, accumulators, pressure transducers, a core holder, and an oven. Three surfactants (FS-51, AOS, and Witcolate) were prepared at their critical micelle concentrations (CMC). Steady-state foam flooding experiments were performed in Berea Grey Sandstone cores (12" length, 1.5" diameter) under supercritical conditions (back-pressure 1300-1500 psi, temperature 50°C). Foam stability was monitored via pressure drop (ΔP) across the core. A high-pressure and high-temperature (HPHT) visual cell was used to capture foam images, which were then analyzed using ImageJ software to evaluate texture based on bubble size distribution.

## Data and Materials

The study used Berea Sandstone cores with a measured porosity of 19.7% and permeability of approximately 18-19 mD. Three surfactants (FS-51, and AOS, and Witcolate) were tested at various concentrations above their respective CMCs. Experimental conditions included a temperature of 50°C and pressures ranging from 1300 to 2000 psi.

## Key Findings

- Adding N2 to CO2 above its supercritical conditions resulted in improved foam strength, evidenced by an increase in steady-state pressure drop (ΔP) across the core. ([[2016-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2016, p. 16]])
- Analysis of captured foam images showed that the circularity of foam bubbles was significantly enhanced with the addition of N2, providing evidence of increased foam strength. ([[2016-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2016, p. 16]])
- The study confirmed that for each test performed, resistance factor ranged from 3-63, indicating foam was generated at all conditions tested and a minimum pressure or velocity was not required. ([[2016-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2016, p. 32]])
- In the presence of oil, C16 AOS formed stronger foam at reservoir conditions (higher pressure) compared to other surfactants like betaine which showed different behaviors under varying pressures. ([[2016-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2016, p. 33]])
- The study concluded that stability of foam in the bulk phase can be correlated with performance in porous media, where greater foam stability leads to more mobility reduction. ([[2016-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2016, p. 34]])
- For foams with a narrow bubble size distribution (fine texture), it was determined that foam is more stable and has lower mobility due to reduced relative permeability of gas and increased effective viscosity. ([[2016-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2016, p. 24]])
- The CMC for fluorosurfactant FS-51 was found to be approximately 0.07 vol%. ([[2016-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2016, p. 58]])
- The CMC for AOS surfactant was determined to be around 0.3 vol%. ([[2016-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2016, p. 60]])
- No significant change in permeability (from ~19 mD to ~18 mD) was observed after foam flooding, indicating that surfactants did not cause significant damage to the sandstone core. ([[2016-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2016, p. 65]])
- For FS-51, adding N2 increased pressure drop (ΔP) significantly at lower foam qualities; for example, at a quality of 0.70, 5% N2 increased ΔP by 33 psi. ([[2016-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2016, p. 67]])
- For FS-51, the critical foam quality (qg*) was identified as 0.90; above this point, foam became too dry and weakened. ([[2016-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2016, p. 68]])
- The optimal foam quality for FS-51 was determined to be 0.80, where the highest ΔP values were observed. ([[2016-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2016, p. 68]])
- For AOS, the optimum foam quality was found to be 0.90, yielding the highest pressure drops (up to 260 psi at 20% N2). ([[2016-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2016, p. 71]])
- The addition of N2 had a negligible effect on foam strength when the foam quality was above 0.90. ([[2016-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2016, p. 71]])
- Witcolate was found to be a very poor foaming agent, producing no foam with CO2, N2, or their mixture. ([[2016-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2016, p. 73]])

## Limitations

Witcolate did not form any foam, making image analysis impossible for that specific surfactant.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Perform core-flooding experiments at optimum foam quality and N2/CO2 ratio to determine impact on oil recovery.
- Conduct visual core-flood experiments to track the propagation of mixed CO2/N2 foam.
- Study the effects of temperature and pressure on the properties of mixed CO2/N2-foam.
