# Sequestration of Contaminants by Mangrove (Avicennia marina) Ecosystem from the Gulf Coast of Saudi Arabia

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the potential of Avicennia marina mangroves on the Gulf Coast of Saudi Arabia to sequester heavy metals and petroleum hydrocarbons from marine sediments. It evaluates contamination levels in sediment across four sites (Abu Ali Island, Tarut Bay, Rahima, and Gurmah Island) and quantifies the bioaccumulation and translocation of these contaminants into different plant tissues.

---

## Research Problem

The accumulation of hazardous pollutants like heavy metals and petroleum hydrocarbons in marine environments poses a risk to ecosystems; however, the specific role of Avicennia marina as a natural filter for these contaminants in the Gulf Coast of Saudi Arabia is under-studied.

## Methods

Field surveys were conducted in 2018 to collect sediment samples from subtidal, tidal, and super-tidal zones. Plant tissues (leaves, pneumatophores, bark) were collected from Avicennia marina. Sediment analysis included sieve analysis for texture and chemical digestion (EPA Method 3050B). Metal analysis was performed using ICP-OES (Perkin Elmer DV 8000) and a mercury analyzer (Nippon MA 3000). PAH and TPH were analyzed via GC-MS and GC-FID following EPA methods 8270C, 3545A, 3611, and 4630. Experimental studies involved growing Avicennia marina in pots with three diesel concentrations (75, 50, and 25 ml/kg) for 90 days.

## Data and Materials

Data includes chemical concentrations of 5 heavy metals (Cr, Cu, Ni, Hg, Zn) in various tissues; PAH and TPH levels in sediment and plant parts across four field sites; and a 90-day experimental study using Avicennia marina seedlings in soil with three distinct diesel contamination levels.

## Key Findings

- Maximum concentrations of heavy metals in plant tissue were: Cr (12 mg/kg in root), Cu (102.5 mg/kg in bark), Ni (9 mg/kg in root), Hg (0.063 mg/kg in leaf), and Zn (8.6 mg/kg in root). ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 15]])
- Mean concentrations of total polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) in sediment, leaf, bark, and root were 44.26, 192.4, 258.4, and 511.7 ng/g, respectively. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 15]])
- The average trend of total petroleum hydrocarbon (TPH) concentration followed a descending order: sediment > bark > leaf > root. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 15]])
- Observed removal percentages of PAHs and TPH were 92.91% and 96.15%, respectively. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 16]])
- The maximum bioaccumulation factor (BAF) was 120 for root and the maximum translocation factor (TF) was 7.15 for leaf. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 16]])
- Avicennia marina is identified as an efficient plant species for the sequestration of petroleum hydrocarbons. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 16]])
- Avicennia marina is the only dominant mangrove species in the Arabian Gulf coastlines due to its ability to withstand high salinity and temperature. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 28]])
- Mangrove sediments are characterized by low energy, anoxic conditions (1-3 cm from surface), and high organic carbon/sulfide content, favoring the accumulation of PAHs. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 33]])
- Heavy metals such as Cu, Zn, Pb, Cd, Cr, Mn, and Hg are known to accumulate primarily in root tissues rather than foliage in many mangrove species including A. marina. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 31]])
- The concentration of heavy metals in plant tissues is influenced by metabolic requirements for essential micronutrients like Cu and Zn. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 31]])
- LMW PAHs (2-3 aromatic rings) are more susceptible to degradation than HMW PAHs (4+ rings) in mangrove sediments due to higher microbial activity from litter components. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 34]])
- The study identifies Avicennia marina as a potential biological filter for heavy metals and hydrocarbons in the Arabian Gulf region. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 23]])
- The study identified the primary mangrove species in the Arabian Gulf coast as Avicennia marina due to its adaptation to hyper-environments. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 41]])
- Sediment samples were collected from three zones: subtidal, tidal, and super-idal. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 47]])
- The experimental study used three diesel concentrations (75, 50, and 25 ml/kg) to evaluate plant response over 90 days. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Investigate phytoremediation efficiencies for different concentrations of heavy metals.
- Monitor the effect of PAHs on growth, germination, and reproduction by raising seedlings on contaminated media.
- Evaluate the effect of microorganisms on the dissipation of PAHs.
- Evaluate other types of aquatic plants like seagrass and micro-algae for similar studies.
