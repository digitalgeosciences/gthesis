# Sequestration of Contaminants by Mangrove (Avicennia marina) Ecosystem from the Gulf Coast of Saudi Arabia

**Summary**: The thesis investigated the sequestration potential of Avicennia marina in removing heavy metals and petroleum hydrocarbons (PAHs and TPH) from sediment along the Saudi coast of the Arabian Gulf. It concluded that Avicennia marina is a viable candidate for bioremediation due to its high accumulation of contaminants in roots and its ability to survive in harsh arid environments.

---

## Research Problem

The lack of specific data regarding the concentration and sequestration efficiency of heavy metals and petroleum hydrocarbons (PAHs and TPH) within Avicennia marina ecosystems along the Saudi coast of the Arabian Gulf, despite the increasing anthropogenic pressure on these coastal zones.

## Objectives

- To study and compare the level of selected trace metals and hydrocarbons in sediment samples at mangrove and non-mangrove areas.
- To study the accumulation of selected trace metals and hydrocarbons in different part of mangrove plants.
- To study the accumulation of petroleum hydrocarbon from diesel contaminated soil by Avicennia marina based on an experimental study.
- Assess the concentration of heavy metals (Cr, Cu, Ni, Hg, Zn) and hydrocarbons (PAHs, TPH) in mangrove and non-mangrove sediment samples.
- Evaluate the accumulation potential of Avicennia marina using Bioaccumulation Factor (BAF) and Translocation Factor (TF).
- Determine the remediation efficiency and degradation kinetics of petroleum hydrocarbons in diesel-contaminated soil using Avicennia marina.

## Methods

Field sampling was conducted at four locations: Abu Ali Island, Tarut Bay, Rahima, and Gurmah Island. Heavy metals were analyzed via ICP-OES (Perkin Elmer DV 8000) after acid digestion. Hydrocarbons (PAH and TPH) were extracted from sediment and plant tissues using solvent extraction followed by GC-MS and GC-FID analysis. Pollution indices including Enrichment Factor (EF), Geoaccumulation Index (Igeo), Pollution Load Index (PLI), and Potential Ecological Risk (ER) were calculated to assess sediment contamination. A 90-day pot experiment was conducted with Avicennia marina seedlings in diesel-contaminated soil (0, 25, 50, and 75 ml/kg) to evaluate phytoremediation efficiency and first-order kinetics.

## Data and Materials

Samples included sediment and plant tissues (roots, stems, leaves) from the Arabian Gulf coast. Experimental data included three different diesel concentrations (25, 50, 75 ml/kg) monitored over a 90-day period for multiple samples of Avicennia marina.

## Key Findings

- Maximum concentrations of heavy metals in plant tissues were: Cr (12 mg/kg in root), Cu (102.5 mg/kg in bark), Ni (9 mg/kg in leaf), Hg (0.063 mg/kg in leaf), and Zn (8.6 mg/kg in root). ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 15]])
- Mean concentrations of total polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) in sediment, leaf, bark, and root were 44.26, 192.4, 258.4, and 511.7 ng/g respectively. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 15]])
- The average trend of total petroleum hydrocarbon (TPH) concentration followed a descending order: sediment > bark > leaf > root. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 15]])
- Observed removal percentages for PAHs and TPH were 92.91% and 96.15%, respectively. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 16]])
- The maximum bioaccumulation factor (BAF) was 120 for root and the translocation factor (TF) was 7.15 for leaf. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 16]])
- Avicennia marina is identified as an efficient plant species for the sequestration of petroleum hydrocarbons. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 16]])
- Average concentrations of PAHs, TPH1, TPH2, and TOC were higher in mangrove sediments than non-mangrove sediments (e.g., TPH2 max of 9971.19 mg/kg in mangroves). ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 64]])
- Heavy metals (Cr, Cu, Ni) were found at higher concentrations in mangrove sediments compared to non-mangrove areas. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 64]])
- The average concentration of TPH1 was 47.20 mg/kg and TPH2 was 737.80 mg/kg in mangrove samples, while they were 30.00 mg/kg and 367.40 mg/kg in non-mangrove samples. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 65]])
- The average concentration of Zn was similar between mangrove (11.40 mg/kg) and non-mangrove (11.61 mg/kg) sediments. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 65]])
- All heavy metals in sediment samples were classified as having low ecological risk (ER < 80) and low pollution load index (PLI < 1). ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 73]])
- The Bioaccumulation Factor (BAF) for Cu, Hg, and Zn exceeded 1 in all plant parts, indicating efficient transfer from sediment to tissue. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 78]])
- Translocation Factors (TF) for Hg were greater than one in all leaves, barks, and root samples, confirming its role as a potential accumulator. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 82]])
- The removal efficiency of PAHs from diesel-contaminated soil using Avicennia marina reached up to 92% for the 75 ml/kg concentration. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 103]])
- The average TPH removal percentages were 76%, 80%, and 91% for concentrations of 75, 50, and 25 ml/kg respectively. ([[2019-alamran|AlAmran, 2019, p. 103]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Investigate phytoremediation efficiencies for different concentrations of heavy metals.
- Monitor the effect of PAHs on growth, germination, and reproduction of seedlings.
- Evaluate the effect of microorganisms on the dissipation of PAHs.
- Evaluate other types of aquatic plants like seagrass and micro-algae for similar studies.
