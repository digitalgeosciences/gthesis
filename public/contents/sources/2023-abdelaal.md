# DEVELOPMENT OF GEOPOLYMER SYSTEMS FOR OIL AND GAS WELL CEMENTING

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the development of geopolymer systems as an eco-friendly and durable alternative to Ordinary Portland Cement (OPC) for oil and gas well cementing, particularly in high-pressure and high-temperature (HPHT) environments. The study evaluates various fly ash-based formulations with different weighting agents like hematite and Micromax, while also exploring additives to improve pumpability and manage sedimentation issues.

---

## Research Problem

Ordinary Portland Cement (OPC) faces significant technical challenges in deep well cementing, including gas invasion, thermal instability at high temperatures, and durability issues in corrosive environments. Additionally, OPC production is environmentally taxing due to high CO2 emissions and energy consumption. There is a lack of research on high-density geopolymer systems using specific weighting agents for HPHT conditions.

## Objectives

- Develop an environmentally friendly and cost-effective moderate to high density geopolymer cement system to replace OPC in cementing operations across various temperatures using aluminosilicate materials like fly ash and volcanic tephra.
- Study the impact of various weighting agents such as hematite and Micromax on geopolymer properties under HPHT conditions.
- Introduce different solutions for pumpability and sedimentation issues associated with hematite-based geopolymer cement systems.
- Investigate the effect of hematite as a weighting agent on FFA geopolymer pumpability for oil and gas well cementing.
- Find a solution to increase the thickening time of the high-density hematite based FFA geopolymers for possible use in well cementing.
- Evaluate the impact of a mixture of additives (retarder and superplasticizer) on slurry rheology, thickening time, and mechanical properties.

## Methods

The study utilized a multi-stage methodology: 1) Material Characterization: XRD, XRF, and SEM were used to analyze FFA (fly ash), hematite, and perlite/laponite. Particle size distribution was measured via laser diffraction. 2) Slurry Preparation: A wet mixing process was used where superplasticizers and retarders were mixed with activation solutions (4M NaOH) before adding solids. 3) Sedimentation Evaluation: Three techniques—API method (density profiling), NMR (porosity analysis), and CT-scan (visualizing distribution)—were used to assess sedimentation. 4) Rheology: Measured using a viscometer at temperatures of 82°F and 195°F, modeled using the Bingham Plastic flow model. 5. Mechanical Properties: Compressive strength was determined via scratch tests; Young's Modulus (YM) and Poisson's Ratio (PR) were derived from ultrasonic velocity measurements.

## Data and Materials

The study analyzed three types of additives (perlite, laponite, and a mixed system). Materials included Class F Fly Ash (FFA), hematite (weighting agent), and 4M NaOH activator. Specific gravity values for FFA (2.25) and hematite (5.05) were recorded. Perlite concentrations of 0, 1.5, and 3% BWOB were tested; Laponite concentrations of 0 to 3% (9 levels) were tested.

## Key Findings

- Geopolymer systems exhibited lower shrinkage than OPC systems (e.g., Class H cement), making them suitable for ensuring well integrity in P&A operations. ([[2023-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2023, p. 43]])
- Geopolymers showed higher resistance to acid attacks compared to OPC, with only a 1.76% strength reduction in acidic environments versus 28% for OPC. ([[2023-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2023, p. 40]])
- Geopolymer systems demonstrated superior durability and lower permeability (less than 10^-20 m^2) compared to conventional cement systems, making them suitable for CO2 sequestration. ([[2023-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2023, p. 38]])
- The addition of Micromax as a weighting agent helped mitigate the sedimentation issues associated with hematite in geopolymer systems. ([[2023-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2023, p. 33]])
- A mixture of modified lignosulfonate retarder and a PNS superplasticizer was used to address pumpability and thickening time challenges for high-density hematite-based geopolymers. ([[2023-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2023, p. 33]])
- Perlite was found effective in addressing sedimentation and stability issues while improving compressive strength in high-density systems. ([[2023-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2023, p. 34]])
- Laponite was shown to improve density uniformity, compressive strength, and flexibility in hematite-based geopolymer samples. ([[2023-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2023, p. 34]])
- A new volcanic tephra-based geopolymer system was developed using only 4M NaOH as an activator to reduce costs and environmental impact. ([[2023-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2023, p. 35]])
- Increasing the hematite percentage from 25% to 75% BWOB significantly reduced thickening time at 195°F from 430 min to 80 min. ([[2023-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2023, p. 86]])
- A mixture of a retarder and a superplasticizer (5% BWOB each) increased the thickening time of the hematite-based geopolymer from 80 min to 580 min at 195°F. ([[2023-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2023, p. 87]])
- The developed geopolymer slurry had a fluid loss of 38 mL/30 min, meeting the <50 mL/30 min requirement for high-performance cementing applications. ([[2023-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2023, p. 67]])
- The geopolymer system exhibited a plasticity viscosity of 192.49 cP and a yield point of 10.45 lb/100ft² at 186.6°F. ([[2023-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2023, p. 65]])
- The mixed geopolymer systems (both perlite and laponite) exhibited Young's Modulus (YM) between 4.8 and 6.9 GPa and Poisson's Ratio (PR) between 0.23 and 0.27, making them more flexible than Class G cement. ([[2023-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2023, p. 139]])
- The geopolymer system had a permeability of 0.01 mD and a porosity of 0.38. ([[2023-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2023, p. 70]])
- Replacing 50% of hematite with Micromax reduced the density variation (DV) from 32.6% to 0.95%, significantly improving homogeneity. ([[2023-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2023, p. 115]])

## Limitations

Geopolymers are more susceptible to water-based mud (WBM) compared to oil-based mud (OBM). Geopolymer strength is significantly lower at temperatures below 86°F due to slow geopolymerization rates. Conventional geopolymers exhibit high brittleness.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study suggests that further research is required to investigate the use of geopolymers in HPHT wells specifically regarding their rapid gelation at high temperatures and their susceptibility to W1M.
