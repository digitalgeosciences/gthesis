# Production Enhancement Using CO2-Rich Flue Gases

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the effectiveness of using CO2-rich flue gas for enhanced oil recovery (EOR) in both crude oil and condensate systems. Through core flooding experiments and interfacial tension (IFT) measurements, it compares continuous and cyclic (Huff and Puff) injection methods across different rock types.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the challenge of improving oil and condensate recovery using environmentally friendly alternatives to traditional EOR fluids, specifically focusing on the impact of flue gas composition and mobility on production enhancement in limestone and sandstone reservoirs.

## Objectives

- Investigate the potential application of flue gas for hydrocarbon production enhancement.
- Evaluate the performance of flue gas injection in low permeability carbonate and sandstone reservoirs.
- Evaluate the injection strategy continuous or cyclic injection of the flue of gases.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of continuous flue gas injection compared to N2 flooding.
- Assess the performance of cyclic (Huff and Puff) gas injection for crude oil and condensate.
- Measure and analyze the interfacial tension (IFT) between flue gas and both crude oil and condensate under varying pressure and temperature conditions.

## Methods

The study utilized core-flooding experiments on Indiana limestone and Bandera Gray sandstone. Flue gas was prepared with a specific composition of CO2, nitrogen, and oxygen and analyzed using a Gas Chromatograph (GC Varian 3600). Interfacial tension (IFT) was measured using the pendant drop method in a visual cell to determine how pressure and temperature affect the interaction between flue gas and oil/condensate. Core flooding included both continuous injection and cyclic (Huff and Puff) modes, with results monitored via pressure profiles and recovery percentages.

## Data and Materials

The study involved 8 core flooding experiments using two rock types: Indiana limestone (IL) and Bandera Gray sandstone (BG). Two fluid samples were used: a crude oil (30% API) and a condensate (40% API). Flue gas composition was approximately 11.33% CO2, 70.66% N2, and 18.01% O2.

## Key Findings

- Cyclic injection produced more oil than continuous gas injection in both carbonate and sandstone rocks. ([[2021-almarri|AlMarri, 2021, p. 16]])
- Condensate exhibited a lower interfacial tension (IFT) of approximately 15 mN/m compared to crude oil at 18 mN/m when paired with flue gas. ([[2021-almarri|AlMarri, 2021, p. 16]])
- The IFT value for the condensate/flue gas system was lower than that of crude oil with flue gas, justifying higher recovery for condensate. ([[2021-almarri|AlMarri, 2021, p. 16]])
- Wettability, production rate, and rock properties had only a slight effect on the performance of the core-floods tests. ([[2021-almarri|AlMarri, 2021, p. 16]])
- The prepared flue gas mixture in the lab (11.33% CO2, 70.66% N2, 18.01% O2) was nearly identical to the composition of cement factory flue gas. ([[2021-almarri|AlMarri, 2021, p. 50]])
- The Indiana limestone cores had a wettability index of -0.15 (neutral wet), while Bandera gray sandstone had an index of +0.35 (slightly water wet). ([[2021-almarri|AlMarri, 2021, p. 51]])
- Continuous flue gas injection in Indiana limestone (60 md) yielded a 4% increase in recovery over N2 flooding. ([[2021-almarri|AlMarri, 2021, p. 53]])
- In Bandera Gray sandstone (10 md), continuous flue gas injection resulted in an 8% higher recovery than N2 flooding. ([[2021-almarri|AlMarri, 2021, p. 74]])
- Cyclic gas injection of flue gas into Indiana limestone with medium viscosity oil (15 cp) achieved a total recovery of 33%, with the first cycle contributing 20%. ([[2021-almarri|AlMarri, 2021, p. 80]])
- Cyclic gas injection in Bandera Gray sandstone (10 md) using medium viscosity oil reached 45% total recovery. ([[2021-almarri|AlMarri, 2021, p. 80]])
- Cyclic gas injection with condensate in Indiana limestone achieved a high recovery of 80% of OOIP. ([[2021-almarri|AlMarri, 2021, p. 82]])
- The IFT for the crude oil/flue gas system was found to be approximately 18 mN/m at 1600 psi, while the condensate/flue gas system was lower at 15 mN/m. ([[2021-almarri|AlMarri, 2021, p. 100]])
- IFT values for both crude oil and condensate decreased as pressure increased due to reduced density contrast and increased mutual solubility. ([[2021-almarri|AlMarri, 2021, p. 100]])

## Limitations

The study did not consider a wide enough range of temperatures and pressures, nor did it evaluate the specific interaction between flue gas and oil composition in terms of PVT properties.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future work should include IFT measurements with different oil samples and varying flue gas compositions. It is also recommended to investigate the WAG (Water Alternating Gas) process to manage the high mobility of flue gas and improve volumetric sweep efficiency.
