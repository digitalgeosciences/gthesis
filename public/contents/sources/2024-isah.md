# SYSTEMATIC INVESTIGATION OF ROCK/FLUID INTERACTIONS IN RESERVOIRS AT NANO –TO – MILLIMETER SCALE: IMPLICATIONS FOR ENHANCED OIL RECOVERY AND CO2 STORAGE

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the mineral transformation of anhydrite-rich rocks during interaction with supercritical CO2-saturated brine, both with and without additives like SrCl2 and BaCl2. It concludes that these interactions lead to significant carbon mineralization into stable minerals (calcite, dolomite, magnesite, strontianite, celestine), making such formations promising for long-term CO2 storage.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the lack of understanding regarding how specific mineralogical components (specifically anhydrite) influence pore structure complexity, petrophysical properties, and fluid flow behavior in tight carbonate reservoirs, which are candidates for EOR and CO2 storage.

## Objectives

- Investigate rock/fluid interactions at multiple scales (nano, micro, and core).
- Evaluate the impact of mineralogical heterogeneity (anhydrite) on carbonate wettability across various scales.
- To establish a relationship between rock surface roughness, mineralogy, rocks’ nanomechanical properties with wetting characteristics of carbonate, pre- and post CO2- treatments.
- Analyze the implications of these interactions on EOR before and after exposure to oil/CO2/CO2-foam/brine.
- Investigate the potential of anhydrite-rich rocks as a medium for carbon mineralization and storage through interaction with CO2-brine and SrCl2.
- To investigate the impact of mineralogical heterogeneity (i.e. anhydrite, dolomite and calcite) on wettibility of oil/brine/rock system at various length scales.

## Methods

The study employs a multi-phase methodology: Phase I involves microstructural characterization using NMR (T2_distribution), micro-CT (3D pore network visualization), QEMSCAN (mineral distribution), XRD/XRF (bulk mineralogy), and zeta potential measurements to assess surface charge. Phase II focuses on nanomechanical properties via nanoindentation, surface roughness analysis via AFM/Optical Microscopy, and contact angle measurements using the tilted plate sessile drop method. Phase III involves macro-scale studies including core flooding and Amott-Harvey wettibility index calculations. Synthetic rocks were fabricated using a sintering technique to control anhydrite content (1% to 25%) in calcite and dolomite matrices.

## Data and Materials

The study utilizes mineral samples of calcite, dolomite, and anhydrite with average particle sizes of 19.01 μm for zeta potential and 50 μm for flotation. Synthetic rocks were created in two sets (A: Anhydrite-Calcite; B: Anhydrite-Dolomite) with varying anhydrite concentrations (1, 5, 10, 15, 20, 25 wt%). Fluids included Arab light crude oil and three types of brine: deionized water (DI), Arabian Gulf seawater (SW), and formation water (FW).

## Key Findings

- Pure anhydrite is water-wet, while calcite and dolomite are intermediate to oil-wet. ([[2024-isah|Isah, 2024, p. 17]])
- Anhydrite influences the surface charge at the rock/fluid interface and increases the water wetness in carbonates. ([[2024-isah|Isah, 2024, p. 17]])
- CO2 injection proves more effective than surfactant flooding in anhydrite-bearing carbonates. ([[2024-isah|Isah, 2024, p. 17]])
- Anhydrite undergoes mineral transformation when exposed to CO2-saturated brine, making it a potential CO2 sink. ([[2024-isah|Isah, 2024, p. 17]])
- Rock surface roughness (RMS) can range from micrometers to nanometers and significantly impacts contact angle measurements. ([[2024-isah|Isah, 2024, p. 33]])
- Zeta potential is a unique characteristic of the charge state of interfaces, independent of the measurement technique used. ([[2024-isah|Isah, 2024, p. 46]])
- Anhydrite dissolution was confirmed in DI, SW, and FW as evidenced by the increase in sulfate ion concentration with higher anhydrite content. ([[2024-isah|Isah, 2024, p. 70]])
- Zeta potential measurements showed an unstable colloidal system for anhydrite-carbonate-brine systems, indicated by near-zero or low values (less than ±10). ([[2024-isah|Isah, 2024, p. 70]])
- The presence of anhydrite has a tendency to alter the wetting state of anhydrite-carbonate-brine-oil systems toward more water-wet. ([[2024-isah|Isah, 2024, p. 70]])
- Anhydrite is significantly more soluble than calcite and dolomite (by approximately 2 orders of magnitude). ([[2024-isah|Isah, 2024, p. 59]])
- Porosity of the samples ranged from 0.35% to 6.55%, with a notable correlation between decreasing anhydrite content and increased dolomite composition leading to higher porosity. ([[2024-isah|Isah, 2024, p. 128]])
- Permeability values were recorded between 0.040 and 0.165 mD. ([[2024-isah|Isah, 2024, p. 128]])
- Fractal dimension of macropores (D3) ranged from 2.41 to 2.96, indicating a medium complexity pore structure. ([[2024-isah|Isah, 2024, p. 115]])
- The fractal dimension of mesopores (D2) was between 2.68 and 2.99, identifying them as the most complex pore structures. ([[2024-isah|Isah, 2024, p. 115]])
- Note: No specific value for D1 was reported as no apparent trend was observed. ([[2024-isah|Isah, 2024]])

## Limitations

Zeta potential was found to be not a general or valid candidate for justifying the wettibility behavior of heterogeneous carbonates due to its sensitivity to environmental conditions and non-monotonic behavior with brine salinity/pH.

## Recommendations and Future Work

More work is required to understand the compositional dependence of mechanisms of CO2 mineralization as well as the kinetics of the coupled dissolution/precipitation of anhydrite-rich rocks for carbon subsurface storage.
