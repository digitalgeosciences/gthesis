# Characterizing fluid contacts from seismic data by joint inversion of Acoustic velocity and Impedance

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the characterization of pore fluids (gas, oil, and brine) at the reservoir level using seismic data through a joint inversion of acoustic velocity and impedance. The study utilizes Gassmann's equation for fluid substitution and demonstrates that joint inversion provides accurate results with near-zero percent error when identifying fluid properties in both synthetic cases and the Arab formation.

---

## Research Problem

Identifying and characterizing fluid contacts (gas/oil and oil/water) in reservoirs is challenging due to the complexities of seismic signals; this research addresses how joint inversion of acoustic velocity and impedance can improve these identifications from seismic data.

## Objectives

- Characterize the pore fluid in a reservoir by the joint inversion of the acoustic (P-wave) velocity and (P-wave) impedance calculated from surface seismic data with no input from a well log or other direct methods.
- Generation of synthetic seismic data
- Inversion of AI
- Computation of density and compressional modulus (M)
- Inverting the AI, acoustic velocity, density, and compressional modulus for the pore fluid density and velocity.
- Characterize pore fluids at the reservoir level from seismic data
- Determine fluid properties (gas, live oil, brine) using Gassmann's equations
- Invert acoustic impedance and interval velocity into fluid velocity and density
- Evaluate synthetic seismic data to model a typical anticline and an Arab limestone reservoir
- Characterize pore fluids at the reservoir level using seismic data.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of joint inversion of acoustic impedance and velocity for fluid identification.
- Assess the impact of varying porosity levels on the accuracy of fluid density and fluid velocity inversions.
- Utilize joint inversion of acoustic velocity and impedance to identify fluid contacts

## Methods

The study uses a multi-step workflow: 1) Generation of synthetic seismic data based on a three-layer geological anticline model. 2) Calculation of saturated rock density (Rhos) and compressional modulus (M) from P-wave velocity and Acoustic Impedance (AI). 3) Application of Gassmann's equation for fluid substitution to determine the effects of different fluids (gas, live oil, brine) on seismic properties. 4) Joint inversion of AI, P-wave velocity, density, and compressional modulus to derive pore fluid density and velocity. 5) Validation of the method using synthetic cases and data representing the Arab formation across various porosity levels.

## Data and Materials

The study uses a three-layer geological model with dimensions of 4000m x 3000m. It includes calculations for three fluid types (gas, live oil, brine) at specific reservoir conditions (150°C, ~21 MPa). Synthetic seismic traces are generated for two scenarios: gas over brine and live oil over brine.

## Key Findings

- In both cases, AI, compressional bulk modulus, and saturated rock density inversion gave good evidence of the presence of two different pore fluids in the reservoir. ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 14]])
- Results of fluid density and fluid velocity give good evidence and information about the pore fluids and are a helpful tool in identifying the pore fluids in the reservoir. ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 14]])
- The percent error between the inverted fluid density/velocity and the computed fluid density/velocity for gas, live oil, and brine is almost 0% for cases 1 and 2. ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 14]])
- The inversion of synthetic seismic traces representing the Arab formation across a range of porosities resulted in similarly small errors between the inverted and true fluid properties. ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 14]])
- The study determined that P-wave transit time in oil is higher than water due to lower density in a reservoir with identical lithology and porosity. ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 23]])
- Acoustic log hydrocarbon indicator (ALHI) was shown to be effective for differentiating water-bearing zones from hydrocarbon-bearing zones in clastic rocks, especially in younger/unconsolidated formations. ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 25]])
- Note: The specific results of the joint inversion on the actual Arab limestone reservoir are discussed in Chapter 4 (not fully provided in text). ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016]])
- The model assumes a cap rock thickness of 400m and an oil column height of 200m. ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 31]])
- Calculated bulk modulus for brine water was 2.38e9 Pascal at 84,000 ppm salinity. ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 39]])
- AI inversion successfully separated the upper hydrocarbon zone from the lower brine zone in both Case-1 and Case-2, with percentage changes of 7.30% and 3.80% respectively. ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 51]])
- Compressional modulus (M) inversion showed a change of 5.70% in Case-1 and 4.60% in Case-2 at the fluid contact. ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 53]])
- Saturated rock density (rho_s) showed a jump of 9.03% in Case-1 and 3% in Case-2 at the transition from hydrocarbon to brine. ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 55]])
- Fluid density inversion provided high sensitivity, showing changes of 1014.74% in Case-1 and 46.70% in Case-2 at the contact. ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 57]])
- Fluid velocity (Vf) inversion showed significant jumps of 2900% in Case-1 and 101.30% in Case-2, making it a highly effective tool for fluid identification. ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 59]])
- In the Arab formation study (Case-3), fluid density inversions showed very low error rates (0.0138% to 0.2714%) across different porosity levels (10%, 20%, 30%). ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 88]])

## Limitations

The primary limitation noted is that the model assumes a fixed porosity value which was not calculated from actual data but rather assumed for the study's scope.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Integrate the proposed joint inversion model with AVO analysis for improved fluid characterization.
- Calibrate AI inversion results with well log data to improve accuracy.
