# Characterizing fluid contacts from seismic data by joint inversion of Acoustic velocity and Impedance

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the identification of pore fluids (gas, oil, and brine) in a reservoir using seismic data through the joint inversion of acoustic velocity and acoustic impedance. The study utilizes Gassmann's equation for fluid substitution and applies the methodology to both a synthetic three-layer model and the Arab formation.

---

## Research Problem

Identifying pore fluids at the reservoir level from seismic data without requiring well log data, specifically focusing on distinguishing between gas, oil, and brine in formations like the Arab formation.

## Objectives

- Characterize pore fluid in a reservoir by joint inversion of acoustic (P-wave) velocity and (P-wave) impedance from surface seismic data.
- Generate synthetic seismic data for a three-layer geological model.
- Invert Acoustic Impedance (AI).
- Compute density and compressional modulus (M) from seismic data.
- Invert AI, acoustic velocity, density, and compressional modulus to determine pore fluid density and velocity.
- Characterize pore fluids in the target reservoir using seismic data.

## Methods

The study uses a three-layer model (overburden, cap rock, and reservoir). Gassmann's equation is used for fluid substitution to calculate saturated rock bulk modulus. Acoustic impedance (AI) and P-wave velocity are derived from seismic data. A recursive inversion method is applied to AI to determine the compressional modulus (M), which is then combined with density calculations to invert for fluid density and fluid velocity. The study evaluates three cases: Case 1 (gas/brine), Case 2 (oil/brine) on a synthetic model, and Case 3 (oil/brine) using actual Arab formation porosity data.

## Data and Materials

Synthetic seismic traces generated from a three-layer model; parameters for the Arab formation including porosity (10-30%), depth (1600m), temperature (150°C), and pressure (21.16 MPa).

## Key Findings

- In Case 1, fluid density inversion showed a jump from 95 g/cm³ to 1059 g/cm³ at the contact, identifying gas and brine. ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 58]])
- In Case 2, fluid velocity inversion showed values of 745 m/s (oil) and 1500 m/s (brine), successfully distinguishing the two fluids. ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 70]])
- The error between inverted fluid properties and calculated values for gas, oil, and brine was nearly 0% in both synthetic cases. ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 14]])
- In Case 3 (Arab formation), a 10% porosity assumption resulted in a fluid density of 723 g/cm³ for the oil zone and 1062 g/cm³ for the brine zone. ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 75]])
- Acoustic impedance (AI) showed a sudden change at 1.07s in both cases, providing evidence of two different pore fluids. ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 52]])
- AI, compressional bulk modulus, and saturated rock density inversion provide evidence of two different pore fluids due to sudden changes between upper and lower zones. ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 86]])
- Fluid density and fluid velocity inversions provide better evidence and more information for identifying pore fluids compared to other parameters. ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 87]])
- The percent error between inverted fluid velocity and computed values for gas, live oil, and brine was nearly 0% in both Case-1 and Case-2. ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 87]])
- For the Arab formation at 10% porosity, the inferred fluid velocity for oil was 746 m/s and for brine was 1481 m/s. ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 76]])
- In the Arab formation at 10% porosity, the error for inverted fluid density (oil) was 0.0138% and for brine was 0.2238%. ([[2016-ali|Ali, 2016, p. 88]])

## Limitations

The study notes that the primary constraint is porosity, which was assumed for the model rather than being calculated from independent means.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Integrate the current model with AVO analysis for fluid characterization.
- Calibrate AI inversion with well data to improve accuracy.
