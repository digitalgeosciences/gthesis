# WELLBORE INSTABILITY ANALYSIS FROM A HIGHLY FRACTURED CARBONATE GAS RESERVOIRS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates wellbore instability, specifically severe loss circulation, in a naturally fractured carbonate gas reservoir. It utilizes rock mechanics laboratory testing (triaxial), image log analysis, and Mechanical Earth Modeling (MEM) to determine the root causes of instability and provide recommendations for future drilling programs.

---

## Research Problem

Severe loss circulation events in a naturally fractured carbonate gas reservoirs led to significant lost drilling time and potential well loss due to the mismatch between mud weight and formation stress/fracture presence.

## Objectives

- Gathering and reviewing existing data from wells.
- Conducting Rock Mechanical testing for the available core plugs.
- Modeling of rock mechanical static and dynamic properties.
- Calibration of rock mechanical properties using available core data.
- Construct single well Mechanical Earth Models for selected wells.
- Characterization of Natural fractures using available image logs data.

## Methods

The study utilized a multi-step geomechanical workflow: 1. Overburden stress was calculated using a global bulk density extrapolation method (Equation 4.3) which showed lower error than the Amoco method. 2. Pore pressure was determined using MDT and DST data to establish a hydrostatic gradient. 3. Rock mechanical properties (Young's Modulus, Poisson's ratio, UCS, and Friction Angle) were derived from sonic logs and calibrated against triaxial compression test results from core plugs. 4. A Mohr-Coulomb Stress Model and Poro-Elastic Horizontal Strain Model were used to determine safe mud weight windows. 5. Image log analysis was performed to identify natural vs. induced fractures. 6. Statistical correlations (linear regression) were applied to link loss circulation volumes with rock properties and seismic ratios.

## Data and Materials

Data from six wells (Well-A, Well-B, Well-C, Well-D, Well-E, and Well-F) in a carbonate gas reservoir. Includes: Daily Drilling Reports (DDR), MDT/DST pressure tests, RHOB and Sonic logs, triaxial compression test results on 20 core plugs from two wells, and 3D seismic data.

## Key Findings

- The study identified 32 loss circulation events across the analyzed wells in the reservoir section. ([[2019-al-nutaifi|Al-Nutaifi, 2019, p. 60]])
- Core samples from Well-A and Well-F showed a multi-mode distribution of porosity, indicating significant heterogeneity in the carbonate reservoir. ([[2019-al-nutaifi|Al-Nutaifi, 2019, p. 51]])
- Triaxial testing results for sample #1 yielded a static Young's modulus of 17,254.6 psi and a dynamic Young's modulus of 12,360.2 psi. ([[2019-al-nutaifi|Al-Nutaifi, 2019, p. 58]])
- Sample #10 showed a static Young's modulus of 21,454.9 psi and a dynamic Young's modulus of 17,969.5 psi with a friction angle of 66.5 degrees. ([[2019-al-nutaifi|Al-Nutaifi, 2019, p. 58]])
- The study confirmed that the presence of natural fractures significantly increases the risk of loss circulation due to their high permeability and potential for mud invasion. ([[2019-al-nutaifi|Al-Nutaifi, 2019, p. 25]])
- The global bulk density extrapolation method yielded an average error of 5.1% (max 30%) for overburden stress calculation, significantly outperforming the Amoco method (6% avg, 114% max). ([[2019-al-nutaifi|Al-Nutaifi, 2019, p. 47]])
- MDT data provided a clear gas gradient of 0.09 psi/ft across the field for pore pressure modeling. ([[2019-al-nutaifi|Al-Nutaifi, 2019, p. 51]])
- A linear correlation between dynamic and static Young's Modulus was established with a regression factor of 0.87 (Eq. 4.8). ([[2019-al-nutaifi|Al-Nutaifi, 2019, p. 57]])
- A linear relationship between bulk density and UCS was established: UCS = 12,825 * rho_log - 19,895. ([[2019-al-nutaifi|Al-Nutaifi, 2019, p. 77]])
- A correlation for friction angle (FANG) was established as FANG = 17.489 * rho_log - 5.1471. ([[2019-al-nutaifi|Al-Nutaifi, 2019, p. 78]])
- Loss circulation events were categorized into two types: those where mud weight exceeded the safe window (induced fractures) and those within the safe window but occurring in natural fracture zones. ([[2019-al-nutaifi|Al-Nutaifi, 2019, p. 93]])
- The Vp/Vs ratio showed a linear best fit with a regression error of 0.82 when correlated against loss circulation volume, suggesting its use for predicting circulation zones from seismic data. ([[2019-al-nutaifi|Al-Nutaifi, 2019, p. 89]])
- A strong relationship was found between the loss circulation gradient (BBL/ft) and Young's Modulus (YM). ([[2019-al-nutaifi|Al-Nutaifi, 2019, p. 109]])

## Limitations

The study noted that several standard correlation methods for estimating YM, UCS, and Friction Angle from logs had high statistical errors, necessitating the development of local correlations using core data. Additionally, a lack of extensive Leak-Off Test (LOT) results led to the use of fixed values for certain calibration parameters.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study recommends coupling Image log data with 1-D MEM modeling for all wells to build confidence intervals for wellbore stability. It also suggests using Vp/Vs ratios from seismic data to construct 3D models for predicting loss circulation zones.
