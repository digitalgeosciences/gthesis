# The Effect of retarder on gelation time and stability of polyacrylamide chromium (III) gel system

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the effect of sodium lactate as a retarder on the gelation time and stability of polyacrylamide chromium (III) gel systems used for water shutoff. It concludes that sodium lactate effectively extends gelation time from hours to days, while the resulting gel remains stable and significantly reduces core permeability.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the need for effective chemical diversion agents in oilfields, specifically investigating how different concentrations of a retarder (sodium lactate) affect the performance of a polyacrylamide-chromium acetate crosslinked gel system under varying temperatures and shear rates to plug porous media.

## Objectives

- Study the effect of retarder on the stability and gelation time of polyacrylamide chromium (III) gel system
- Test the effectiveness of the gel as a plugging agent in porous media at high temperatures
- Evaluate the effect of sodium lactate concentration on the gelation time of polyacrylamide chromium systems.
- Assess the stability of various polyacrylamide chromium formulations at temperatures up to 120°C.
- Determine the impact of different chemical concentrations and temperatures on viscosity and core permeability reduction.
- Evaluate the impact of temperature and shear rate on gel solution viscosity.

## Methods

The study utilized bottle testing (Sydansk method) for rapid screening of gelation time and stability, where samples were assigned a code (A-J) based on flow behavior. Viscosity was measured using a Wells-Brookfield cone/plate viscometer at 40°C, 60°C, and 90°C. Coreflooding experiments were conducted in Hassle core holders to measure permeability reduction in sandstone and limestone samples. Chemical components included polyacrylamide (FLOPAAM 0331 L), chromium acetate (X-LINK 5000) as a crosslinker, and sodium lactate as the retarder.

## Data and Materials

60 total experiments were conducted: 43 for gelation time measurement, 9 for viscosity measurement, and 8 for coreflooding. Materials included polyacrylamide (15% concentration, <1 mol% hydrolysis), chromium acetate (11.5% concentration), and sodium lactate (60% solution). Core samples consisted of sandstone and limestone.

## Key Findings

- At 90°C, gelation time for 1.0 wt.% sodium lactate was 45 hours, for 2.0 wt.% it was 96 hours, and for 3.0 wt.% it was 156 hours. ([[1997-al-nashmi|Al-Nashmi, 1997, p. 103]])
- The gel solution was found to be stable over a period of 100 days. ([[1997-al-nashmi|Al-Nashmi, 1997, p. 133]])
- The viscosity of the gel solution decreased with increasing shear rate but increased with increasing time and temperature. ([[1997-al-nashmi|Al-Nashmi, 1997, p. 133]])
- The gel viscosity decreased if shear rate is increased. ([[1997-al-nashmi|Al-Nashmi, 1997, p. 16]])
- The permeability of the cores was reduced to less than 1.0% of its original value. ([[1997-al-nashmi|Al-Nashmi, 1997, p. 16]])
- Thiourea and sodium bisulfite were used as reducing agents; thiourea provided a gelation time 50 to 70 times longer than sodium bisulfite. ([[1997-al-nashmi|Al-Nashmi, 1997, p. 45]])
- The gelation time of polyacrylamide Cr(III) decreased with increasing temperature, following a linear relationship between the logarithm of the gelation time and the reciprocal of the absolute reaction temperature. ([[1997-al-nashmi|Al-Nashmi, 1997, p. 46]])
- Shear rate at the wall was estimated to be 0.5 sec⁻¹; higher shear rates were found to shorten gelation time. ([[1997-al-nashmi|Al-Nashmi, 1997, p. 47]])
- The study of different ligands showed that malonate gave a gelation time of 33, salicylate 27, glycolate 12, and lactate 9 (relative to acetate) at 120°C. ([[1997-al-nashmi|Al-Nashmi, 1997, p. 53]])
- The permeability reduction was found to be due to the separation of oil and water pathways through a porous medium rather than gravity or lubrication effects. ([[1997-al-nashmi|Al-Nashmi, 1997, p. 39]])
- The addition of sodium lactate successfully increased the gelation time for polyacrylamide chromium systems, allowing for better placement in treatment zones. ([[1997-al-nashmi|Al-Nashmi, 1997, p. 89]])
- At 60°C, a ratio of 30 (6.0% polyacrylamide to 0.2% chromium acetate) was found to be an appropriate balance for gelation time control. ([[1997-al-nashmi|Al-Nashmi, 1997, p. 91]])
- Gelation time decreased as temperature increased from room temperature to 120°C, following the Arrhenius relationship. ([[1997-al-nashmi|Al-Nashmi, 1997, p. 101]])
- The stability of a specific formulation (6.0% PA, 0.2% CA) was found to be 120 days at room temperature and 72 days at 60°C. ([[1997-al-nashmi|Al-Nashmi, 1997, p. 93]])
- A coreflooding experiment showed that the apparent gelation time in porous media (85 hours) occurred much earlier than in a beaker (240 hours). ([[1997-al-nashmi|Al-Nashmi, 1997, p. 62]])

## Limitations

At 120°C, it was not possible to evaluate the stability of the gel because the solution evaporated quickly in the vial.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Repeat study using different retarder types.
- Investigate if oxygen or other factors cause degelation at high temperatures (90°C and 120°C) using sealed, degassed ampules.
- Test the flow of the formulated gel in fractures and sandpacks.
- Study the phase stability of the formulated gel with Arab-D brine under reservoir conditions.
