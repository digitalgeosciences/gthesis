# Texture, Pore Type, Mechanical, and Natural Fracture Characterization of Paleozoic and Mesozoic Mud Rocks and Shale, Saudi Arabia.

**Summary**: The thesis characterizes the pore systems, mineralogy, and petrography of Paleozoic and Mesozoic mudrocks (Hanifa Formation, Tuwaiq Mountain Limestone, and Marrat Formation) in Central Saudi Arabia. It utilizes a multi-analytical approach including NMR, N2 adsorption, and μCT scanning to link specific pore types—such as intra-skeletal, interparticle, and organic matter-hosted pores—to diagenetic processes and reservoir properties.

---

## Research Problem

The high heterogeneity of fine-grained sedimentary rocks (mudrocks) at small scales makes it difficult to predict pore system characteristics and mechanical properties based solely on large-scale data. This study addresses how lithofacies variations in the Qusaiba Member and Jurassic carbonate mudrocks influence these critical reservoir parameters.

## Objectives

- Understand the effect of facies and sedimentary parameters on pore system characteristics (types, sizes, and structures).
- Characterize the impact of small-scale sedimentary structures (physical and biogenic) on the porosity of the Qusaiba Member.
- Evaluate the mechanical properties of Jurassic carbonate mudrocks to identify brittle zones and optimize hydraulic fracturing plans.
- Quantify the impact of different rock fabrics (contorted, laminated, bioturbated) on permeability anisotropy.
- Evaluate the role of mineralogy and pore types in determining reservoir potential.
- Develop a conceptual model for understanding pore evolution over time.

## Methods

The study utilized a multi-scale approach: 1. Petrography and thin-section analysis were used to identify lithofacies based on mineralogy, texture, and sedimentary structures. 2. XRD (Malvern Panalytical) was used to quantify mineral content for 60 samples. 3. Helium porosity was measured using a TPI instrument on core plugs. 4. Ultrasonic velocity measurements (P-wave and S-wave) were conducted under varying confining pressures (5-50 MPa) using the NER Autolab 500. 5. Rock strength was assessed via Unconfined Compressive Strength (UCS) and Brazilian tensile strength tests. 6. Impulse hammer testing (NER AutoScan II) provided hardness indices.

## Data and Materials

The study analyzed samples from the Early Silurian Qusaiba Member (Qaliba Formation) and Middle to Upper Jurassic carbonate mudrocks (Hawtah member of Hanifa Formation and Tuwaiq Mountain Limestone Formation). Analysis included 40 thin sections, 10 core plugs for NMR analysis, and approximately 50 mg samples for TOC determination.

## Key Findings

- The Qusaiba Member lithofacies were categorized into three groups: proximal (LF1, LF2, LF4, LF6), medial (LF3, LF7), and distal (LF5) shelf deposits. ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 37]])
- Interparticle pores in the Qusaiba Member were found to be elongated or triangular, with sizes ranging from 500 nm to 4 μm; those in LF4 ranged from 2 to 7 μm. ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 39]])
- Intraparticle pores within clay minerals (kaolinite, chlorite, illite) were found to be smaller than interparticle pores, with sizes ranging from 100 nm to 500 nm in various lithofacies. ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 40]])
- NMR-derived T2 distribution curves for the Qusaiba Member showed bimodal distributions in proximal and distal shelf lithofacies, while medial lithofacies exhibited unimodal distributions. ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 41]])
- The total NMR porosity of storm-wave-dominated medial shelf lithofacies ranged from 17.3% (LF1) to 19% (LF3). ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 41]])
- TML mudrocks exhibited less brittleness than Hanifa mudrocks due to higher clay content and more abundant laminations. ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 20]])
- Small-scale sedimentary structures (physical and biogenic) significantly impacted the permeability of offshore samples, while bioturbation affected the lower shoreface portions. ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 19]])
- Lithofacies F4 (planar-laminated) showed higher anisotropy and a mean spot permeability of 19 mD compared to the core plug average of 8 mD due to calcitic cementation in Si-rich laminae. ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 62]])
- F7 (bioturbated) showed significant permeability enhancement in sand-filled burrows, with spot permeability reaching up to 46 mD compared to the matrix's 2-10 mD. ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 63]])
- Thalassinoides trace fossils (horizontal) produced the most anisotropic variance map in F7, with a mean spot permeability of 28 mD. ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 64]])
- Gypsum-filled veins in F7 showed higher permeability (10-25 mD) compared to the host rock (2-10 mD). ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 64]])
- F1 and F3 were identified as being deposited below the storm-wave base in an offshore setting. ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 57]])
- The study identified seven lithofacies (F1-F7) based on mineralogy, texture, and sedimentary structures. ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 60]])
- Hanifa mudstone LFH3 showed higher pore abundance (9.1 p.u.) than LFH2 (9.1 p.u.) but lower than LFH1 (18.4 p.u.). ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 130]])
- TML mudstone LFT2 had higher total NMR porosity (12.8 p.u.) than LFT1 (11 p.u.), but a lower peak T2 value (2.8 ms vs 3.2 ms). ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 130]])

## Limitations

The author notes the limitations of outcrop studies regarding sample size but highlights their necessity for constraining depositional and stratigraphic variations in large-scale systems.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The results are intended to help overcome subsurface data limitations by providing a model for how lithofacies variation controls petrophysical properties in similar storm-wave influenced mudrock successions.
