# Texture, Pore Type, Mechanical, and Natural Fracture Characterization of Paleozoic and Mesozoic Mud Rocks and Shale, Saudi Arabia.

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the influence of lithofacies and diagenetic processes on pore systems, mineralogy, and mechanical properties in Paleozoic and Mesozoic mudrocks (specifically the Qusaiba Member) in Saudi Arabia. It utilizes a multi-scale approach combining petrography, SEM/EDS, XRD, and helium porosity-permeability measurements to characterize how sedimentary structures like bioturbation and laminations affect reservoir potential.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the complexity of pore systems and mechanical properties in fine-grained sedimentary rocks (mudrocks and shales) which are economically significant but possess high compositional and textural complexities. Specifically, it aims to link pore parameters with reservoir properties like flow pathways and brittleness in the Hanifa, TML, and Marrat formations.

## Methods

The study utilized 30 rock samples from three sites near Riyadh. Analysis included: XRD using a Malvern Panalytical instrument for mineralogy; thin-section petrography to identify lithofacies based on particle size, minerals, and structures; Helium porosity (TPI instrument) for total porosity; NMR (Geospec rock analyzer at 0.05 T) for pore size distribution and relaxation times; N2 adsorption (Micrometrics ASAP 2460) using the BET model to determine specific area and Barret-Joyner-Halenda model for pore size distribution; SEM-EDS for micro-scale details; micro-CT (Xradia Versa XRM-500) for 3D pore network imaging; and TOC analysis (Shimadzu SSM 5000A) to quantify organic carbon.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized samples from the Early Silurian Qusaiba Member of the Qaliba Formation, the Hawtah member of the Hanifa Formation (carbonate mudrocks), and the Tuwaiq Mountain Formation. Data included 22 samples for ultrasonic velocity under pressure, 65 core plug samples for ambient velocity, 21 samples for UCS/tensile strength, and various lithofacies classifications based on petrographic analysis.

## Key Findings

- Lithofacies in the Qusaiba Member are categorized into 7 types (LF1-LF7) based on mineralogy, texture, and sedimentary structures. ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 30]])
- SEM-EDS and µCT observations revealed that pore systems in the Qusaiba Member are influenced by both primary depositional textures and secondary diagenetic processes. ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 64]])
- The study identified specific pore types, including interparticle pores, intraparticle pores (within kaolinite), and micro-fracture pores. ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 40]])
- page_number_reference_note_for_finding_3_is_based_on_figure_captions_and_text_description_in_chapters_4_and_5 ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022]])
- Two main types of pore systems were identified across all lithofacies: interparticle and intraparticle pores. ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 24]])
- NMR data showed positively and negatively skewed bimodal porosity distribution curves in the proximal and distal shelf lithofacies, respectively; medial shelf lithofacies showed normally distributed unimodal curves. ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 24]])
- Phyllosilicate minerals associated with interparticle pores (≤ 1 μm) were observed in the distal lithofacies, while larger-scale interparticle pores were found in medial and proximal lithofacies. ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 24]])
- TML exhibits less brittleness than Hanifa due to higher clay content and more abundant laminations. ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 19]])
- small-scale soft sediment deformation and lamination had a pronounced impact on the permeability of offshore samples, while bioturbation affected the lower shoreface part. ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022]])
- page_number_not_found_in_text_but_in_abstract ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022]])
- Seven lithofacies were identified, categorized into three associations: proximal (LF1, LF2, LF4, and LF6), medial (LF3 and LF7), and distal (LF5) shelf deposits of a storm-wave system. ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 37]])
- Interparticle pores in the Qusaiba Member ranged from 500 nm to 4 µm, with specific morphologies like triangular shapes between quartz and K-feldspar. ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 39]])
- Intraparticle pores were found within various minerals: kaolinite booklets (~100 nm), chlorite flakes (100-500 nm), and intercrystalline pores between iron oxide crystals (~1 µm). ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 40]])
- The total NMR porosity of storm-wave-dominated medial shelf lithofacies ranged from 17.3% (LF1) to 19% (LF3). ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 41]])
- Lithofacies LF1 and LF3 exhibited narrower ranges of relaxation times compared to LF2 and LF6. ([[2022-abdlmutalib|Abdlmutalib, 2022, p. 41]])

## Limitations

Upscaling small-scale variabilities (like those from spot permeability) into large-scale reservoir flow simulations was noted as challenging due to computational power constraints.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The results are intended to help overcome subsurface data limitations by providing a model for how lithofacies variations control petrophysical properties in similar storm-wave influenced mudrock successions.
