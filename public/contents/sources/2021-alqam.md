# ROLE OF ROCK MINERALOGY AND OIL COMPOSITION IN WETTABILITY ALTERATION OF CARBONATE RESERVOIRS BY SUBCRITICAL CARBON DIOXIDE

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of rock mineralogy (dolomite vs. calcite) and oil composition (specifically asphaltene content, sulfur, and nitrogen levels) on the wettability of carbonate rocks in the presence of brine and carbonated water. It concludes that high asphaltenes content significantly promotes oil-wet behavior, while specific mineralogical compositions influence the degree of water-wetness.

---

## Research Problem

The lack of an integrated approach to understanding how both rock mineralogy (dolomite/calcite distribution) and complex oil chemistry (asphaltene structure and concentration) jointly affect wettibility alteration in carbonate reservoirs, especially during carbonated water injection.

## Objectives

- Investigate the role played by rock mineralogy, brine composition, and polar compounds present in a crude oil on rock wettability under conditions of a typical shallow reservoir.
- Study the effect of pressure, temperature, polar compounds, and rock mineralogy on wettability alteration caused by exposure to subcritical CO2.
- Employ a variety of dead from Arabian crude oils (over a range of API gravity) and core plugs from Arabian carbonate reservoirs in the study.
- Correlate rock wettability alteration with all investigated parameters.
- Investigate the role of rock mineralogy on the destabilization of the aqueous film separating oil from carbonate surfaces.
- Characterize the impact of different crude oil compositions (based on asphaltene content) on wettability.

## Methods

The research utilized a multi-scale approach combining experimental measurements and computational modeling. Pendant drop techniques were used to measure contact angles and interfacial tension (IFT) for various oil types on four carbonate rock samples (100% Dolomite, 100% Calcite, 67% Dolomite + 33% Calcite, and 37% Dolomite + 63% Calcite). SARA analysis was performed to characterize the crude oils. Fourier Transform Ion Cyclotron Resonance Mass Spectrometry (FT-ICR MS) was used for detailed asphaltene characterization. Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulations were employed to model the behavior of asphaltenes and carbonated water at the molecular level, specifically calculating free energy to predict contact angles and IFT.

## Data and Materials

Two carbonate rock samples: C63D37 (63% calcite, 37% dolomite) and D67C33 (67% dolomite, 33% calcite). One crude oil sample (Type V) with specific SARA values (Saturate 38.5%, Aromatic 31.9%, Asphaltene 11.6%, Resin 18.0%). Brine with TDS of 57,670 ppm used to create carbonated water.

## Key Findings

- The injection of carbonated water caused the contact angle to change from weakly oil-wet to intermediate or water-wet wettability. ([[2021-alqam|ALQAM, 2021, p. 42]])
- Molecular simulation results showed that IFT was reduced by 13-17% in asphaltenes-carbonated water systems. ([[2021-alqam|ALQAM, 2021, p. 42]])
- The study identified four main factors influencing the crude oil/brine/rock system: polar interaction, surface precipitation, acid/base interaction, and ion binding. ([[2021-alqam|ALQAM, 2021]])
- page_number_placeholder_for_finding_3_is_not_explicitly_given_but_context_is_page_35 ([[2021-alqam|ALQAM, 2021]])
- Asphaltenes with larger poly-aromatic rings tended to precipitate easier and altered the wettability at high pressure. ([[2021-alqam|ALQAM, 2021, p. 37]])
- The study confirmed that carbonate rocks are more sensitive to components of crude oil than sandstone. ([[2021-alqam|ALQAM, 2021, p. 33]])
- Rock sample D(100) showed a contact angle of 136.1° with Type-V oil (high asphaltenes), indicating strong oil-wet behavior, while the same rock with Type-IV oil (lower asphaltenes) showed 87.8°. ([[2021-alqam|ALQAM, 2021, p. 43]])
- In mixed systems, decreasing dolomite content from D67+C33 to D37+C63 caused a shift in contact angle from 92.7° to 128.8° for Type-V oil. ([[2021-alqam|ALQAM, 2021, p. 43]])
- The use of carbonated water as an external phase on pure dolomite D*(100) resulted in a contact angle increase from 111.2° to 135.5° when pressure decreased from 2500 to 500 psig. ([[2021-alqam|ALQAM, 2021, p. 68]])
- Type-V crude oil (11.6 wt% asphaltenes) consistently produced higher contact angles and more oil-wet behavior across all rock types compared to Type-IV crude oil (6.4 wt% asphaltenes). ([[2021-alqam|ALQAM, 2021, p. 65]])
- Adhesion tension for D100 with Type-I oil was +14.40 dynes/cm, while C63D37 with Type-V oil was -9.13 dynes/cm. ([[2021-alqam|ALQAM, 2021, p. 95]])
- Initial wettability on carbonate rocks was found to be weakly oil-wet (C63D37) and neutral (D67C33) when using brine as the external phase. ([[2021-alqam|ALQAM, 2021, p. 120]])
- Injection of carbonated water shifted wettibility from weakly oil-wet to neutral and water-wet for both rock samples. ([[2021-alqam|ALQAM, 2021, p. 120]])
- Molecular simulation showed that the addition of CO2 reduced the interfacial tension (IFT) by 8% to 15.5% across different asphaltene types. ([[2021-alqam|ALQAM, 2021, p. 124]])
- Asphaltene structures were found to be chemically similar, with average condensed aromatic cores of 8 rings for hydrocarbons and 9 rings for sulfur-containing compounds. ([[2021-alqam|ALQAM, 2021, p. 101]])

## Limitations

The study notes that more work is needed to reach a fully conclusive conclusion regarding the specific effects of carbonated water on various rock and oil combinations due to limited sample variety in those specific tests.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future investigations should be conducted with additional rock and oil samples specifically using carbonated water to draw firmer conclusions on its impact on wettibility.
