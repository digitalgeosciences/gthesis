# Effect of Liquid Drop-out on the Productivity of a Rich Gas Condensate Reservoir

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of liquid drop-out on the productivity of a rich gas condensate reservoir using a 2-D radial compositional simulation. It concludes that critical condensate saturation and high production rates significantly affect well performance, with a 91% reduction in gas flow productivity observed across all studied cases.

---

## Research Problem

The loss of well productivity in rich gas condensate reservoirs when pressure falls below the dew point, leading to the accumulation of liquid hydrocarbons (condensate) near the wellbore and subsequent impairment of inflow performance.

## Objectives

- Identification of the parameters controlling the productivity of the gas condensate well.
- Evaluation of the effect of condensate drop-out near the wellbore on the productivity of the gas condensate well.
- Determination of optimum production rate to achieve the optimum phase production from the gas condensate reservoir.
- Investigation of the extent of condensate banks in different layers.
- To construct a geological model based on petrophysical and core analysis.
- To match PVT data using an equation of state (EOS) model.

## Methods

The study utilized a single-well radial compositional model (2D, r-z coordinate system) using the CHEARS simulator. Core analysis and petrophysical data were used to define a 7-layer reservoir model (reduced to 4 active layers). PVT properties were modeled using an EOS approach with the Chevron Phase Calculation Program. A two-phase relative permeability model was implemented based on pore size distribution indices. Sensitivity analyses were conducted on grid cell count, first cell radius, critical condensate saturation (0% to 40%), and various production rates (20 to 60 MMSCF/D).

## Data and Materials

The study used core samples for petrophysical analysis, a multi-layer reservoir model with 4 active layers (total thickness of 160 ft), and PVT data including composition, dew point pressure (5153 psia), and various fluid properties. Simulation runs were conducted over a 10-year period.

## Key Findings

- Gas condensate well productivity is reduced as the critical condensate saturation increases. ([[1999-fawumi|Fawumi, 1999, p. 18]])
- A 91% reduction in gas flow productivity was observed for all production rates investigated. ([[1999-fawumi|Fawumi, 1999, p. 18]])
- Gas production rate more than 40MMSCF/D offers no significant improvement on cumulative gas and condensate produced. ([[1999-fawumi|Fawumi, 1999, p. 18]])
- The percent liquid condensed near the wellbore in all layers ranged between 37 to 44 percent. ([[1999-fawumi|Fawumi, 1999, p. 18]])
- Pressure distribution remains consistent at a distance greater than 100 ft from the wellbore, but shows significant variation within the near-wellbore region. ([[1999-fawumi|Fawumi, 1999, p. 18]])
- The pore size distribution index (A) was calculated as 0.73 based on J-function analysis of core samples. ([[1999-fawumi|Fawumi, 1999, p. 56]])
- Initial water saturation for the reservoir rock was determined to be 0.33. ([[1999-fawumi|Fawumi, 1999, p. 56]])
- The critical condensate saturation was determined to be 10% for the study's primary model. ([[1999-fawumi|Fawumi, 1999, p. 89]])
- Using more than 20 radial grid cells away from the wellbore showed minimal difference in results, with 30 cells being sufficient to capture condensation effects accurately. ([[1999-fawumi|Fawumi, 1999, p. 81]])
- The size of the first radial grid cell (r1) must be small; values between 0.4 and 1.0 ft showed no significant difference at high production rates, with 0.4 ft being used in this study. ([[1999-fawumi|Fawumi, 1999, p. 83]])
- Higher critical condensate saturation leads to lower well productivity; for example, a 20 MMSCF/D rate resulted in different stabilized gas productions of 13500, 1000, 7500, 4000, and 2000 MSCF/D for critical saturations of 0, 10, 20, 30, and 40% respectively. ([[1999-fawumi|Fawumi, 1999, p. 89]])
- The well productivity index (PI) dropped significantly from approximately 22-23 MSCF/D/psia to roughly 2.1 MSCF/D/psia once the pressure fell below the dew point and condensate blocked the flow. ([[1999-fawumi|Fawumi, 1999, p. 98]])
- The average reservoir pressure in the entire reservoir did not fall below the dew point (5153 psia) until much later than the wellbore pressure, which dropped rapidly due to local condensation. ([[1999-fawumi|Fawumi, 1999, p. 98]])
- A production rate of approximately 40 MMSCF/D was determined as the optimal balance for maximizing recovery while managing condensate dropout effects. ([[1999-fawumi|Fawumi, 1999, p. 100]])
- Increasing critical condensate saturation reduces the stabilized gas production rates and well productivity. ([[1999-fawumi|Fawumi, 1999, p. 160]])

## Limitations

Factors such as reservoir dip and non-Darcy flow were not considered in this study.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study suggests that accurate modeling of relative permeability as a function of capillary number (or its equivalent, the trapping number) is essential for predicting well performance in gas condensate reservoirs.
