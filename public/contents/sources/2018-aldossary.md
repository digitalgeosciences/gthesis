# EFFECT OF PORE PRESSURE DEPLETION ON WELLBORE STABILITY AND HYDRAULIC FRACTURING IN SANDSTONE RESERVOIR

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of pore pressure depletion on wellbore stability and hydraulic fracturing in a Saudi Arabian sandstone gas reservoir. It utilizes 3D and 4D geomechanical modeling to evaluate how stress changes due to production affect rock mechanics, fracture growth, and drilling safety.

---

## Research Problem

Depletion in tight gas reservoirs creates complex stress changes that complicate both wellbore stability during drilling and the predictability of hydraulic fracture growth, requiring advanced geomechanical modeling to navigate narrow mud weight windows and identify stress barriers.

## Objectives

- Characterize stresses, strain, and rock failure due to changes in pore pressure from reservoir injection/depletion.
- Evaluate how geomechanical changes in stress and strain impact wellbore stability and stimulation operations.
- Determine stress rotation around faulted zones.
- Analyze the effect of stress changes due to depletion on hydraulic fracture propagation.
- Evaluate the impact of pore pressure depletion on wellbore stability in a depleted reservoir.
- Develop 1D and 3D geomechanical models to predict fracture containment and identify potential hazards.

## Methods

The study utilizes a multi-step modeling workflow: (1) Construction of calibrated 1D geomechanical models using acoustic and density logs to derive dynamic properties (Young's modulus, Poisson's ratio) and strength properties (compressive confining strength, friction angle, cohesion). (2) Conversion of dynamic properties to static properties using core data. (3) Calculation of overburden stress from density logs and minimum horizontal stress via the poroelastic equation, calibrated with minifrac tests. (4) Upscaling 1D properties to 3D geomechanical models using Sequential Gaussian Simulation (SGS). (5) Coupling 3D geomechanical models with fluid flow (one-way coupling) to simulate the effects of pore pressure changes on drilling and stimulation.

## Data and Materials

The study utilizes acoustic logs, density logs, core data, and minifrac test results from wells in a tight gas sandstone reservoir (Khuff Formation). Two specific hydraulic fracturing case studies were modeled: Case 1 (two depletion scenarios) and Case 2 (base case vs. depletion case).

## Key Findings

- The 3D geomechanics model successfully captured stress variations at stratigraphic boundaries where abrupt changes in elastic properties occur. ([[2018-aldossary|AlDossary, 2018, p. 60]])
- Pore pressure in the study area was predicted to range from 6,600 to 7,800 psi in 2017 and deplete to 3,300 to 3,900 psi by 2038. ([[2018-aldossary|AlDossary, 2018, p. 61]])
- Overburden stress was calculated to range between 11,500 and 14,000 psi. ([[2018-aldossary|AlDossary, 2018, p. 60]])
- Stress magnitude in the reservoir decreases during depletion, causing an increase of horizontal stress (σH) in adjacent layers, creating a stress barrier for hydraulic fractures. ([[2018-aldossary|AlDossary, 2018, p. 63]])
- The study identified that triaxial tests are essential for determining static Young's modulus and Poisson's ratio to accurately model rock behavior under high-stress conditions. ([[2018-aldossary|AlDossary, 2018, p. 35]])
- A correlation of $E_{sta} = 0.8029 \times E_{dyn} - 0.1666$ was established for the sandstone reservoir to convert dynamic logs to static properties. ([[2018-aldossary|AlDossary, 2018, p. 48]])
- The relationship between $V_s$ and $V_p$ followed a best-fit curve of $V_s = 0.87765 \times V_p - 0.809$. ([[2018-aldossary|AlDossary, 2018, p. 47]])
- The study confirmed that stress rotation occurs near faulted zones, which can significantly impact the orientation of hydraulic fractures. ([[2018-aldossary|AlDossary, 2018, p. 13]])
- In Scenario-A, the closure pressure was 8,969 psi (gradient of 0.63 psi/ft), while in Scenario-B it was 8,304 psi (gradient of 0.58 psi/ft). ([[2018-aldossary|AlDossary, 2018, p. 68]])
- In Case 1, Scenario-A resulted in a hydraulic fracture half-length of 401 ft and propped length of 397 ft; Scenario-B resulted in a half-length of 366 ft and propped length of 362 ft. ([[2018-aldossary|AlDossary, 2018, p. 69]])
- In Case 2, the base case had a half-length of 337 ft, while the depletion case showed an increased half-length of 458 ft and propped length of 444 ft. ([[2018-aldossary|AlDossary, 2018, p. 72]])
- Depletion causes the magnitude of the fracture gradient to decrease, creating distinct barriers between reservoirs and adjacent layers, which aids in predicting fracture heights. ([[2018-aldossary|AlDossary, 2018, p. 79]])
- 3D geomechanical models revealed that while sigma_H decreases in depleted areas, adjoining layers experience higher sigma_H, increasing stress contrast. ([[2018-aldossary|AlDossary, 2018, p. 80]])
- Wellbore stability analysis showed that breakouts occur primarily in the minimum stress direction in both depletion scenarios. ([[2018-aldossary|AlDossary, 2018, p. 80]])
- The potential for collapse and losses is significantly higher in more depleted areas due to the shift in the mud weight window. ([[2018-aldossary|AlDossary, 2018, p. 80]])

## Limitations

1D geomechanical models are insufficient for estimating stress contrasts in adjoining layers caused by depletion; 3D modeling is required for accurate fracture design in these complex environments.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The results are intended to enhance future drilling operations and production forecasting by providing a better understanding of cap-rock integrity and fracture containment.
