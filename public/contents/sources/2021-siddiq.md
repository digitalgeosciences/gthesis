# EVALUATING POTENTIAL OF DATE-PALM BIOCHAR FOR ARSENIC REMOVAL FROM WATER, COUPLED WITH SPECTRAL-INDUCED POLARIZATION (SIP) FOR REAL-TIME MONITORING

**Summary**: The thesis evaluates the use of date-palm leaf biochar as an adsorbent for removing arsenic from contaminated water, enhanced by iron impregnation. It further investigates the integration of Spectral-Induced Polarization (SIP) as a geophysical tool for real-time monitoring of the adsorption process in column systems.

---

## Research Problem

Arsenic contamination in groundwater poses significant health risks, and current treatment methods often lack cost-effective, eco-friendly options. There is also a need for real-time monitoring techniques to optimize adsorbent usage in large-scale applications.

## Objectives

- To synthesize a biochar formulation from the date palm biomass (DPBs) by pyrolysis method.
- To identify the main morphological and chemical characteristics of DPBs using SEM, XRD, and FTIR techniques.
- To model the DPBCs sorption capacity using adsorption modeling techniques.
- To investigate the removal of As, in lab-scale batch sorption, under various parameters, including the initial concentration, flow rate and pH of As.
- To evaluate and monitor the breakthrough and exhaust points of contaminant sorption with the SIP geophysical method's aid.
- Evaluate the potential of date-palm leaf (DPL) biochar as an adsorbent for arsenic removal.

## Methods

The study was divided into two parts: (1) Batch-sorption experiments to optimize biochar for arsenic removal. Biochar was produced from date-palm leaves via pyrolysis at 500°C and 800°C, then modified with iron nitrate. Characterization included proximate analysis, FTIR, XRD, and SEM. Adsorption kinetics were modeled using pseudo-first and second-order equations, while isotherms (Langmuir, Freundlich, Temkin) were used to determine capacity. (2) SIP monitoring of a column system. Two columns (5% and 10% biochar added to sand) were monitored using a Spectral Induced Polarization (SIP) instrument over 16 hours at a flow rate of 0.16 ml/min, with results analyzed via ICP-OES.

## Data and Materials

Samples included raw biochar, Fe-modified biochar (Fe-BC500 and Fe-BC800), and contaminated water containing 1 ppm and 5 ppm of arsenic. SIP measurements were conducted on columns with 5% and 10% biochar content.

## Key Findings

- Biochar produced at 500°C (BC-500) and 800°C (BC-800) showed significantly higher arsenic removal (15% and 22%) compared to raw date-palm leaves. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 46]])
- Iron modification of biochar led to a significant increase in adsorption capacity, with both Fe-BC500 and Fe-BC800 removing 76% of arsenic within 2 hours. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 48]])
- The optimal pH for arsenic sorption was identified between 5 and 9, with the highest removal (98%) achieved at pH 5. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 50]])
- Langmuir model provided a high R2 value of 0.9437 for Fe-BC500, indicating it as a suitable model for describing the sorption mechanism. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 49]])
- The inclusion of biochar in columns (5% and 10%) was successfully monitored using SIP signals consistent with chemical analysis of eluates. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 12]])
- A dosage of 0.2g/50ml (4g/L) was identified as the optimal dosage for arsenic removal. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 41]])
- Stirring rates between 200-250 rpm were sufficient, while rates below 100 rpm were insufficient. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 41]])
- The Freundlich model best described the adsorption mechanism for Fe-BC500 (R2 = 0.9609). ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 43]])
- FTIR confirmed that iron modification enhanced the intensity of functional groups, particularly those related to carboxyl groups. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 44]])
- XRD showed that Fe-modified biochar had a more disordered structure compared to raw biochar due to the formation of an iron hydroxide coating. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 45]])
- SEM imaging revealed a porous structure for both types of biochar, with higher temperatures producing more pores per area. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 46]])
- Both 5% and 10% biochar-amended columns achieved over 98% arsenic removal compared to the reference column (17%). ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 50]])
- SIP measurements showed that real and imaginary conductivity changed by approximately 3 orders of magnitude in response to biochar addition. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 52]])
- PCA successfully differentiated the presence of biochar and the progress of arsenic adsorption based on SIP signals. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 54]])

## Limitations

Only initial analysis was conducted for analyzing SIP response from ongoing adsorption experiments; more research is needed to establish definitive links between specific parameters and rates.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Establish quantitative links between SIP parameters and the rate of arsenic sorption.
- Evaluate other biochar modification techniques like steam activation or magnetization.
- Test biochar derived from other date palm parts (seeds, trunks).
