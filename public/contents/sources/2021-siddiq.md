# EVALUATING POTENTIAL OF DATE-PALM BIOCHAR FOR ARSENIC REMOVAL FROM WATER, COUPLED WITH SPECTRAL-INDUCED POLARIZATION (SIP) FOR REAL-TIME MONITORING

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the use of biochar derived from Date-Palm leaves (DPLs) as an adsorbent for removing arsenic from contaminated water. It evaluates three biochar formulations at different pyrolysis temperatures and explores the enhancement of adsorption capacity through iron (Fe) impregnation, while proposing Spectral Induced Polarization (SIP) for real-time monitoring.

---

## Research Problem

Arsenic contamination in groundwater poses significant health risks, and there is a need for cost-effective, eco-friendly treatment methods combined with monitoring technologies. The study specifically addresses the lack of research on natural adsorbents like biochar and their potential integration with geophysical sensing (SIP) for real-time tracking of contaminant removal.

## Objectives

- Prepare three biochar formulations by varying pyrolysis temperature.
- Investigate the ability of these biochars to adsorb arsenic.
- Enhance adsorption capacity through iron (Fe) impregnation.
- Characterize biochar morphology and surface functional groups using FTIR, XRD, and SEM.
- Determine optimal parameters for adsorption (pH, dosage, loading, stirring rate).
- Evaluate the potential of Spectral Induced Polarization (SIP) for real-time monitoring of arsenic removal in amended columns.
- To synthesize a biochar formulation from the date palm biomass (DPBs) by pyrolysis method.
- To identify the main morphological and chemical characteristics of DPBs using SEM, XRD, and FTIR techniques.
- To model the DPBCs sorption capacity using adsorption modeling techniques.
- To investigate the removal of As, in lab-scale batch sorption, under various parameters, including the initial concentration, flow rate and pH of As.
- To evaluate and monitor the breakthrough and exhaust points of contaminant sorption with the SIP geophysical method's aid.
- Prepare and characterize different biochar formulations from date-palm leaves (DPL) at varying pyrolysis temperatures.
- Evaluate the arsenic adsorption capacity of raw and iron-modified biochars.
- Determine optimal parameters for arsenic sorption, including pH, dosage, and stirring rate.
- Assess the capability of Spectral Induced Polarization (SIP) to monitor arsenic concentration changes in real-time within a porous medium.
- Evaluate the potential of date-palm leaf biochar as an adsorbent for arsenic removal from water.
- Investigate the impact of different pyrolysis temperatures on biochar's adsorption capacity.
- Assess the effectiveness of Fe-modified biochar compared to raw biochar.
- Utilize Spectral Induced Polarization (SIP) to monitor real-time changes in the electrical properties of the medium during arsenic sorption.
- To prepare biochar from Date-Palm leaves (DPLs) at different pyrolysis temperatures (500°C and 800°C).
- To characterize the physical and chemical properties of the produced biochar using various analytical techniques.
- To evaluate the adsorption capacity of plain and Fe-modified biochar for arsenic removal from water.
- To investigate the potential of Spectral Induced Polarization (SIP) as a real-time monitoring tool for tracking contaminant movement.

## Methods

Biochar was produced from date-palm leaves at 300°C, 550°C, and 800°C. Modification involved mixing biochar with Fe(NO3)3 and adjusting pH to 11 before washing and drying. Characterization included proximate analysis (ASTM D 1762-84-1990), Elemental Analysis (PerkinElmer EA-2400), SEM (Tescan Lyra-3), FTIR (Smart iTR NICOLET iS10), and XRD (Ultima IV). Batch sorption experiments were conducted in 50ml vessels with varying arsenic concentrations, pH levels, and stirring rates. Analysis of residual arsenic was performed via ICP-OES (Plasma Quant 9000). SIP monitoring involved a portable field/lab unit (Ontash & Ermac) using columns filled with sand and biochar-amended sand to measure conductivity and impedance.

## Data and Materials

3 types of raw biochars (BC-300, BC-550, BC-800); Fe-modified versions (Fe-BC500, Fe-BC800); 1000 μg/ml arsenic standard; 2.8m to 160μm sand particles; 50ml glass vessels; 2-3 samples of varying concentrations (1ppm and 5ppm) for SIP monitoring.

## Key Findings

- Iron impregnation of biochar significantly increased adsorption capacity from 20% to 95%. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 12]])
- Fe-modified biochar achieved 99% arsenic removal in batch sorption studies. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 13]])
- Biochar produced at 500°C and 800°C was characterized using FTIR, XRD, and SEM to determine changes in morphology and surface functional groups. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 12]])
- SIP monitoring over a 16-hour period showed changes in recorded signals consistent with arsenic removal as confirmed by chemical analysis. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 13]])
- The study identified optimal parameters for adsorption including pH, adsorbent dosage, contaminant loading, and stirring rate. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 12]])
- Biochar prepared by varying pyrolysis temperature and its ability to adsorb arsenic was investigated, showing that impregnation with Fe significantly increased adsorption capacity (20% to 95%). ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 19]])
- Arsenic is commonly found in iron-pyrites and other minerals where it can be released into groundwater through desorption under high alkaline and reducing environments. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 22]])
- Coagulation-flocculation effectively removes 90% of As loading when using alum and titanium (IV) Sulfate as the coagulant. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 25]])
- Membrane systems like nano-filtration and reverse osmosis demonstrated high (up to 85%) removal capabilities for arsenate. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 25]])
- Note: The text mentions that adsorption can achieve high removal rates, has easy operational controls, is cheap, and has minimum secondary hazardous waste materials compared to other methods. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021]])

- Raw biochar formulations showed only marginal arsenic removal (BC-500: 15%, BC-800: 22%). ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 46]])
- Iron modification significantly enhanced adsorption; both Fe-BC500 and Fe-BC800 achieved 76% arsenic removal in 2 hours. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 48]])
- The optimal pH range for arsenic sorption was determined to be between 5 and 9, with 98% removal observed at pH 5. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 50]])
- A biochar dosage of 0.2g per 50ml (4g/L) was identified as the optimal balance between cost and efficiency. ([[2021-siddiq|Siddiq, 2021, p. 51]])
