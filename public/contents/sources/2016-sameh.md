# INVESTIGATING THE POTENTIAL FOR TREATING WATER CONTAMINATED WITH TOLUENE USING ULTRAVIOLET-HYDROGEN PEROXIDE PROCESS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the effectiveness of the UV/H2O2 advanced oxidation process (AOP) for treating water contaminated with toluene, a common component in diesel-based drilling fluids. The study evaluates various parameters including UV lamp type (low pressure vs. medium pressure), H2O2 concentration, pH, and salinity to determine optimal treatment conditions.

---

## Research Problem

Groundwater sources near oil and gas exploration sites are susceptible to contamination from hydrocarbons like toluene (a component of BTEX) due to the use of diesel-based drilling fluids. Existing methods like adsorption or biological degradation have limitations, necessitating more efficient advanced oxidation processes (AOPs).

## Objectives

- Study the efficiency of the UV-H2O2 process for the removal of toluene spiked into water and determining the removal efficiency under various treatment conditions including UV lamp intensity, H2O2 concentrations, pH, Toluene concentrations, and contact time.
- Identify any by-products formed during the treatment process.
- Assess the kinetics of the treatment.
- Investigate the efficiency of UV/H2O2 process for removing toluene under various conditions (UV type, pH, H2O2 dose, concentration, salinity).
- Assess the formation and removal of toluene degradation by-products like benzaldehyde.
- Determine optimal treatment conditions for toluene removal in both distilled water and real groundwater samples.

## Methods

The study utilized a photoreactor equipped with Low Pressure (LP) 15W and Medium Pressure (MP) 150W UV lamps. Toluene was added to distilled water (DIW) and local groundwater (GW). Analytical techniques included GC-MS for quantifying toluene and benzaldehyde concentrations, and UV-Vis spectrophotometry for monitoring by-products. Groundwater characterization involved Ion Chromatography (Metrohm 850 Professional) and ICP-OES (Optima 8000). Experimental variables included pH levels (4, 7, 9), H2O2 dosages (50, 100, 200 ppm), initial toluene concentrations (5, 10, 15 ppm), and three salinity levels (5000, 2500, 1250 ppm TDS). Kinetic studies were performed using first-order models.

## Data and Materials

Samples included distilled water (DIW) with varying toluene concentrations (5, 10, 15 ppm), groundwater (GW) from KFUPM well number 9 with three salinity levels (5000, 2500, and 1250 ppm TDS). Hydrogen peroxide was used at dosages of 50, 100, and 200 ppm. Analysis included GC-MS for toluene/benzaldehyde and ICP-OES/Ion Chromatography for groundwater minerals.

## Key Findings

- The UV-H2O2 process achieved 91% removal of Toluene in 10 minutes and 97% in 15 minutes in deionized water. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 15]])
- The optimal treatment conditions for removing 10 ppm Toluene were pH 4, 100 ppm H2O2, and a 15 Watt low pressure UV lamp. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 15]])
- As salinity increased from 1250 ppm to 5000 ppm, the removal efficiency of Toluene decreased from 89% to 67% at pH 4 after 15 minutes contact time. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 15]])
- The treatment process follows first-order kinetics with a removal rate k = 0.3593. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 15]])
- Blank runs showed no loss of toluene due to stirring alone, confirming that any observed losses in other trials were due to the treatment process. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 55]])
- H2O2 alone was not effective for toluene removal; even at 100 ppm H2O2, only a 1% to 8.2% reduction in toluene was observed. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 57]])
- UV-Vis spectrophotometry showed an increase in absorbance at 262 nm (up to 300% for LP and 250% for MP) during UV treatment, indicating the formation of by-products like benzaldehyde which have stronger absorption than toluene. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 59]])
- LP UV lamps achieved a removal rate of 94.5% for toluene in 60 minutes, while MP lamps showed similar kinetics with a rate constant k = 0.0442 (R2=0.97) and k = 0.048 (R2=0.96) respectively. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 61]])
- Benzaldehyde was identified as the primary by-product of toluene degradation, showing much higher absorbance in UV-Vis spectra than toluene due to its C=O bonds. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 63]])
- Toluene removal was significantly faster and more efficient in acidic media (pH 4) compared to neutral (pH 7) or alkaline (pH 9) conditions, where H2O2 decomposes rapidly. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 80]])
- An H2O2 dose of 100 ppm was found optimal; 50 ppm provided insufficient radicals, while 200 ppm acted as a radical scavenger. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 81]])
- Higher initial toluene concentrations (e.g., 15 ppm) resulted in lower treatment rates due to competition between target pollutants and by-products. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 83]])
- In groundwater, removal efficiency decreased as salinity increased: 89% for 1250 ppm TDS, 77% for 2500 ppm, and 67% for 5000 ppm at 15 minutes. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 87]])
- The UV/H2O2 process followed first-order kinetics with a rate constant k = 0.3593 (R2=0.92) for a Toluene:H2O2 ratio of 1:10. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 92]])
- The estimated operating cost for treating 1 m3 of contaminated groundwater was 19.2 SR/m3, while distilled water was 3.1 SR/m3. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 98]])

## Limitations

The study was conducted on a bench-scale level; results may vary at pilot or full-scale operations. The photoreactor had limitations in sampling frequency (minimum 1-minute intervals).

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Test the UV/H2O2 process under pilot scale conditions.
- Conduct detailed analyses for other by-products and their removal.
- Investigate other AOPs such as those based on ozone (UV/O3, H2O2/O3, and UV/H2O2/O3).
- Further investigate the effect of salinity on toluene removal by various AOPs.
- Perform cost estimation for AOPs at pilot scale.
