# INVESTIGATING THE POTENTIAL FOR TREATING WATER CONTAMINATED WITH TOLUENE USING ULTRAVIOLET-HYDROGEN PEROXIDE PROCESS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the efficacy of the UV-H2O2 advanced oxidation process (AOP) for removing toluene from water contaminated by diesel-based drilling fluids. It evaluates various treatment parameters including UV intensity, H2O2 concentration, pH, and contact time to determine removal efficiency and identify degradation by-products.

---

## Research Problem

Groundwater resources are susceptible to contamination from petroleum hydrocarbons like toluene due to the use of diesel-based drilling fluids in oil and gas exploration. Existing methods for removing these contaminants need to be evaluated for effectiveness and economic viability, specifically focusing on the UV-H2O2 process as a potential solution.

## Objectives

- Demonstrate the efficiency of UV/H2O2 as an advanced oxidation process (AOP) for remediating water contaminated with diesel-based fluids.
- Identify optimal removal conditions for toluene under various bench-scale experimental conditions including UV lamp type, H2O2 concentration, pH, water salinity, and contact time.
- Study the efficiency of the UV-H2O2 process for the removal of toluene spiked into water
- Determine the removal efficiency of toluene under various treatment conditions (UV lamps intensity, H2O2 concentrations, pH, Toluene concentrations, and contact time)
- Identify any by-products formed during the treatment
- Assess the kinetics of the treatment
- Evaluate the effectiveness of UV-H2O2 process for removing toluene from water
- Determine the impact of different H2O2 concentrations on toluene degradation
- Compare the performance of low pressure (LP) and medium pressure (MP) UV lamps in the treatment process
- Identify and quantify by-products such as benzaldehyde formed during the treatment
- Investigate the efficiency of UV/H2O2 process to remove toluene under various conditions (UV type, intensity, pH, H2O2 dose, initial concentration, and salinity).
- Assess the formation and removal of toluene degradation by-products (e.g., benzaldehyde).
- Evaluate the impact of groundwater salinity on treatment efficiency.
- Perform kinetic studies to determine the rate constants for toluene degradation.
- Estimate the electrical energy per order (EE/O) and operational costs for the treatment.
- Investigate the potential of UV/H2O2 process for treating water contaminated with toluene
- Evaluate the degradation kinetics of toluene in an aqueous solution using advanced oxidation processes

## Methods

The study utilized a bench-scale photoreactor with forced liquid circulation to treat toluene in water. Two types of UV lamps were used: low pressure (LP) at 15W and medium pressure (MP) at 150W. Analysis of toluene and its by-products was conducted using an Agilent GC-MS system (model 5890N) with a J&W GC DB5 capillary column following EPA 8260A methods. A UV-VIS spectrophotometer (Analytik Jena SPECORD® 50) was used to monitor toluene and by-products during treatment. Stock solutions were prepared using deionized water, and various parameters like pH and concentration were varied in a controlled experimental setup.

## Data and Materials

Chemicals included Toluene (99.95% purity), Hydrogen peroxide (30% w/w), Acetic acid (99.7%), Na2CO3 (≥ 99.5%), Sodium thiosulfate (≥ 98%), and Benzaldehyde (> 99%). The system used a 500 mL reactor vessel with two types of UV lamps (15W LP and 150W MP). GC-MS analysis was performed on 10 mL samples extracted from 22 mL of experimental sample.

## Key Findings

- The UV-H2O2 process achieved 91% removal of Toluene in 10 minutes and 97% in 15 minutes in deionized water. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 15]])
- Optimal treatment conditions for removing 10 ppm Toluene were identified as pH 4, 100 ppm H2O2, and a 15 Watt low pressure (LP) UV lamp. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 15]])
- As salinity increased from 1250 ppm to 5000 ppm, the removal efficiency of Toluene decreased from 89% to 67% at pH 4 after 15 minutes contact time. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 17]])
- The treatment process follows first-order kinetics with a removal rate k = 0.3593. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 17]])
- Toluene solubility in water is 0.561 g/L at 25°C. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 20]])
- Hydroxyl radicals (OH•) have a high reduction potential (E0 = 2.8 V), making them highly effective for the oxidation of organic pollutants like toluene. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 23]])
- The rate constant of OH•-based reactions are significantly higher than those using O3 for various compounds including aromatics and phenols. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 24]])
- UV-H2O2 was able to mineralize 71% of organic content in some studies, while UV photolysis alone achieved only 28%. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 29]])
- The medium pressure (MP) lamp provides a broader spectrum (200-300 nm) and higher intensity compared to the low pressure (LP) lamp, which is primarily at 254 nm. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 38]])
- The LP lamp produces radiation mainly at 254 nm with an intensity of 15 Watts, while the MP lamp delivers a broader spectrum with an intensity of 150 Watts. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 39]])
- Toluene removal by O3-H2O2 reached 68%, 76%, and 96% at 5, 20, and 60 minutes respectively. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 32]])
- The study of UV-H2O2 is necessary because other methods like O3-PCO or TiO2/UV were conducted in gaseous media rather than aqueous ones. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 33]])
- Stirring alone resulted in no significant loss of toluene (maximum observed decrease was 8.7% due to volatilization). ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 55]])
- H2O2 alone at concentrations of 20, 50, and 100 ppm did not effectively remove toluene, with maximum removal being only 8.2%. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 58]])
- UV irradiation (LP) resulted in 94.5% removal of toluene after 60 minutes, but showed a significant increase in absorbance at 262 nm due to the formation of benzaldehyde. ([[2016-sameh|Sameh, 2016, p. 61]])

## Limitations

The study notes that the photoreactor system has limitations on collecting samples at intervals less than 1 minute, which affected the granularity of kinetic data for very rapid reactions.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Test the UV/H2O2 process under pilot-scale conditions as it is currently only proven at bench-scale.
- Conduct detailed analyses for by-products generated and their removal.
- Investigate other AOPs such as those based on ozone (UV/O3, H2O2/O3, and UV/H2O2/O3).
- Further investigate the effect of salinity on toluene removal by various AOPs.
- Perform further investigation into cost estimations for AOPs at pilot scale.
