# Experimental Investigation of Various Physical Factors Affecting the Stability of Water-in-Oil Emulsions in Oil Production

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the stability of oil-water emulsions produced from two different wells (Well-B and Well-A) under various temperatures, chemical concentrations, and flow conditions. It concludes that high asphaltene content and lower temperatures are primary drivers of emulsion stability, and identifies specific demulsifiers to optimize field operations.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the challenge of stable oil-in-water emulsions in petroleum production, which can hinder flow and processing. Specifically, it investigates how physical factors such as asphaltenes, solids, and dynamic conditions (simulating field operations) affect emulsion stability and demulsification efficiency.

## Objectives

- Investigate the effect of temperature on the stability of field-produced emulsions.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of four commercially available demulsifying chemicals under dynamic conditions.
- Determine the optimal concentration of the best demulsifying chemicals for breaking tight emulsions.
- Simulate various aspects of emulsion flow in the field using a laboratory system.
- Compare the effectiveness of various commercially available demulsifiers (Champion, Atros, Petrolite, Exxon) under dynamic conditions.
- Assess the impact of asphaltene content and other chemical components on emulsion formation.

## Methods

The study utilized a laboratory system designed to simulate field conditions (temperature, pressure, and electrostatic fields). Experimental procedures included: 1) Testing five temperatures (150, 130, 110, 90, and 70 °F); 2) Evaluating four demulsifiers (Champion, Atros, Petrolite, and a mixture); 3) Comparing static tests with dynamic flow tests; 4) Performing SARA analysis on oil samples from Well-A and Well-B; 5) Conducting X-Ray Diffraction (XRD) analysis of solids separated from emulsion samples.

## Data and Materials

Samples were collected from two specific wells: Well-A (non-problem well) and Well-B (problem well). The study tested four types of demulsifiers and five different temperature points. SARA analysis was performed on both produced fluids.

## Key Findings

- At reservoir temperatures of approximately 220 °F, emulsions are found to be unstable and are mostly formed after entering the wellbore during flow into GOSPs. ([[1995-al-jaziri|Al-Jaziri, 1995, p. 14]])
- Temperature plays a critical role; heating these emulsions helps in breaking them into two phases (oil and water). ([[1995-al-jaziri|Al-Jaziri, 1995, p. 14]])
- The addition of demulsifiers reduces the emulsification tendency of produced oil extensively. ([[1995-al-jaziri|Al-Jaziri, 1995, p. 14]])
- Champion demulsifier was identified as the most effective among four commercially available options for resolving tight emulsions. ([[1995-al-jaziri|Al-Jaziri, 1995, p. 14]])
- The concentration of Champion demulsifier required to resolve tight emulsions from problem wells is over 10 times greater than that required for non-problem wells. ([[1995-al-jaziri|Al-Jaziri, 1995, p. 14]])
- Demulsification under dynamic conditions requires three to four times less demulsifier than under static conditions. ([[1995-al-jaziri|Al-Jaziri, 1995, p. 15]])
- The viscosity of the oil phase was identified as the most important oil-related parameter controlling demulsification rates in the presence of an electrostatic field. ([[1995-al-jaziri|Al-Jaziri, 1995, p. 24]])
- At pulsation frequencies between 8 Hz and 11 Hz, the separation efficiency of emulsions was highest. ([[1995-al-jaziri|Al-Jaziri, 1995, p. 24]])
- At 220°F, field emulsions from Well-B were completely broken down into oil and water without the addition of any demulsifying chemical. ([[1995-al-jaziri|Al-Jaziri, 1995, p. 92]])
- Emulsion stability was significantly higher at lower temperatures; for example, at 70°F, 85% of the fluid remained in emulsion form compared to only 39% at 190°F. ([[1995-al-jaziri|Al-Jaziri, 1995, p. 63]])
- The bottom layer (over 90% of produced fluid) from Well-B was a high viscosity (>300 cp) water-in-oil emulsion with a water content of 50-65%. ([[1995-al-jaziri|Al-Jaziri, 1995, p. 92]])
- Asphaltene content in the oil separated by gravity (Well-B) was 4.3%, while higher asphaltene concentrations were found in samples requiring more demulsifier or centrifugation. ([[1995-al-jaziri|Al-Jaziri, 1995, p. 85]])
- Champion was identified as the most effective demulsifier, requiring only 6 ppm at 190°F to resolve emulsions. ([[1995-al-jaziri|Al-Jaziri, 1995, p. 71]])
- At temperatures of 150°F and above, Petrolite outperformed Atros in resolving field emulsions. ([[1995-al-jaziri|Al-Jaziri, 1995, p. 71]])
- Dynamic flow conditions with an electrostatic field significantly improved demulsification compared to static bottle tests. ([[1995-al-jaziri|Al-Jaziri, 1995, p. 87]])

## Limitations

The study notes that while some solids like calcite and graphite were present, their quantities were too small to significantly impact emulsion stability.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Demulsifying chemicals should be added at the well-head to ensure adequate mixing and prevent further emulsification as temperatures drop during transport to the GOSP.
