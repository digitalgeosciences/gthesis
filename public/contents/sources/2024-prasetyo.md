# Understanding the Hydrological Dynamics of the Soil-Plant Interface Under Different Irrigation Scenarios in Arid Region - A Scaled Experiment

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the hydrological dynamics of the soil-plant interface in an arid region by mapping lemon tree root zones using GPR and ERT. It utilizes HYDRUS-1D to simulate soil water balance and evaluate different irrigation scenarios, concluding that a 10-day irrigation interval (I10) optimizes transpiration while minimizing evaporation.

---

## Research Problem

The difficulty of non-invasively monitoring root growth and dynamics in arid environments where traditional methods are invasive or impractical. Specifically, the study addresses how to optimize irrigation for lemon trees under Saudi Arabia's arid conditions while ensuring water efficiency.

## Objectives

- Develop a non-invasive investigation protocol that can capture the root activity and distribution of active roots.
- Analyze the pattern of active root water uptake zone by using time-lapse ERT.
- Assess and confirm the vertical and lateral fine root distribution using GPR.
- Identify the diurnal pattern and magnitude of RWU under different seasonal variations using combined HYDRUS-1D and ERT.
- Determine the most suitable irrigation scenario for a lemon plantation supporting the Saudi Green Initiative program.
- Detect and map fine plant roots in lemon trees using GPR and ERT under controlled conditions.

## Methods

The study employed a dual geophysical approach: 400 MHz GPR to identify fine root concentrations and 3D/4D Electrical Resistivity Tomography (ERT) to monitor soil moisture and root zone changes over seven months. Soil hydraulic parameters were estimated using the ROSETTA neural network and calibrated via ERT data in HY1DRUS-1D. Irrigation scenarios (I1, I2, I3, I4, I5, I7, I10, I15) were modeled using CROPWAT 8.0 to determine optimal irrigation intervals based on the Ta/Tp ratio.

## Data and Materials

A controlled experiment involving a lemon tree in an arid environment; 71 GPR profiles; 15,609 ERT data points for 3D inversion; and 4D time-lapse ERT data from March to September 2023.

## Key Findings

- GPR successfully identified root concentrations in the topsoil, with a sense of concentration increasing toward the surface (0-31.5 cm). ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 63]])
- ERT captured root activity variations over seven months, highlighting its potential for precision agriculture. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 14]])
- HYDRUS-1D and ERT revealed peak root water uptake (RWU) in the morning and deeper RWU with rising temperatures. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 14]])
- Irrigation frequency every 10 days (I10) was found to be the most optimum scenario, yielding the highest ratio of actual crop transpiration to potential transpiration (Ta/Tp). ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 14]])
- Less frequent irrigation with larger doses minimized evaporation and maximized transpiration. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 14]])
- The target area for monitoring the root zone was limited to the upper 50cm, with the highest density of the root zone expected at a depth of 30-40 cm. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 33]])
- ERT 3D inversion showed a high resistivity zone (root zone) at depths of 28-34 cm and 42-51 cm, with an RMS error of 5.58-10.20%. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 54]])
- The root zone was confirmed by ground-truthing to extend from 0 cm to 40 cm depth with a radius of approximately 20 cm. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 60]])
- Soil moisture content (SMC) was successfully derived from ERT resistivity values, showing a parabolic distribution peaking at 38 cm depth. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 70]])
- The morning period (08:00–11:00) was identified as the optimal irrigation timing due to the highest Root Water Uptake (RWU) rates. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 74]])
- Soil evaporation was the primary cause of water loss in all scenarios, while deep percolation remained relatively low. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 84]])
- The I10 irrigation scenario (every 10 days) provided the highest Ta/Tp ratio, making it the most efficient for maximizing transpiration. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 85]])

## Limitations

High temperatures in summer (July-September) caused significant distortion in ERT tomographic images due to soil drying. The 4D inversion had higher RMS errors than 3D models because of the additional time parameter.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study suggests using a combination of GPR and ERT for real-time monitoring; implementing organic or plastic-film mulching to reduce evaporation; and adopting the I10 irrigation interval to support the Saudi Green Initiative.
