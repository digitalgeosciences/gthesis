# Understanding the Hydrological Dynamics of the Soil-Plant Interface Under Different Irrigation Scenarios in Arid Region - A Scaled Experiment

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the hydrological dynamics of the soil-plant interface in an arid region by mapping fine root systems and modeling water uptake. It utilizes GPR and ERT to characterize the root zone and HYDRUS-1D to simulate irrigation scenarios, concluding that different irrigation intervals significantly impact transpiration, evaporation, and water stress.

---

## Research Problem

The difficulty of studying plant roots due to their inaccessibility makes it challenging to understand how they interact with soil moisture and nutrients, especially in arid environments where irrigation management is critical.

## Methods

The study used a scaled experimental tank to simulate an arid environment. ERT data were acquired using a dipole-dipole (DD) array and processed using Res3dinvx64 software with both 3D and 4D (time-lapse) inversion techniques. GPR was conducted using the SIR-4000 system with a 400 MHz antenna to map root zones in dry and wet soil conditions, followed by processing in Reflexw and Voxler. Hydrological modeling of soil water balance and root water uptake was performed using HYDRUS-1D, with parameters like saturated water content and hydraulic conductivity estimated via ROSSETA. Irrigation requirements were calculated using CROPWAT 8.0.

## Data and Materials

The study involved a one-year-old lemon tree with a root system concentrated in the 0-40 cm depth range. Data were collected over several months (April-September 2023) to capture seasonal variations and impacts of different irrigation scenarios (e.g., I1, I10, I15).

## Key Findings

- GPR successfully identified root concentrations, particularly in the topsoil. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 14]])
- ERT captured root activity variations over seven months, highlighting its potential for precision agriculture. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 14]])
- The study revealed peak Root Water Uptake (RWU) in the morning and deeper RWU with rising temperatures. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 14]])
- Irrigation frequency every 10 days (I10) was found to be the most optimum scenario, yielding the highest ratio of actual crop transpiration to potential transpiration (Ta/Tp). ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 14]])
- Less frequent irrigation with larger doses minimized evaporation and maximized transpiration. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 14]])
- CLIMWAT underestimated the actual Eto, meaning scheduled irrigation based solely on it might not fulfill crop water requirements. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 12]])
- ERT is sensitive to changes in soil electrical resistivity attributed to factors like soil moisture, making it a proxy for identifying active root zones. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 25]])
- GPR can identify and locate root density rapidly at large spatial scales and distinguish between live (well-defined hyperbola) and dead roots. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 28]])
- The target area for monitoring the root zone in lemon trees is limited to the upper 50cm, with the highest density expected at 30-40 cm depth. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 33]])
- TDS of domestic water used for irrigation was measured between 500-700 ppm. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 33]])
- The study utilized a custom-made multiplexer to combine two sets of 96-electrode data from groups A and B boreholes into one 192-electrode system for higher resolution. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 36]])
- The 3D inversion of ERT data showed a resistive zone below 3 cm depth, extending to 50 cm, corresponding to the expected spatial distribution of the root zone. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 42]])
- The 4D inversion (time-lapse) successfully captured temporal variations in resistivity with an RMS error of 15.5% after 5 iterations. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 55]])
- Resistivity changes in the root zone were observed to vary between -180% and 180% from T2 to T7. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 42]])
- GPR data acquisition with a 400 MHz antenna provided sufficient resolution for identifying root zones in both dry and wet soil conditions. ([[2024-prasetyo|Prasetyo, 2024, p. 31]])

## Limitations

The study used a scaled experimental tank, which may have limitations in replicating full-scale field conditions. The 4D inversion had higher RMS errors (15.5%) compared to 3D models due to the inclusion of the time parameter and removal of data points to ensure consistency across time steps.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future work should include using sensors to provide real-time water saturation data and integrating different water qualities to evaluate their impact on the root zone and tree health.
