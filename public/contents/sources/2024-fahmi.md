# QUANTIFYING THE DYNAMICS OF MIXED SILICICLASTIC AND CARBONATE SYSTEM OF THE TOARCIAN MARRAT FORMATION, SAUDI ARABIA: INSIGHTS FROM 3D STRATIGRAPHIC FORWARD MODELING

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the interplay between clastic influx and carbonate factories in the Toarcian Marrat Formation, a mixed siliciclastic and carbonate system. Using 3D stratigraphic forward modeling (SFM) with DionisosFlow software, the study quantifies how sediment supply, carbonate production, and sea-level changes influence sedimentary architecture.

---

## Research Problem

Understanding and quantifying the controlling processes behind the development of mixed carbonate-siliciclastic systems (specifically the Marrat Formation) in a transgressive-regressive sequence is difficult due to complex interactions between clastic influx and carbonate factories.

## Objectives

- To understand the interplay between siliciclastic and carbonate sedimentation in Marrat Formation.
- To test multiple scenarios that best-fit with the deposition in Marrat Formation with stratigraphic forward modelling.
- To understand the impact of different input parameters governing the sedimentation using sensitivity analysis with stratigraphic forward modelling.
- To build sequence architecture (lateral and vertical lithology/facies variations) that captured the distribution of reservoir/source rock potential with stratigraphic forward modelling.
- Quantify the dynamics of the mixed siliciclastic and carbonate system of the Toarcian Marrat Formation.
- Evaluate the impact of various parameters (sediment supply, carbonate production, subsidence, sea level, wave energy) on sequence architecture using sensitivity analysis.

## Methods

The study employs 3D Stratigraphic Forward Modeling (SFM) using the Cougar Flow software. The methodology includes: 1) Data preparation from outcrop and well logs; 2) Defining a range of uncertain parameters (sediment supply, carbonate production, subsidence, sea level, wave energy); 3) Running multiple simulations using Latin Hypercube Sampling to create a Response Surface Model (RSM); 4) Performing Global Sensitivity Analysis to identify the most influential factors on thickness and lithology; 5) Validating the model against actual outcrop and well data from the Marrat Formation.

## Data and Materials

12 outcrop measured sections (including Ushaiger, Kumayt Qarayan, Khashm adh Dhibi, Wadi Al Jufayr, Faridat Balum, Khashm Al Khalta, Khashm Disman, Wadi Birk, Fara'id al Ahmar, and Khashm Munayyifih), 2 well logs (Riyadh well and Khurais well), and biostratigraphic data for the Marrat Formation.

## Key Findings

- The best-fit reference model was identified as a double sediment supply source scenario, which successfully filled the basin and showed good agreement with outcrop and subsurface observations. ([[2024-fahmi|Fahmi, 2024, p. 40]])
- Sensitivity analysis indicates that sediment supply and carbonate production exert greater control on sedimentary architecture than sea level changes. ([[2024-fahmi|Fahmi, 2024, p. 17]])
- The Marrat Formation is characterized by a complex lithological composition including interbedded sandstones, mudstones (claystone), limestone, and anhydrites. ([[2024-fahmi|Fahmi, 2024, p. 45]])
- Lower Marrat member thickness can reach up to 47 meters and is characterized by low abundance of faunal assemblages. ([[2024-fahmi|Fahmi, 2024, p. 45]])
- Middle Marrat member has a thickness of up to 40 meters and includes a significant reduction in thickness towards the southern areas where it is replaced by sandstone-rich deposits. ([[2024-fahmi|Fahmi, 2024, p. 48]])
- The upper part of the Marrat Formation is a bioturbated limestone deposited under a lagoonal environment, overlain by a 7 meters thick anhydrite layer. ([[2024-fahmi|Fahmi, 2024, p. 48]])
- The model successfully captured aggradation-progradation geometries within the Marrat Formation in the third stratigraphic order. ([[2024-fahmi|Fahmi, 2024, p. 40]])
- Quantification of sediment supply allows for more precise spatial delineation of siliciclastic and carbonate distributions, particularly capturing along-strike variability. ([[2024-fahmi|Fahmi, 2024, p. 17]])
- The study identified sediment supply and carbonate production as the most influential parameters in determining the sequence architecture of the Marrat Formation. ([[2024-fahmi|Fahmi, 2024, p. 103]])
- Specifically, mud carbonate production was the primary driver for the Upper Marrat member (179.3 Ma - 180.4 Ma). ([[2024-fahmi|Fahmi, 2024, p. 104]])
- In the Middle and Lower Marrat members, sediment supply (particularly from southern and western sources) was the most influential parameter. ([[2024-fahmi|Fahmi, 2024, p. 104]])
- The model achieved up to 95% accuracy in thickness prediction and 90% accuracy in lithology prediction compared to actual data. ([[2024-fahmi|Fahmi, 2024, p. 79]])
- Lower Marrat member (183 Ma - 181.5 Ma) showed an aggradation-progradation sequence with a thickness of approximately 55 meters. ([[2024-fahmi|Fahmi, 2024, p. 68]])
- Middle Marrat member (181.5 Ma - 180.1 Ma) exhibited progradation-retrogradation sequences due to high sediment supply from both southern and western sources. ([[2024-fahmi|Fahmi, 2024, p. 70]])
- Upper Marrat member (180.1 Ma - 179.3 Ma) showed carbonate aggradation-progradation with a thickness of approximately 40-50 meters in the middle section. ([[2024-fahmi|Fahmi, 2024, p. 72]])

## Limitations

The study faces uncertainties due to a lack of tectonic subsidence studies, uncertainty in global/regional eustasy, variable ecological constraints on carbonate production, and the absence of distributed well data and seismic data.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future improvements should include higher resolution sequence stratigraphy (4th-5th order), biostratigraphy for better paleobathymetry, cyclostratigraphy for sea-level proxies, and integration of seismic data for validation.
