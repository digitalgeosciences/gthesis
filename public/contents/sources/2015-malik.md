# INVESTIGATION OF VARIOUS BACTERIAL ISOLATES IN DEGRADATION OF SPECIFIC HYDROCARBONS

**Summary**: The thesis investigated the biodegradation of three specific polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs)—naphthalene, phenanthrene, and pyrene—by seven bacterial strains and three consortia. The study evaluated these processes under varying temperature ranges (10°C to 37°C) and pH levels (5.0 to 9.0), concluding that specific isolates like Proteus mirabilis and consortium C7 showed high degradation efficiency.

---

## Research Problem

The accumulation of persistent, toxic, and potentially carcinogenic polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) in the environment requires effective remediation techniques; this study addresses the need for identifying efficient bacterial strains and consortia for bioremediation under varying environmental conditions.

## Objectives

- Evaluate bacterial isolates potential to mineralize specific PAHs
- Assess bacterial consortium PAHs degrading efficiency
- Identify optimum temperature and pH necessary to degrade selected PAHs
- To evaluate the biodegradation of naphthalene, phenanthrene, and pyrene by seven individual bacterial strains.
- To assess the impact of temperature variations (10°C, 25°C, and 37°C) on the degradation efficiency of these hydrocarbons.
- To investigate the effect of pH levels (5.0, 7.0, and 9.0) on the biodegradation process.

## Methods

The study utilized seven bacterial strains (Proteus mirabilis, Burkholderia fungorum, Caulobacter sp, Ralstonia sp, Paenibacillus borealis, Brevibacillus brevis, and Rhodococcus qinshengi) and three consortia (C2, C4, and C7). Biodegradation experiments were conducted in 18-day incubation periods at temperatures of 10°C, 25°C, and 37°C, with pH levels adjusted to 5.0, 7.0, and 9.0. Samples were prepared using Bushnell Haas (BH) mineral medium. Extraction of residual PAHs was performed using Solid Phase Micro Extraction (SPME) with polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) coated fibers. Analysis of the extracted compounds was conducted via Gas Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry (GC/MS). Morphological characterization of the bacterial strains was performed using Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM).

## Data and Materials

The study involved 3 specific types of PAHs (naphthalene, phenanthrene, pyrene), 7 bacterial isolates (including Caulobacter sp, P. mirabilis, B. brevis, P. borealis, B. fungorum, R. quinshengi, and Ralstonia sp), and 3 microbial consortia (C2, C4, C7). Initial concentrations were maintained at 100 ppm for all PAH types.

## Key Findings

- Proteus mirabilis successfully metabolized 95% of phenanthrene under optimal conditions. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 15]])
- Brevibacillus brevis degraded approximately 96.5% of phenanthrene at 37°C. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 15]])
- Consortium C7 metabolized 78% of pyrene, while C4 and C2 biodegraded 64% and 49% respectively. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 67]])
- Naphthalene was completely mineralized in consortium C7, C4, and by bacterial isolates Burkholderia fungorum and Rhodococcus qinshengi at 37°C. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 15]])
- In neutral pH (7.0) medium, Rhodococcus qinshengi and Burkholderia fungorum degraded over 90% phenanthrene. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 15]])
- Brevibacillus brevis and Paenibacillus borealis both mineralized not less than 96% phenanthrene at pH 7.0. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 15]])
- In acidic medium, 75% pyrene biodegradation occurred in flasks inoculated with consortium C7. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 15]])
- Burkholderia fungorum and Rhodococcus qinshengi exhibited consistent metabolism across varying media pH, degrading 59-61% pyrene at pH 5.0. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 15]])
- At 37°C, Caulobacter sp resulted in approximately 91% mineralization of phenanthrene (8.93 ppm residual). ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 51]])
- P. mirabilis showed 94.8% biodegradation of phenanthrene at 37°C, with concentration reducing to 5.23 ppm. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 51]])
- At 10°C, the amount of phenanthrene biodegraded failed to exceed 60% for all strains investigated. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 52]])
- Consortium C7 showed total disappearance (ND) of phenanthrene at 37°C. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 63]])
- At mesothermic temperature (37°C) and neutral pH (7.0), the consortium C7 achieved complete degradation of phenanthrene. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 83]])
- Under room temperature (25°C), the bacterial isolate P. mirabilis degraded 94.6% of naphthalene, leaving a residual amount of 5.43 ppm. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 70]])
- At low temperatures (10°C), biodegradation for all strains did not exceed 60%, with B. fungorum and R. quinshengi showing the highest degradation at approximately 59% and 57% respectively. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 94]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Conduct research on pH and temperatures outside the current range (5.0–9.0 and 10°C–37°C) to study degradation in extreme environments.
- Investigate whether bacterial isolates produce toxic intermediate metabolites during PAH degradation.
- Study the impact of salinity on the biodegradation of PAHs by these bacterial isolates.
- Trial different combinations of strains to determine the best consortium for specific bioremediation needs.
- Perform field (in situ) trials to assess efficiency in natural environments.
