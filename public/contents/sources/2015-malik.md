# INVESTIGATION OF VARIOUS BACTERIAL ISOLATES IN DEGRADATION OF SPECIFIC HYDROCARBONS

**Summary**: The thesis investigated the biodegradation of specific polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs)—naphthalene, phenanthrene, and pyrene—by seven bacterial isolates and three microbial consortia under varying pH levels (5.0–9.0) and temperatures (10°C–37°C). The study concluded that while certain strains like P. mirabilis and R. qinshengi showed high degradation rates for phenanthrene, the use of consortia generally provided more consistent results across different environmental conditions.

---

## Research Problem

The persistence of polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs), such as phenanthrene, pyrene, and naphthalene, in the environment poses significant risks due to their low solubility and high toxicity. There is a need to identify specific bacterial isolates and consortia that can effectively degrade these compounds under varying environmental conditions like fluctuating pH and temperature.

## Objectives

- To study the biodegradation of naphthalene, phenanthrene, and pyrene using 7 bacterial strains.
- To evaluate the effect of temperature (10°C–37°C) on the degradation of these PAHs by isolates and a consortium.
- To evaluate the effect of pH (5.0–9.0) on the biodegradation of these PAHs.
- To identify potential bacterial candidates for bioremediation of contaminated environments.
- Evaluate bacterial isolates potential to mineralize specific PAHs
- Assess bacterial consortium PAHs degrading efficiency
- Identify optimum temperature and pH necessary to degrade selected PAHs
- To evaluate the degradation potential of seven specific bacterial isolates on three types of PAHs.
- To assess the impact of temperature variations (10°C, 25°C, 37°C) on the biodegradation of naphthalene, phenanthrene, and pyrene.
- To investigate the effectiveness of bacterial consortia (C2, C4, and C7) compared to individual strains in degrading PAHs.
- To determine the influence of pH levels (5.0, 7.0, 9.0) on the biodegradation efficiency of the selected bacterial isolates.
- To evaluate the biodegradation of phenanthrene by various bacterial isolates and consortia at different pH levels.
- To assess the degradation of pyrene by selected bacterial strains and consortia across a range of pH values.
- To investigate the biodegradation of naphthalene by specific bacterial isolates and consortia.
- To determine the effect of temperature on the biodegradation efficiency of both individual isolates and microbial consortia.
- To evaluate the biodegradation of naphthalene, phenanthrene, and pyrene by seven different bacterial strains.
- To assess the impact of pH levels (5.0, 7.0, and 9.0) on the degradation rates of these PAHs.
- To investigate the effect of temperature (10°C, 25°C, and 37°C) on the biodegradation process.
- To compare the performance of individual bacterial isolates against microbial consortia in degrading specific hydrocarbons.
- To isolate and characterize various bacterial strains from contaminated environments.
- To study the biodegradation of naphthalene, phenanthrene, and pyrene by different bacterial isolates.
- To evaluate the effect of temperature and pH on the degradation rates of these hydrocarbons.
- To evaluate the effect of temperature (10°C - 37°C) and pH (5.0 - 9.0) on the degradation process.

## Methods

The study utilized seven bacterial strains (Proteus mirabilis, Burkholderia fungorum, Caulobacter sp., Ralstonia sp., Paenibacillus borealis, Brevibacillus brevis, and Rhodococcus qingshengii) identified via 16S rRNA sequencing. Samples were prepared using Bushnell Haas Minerals Medium (BH). Biodegradation experiments were conducted over 18 days in Erlenmeyer flasks with initial PAH concentrations of 100 ppm. Temperature was varied between 10°C, 25°C, and 37°C, while pH was adjusted to 5, 7, and 9. Extraction of residual PAHs and metabolites was performed using Solid Phase Micro Extraction (SPME) with a polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) coating. Analysis of the extracted compounds was conducted via Gas Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry (GC-MS) using an Agilent Technologies system. Morphological characterization of the bacterial strains was performed using Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM).

## Data and Materials

The study utilized 7 bacterial strains and 3 microbial consortia (C2: P. mirabilis + P. borealis; C4: Caulobacter sp + B. fungorum + P. mirabilis + P. borealis; C7: a mixture of all 7 strains). Initial concentrations for phenanthrene, pyrene, and naphthalene were maintained at 100 ppm.

## Key Findings

- Proteus mirabilis metabolized 95% of phenanthrene during the 18-day incubation period. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 15]])
- Brevibacillus brevis degraded approximately 96.5% of phenanthrene at a temperature of 37°C. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 15]])
- Bacterial consortium C7 co-metabolized 78% of the target hydrocarbons. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 15]])
- Consortia C4 and C2 biodegraded over 50% of pyrene. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 15]])
- Burkholderia fungorum and Rhodococcus qinshengi utilized nearly 56% and 58% of pyrene, respectively, at mesothermic temperatures (37°C). ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 15]])
- Naphthalene was completely mineralized in consortium C7, C4, and by bacterial isolates Burkholderia fungorum and Rhodococcus qinshengi at 37°C. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 15]])
- In neutral pH (7.0) medium, Rhodococcus qinshengi and Burkholderia fungorum degraded over 90% of phenanthrene. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 16]])
- Brevibacillus brevis and Paenibacillus borealis both mineralized not less than 96% phenanthrene at pH 7.0. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 16]])
- In acidic medium (pH 5.0), consortium C7 showed 75% pyrene biodegradation, while consortium C4 mineralized not less than 70%. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 16]])
- Burkholderia fungorum and Rhodococcus qinshengi exhibited consistent metabolism across varying pH levels, degrading 59-61% of pyrene at pH 5.0. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 16]])
- Naphthalene has a solubility in water (Sw) of 31 gm-3 and a LogKow value of 3.37; Anthracene has Sw of 0.045 gm-3 and LogKow of 4.57; Phenanthrene has Sw of 1.1 gm-3 and LogKow of 4.54; Pyrene has Sw of 0.132 gm-3 and LogKow of 5.18. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 20]])
- Biodegradation of bunker fuel showed that 41–85% of benzene-soluble fraction diminished within 7 days at 15 °C, while 21–52% bio-mineralization was observed after 14 days at 5 °C. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 23]])
- A C:N ratio greater than 25:1 is optimal for microbial growth and ultimate degradation of creosote. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 24]])
- Phenanthrene removal was 40% at pH 5.5 but exceeded 80% at pH 7.5 in a 16-day incubation period. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 25]])
- Brevibacillus laterosporus can degrade approximately 90% of the total n-alkanes in Arabian light crude and Al-Shaheen crude within 1 week at 30 °C. ([[2015-malik|Malik, 2015, p. 33]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Further research capturing pH and temperatures outside the ranges of 5.0–9.0 and 10°C–37°C to evaluate potential in extreme environments.
- Investigation into whether bacterial isolates produce toxic intermediate metabolites during PAH degradation.
- Study of the impact of salinity on the biodegradation capabilities of the bacterial isolates.
- Trialing different combinations of strains under various conditions to determine the best consortium for bioremediation.
