# IN-SITU GENERATED NITROGEN GAS BY THERMOCHEMICAL REACTION FOR ACID DIVERSION

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the use of in-situ generated nitrogen gas via thermochemical reactions (ammonium chloride and sodium nitrate) to divert acid in heterogeneous carbonate formations. It combines a MATLAB-based analytical model with laboratory core-flood experiments to evaluate how this method can overcome the hurdles of traditional acid diversion techniques.

---

## Research Problem

Ensuring effective acid treatment in multi-layered carbonate reservoirs where high-permeability zones tend to consume the majority of the acid, leaving lower-permeability zones untreated. The study specifically addresses how thermochemical reactions can create a pressure and viscosity contrast to divert acid into tighter zones.

## Objectives

- Develop an analytical solution and application for acid diversion in heterogeneous carbonate formations using thermochemical reaction-generated gas.
- Conduct laboratory experiments on carbonate cores to analyze the diversion effect of thermochemical reactions.
- Perform a sensitivity analysis of injected acid volumes and thermochemical reaction products.
- Develop a mathematical model to simulate acid diversion using in-situ generated gas from thermochemical reactions.
- Evaluate the impact of various parameters (pressure difference, porosity, permeability ratio) on diversion performance in both gas and liquid formations.
- Conduct laboratory experiments using core-flooding to validate the effectiveness of thermochemical fluids for acid diversion.

## Methods

The study utilizes a MATLAB-based analytical model incorporating equations for viscous skin factor, pressure drop, and volume calculation. Laboratory experiments were conducted using two carbonate core samples with different permeability values (94 mD and 27 mD; 100 mD and 10 mD) in both single and parallel-coreflood setups. Core analysis included monitoring wormhole growth via CT scan and tracking acid volumes to reach breakthrough. A thermochemical reaction setup was used to study the kinetics of nitrogen gas generation from ammonium chloride and sodium nitrate.

## Data and Materials

The research utilized a mathematical model for simulation and two sets of core samples for laboratory experiments. Core samples were limestone, some saturated with fresh water or brine, with permeability values ranging from 10 mD to 100 mD. The acid used was 15 wt% HCl.

## Key Findings

- Thermochemical reactions involving ammonium chloride and sodium nitrate generate nitrogen gas and heat, which can reach temperatures up to 600 F. ([[2019-alawi|ALAWI, 2019, p. 47]])
- In a study by Shukla et al. (2003), pre-injecting nitrogen gas before acid reduced the required acid pore volume for wormhole breakthrough by 1.5 to 3 times in water-saturated cores. ([[2019-alawi|ALAWI, 2019, p. 38]])
- The thermochemical reaction can produce a pressure increase of up to 5000 psi and heat that reduces formation fluid viscosity. ([[2019-alawi|ALAWI, 2019, p. 47]])
- In the simulation for Scenario 1, the diversion effect was observed as the injection rate ratio between high and low permeability zones stabilized at approximately 3.5. ([[2019-alawi|ALAWI, 2019, p. 39]])
- Alternating thermochemical and acid fluid injections over 16 cycles reduced the required cumulative acid volume from 31 cu.ft/ft to 8.67 cu.ft/ft for both zones to reach full treatment. ([[2019-alawi|ALAWI, 2019, p. 43]])
- The thermochemical reaction can increase temperature from 73.4 F to 176 F in just 7 minutes under specific conditions. ([[2019-alawi|ALAWI, 2019, p. 44]])
- In gas formations, a lower pressure difference between the reservoir and bottom-hole resulted in better diversion of acid between two layers compared to higher pressure differences. ([[2019-alawi|ALAWI, 2019, p. 63]])
- Higher porosity values allowed for more acid to be injected and distributed towards both layers, while lower porosity led to acid being mostly consumed by the high-permeability layer. ([[2019-alawi|ALAWI, 2019, p. 64]])
- As permeability ratio increases (from 5 mD to 50 mD), the model predicts that more acid will treat the top layer initially, with only small amounts diverted to the bottom one. ([[2019-alawi|ALAWI, 2019, p. 65]])
- In liquid formations, viscosity was identified as the key factor for diverting acid between layers rather than pressure difference due to the incompressibility of liquids. ([[2019-alawi|ALAWI, 2019, p. 67]])
- Alternating thermochemical and acid fluids over 19 cycles resulted in complete treatment of a liquid formation with only 5.6 ft3/ft of acid volume cumulatively. ([[2019-alawi|ALAWI, 2019, p. 70]])
- In the laboratory experiment (Experiment #1), thermochemical fluid injection prior to acid treatment successfully diverted acid into the lower permeability core, resulting in wormhole breakthrough in both cores. ([[2019-alawi|ALAWI, 2019, p. 74]])
- In Experiment #2, alternating thermochemical and HCl fluids resulted in wormholes being generated in both rock samples (10 mD and 100 mD) as evidenced by CT scans. ([[2019-alawi|ALAWI, 2019, p. 80]])
- Alternating the thermochemical fluids and acids reduced the cumulative acid volumes required for wormhole breakthrough by approximately 5 ft3/ft for liquid formations and 22 ft3/ft for gas formations compared to standard injection. ([[2019-alawi|ALAWI, 2019, p. 83]])

## Limitations

The study is considered an initial proof of concept. Factors such as reservoir temperature, fluid quality in extended-reach wells, reaction rates, pre- and post-flush compatibility, and pressure pulses from the thermochemical reaction are not fully reflected in the current model or lab experiments.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Integrate the mathematical model with a specific thermochemical reaction model to include real-time pressure/temperature changes. Incorporate more oil/gas types and study the role of reaction products. Adapt the model for formations with faults and incorporate wormhole propagation models into skin calculations.
