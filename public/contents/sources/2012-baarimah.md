# The Effects of Well and Reservoir Parameters on Horizontal and Vertical Wells Performance in Gas Condensate Reservoir

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of reservoir parameters, specifically horizontal permeability (kh), vertical-to-horizontal permeability ratio (kv/kh), and formation thickness (h), on the recovery factors of oil and gas in both horizontal and vertical wells. Through simulation studies, it determines optimal well types based on specific geological conditions like low vs. high permeability and thin vs. thick formations.

---

## Research Problem

The decline of reservoir pressure below the dew-point pressure in retrograde gas--condensate reservoirs leads to condensate banking, which significantly reduces well productivity. There is a lack of generalized models and simple algebraic equations to quantify the impact of specific well and reservoir parameters on horizontal vs. vertical well performance.

## Objectives

- Conduct compositional reservoir simulation studies to determine the effects of all relevant well and reservoir parameters on the performance of horizontal and vertical wells in retrograde gas-condensate reservoirs.
- Evaluate the influence of the length of the horizontal section of the well.
- Investigate the influence of reservoir thickness on the performance of horizontal vs. vertical wells.
- Evaluate the influence of the vertical to horizontal permeability ratio (kv/kh).
- Analyze the effect of drainage area size and shape on recovery factors.
- Develop simple algebraic equations to calculate abandonment time as a function of well and reservoir parameters.

## Methods

The study utilized a 3D reservoir simulation approach. Two well models were developed: a vertical well model (radial/Cartesian) and a horizontal well model (Cartesian). Simulation parameters included varying drainage areas (80 and 160 acres), formation thicknesses (25, 50, and 100 ft), horizontal permeabilities (1, 10, and 100 mD), and vertical-to-horizontal permeability ratios (kv/kh of 0.1, 0.5, and 1). Fluid properties were modeled using the Peng-Robinson Equation of State (EOS) based on data from the Cusiana Field.

## Data and Materials

Simulation data for a retrograde gas-condensate reservoir. Parameters included: drainage areas (80 and 160 acres), horizontal permeabilities (1, 10, 100 md), vertical to horizontal permeability ratios (0.1, 0.5, 1.0), formation thicknesses (25, 50, 100 ft), and horizontal well penetration ratios (0.2, 0.4, 0.8).

## Key Findings

- For horizontal wells, lower values of formation permeability, formation thickness, kv/kh ratio, and horizontal well penetration ratio lead to higher average reservoir pressure at abandonment time. ([[2012-baarimah|BAARIMAH, 2012, p. 18]])
- Horizontal wells perform better than vertical wells when horizontal permeability and formation thickness are low, while kv/kh and horizontal well penetration ratio are high. ([[2012-baarimah|BAARIMAH, 2012, p. 18]])
- The average field oil saturation in the MBO model was within 5% of the compositional model for gas injection with reduced vertical communication. ([[2012-baarimah|BAARIMAH, 2012, p. 35]])
- Horizontal wells have a smaller drawdown pressure than vertical wells, leading to delayed dew-point pressure reaching. ([[2012-baarimah|BAARIMAH, 2012, p. 39]])
- Oil saturation buildup in the near wellbore is 1.35 times lower in horizontal wells compared to vertical wells before the dew point is reached. ([[2012-baarimah|BAARIMAH, 2012, p. 39]])
- The ratio of horizontal well productivity index to vertical well productivity index is 3.38 before the dew-point pressure is reached and 6.11 after it is reached. ([[2012-baarimah|BAARIMAH, 2012, p. 39]])
- In heterogeneous systems, horizontal wells improve flow performance by reaching total production plateau faster than vertical wells. ([[2012-baarimah|BAARIMAH, 2012, p. 38]])
- The preference for horizontal wells is economically more defendable at lower kv/kh ratios. ([[2012-baarimah|BAARIMAH, 2012, p. 39]])
- In low-permeability reservoirs (kh = 1 mD), horizontal wells produced reserves approximately 3.2 times faster than vertical wells when the formation thickness was 25 feet. ([[2012-baarimah|BAARIMAH, 2012, p. 70]])
- In high-permeability reservoirs (kh = 10 md and 100 md) with kv/kh = 1, the performance of horizontal and vertical wells was considered identical regardless of formation thickness. ([[2012-baarimah|BAARIMAH, 2012, p. 70]])
- For a reservoir with kh = 1 mD and a drainage area of 80 acres, the ultimate recovery factor for oil in a horizontal well is approximately 2-3% higher than in a vertical well. ([[2012-baarimah|BAARIMAH, 2012, p. 61]])
- In cases where kh = 1 md, the gross-gas and free-gas recovery factors for horizontal wells are about 10% higher than those of vertical wells when formation thickness is 25 feet. ([[2012-baarimah|BAARIMAH, 2012, p. 70]])
- Vertical well performance becomes very poor as formation permeability decreases from 10 md to 1 md. ([[2012-baarimah|BAARIMAH, 2012, p. 70]])
- In thin formations (25 ft) with low horizontal permeability (1 md) and kv/kh=1, a horizontal well with a penetration ratio of 0.8 performs significantly better than vertical wells or horizontal wells with lower penetration ratios. ([[2012-baarimah|BAARIMAH, 2012, p. 76]])
- In thin formations (25 ft) with high horizontal permeability (100 md), the performance of horizontal and vertical wells becomes nearly identical for all penetration ratios, suggesting a vertical well is sufficient. ([[2012-baarimah|BAARIMAH, 2012, p. 77]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study provides specific recommendations for well selection based on reservoir parameters: use vertical wells for high permeability (100 md) or low kv/kh (0.1) with low permeability, and horizontal wells with high penetration ratios (0.8) for lower permeability cases.
