# AN EXPERIMENTAL STUDY OF OIL RECOVERY FROM CARBONATE RESERVOIRS BY IN-SITU GENERATED CO2

**Summary**: The thesis investigates enhanced oil recovery (EOR) in carbonate reservoirs using citric acid to trigger the in-situ generation of CO2. The study combines laboratory core flooding experiments with CMG reservoir simulations to evaluate different well configurations and chemical concentrations.

---

## Research Problem

Standard CO2 EOR faces challenges like gravity override, viscous fingering, and poor foam stability in high-salinity carbonate environments; in-situ generation aims to bypass these issues by producing CO2 directly within the reservoir.

## Objectives

- Study the amount of oil that can be recovered using in-situ generation of CO2 in carbonate reservoirs using citric acid at different forms and concentrations.
- Validate the concept of in-situ CO2 generation on a large scale using CMG simulations.
- Evaluate the effect of in-situ generated CO2 on oil viscosity and saturation pressure.
- Compare the effectiveness of different concentrations of citric acid (2%, 5%, 7.5%, 10%) on oil recovery.
- Evaluate the impact of encapsulating citric acid to control its reaction and placement.
- Measure the contribution of IFT reduction from spent citric acid (calcium citrate byproduct) on oil recovery.

## Methods

The study utilized core flooding experiments using the FDES-645Z system. Procedures included: 1) Preparing citric acid solutions in sea water at various concentrations; 2) Measuring core properties (porosity, permeability); 3) Core saturation with formation brine and oil (Uthmania oil); 4) Conducting core flood tests to determine mobility and recovery; 5) Injecting varying amounts of citric acid or encapsulated citric acid; 6) Performing IFT measurements to evaluate the role of calcium citrate byproducts.

## Data and Materials

The study used Indiana limestone cores with initial porosity ranging from 0.15 to 0.18 and permeability between 10.8 md and 73 md. IFT measurements were performed on oil (30° API) with various citric acid concentrations. CMG simulations were modeled over a period of 20 years (2015-2034).

## Key Findings

- Core flooding with 7.5wt% citric acid resulted in a recovery factor of 74.05%. ([[2014-alam|ALAM, 2014, p. 18]])
- Core flooding with 10wt% encapsulated citric acid yielded the highest recovery factor of 84%. ([[2014-alam|ALAM, 2014, p. 18]])
- Spent citric acid resulted in an 8% higher recovery than sea water, confirming IFT reduction due to calcium citrate. ([[2014-alam|ALAM, 2014, p. 18]])
- CO2 can dissolve up to 60-80 mol% in oil; for temperatures below the critical point (304.13 K), solubility increases until it reaches liquefaction pressure (5.88 MPa). ([[2014-alam|ALAM, 2014, p. 45]])
- CO2 injection can cause oil swelling of up to 50-60%. ([[2014-alam|ALAM, 2014, p. 46]])
- Oil viscosity reduces significantly as CO2 is dissolved in it; for example, at 19 MPa, a 97.6% reduction was observed for oil with 15°API. ([[2014-alam|ALAM, 2014, p. 50]])
- In-situ generation of CO2 using ammonium carbamate showed enhanced crude oil recovery by 14.1% when the volume of surfactant solution was increased from 0.1 PV to 0.3 PV. ([[2014-alam|ALAM, 2014, p. 54]])
- HEDTA chelating agents at low pH used for in-situ CO2 generation in carbonate cores increased oil recovery by 35% after sea water injection. ([[2014-alam|ALAM, 2014, p. 55]])
- The study determined that a core with 10.8 md permeability and 0.15 porosity was suitable for testing encapsulated citric acid as the pore throat size (10.35 µm) exceeded the capsule diameter (200 nm). ([[2014-alam|ALAM, 2014]])
- page ([[2014-alam|ALAM, 2014]])
- Citric acid concentration directly correlates with recovery: 2% citric acid yielded an additional 11.6% recovery, while 10% citric acid yielded 42.5% extra recovery in core flooding. ([[2014-alam|ALAM, 2014, p. 93]])
- The 10% spent citric acid solution showed a significant reduction in IFT (from 20 mN/m for sea water to 4.75 mN/m) at atmospheric pressure. ([[2014-alam|ALAM, 2014, p. 118]])
- IFT values for 10% spent citric acid were found to be independent of pressure but highly dependent on temperature, dropping from 11.78 mN/m at 30°C to 8.98 mN/m at 100°C. ([[2014-alam|ALAM, 2014, p. 119]])
- In the aquifer injection scenario, vertical injector and producer configurations provided better sweep and were preferred over horizontal designs. ([[2014-alam|ALAM, 2014, p. 123]])
- The study confirmed that IFT reduction is a primary mechanism for additional oil recovery when using spent citric acid (8.03% extra recovery compared to sea water). ([[2014-alam|ALAM, 2014, p. 109]])
