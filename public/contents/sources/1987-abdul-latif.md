# Geostatistical estimation of reserves in the Abu-Tartur phosphate deposits western desert, Egypt.

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the Abu Tartur phosphate deposits in the Western Desert of Egypt, specifically focusing on the estimation of reserves within the lower phosphate horizon. The study utilizes geostatistical methods, including block kriging, to model and estimate the distribution of phosphate grades across the deposit.

---

## Research Problem

Accurate determination of the quantity (tonnage) and quality (grade) of phosphate minerals in the Abu Tartur deposit, specifically addressing the limitations of classical estimation methods like polygonal or triangular methods which lack a statistical basis for precision.

## Objectives

- Perform initial statistical treatment of drilling data.
- Compute horizontal and vertical variograms for phosphate grade, thickness, and accumulation.
- Fit theoretical models to the computed variograms.
- Conduct global estimation of total tonnage, total phosphate content, and mean grade.
- Perform local estimation (kriging) to provide block-by-block estimates of the deposit.
- Evaluate the mineralogy and geological characteristics of the Abu Tartur phosphate deposits.
- Assess the thickness and grade (P2O5) of the lower and upper phosphate horizons.
- Compare classical reserve estimation methods with geostatistical methods to determine more accurate tonnage and grade estimates.
- Perform a critical review of data from the Abu Tartur phosphate deposit.
- Construct and interpret experimental variograms for P2O5 content, thickness, and accumulation.
- Fit mathematical models (spherical/compound spherical) to these variograms.
- Calculate global estimates for total tonnage, total phosphate content, and mean grade.
- Perform local estimation of ore reserves using kriging.
- Perform statistical analysis of available drilling data for P2O5 grade, thickness, and accumulation.
- Develop variogram models for phosphate grade, thickness, and accumulation.
- Implement block kriging to provide local estimates of phosphate grades.
- Establish a grade-tonnage relationship to assist in production planning.
- Determine the geological characteristics of the Abu Tartur phosphate deposits.
- Establish variogram models for different parameters like P205, thickness, and accumulation.
- Apply kriging techniques to estimate the volume and grade of the phosphate reserves.
- Estimate the reserves in the lower phosphate horizon of the Abu Tartur deposit.
- Apply geostatistical methods to model and estimate mineral content.
- Apply geostatistical methods to model and estimate phosphate grades.

## Methods

The study utilizes a dataset from 347 drill holes on a 400m x 400m grid. The methodology includes: 1) Preliminary statistical analysis (histograms, cumulative frequency plots). 2) Variogram modeling to determine spatial continuity for grade (P2O5%), thickness, and accumulation. 3) Global estimation using the Random Stratified Grid method to calculate total tonnage and mean grades with associated variances. 4) Local estimation using Block Kriging to provide block-by-block estimates and a grade-tonnage relationship curve based on 25m x 25m x 1m selective mining units.

## Data and Materials

Data derived from a drilling program conducted by the Egyptian Geological Survey, including: 1297 samples of P2O5% (from 1-m composites), 347 drill-holes providing data for thickness and accumulation measurements. A random stratified grid (RSG) with 400m x 400m dimensions was applied to the deposit.

## Key Findings

- The lower phosphate horizon has an average phosphate (P2O5) grade of 26% and thickness ranging between 0.3 and 8.0 m. ([[1987-abdul-latif|Abdul-Latif, 1987, p. 23]])
- The upper phosphate horizon is of minor economic value, with an average grade of 17% and a thickness ranging between 0.2 and 4.0 m. ([[1987-abdul-latif|Abdul-Latif, 1987, p. 23]])
- Spherical models were successfully fitted to all experimental variograms for the deposit. ([[1987-abdul-latif|Abdul-Latif, 1987, p. 18]])
- The estimation of total tonnage was calculated as 308.36 ± 13.56 Mt at a 95% confidence level. ([[1987-abdul-latif|Abdul-Latif, 1987, p. 18]])
- The estimated total phosphate content is 80.05 ± 0.15 Mt at a 95% confidence level. ([[1987-abdul-latif|Abdul-Latif, 1987, p. 18]])
- The mean grade was estimated at 26.00 ± 0.19% at a 95% confidence level. ([[1987-abdul-latif|Abdul-Latif, 1987, p. 18]])
- Kriging standard deviations for the 3752 blocks were found to be relatively small, indicating high reliability of the kriged estimates. ([[1987-abdul-latif|Abdul-Latif, 1987, p. 18]])
- The lower phosphate horizon has an average grade of 26% P2O5 and a thickness ranging from 1.8 m to 8.0 m. ([[1987-abdul-latif|Abdul-Latif, 1987, p. 34]])
- The upper phosphate horizon has an average grade of 17% P2O5 and a thickness ranging from 0.4 m to 7.3 m. ([[1987-abdul-latif|Abdul-Latif, 1987, p. 35]])
- Quartz constitutes 10-15% of the main phosphate bed in its lower part and 5-10% in its upper part. ([[1987-abdul-latif|Abdul-Latif, 1987, p. 41]])
- The isograde map shows P2O5 content varies between approximately 18% and 28%. ([[1987-abdul-latif|Abdul-Latif, 1987, p. 53]])
- The isopach map indicates the thickness of the main phosphate horizon ranges from about 100 cm to 500 cm. ([[1987-abdul-latif|Abdul-Latif, 1987, p. 53]])
- The mean values for the variables were determined as: P2O5% = 26.58%, Thickness = 2.95 m, and Accumulation = 76.58 %m. ([[1987-abdul-latif|Abdul-Latif, 1987, p. 61]])
- The standard deviations for the variables were calculated as: P2O5% = 2.36, Thickness = 1.61, and Accumulation = 35.95. ([[1987-abdul-latif|Abdul-Latif, 1987, p. 61]])
- The correlation coefficient between accumulation and thickness was estimated to be 0.96. ([[1987-abdul-latif|Abdul-Latif, 1987, p. 93]])

## Limitations

Classical estimation methods (polygonal, triangular) are criticized for having arbitrary zones of influence and lacking a way to determine precision or confidence limits.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The thesis advocates for the use of geostatistical methods over classical ones because they incorporate geological characteristics and provide a basis for determining the precision of estimates.
