# METHODS FOR ENHANCING FRAC-TURING PROPPANT CONDUCTIVITY

**Summary**: The thesis investigates methods to enhance fracturing proppant conductivity by evaluating the mechanical behavior and short-term/long-term conductivity of various proppants, including 40/70 sand, resin-coated sand (RCS), 40/70 ceramic, and 100-mesh sand. It concludes that resin coating significantly improves the durability and conductivity of raw sand by reducing crushing and encapsulating fines.

---

## Research Problem

The high cost of premium proppants like ceramics in hydraulic fracturing necessitates finding ways to enhance lower-cost materials (like local sands) without compromising fracture conductivity, especially under high closure stresses.

## Objectives

- Identify the characteristics of fracturing sand.
- Test the raw and improved sand samples in terms of fracture conductivity and adherence to common industry standards, and compare them with proppants used in the industry.
- Investigating the performance and long-term stability of sand proppants at high-pressure condition.
- Identify ideal sand mixture for optimum frac sand
- Evaluate the conductivity of 40/70 sand, 40/70 resin-coated sand (RCS), and 40/70 ceramic under various stress conditions.
- Assess the impact of proppant mixtures (e.g., 40/70 sand and 40/70 ceramic) on conductivity.

## Methods

The study was conducted in three phases: 1) Characterization of proppants (40/70 raw sand, RCS 40/70, 40/70 ceramic, and 100-mesh sand) and sandstone rock slabs using bulk density measurement, sphericity/roundness determination via Leica S9i digital stereo microscope, XRD analysis for mineralogy (Malvern Panalytical), sieve analysis (Retsch AS 200), and surface roughness measurement (KRÜSS analyzer). 2) Conductivity measurements using a PCM1000 Proppant Conductivity Meter with an API conductivity cell. Short-term tests were conducted up to 6,000 psi for sand types and up to 9,000 psi for ceramic/100-mesh samples. Long-term tests (up to 8,000 psi) were performed on 40/70 ceramic. 3) Post-experiment characterization including particle size distribution analysis using a Camsizer X2.

## Data and Materials

Four types of proppants: 40/70 raw sand (sourced from a mine south of Riyadh, Saudi Arabia), resin-coated sand (RCS 40/70), 40/70 ceramic, and 100-mesh sand. Experimental materials included sandstone slabs and steel platens (7x1.5x0.35 inches).

## Key Findings

- 40/70 sand exhibited a sphericity of 0.77 and a roundness of 0.3, failing the recommended threshold for roundness (≥0.6). ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 38]])
- Resin coating on 40/70 sand increased sphericity from 0.77 to 0.82. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 39]])
- 40/70 ceramic proppant showed high sphericity and roundness (both 0.9), indicating a near-ideal spherical shape. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 40]])
- XRD analysis of 40/70 ceramic revealed it is primarily composed of mullite (71.3%), with silicon oxide (28.3%) and 0.4% eucryptite. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 41]])
- 40/70 sand showed a high crush rate, producing 46.8% crushed material at 10,000 psi. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 42]])
- RCS 40/70 demonstrated significantly improved crush resistance, with only 4.7% crushed material at 10,000 psi. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 42]])
- 40/70 ceramic showed excellent crush resistance, with a crushing rate of only 2.8% at 15,000 psi. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 42]])
- Coating the 40/70 sand with phenolic resin resulted in a five-fold increase in conductivity at 6,000 psi compared to uncoated 40/70 sand. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 44]])
- After short-term experiments at 6,000 psi, 40/70 sand yielded 65.18% fines smaller than 212 microns (down from 315-425 μm range). ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 46]])
- RCS 40/70 produced only 14.08% fines below 212 microns after the experiment, a significant improvement over raw sand. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 46]])
- 40/70 sand produced 47% crushed material at 6000 psi, leading to severe conductivity reduction due to fines migration. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 82]])
- Resin-coated sand (RCS) showed significantly reduced fines generation (4.67% at 10,000 psi compared to 47% for uncoated sand). ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 82]])
- Adding 40/70 ceramic to 40/70 sand in a 3:1 ratio improved conductivity by 1.34 to 1.56 times at various stress levels. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 83]])
- Mixing 100-mesh sand with 25% of 40/70 ceramic increased conductivity by 1.55 to 3.13 times compared to pure 100-mesh sand. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 83]])
- The tail-in design of 100-mesh sand and 20/40 sand achieved up to 50 times greater conductivity compared to a pack of only 100-mesh sand. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 79]])

## Limitations

The results were obtained under ideal laboratory conditions; further research is needed to evaluate proppant performance under reservoir-specific conditions (high temperatures, formation fluids, and cyclic stresses).

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Conduct conductivity experiments under simulated reservoir conditions (temperature, oil composition).
- Investigate the effect of cyclic stress.
- Use rock samples from actual fields.
- Study other polymers for sand coating (epoxy resin, furan, polyester, vinyl ester).
- Optimize proppant blends using hybrid designs like even blending or tail-in design.
