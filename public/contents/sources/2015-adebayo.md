# An Experimental study of the effects of CO2 sequestration on electrical properties of carbonate rocks

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of CO2 sequestration and subsequent Carbonic Acid/Brine-Rock Interactions (CBRI) on the petrophysical and electrical properties of carbonate rocks. It specifically focuses on how these interactions affect Archie's constants ('a', 'm', 'n') to improve the accuracy of water saturation monitoring in carbonated reservoirs.

---

## Research Problem

Determining whether changes in electrical parameters (saturation exponents and cementation factor) during CO2 sequestration are caused by altered pore structures or measurement artifacts like hysteresis. This is critical for accurately interpreting reservoir properties in carbonate formations under CO2 injection.

## Objectives

- Characterize and study the effect of CO2 storage on rock samples under both flow and static conditions.
- Evaluate the impact of CO2-brine-rock interaction (CBRI) and CO2-oil-brine-rock interaction (COBRI) on electrical properties like saturation exponent.
- Assess the reliability of using resistivity logs for monitoring CO2 migration and identifying rock/fluid interactions.
- To study the effect of CO2 sequestration on petrophysical properties of carbonate rocks such as wettability, permeability, porosity, pore geometry, and mineralogy
- To study the effect of CO2–Brine–Rock interaction and CO2–Brine–Oil-Rock interaction during CO2 sequestration in saline aquifer and during CO2EOR on electrical properties (‘a’, ‘m’, ‘n’, Rt) of carbonate rocks
- To develop a protocol for predicting the change in these values [Archie constants] during CO2 sequestration.

## Methods

The study involved preparing 12 cylindrical core plugs from Indiana limestone. Samples were cleaned using a solvent reflux method (soxhlet) and dried in a vacuum oven. Core properties including pore volume, porosity, and permeability were measured using an automated helium porosimeter-permeameter. Pore size distribution was characterized via Mercury Injection Capillary Pressure (MICP) and NMR T2-relaxometry. CO2 dissolution rates were determined by monitoring pressure changes in a mixing chamber over time. The Duan and Sun model was used to calculate CO2 solubility in brine at various pressures and temperatures.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized carbonate rock core samples. Specific quantities of cores are not detailed in the abstract, but multiple samples were subjected to MICP, NMR, XRF, and CT scans. Analytical data included resistivity measurements, porosity calculations, and chemical analyses of brine components.

## Key Findings

- All core samples were found to be of bimodal pore type and very homogeneous based on MICP and NMR measurements. ([[2015-adebayo|Adebayo, 2015, p. 16]])
- CT scans confirmed that fractures and vugs were not present in the samples. ([[2015-adebayo|Adebayo, 2015, p. 16]])
- XRF spectroscopy was used to identify rock precipitates after exposure. ([[2015-adebayo|Adebayo, 2015]])
- page ([[2015-adebayo|Adebayo, 2015]])
- Carbonate rocks are more complex than siliciclastic rocks due to heterogeneity in pore character (pore size distribution, connectivity, fractures, and dolomitization). ([[2015-adebayo|Adebayo, 2015, p. 47]])
- Well-connected intra-particle micro porosity in carbonate grains tends to be conductive even at low water saturation. ([[2015-adebayo|Adebayo, 2015, p. 48]])
- Carbonate rocks are more inclined to oil wetness than water wetness, complicating petrophysical studies. ([[2015-adebayo|Adebayo, 2015, p. 48]])
- Dissolution of carbonate minerals by carbonic acid can lead to the formation of micro-porous grains (e.g., cherts, fossil fragments) and moldic porosity. ([[2015-adebayo|Adebayo, 2015, p. 56]])
- Dolomitization results in enlarged pore throats, leading to higher porosity, permeability, and easier ion movement. ([[2015-adebayo|Adebayo, 2015, p. 56]])
- Precipitates from CBRI can plug pore throats and ion paths, potentially causing high resistivity measurements. ([[2015-adebayo|Adebayo, 2015, p. 56]])
- A study on Stuttgart sandstone showed that while porosity increased due to dissolution, permeability also increased in most cores after 15 months of CO2 exposure. ([[2015-adebayo|Adebayo, 2015, p. 52]])
- In a carbonate core (67% dolomite, 20% anhydrite), permeability and porosity increased significantly after CO2 flooding at 38°C and 13.79 MPa. ([[2015-adebayo|Adebayo, 2015, p. 53]])
- A study on chalk formation during EOR showed an increase in porosity and permeability, but no significant change in wettibility from NMR T2 relaxation times. ([[2015-adebayo|Adebayo, 2015, p. 54]])
- Resistivity changes as low as 0.05 ohm-meters can be captured by a 21-electrode array to identify water migration between zones. ([[2015-adebayo|Adebayo, 2015, p. 63]])
- The average resistivity measurement for the prepared brine was 0.401 ohm-meter at 22.4°C. ([[2015-adebayo|Adebayo, 2015, p. 73]])

## Limitations

The study notes the difficulty of distinguishing between changes caused by actual pore structure alterations versus measurement errors or hysteresis when evaluating electrical parameters after CO2 exposure.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Incorporate an online pH meter during CO2 storage to correlate pH changes with resistivity/temperature logs. Conduct tests on pipelines and tanks to confirm if similar trends are observed in industrial infrastructure.
