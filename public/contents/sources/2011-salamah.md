# Assessing the Use of Local Indigenous Microorganisms for the Bioremediation of Sites Contaminated With Petroleum Hydrocarbons

**Summary**: The thesis investigated the isolation and characterization of indigenous bacterial strains (Pseudoxanthomonas sp. and Bacillus sp.) from soil contaminated with crude oil at an Aramco site in Jeddah. It evaluated the effectiveness of these strains in degrading total petroleum hydrocarbons (TPH) under varying environmental conditions, specifically temperature, pH, and nutrient concentrations.

---

## Research Problem

Soil and groundwater contamination by petroleum products poses significant environmental risks; therefore, identifying effective local indigenous microorganisms for bioremediation is necessary to develop cost-effective treatment methods for contaminated sites in Saudi Arabia.

## Objectives

- Isolation and characterization of indigenous microorganisms capable of degrading total petroleum hydrocarbons from contaminated sites.
- Evaluate the potential of enhanced biodegradation in the removal of total petroleum hydrocarbons (TPH) from contaminated sites under different conditions of temperatures, pH, nutrients and time.
- Identify the optimum bio-treatment conditions of hydrocarbons contaminated soil.
- Isolate and characterize bacterial strains from Aramco contaminated sandy soil site with crude oil.
- Characterize isolated strains by sequencing the 16S rRNA gene.
- Investigate the removal of total petroleum hydrocarbon (TPH) using a consortium of two strains under three environmental factors: temperature, pH, and nutrient concentrations.

## Methods

The study involved collecting contaminated sandy soil samples from an Aramco site in Jeddah. Soil was prepared by drying at 120°C and sieving through a 0.197 inch mesh. Indigenous microorganisms were isolated and enriched using mineral salt medium (MSM) and nutrient broth. Bacterial identification was performed via 16S rRNA gene sequencing using the NORGEN BIOTEK kit, PCR amplification with universal primers (8-27 F and 1492 R), and agarose gel electrophoresis. TPH removal was quantified using a gravimetric method involving extraction with n-hexane in an ultrasonic bath followed by rotary evaporation. Experimental trials were conducted over 20 days to test the effects of three nutrient sources (Ammonium Chloride, Sodium Nitrate, and Urea) at different concentrations (5, 10, 20 mM), three pH levels (5, 7, 9), and three temperatures (25, 35, 45°C).

## Data and Materials

Soil samples collected from an Aramco site in Jeddah; two bacterial isolates (Pseudoxanthomonas sp. and Bacillus sp.); TPH concentrations measured in 10g soil samples; experimental trials conducted over 20 days with multiple replicates for each condition.

## Key Findings

- Two indigenous strains, Pseudoxanthomonas sp. and Bacillus sp., were successfully isolated and characterized via 16S rRNA sequencing from contaminated sandy soil. ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 44]])
- Ammonium chloride was identified as the most effective nitrogen source for TPH removal, reducing TPH to 44.5% compared to Urea (48%) and Sodium nitrate (49.5%). ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 12]])
- Under a concentration of 5 mM Ammonium chloride, TPH levels were reduced to 30% at 5 mM, 48% at 10 mM, and 77.5% at 20 mM. ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 12]])
- The optimal pH for TPH removal was 7.0, where the bacterial consortium reduced TPH to 55%. ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 12]])
- The best removal efficiency of TPH occurred at 35°C (TPH down to 45%), while 25°C and 45°C resulted in lower removal efficiencies of 57% and 72% respectively. ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 12]])
- The initial four days of the experiment showed the highest rate of biodegradation, particularly with ammonium chloride, before rates slowed as oxygen levels decreased. ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 50]])
- A concentration of 5 mM Ammonium chloride was determined to be the optimal concentration for maximizing TPH removal in the study's specific conditions. ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 57]])
- The initial TPH content in each soil sample was approximately 0.19 grams per 10 grams of soil (roughly 4% by weight). ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 42]])
- The removal of TPH was highest at 35°C, reducing TPH from 0.1929 g (100%) to 0.0859 g (45% reduction) after 20 days. ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 62]])
- The lowest reduction rate was at 45°C, which reduced TPH from 0.1929 g (100%) to 0.1387 g (72% - note: text says 'reduced... to 0.1387= 72%', likely meaning 72% remaining or a calculation error in the source's percentage notation). ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 62]])
- The optimum biodegradation rate was reached at 35°C with 5mM of ammonium chloride at pH=7. ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 67]])
- Biodegradation rates were observed to be slower below 15°C or above 40°C due to the impact on enzyme activity. ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 65]])

## Limitations

The study notes that factors such as moisture and evaporation can significantly affect results; however, weathering was performed to fix moisture content and remove volatile light hydrocarbons (under C16) before testing.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Consider other parameters in more detail, such as oxygen levels and moisture content.
- Identify optimum conditions of the bioremediation treatment process.
- Develop genetically modified bacteria for crude oil biodegradation enhancement.
