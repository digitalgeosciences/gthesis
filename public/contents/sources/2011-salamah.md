# Assessing the Use of Local Indigenous Microorganisms for the Bioremediation of Sites Contaminated With Petroleum Hydrocarbons

**Summary**: The thesis investigated the isolation and characterization of indigenous bacterial strains (Pseudoxanthomonas sp. and Bacillus sp.) from crude oil contaminated soil in Saudi Arabia to evaluate their ability to biodegrade total petroleum hydrocarbons (TPH). The study assessed the impact of three environmental factors—temperature, pH, and nutrient concentration—on the remediation efficiency.

---

## Research Problem

Petroleum hydrocarbon contamination of soil and groundwater poses significant environmental risks; there is a need for cost-effective, non-invasive bioremediation methods using local microorganisms to treat contaminated sites.

## Objectives

- Isolation and characterization of indigenous microorganisms capable of degrading total petroleum hydrocarbons from contaminated sites.
- Evaluate the potential of enhanced biodegradation in the removal of total petroleum hydrocarbons (TPH) from contaminated sites under different conditions of temperatures, pH, nutrients and time.
- Identify the optimum bio-treatment conditions of hydrocarbons contaminated soil.
- Isolate and characterize bacterial isolates capable of degrading crude oil from contaminated sandy soil.
- Investigate the potential of enhanced in situ biodegradation treatment of petroleum hydrocarbon in contaminated soils.
- Evaluate the effect of three environmental factors (pH, temperature, and nutrient concentration) on the removal of total petroleum hydrocarbons (TPH).
- Isolate and characterize bacterial isolates from Aramco contaminated sandy soil site with crude oil
- Sequence the 16S rRNA gene of isolated strains to identify species
- Investigate the removal of total petroleum hydrocarbon (TPH) using a consortium of two strains under varying environmental factors: temperature, pH, and nutrient concentrations

## Methods

Soil samples were collected from a Saudi Aramco oil field site, dried at 120°C, and sieved. Indigenous microorganisms were enriched in mineral salt medium (MSM) and identified via 16S rRNA gene sequencing using universal primers (8-27 F and 1492 R). TPH was analyzed using a gravimetric method involving extraction with n-hexane in an ultrasonic bath followed by rotary evaporation. Experimental trials evaluated the impact of temperature (25°C, 35°C, 45°C), pH (values 5, 7, and 9), and three nitrogen sources (Ammonium Chloride, Sodium Nitrate, and Urea) on biodegradation rates.

## Data and Materials

Soil samples from a Saudi Aramco oil field in Jaddah; two bacterial isolates (Pseudoxanthomonas sp. and Bacillus sp.); TPH measurements of approximately 0.2g per 10g soil sample; experimental trials involving three temperature levels, three pH levels, and three nitrogen source types.

## Key Findings

- Two bacterial strains, Pseudoxanthomonas sp. and Bacillus sp., were successfully isolated and characterized from the contaminated site. ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 11]])
- Ammonium chloride was identified as the best nitrogen source for TPH removal, reducing TPH to 44.5% (compared to Urea at 48% and Sodium nitrate at 49.5%). ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 11]])
- At different concentrations of ammonium chloride, TPH levels were reduced to 30% (at 5 mM), 48% (at 10 mM), and 77.5% (at 20 mM). ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 11]])
- At pH 7.0, the consortium of bacteria was most efficient, reducing TPH to 55%. ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 12]])
- The best removal efficiency of TPH occurred at 35°C (TPH reduced to 45%), while 25°C resulted in 57% and 45°C resulted in 72%. ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 12]])
- Two strains were identified as Pseudoxanthomonas sp. and Bacillus sp. via 16S rRNA gene sequencing. ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 44]])
- The initial TPH content in the contaminated soil samples was approximately 0.2 g per 10 g of soil, representing a concentration of roughly 4% by weight. ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 42]])
- Ammonium chloride (10 mM) showed a higher removal rate of TPH compared to sodium nitrate and urea at 35°C. ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 50]])
- The highest biodegradation rates were observed in the first four days of incubation, particularly with ammonium chloride. ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 50]])
- Biodegradation rates slowed significantly after the first four days as oxygen levels decreased and bacterial growth/diffusion was affected. ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 50]])
- The removal of TPH was highest at 5 mM Ammonium chloride (dropping from 100% to 30%) compared to 10 mM (48%) and 20 mM (77.5%). ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 54]])
- The fastest biodegradation rates were observed in the first four days, especially with 5 mM ammonium chloride. ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 54]])
- The highest TPH removal rate was at pH 7.0 (reduced from 100% to 55% after 20 days), while rates at pH 5 and 9 were lower (79.5% and 81% respectively). ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 58]])
- The highest removal of TPH was observed at 35°C, reducing TPH from 100% to 45% after 20 days. ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 62]])
- The lowest reduction rate for TPH was recorded at 45°C (72%). ([[2011-salamah|Salamah, 2011, p. 62]])

## Limitations

The study notes that high concentrations of nutrients can lead to biofouling or eutrophication, which consumes oxygen and inhibits microbial growth; also, the difficulty of translating laboratory results directly to field applications is noted.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Consider more detailed parameters such as oxygen levels and moisture content.
- Identify optimal conditions for the bioremediation treatment process in various environments.
- Develop genetically modified bacteria for crude oil biodegradation enhancement.
