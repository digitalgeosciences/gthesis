# EFFECT OF CRUDE OIL COMPOSITION ON WETTABILITY ALTERATION DURING IMMISCIBLE CO2 FLOODING

**Summary**: The thesis investigates how different crude oil compositions (varying by API gravity) affect the wettability of carbonate rocks during immiscible CO2 flooding. It utilizes core-flooding experiments and contact angle measurements to determine the impact of oil properties on rock-fluid interactions and validates a mathematical model for predicting these changes.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the lack of understanding regarding how specific crude oil properties (like asphaltenes and API gravity) influence the effectiveness of immiscible CO2 flooding in carbonate reservoirs, particularly at low pressures where gas dissolution leads to significant swelling and wettability changes.

## Objectives

- To study the effect of oil composition on wettability alteration by CO2 in carbonate rocks through core-flooding experiments under immiscible conditions.
- To generate oil swelling data and incorporate it in the mathematical model of Al-Mutairi et al. to verify the model employing the core-flooding data.
- Quantify the recovery mechanisms and processes taking place during immiscible CO2 flooding in carbonate rocks.
- Evaluate the effect of crude oil composition (API gravity and asphaltene content) on wettibility alteration.
- Verify a mathematical model for predicting immiscible CO2 displacement with continuous wettibility alteration.

## Methods

The study involved characterizing three crude oils (40, 34.3, and 28.56 API) using SARA analysis via HPLC. Oil swelling and CO2 solubility were measured using a Chandler PVT system at 500 psig and 55°C. Core-flooding experiments were conducted on Indiana limestone core plugs in a Hassler-type holder under conditions of 900 psi confining pressure and 500 psi back pressure. Wettibility was quantified via contact angle measurements using an optically modified tensiometer to compare pre- and post-flood states.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized three types of crude oils: 40° API (light), 34.30° API (medium), and 28.56° API (heavy). Core plugs were cut from carbonate rock and aged in oil. Experimental data included contact angles, oil swelling factors, solubility values, and viscosity measurements for all three crude oils.

## Key Findings

- Crude Oil 1 (40 API) had a swelling factor of 1.1127, while Crude Oil 3 (28.56 API) had a swelling factor of 1.3630 at 500 psig and 55°C. ([[2014-shakeel|SHAKEEL, 2014, p. 46]])
- The volume of CO2 gas dissolved in 20 cc of Crude Oil 1 was 22.554 std. cc, while for Crude Oil 3 it was 27.260 std. cc. ([[2014-shakeel|SHAKEEL, 2014, p. 46]])
- Indiana limestone core plugs exhibited a porosity of 15% and permeability of 70 mD. ([[2014-shakeel|SHAKEEL, 2014, p. 37]])
- Contact angle measurements are used to determine wettibility, where 0° indicates complete water-wet and 180° indicates complete oil-wet. ([[2014-shakeel|SHAKEEL, 2014, p. 18]])
- Carbonate rocks typically exhibit mixed-wet to strongly oil-wet states, leading to lower recovery during water flooding. ([[2014-shakeel|SHAKEEL, 2014, p. 17]])
- CO2 solubility and the resulting oil swelling factor increase as oil density increases (lower API gravity). ([[2014-shakeel|SHAKEEL, 2014, p. 47]])
- The heavy crude oil (28.56° API) showed the largest reduction in contact angle from 167° to 126°, indicating a significant shift from strongly oil-wet to weakly oil-wet. ([[2014-shakeel|SHAKEEL, 2014, p. 55]])
- The light crude oil (40° API) achieved the highest recovery (67.59%) due to its lower viscosity and better displacement efficiency. ([[2014-shakeel|SHAKEEL, 2014, p. 50]])
- CO2-saturated oil viscosity was reduced by approximately 40% across all three crude oils tested. ([[2014-shakeel|SHAKEEL, 2014, p. 48]])
- The heavy oil (28.56° API) showed the highest incremental recovery beyond breakthrough due to significant wettibility alteration. ([[2014-shakeel|SHAKEEL, 2014, p. 56]])
- Nitrogen flooding, as an inert gas, resulted in significantly lower recovery compared to CO2 (e.g., 42.81% vs 67.59% for 40° API oil). ([[2014-shakeel|SHAKEEL, 2014, p. 65]])
- The mathematical model predicted total oil recovery within a 6% margin of error compared to experimental results for the 40° API crude. ([[2014-shakeel|SHAKEEL, 2014, p. 60]])

## Limitations

The study was conducted under specific pressure and temperature conditions; the model's prediction accuracy is limited by the assumption of instantaneous gas/liquid equilibrium and the use of external correlation parameters for certain variables like viscosity.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Conduct research over a wider range of pressure and temperature conditions.
- Test more crude oils to confirm the relationship between asphaltene content and wettibility alteration.
- Study the physical and chemical interactions between CO2 gas and asphaltene colloids.
- Test different carbonate rock types to evaluate the extent of wettibility alteration.
- Measure oil viscosity changes with dissolved CO2 experimentally rather than using correlations.
