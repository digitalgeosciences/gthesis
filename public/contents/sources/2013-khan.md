# Investigation of Wormhole Penetration in Carbonate Rocks under High Pressure

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the phenomenon of wormhole formation and propagation in carbonate rocks during matrix acidizing under high pressure (up to 5,000 psi) and moderate temperature. It concludes that CO2 solubility at higher pressures significantly impacts dissolution kinetics and wormhole morphology.

---

## Research Problem

Existing models for wormholing often assume 1,000 psi is sufficient to keep CO2 in solution; however, this leads to inaccurate predictions of acid penetration in deep carbonate reservoirs where high pressure affects the phase behavior of CO2 and subsequent dissolution kinetics.

## Objectives

- To conduct core acidizing experiments at pressures up to 5,000 psi and 150°F to study pressure effects on the phenomenon of wormholing.
- To study the effect of core length (up to 24 inches) with varying pressure conditions.
- Investigate the effect of pressure on pore volume to breakthrough (PVBT).
- Evaluate the impact of core length on wormhole propagation.
- Determine the optimum flow rate for acidizing under different pressure conditions.
- Analyze wormhole morphology and diameter using CT scan imaging.

## Methods

The research involved three phases: Rock Characterization, Coreflooding Experiments, and Post Acidizing Characterization. Phase 1 included CT scanning (1mm slice thickness), porosity measurement via weight difference after saturation in deionized water, and permeability calculation from pressure data. Phase 2 involved coreflooding using a DCI Dual Coreflood System where acid was injected into Indiana Limestone cores at varying pressures (1,000 to 5,000 psi) and flow rates (1-10 cc/min). Phase 3 included post-acidizing drying of cores, weight measurement to determine dissolution amount, followed by repeat CT scanning, SEM analysis (with EDX), and thin section petrography.

## Data and Materials

The study analyzed 10 core sections (labeled Section 1 to 10). Core samples were subjected to acidizing at three pressure levels (1,000, 3,000, and 5,000 psi) and four flow rates (1, 2, 5, and 10 cc/min). Five thin sections were prepared for petrography. SEM analysis was performed on samples from core segments IL-16, IL-17, and an untreated sample IL-21.

## Key Findings

- CO2 was found to be in a gaseous state at moderate pressures (e.g., 1,000 psi), which hindered the process of wormholing by promoting face dissolution. ([[2013-khan|Khan, 2013, p. 16]])
- At high pressures (3,000 and 5,000 psi), CO2 was found to be in a solution state, leading to faster tip propagation and less 'face' dissolution compared to low-pressure experiments. ([[2013-khan|Khan, 2013, p. 54]])
- The inclusion of core length (up to 24 inches) showed that longer cores provide more detailed data on the impact of boundary effects on wormhole growth. ([[2013-khan|Khan, 2013, p. 21]])
- A critical pressure point was identified around 2,800 psi where CO2 solubility increases sharply, and at approximately 4,100 psi, all CO2 enters the solution. ([[2013-khan|Khan, 2013, p. 32]])
- The study confirmed that for Indiana limestone, permeability ranged from 0.9 mD to 7 mD and porosity from 13% to 15%. ([[2013-khan|Khan, 2013, p. 41]])
- At lower flow rates (1 & 2 cc/min), the effect of pressure is dominant; at 1,000 psi, CO2 in gaseous phase causes wormhole widening, while at 3,000 and 5,000 psi, dissolved CO2 promotes smoother propagation. ([[2013-khan|Khan, 2013, p. 59]])
- At higher flow rates (5 & 10 cc/min), the effect of pressure diminishes as the process shifts from mass transfer limited to reaction rate limited. ([[2013-khan|Khan, 2013, p. 60]])
- The optimum flow rate for acidizing at 1,000 psi was found to be 5 cc/min, while at higher pressures (3,000 and 5,000 psi), the optimum rate dropped to approximately 2 cc/min. ([[2013-khan|Khan, 2013, p. 76]])
- Wormhole diameter was more consistent at high pressures (3,000 and 5,000 psi) compared to 1,000 psi, where it formed a cone-shaped structure. ([[2013-khan|Khan, 2013, p. 92]])
- Thin section analysis of Core IL-16 showed 20% porosity with pore sizes of 50-1,300 microns. ([[2013-khan|Khan, 2013, p. 99]])
- SEM mapping confirmed the presence of Calcium, Carbon, and Oxygen from carbonate rock, but Chlorine was found in both treated and untreated samples, indicating it was not a unique indicator of acid migration. ([[2013-khan|Khan, 2013, p. 104]])
- At pressures around 1,000 psi, CO2 is largely in the gaseous phase; as pressure increases, solubility of CO2 in spent acid increases and almost all CO2 is consumed at elevated pressures. ([[2013-khan|Khan, 2013, p. 119]])
- The gaseous phase of CO2 appears to enhance mass transfer, which slows propagation of wormhole, reduces its potential length, and produces a fatter hole. ([[2013-khan|Khan, 2013, p. 119]])
- At low flow rates, more PVBT are required for low pressures reservoirs than high pressure reservoirs; at high flow rates, the effect of pressure diminishes. ([[2013-khan|Khan, 2013, p. 119]])
- At low pressures, PVBT is quite sensitive to the flow rate, but at high pressures it is not the case and there is no clear optimum. ([[2013-khan|Khan, 2013, p. 119]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

- More experiments with different flow rates to better understand the phase of CO2 at different pressures.
- Use CT scan images for more detailed study of wormhole propagation and conditions applied.
- Couple coreflood studies with Rotating Disk Experiments to understand kinetics aspects.
- Perform SEM and thin section analysis on a larger number of representative samples.
