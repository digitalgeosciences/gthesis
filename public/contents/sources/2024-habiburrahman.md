# INVESTIGATION OF CO2 HYDRATE FORMATION KINETICS AND PORE-SCALE DYNAMICS FOR ENHANCED CARBON SEQUESTRATION

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the kinetics and pore-scale dynamics of CO2 hydrate formation in porous media using a high-pressure microfluidic system. It compares the behavior of hydrates formed from liquid vs. gaseous CO2 and evaluates the effectiveness of various kinetic promoters (SDS, Gemini surfactants, and Laponite-RD) to optimize carbon capture and storage.

---

## Research Problem

The challenge of mitigating carbon emissions requires efficient long-term storage solutions; CO2 hydrates offer a potential for stable sequestration in cold subsurface environments, but their formation kinetics and pore-scale behavior under various conditions (especially with chemical promoters) need better characterization to improve engineering feasibility.

## Objectives

- Investigate the pore-scale and chip-scale behavior of CO2 hydrates in microfluidic porous media.
- Characterize hydrate morphology and growth under controlled thermodynamic and kinetic conditions.
- Evaluate the kinetics of chemical promoters (SDS, GS-12, Laponite) using HPS-ALTA to improve formation rates.
- Analyze the impact of flow rates and subcooling on CO2 distribution and hydrate propagation.
- To provide detailed observations of CO2 hydrate behavior at both pore and chip scales, enabling an understanding of formation patterns, growth kinetics, and hydrate interactions across interconnected porous media.
- To investigate how varying CO2 injection rates affect hydrate nucleation, distribution, and propagation following the induction period, establishing benchmarks for hydrate-based CCS applications.

## Methods

The study utilized a low-temperature, high--pressure (LTHP) microfluidic system featuring a precision-engineered glass micromodel to simulate porous media. The micromodel contained over 18,000 interconnected pores and was monitored using high-resolution cameras for chip-scale and pore-scale imaging. Image processing via ImageJ FIJI was used to quantify phase saturations based on grayscale values. A High Pressure Stirred Lag Time Apparatus (HPS-ALTA) was employed to evaluate the kinetics of CO2 hydrate formation with various promoters: sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS), a gemini surfactant (GS-12), and Laponite-RD.

## Data and Materials

The study used pure CO2 (99.9%) and deionized water. Experiments were conducted at various injection rates (1, 5, 10, 20, 50, 100 μL/min) and subcooling levels (ΔT). Three chemical promoters (SDS, GS-12, Laponite) were tested at concentrations of 0.05, 0.1, 0.5, 1, and 2 wt%.

## Key Findings

- Liquid CO2 hydrates formed stable, pore-filling structures that drastically reduced permeability. ([[2024-habiburrahman|HABIBURRAHMAN, 2024, p. 43]])
- Gaseous CO2 hydrates underwent a two-stage transformation from porous, dark phases to non-porous, transparent phases. ([[2024-habiburrahman|HABIBURRAHMAN, 2024, p. 43]])
- Higher flow rates promoted shear-induced nucleation and greater subcooling accelerated growth through enhanced thermodynamic driving forces. ([[2024-habiburrahman|HABIBURRAHMAN, 2024, p. 24]])
- GS-12 and SDS demonstrated superior performance in accelerating hydrate formation and optimizing CO2 storage capacity compared to THF. ([[2024-habiburrahman|HABIBURRAHMAN, 2024, p. 24]])
- At higher subcooling (7.3 C), rapid, fine dendritic hydrates formed, while lower subcooling (1.7 C) produced faceted crystals with stronger physical bonds to the glass surface. ([[2024-habiburrahman|HABIBURRAHMAN, 2024, p. 45]])
- Hydrates in microfluidic systems showed that higher dissolved CO2 concentrations accelerated growth rates, transitioning from diffusion-controlled to interfacial reaction-controlled. ([[2024-habiburrahman|HABIBURRAHMAN, 2024, p. 36]])
- The study identified two primary morphologies of methane hydrate at the pore scale: (1) smooth, continuous crystals and (2) honeycomb structures with rough surfaces. ([[2024-habiburrahman|HABIBURRAHMAN, 2024, p. 43]])
- Laponite-RD acts as a layered silicate mineral that can promote hydrate formation by altering water-CO2 interactions. ([[2024-habiburrahman|HABIBURRAHMAN, 2024, p. 102]])
- Liquid CO2 hydrates formed stable, swelling, pore-filling structures with a volume variation index of 3.61, while gaseous CO2 hydrates showed shrinkage (Iv = 0.27) and transitioned from porous to non-porous phases. ([[2024-habiburrahman|HABIBURRAHMAN, 2024, p. 85]])
- SDS exhibited the highest nucleation rates (12.1 s⁻¹) at 0.5 wt%, while GS-12 achieved optimal rates of 8.9 s⁻¹ at 1 wt% and Laponite peaked at 4.0 s⁻¹ at 0.1 wt%. ([[2024-habiburrahman|HABIBURRAHMAN, 2024, p. 56]])
- GS-12 significantly reduced induction time (10 s at 5 μL/min) by minimizing interfacial tension. ([[2024-habiburrahman|HABIBURRAHMAN, 2024, p. 56]])
- THF facilitated hydrate formation under milder conditions (ΔT ≈ 1.8 °C) but had limited effects on growth rate or storage. ([[2024-habiburrahman|HABIBURRAHMAN, 2024, p. 56]])
- Hydrate propagation was observed as a 'chain reaction' where nucleation in one CO2 blob induced growth in adjacent, disconnected blobs by increasing the CO2-water interfacial area. ([[2024-habiburrahman|HABIBURRAHMAN, 2024, p. 83]])
- A crustal fingering phenomenon was observed where gas burst through weak points in hydrate shells to form intestine-like structures, leading to potential pore blockage. ([[2024-habiburrahman|HABIBURRAHMAN, 2024, p. 84]])
- Permeability can drop to zero if hydrates form at the injection point, regardless of the overall saturation level. ([[2024-habiburrahman|HABIBURRAHMAN, 2024, p. 84]])

## Limitations

The study notes that GS's rapid induction time at low flow rates led to premature hydrate formation, which hindered its ability to maximize CO2 occupancy compared to SDS. Additionally, Laponite-RD was found to be unsuitable for porous media due to gel formation.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Assess the scalability of laboratory parameters for field-scale applications using dimensional analysis.
- Conduct advanced flow loop experiments to investigate multi-phase transport dynamics during migration from surface to subsurface.
- Evaluate thermophysical effects of impurities (saline environments, multicomponent gases) on hydrate formation.
- Utilize more complex porous media with heterogeneous permeability and porosity distributions.
- Develop mechanistic growth models incorporating pore-scale physics and interfacial phenomena.
