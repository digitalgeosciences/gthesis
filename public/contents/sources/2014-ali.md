# Lexicographic Approach to Well Placement Optimization for Short, Medium And Long Term Investment Decisions

**Summary**: The thesis investigates well placement optimization using a lexicographic multi-objective approach to account for different investment horizons (short, medium, and long term). It compares this method against single-objective optimization, concluding that the lexicographic approach provides more balanced results across varying project lifespans.

---

## Research Problem

Standard single-objective well placement optimizations often focus on a specific timeframe, which may not yield optimal results if the investment horizon changes. The study addresses how to optimize for multiple timeframes (short, medium, and long term) simultaneously using lexicographic multi-objective optimization.

## Objectives

- Study the effect of production time (short, medium, and long term) on well placement optimization problems.
- Investigate the use of lexicographic multi-objective optimization to simultaneously improve economics across different reservoir life periods.
- Evaluate the effects of adding minimum well spacing constraints to the optimization problem.
- Evaluate the relationship between short, medium, and long term Net Present Value (NPV) optimization.
- Compare the impact of using different objective functions on well placement results.
- Analyze the effect of physical constraints (e.g., minimum well spacing) on well placement optimization.

## Methods

The study utilizes a lexicographic approach for multi-objective optimization where objectives are prioritized (ST-NPV, MT-NPV, LT-NPV). The Covariance Matrix Adaptation - Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) is used as the stochastic optimization algorithm. ECLIPSE 100 is employed as the reservoir simulator to evaluate well configurations. A penalty method is applied to enforce minimum well spacing constraints (5 acres). Two synthetic heterogeneous reservoir examples (a channel reservoir and a reservoir with distributed permeability) are used for testing.

## Data and Materials

Two reservoir simulation models were used: Example 1 (13,125x13,125x200 ft, 74x74x4 cells) and Example 2 (12,800x12,800x225 ft, 64x64x3 cells). Data includes PVT properties, relative permeability curves, and various production constraints.

## Key Findings

- Well placement optimization results in short, medium, and long term are affected by changing the simulated period; using only short-term NPV as an objective does not guarantee optimal results for longer terms. ([[2014-ali|Ali, 2014, p. 15]])
- The lexicographic multi-objective approach yielded well configurations that ensure better results in short, medium, and long term investment scenarios compared to single-objective optimization. ([[2014-ali|Ali, 2014, p. 120]])
- Adding minimum well spacing constraints affects the results of the optimization; this effect varies from negligible to more noticeable depending on the density of wells to be completed. ([[2014-ali|Ali, 2014, p. 15]])
- The lexicographic approach involves sequential optimization, which requires more function evaluations and higher computational costs compared to single-objective methods. ([[2014-ali|Ali, 2014, p. 16]])
- CMA-ES is a robust stochastic method for non-linear problems; it provides better results than Genetic Algorithms (GA) in some cases but requires more evaluation iterations. ([[2014-ali|Ali, 2014, p. 27]])
- The use of the penalty method to enforce minimum well spacing constraints ensures that the optimization avoids physically impossible or undesirable configurations. ([[2014-ali|Ali, 2014, p. 51]])
- In both Example 1 and Example 2, MT-NPV and LT-NPV are highly correlated, while ST-NPV is often uncorrelated with the other two terms. ([[2014-ali|Ali, 2014, p. 43]])
- Optimizing for MT-NPV or LT-NPV typically results in similar well placement outcomes because of the high number of wells and discounting factors on NPV. ([[2014-ali|Ali, 2014, p. 43]])
- In Example 1, unconstrained optimization (Case 1) resulted in some producers/injectors being located very close to each other or within the same grid cell. ([[2014-ali|Ali, 2014, p. 58]])
- Constrained optimization (Case 2) successfully increased the distance between wells compared to unconstrained cases. ([[2014-ali|Ali, 2014, p. 60]])
- Well placements optimized for ST-NPV resulted in a more rapid production decline compared to those optimized for MT-NPV or LT-NPV. ([[2014-ali|Ali, 2014, p. 72]])
- In Example 1, the production plateau was longer for well placements that yielded the best LT-NPV than for those yielding the best ST-NPV. ([[2014-ali|Ali, 2014, p. 70]])
- Adding constraints to the optimization problem in Example 1 resulted in a reduction of approximately 4% in NPV compared to unconstrained cases. ([[2014-ali|Ali, 2014, p. 87]])
- In Example 2, the impact of adding constraints was less severe, likely due to the smaller number of wells or easily achievable minimum well spacing. ([[2014-ali|Ali, 2014, p. 87]])
- The optimization of ST-NPV required fewer function evaluations to find the optimum compared to MT-NPV or LT-NPV. ([[2014-ali|Ali, 2014, p. 90]])

## Limitations

The lexicographic approach requires more function evaluations and higher computational costs due to its sequential nature. The study uses synthetic reservoir models rather than field-specific data.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Study other types of multi-objective optimization methods to optimize well placement for different investment term options due to the high computational cost of the lexicographic approach.
- Investigate the effect of simulation time in well placement optimization using the recovery factor as an objective function instead of NPV to better capture long-term production benefits.
