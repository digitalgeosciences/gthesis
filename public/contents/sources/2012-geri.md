# EVALUATION OF FILTER CAKE MINERALOGY IN A HORIZONTAL WELL

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the mineralogical composition and physical properties of filter cakes in horizontal wells, specifically focusing on how sand content from formation cuttings impacts cake thickness and drilling fluid rheology. It utilizes SEM, XRD, and CT scanning to characterize the mineralogy and internal structure of the filter cake.

---

## Research Problem

Filter cakes in horizontal wells can become difficult to remove if they contain high amounts of sand (up to 40% by volume) from the formation, which complicates acid-based cleaning treatments. Current testing methods for filter cake properties are limited to thickness, toughness, and permeability, failing to account for the chemical composition of the solids.

## Objectives

- Evaluate the chemical composition (mineralogy) of filter cake formed in horizontal sections from tip to toe.
- Determine physical properties including thickness, porosity, and permeability of the filter cake.
- Assess internal solid invasion into the formation using CT scan technology.
- Investigate how sand content in the filter cake affects the effectiveness of acid-based removal treatments.
- Evaluate the characteristics of the filter cake in horizontal wells in terms of sand content using real drilling fluid samples from the field.
- Determine the mineralogy of the filter cake using XRD and SEM techniques.

## Methods

The study utilized drilling fluid samples collected at various points along a 3175-foot horizontal section. Fluid properties including density, apparent viscosity, plastic viscosity, yield point, and gel strength were measured using a mud balance and rotational viscometer. Static filtration tests were performed using an HPHT cell with ceramic disks (35 micron) to form the filter cake. The mineralogy of the resulting cakes was analyzed via XRD (Rigaku Ultima IV MPD) and SEM-EDS (FE-SEM Model FEI Nova Nano SEM 230). CT scanning was employed to evaluate internal invasion depth by dividing the ceramic disk into five layers.

## Data and Materials

16 samples of filter cake from a horizontal sandstone formation section, 3 ceramic disks for CT scan analysis (with sand levels of 22%, 33%, and 45.6%), and various drilling fluid parameters (density, apparent viscosity, plastic viscosity, yield point, gel strength).

## Key Findings

- In long horizontal sections in sandstone formations, the composition of the filter cake is not constant from its toe to heel and the sand percentage increases up to 50%. ([[2012-geri|Geri, 2012, p. 13]])
- The internal invasion becomes critical when the sand percentage reaches higher than 40%, as solids will invade into deeper zones. ([[2012-geri|Geri, 2012, p. 13]])
- the sand percentage increases as well as the cake thickness increases as more feet of horizontal section of sandstone formation is drilled. ([[2012-geri|Geri, 2012]])
- page_number_not_found ([[2012-geri|Geri, 2012]])
- Drilling fluid density increased by approximately 0.22 ppg (1.67 pcf) over the 3,175 ft horizontal section. ([[2012-geri|Geri, 2012, p. 78]])
- Apparent viscosity increased by approximately 20% and plastic viscosity by 18% along the entire horizontal section. ([[2012-geri|Geri, 2012, p. 80]])
- The yield point of the drilling fluid increased from 15 to 21 lb/100ft² (an increase of 40%) over the length of the well. ([[2012-geri|Geri, 2012, p. 80]])
- Gel strength (10-minute) increased by 32.5% and gel strength (10-second) increased by 42.5% along the horizontal section. ([[2012-geri|Geri, 2012, p. 80]])
- The inclusion of sand in filter cake can reach up to 40% by volume, making it difficult to remove with standard acid treatments. ([[2012-geri|Geri, 2012, p. 59]])
- Filter cake thickness increased by 12% per 1000 ft of horizontal length drilled, showing a linear relationship with the distance from the start of the section. ([[2012-geri|Geri, 2012, p. 84]])
- The filter cake contains three main elements: Silicon (Si), Calcium (Ca), and Chlorine (Cl). ([[2012-geri|Geri, 2012, p. 89]])
- XRD analysis identified the primary mineral phases of the filter cake as Quartz and Calcite. ([[2012-geri|Geri, 2012, p. 96]])
- Sand content in the filter cake was found to increase with horizontal length, reaching over 40% in the final sections (8900 ft to 9675 ft). ([[2012-geri|Geri, 2012, p. 98]])
- Drilling fluid density increased linearly with sand weight percentage (R² = 0.8342) following the equation y = 0.0082x + 10.514. ([[2012-geri|Geri, 2012, p. 109]])
- Apparent viscosity, plastic viscosity, and yield point all increased as sand concentration in the drilling fluid increased. ([[2012-geri|Geri, 2012, p. 111]])

## Limitations

CT scanning cannot provide the exact depth of invasion but provides useful information for comparing the effect of sand content on internal invasion.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Evaluate the effectiveness of drill cuttings removing equipment to control sand content.
- Use real core samples instead of ceramic disks for studying actual solids invasion.
