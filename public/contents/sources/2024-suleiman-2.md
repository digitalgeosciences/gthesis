# THE EFFECTS OF ORGANIC ACID ON WATER-GAS CAPILLARY PRESSURE IMPLICATIONS FOR UNDERGROUND GAS STORAGE

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of organic acid (oleic acid) on wettability and capillary pressure in limestone and sandstone rocks to evaluate their suitability for underground gas storage. The study concludes that even low concentrations of organic acid significantly increase hydrophobicity, leading to a substantial reduction in water saturation at specific capillary pressures.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the challenge of accurately modeling gas storage (CO2/H2) in depleted reservoirs where wettability changes due to organic acids or contaminants can lead to inaccurate calculations of residual gas trapping and storage capacity.

## Objectives

- Measure petrophysical properties of Indiana Limestone and Berea Sandstone.
- Assess the influence of varying concentrations of organic acid on rock wettability via contact angle and capillary pressure measurements.
- Investigate the effect of cleaning procedures (methanol, toluene) on sample wettibility.
- Evaluate the impact of organic acid on the reduction of water saturation at specific capillary pressures.
- Investigate the effect of oleic acid on rock wettability and capillary pressure curves.
- Compare the response of carbonate and sandstone rocks to organic acid treatment.

## Methods

The study utilized Indiana Limestone and Berea Sandstone samples. Porosity was measured using a Helium Porosimeter; permeability was measured via Auto-Scan, Nitrogen gas permeameter, and core flooding. Contact angle and capillary pressure were determined using a Drop Shape Analyzer, multi-sample capillary pressure cell (Vinci Technologies), and a centrifuge-based approach. Samples were aged with organic acid concentrations of 0.5, 2, 5, and 10 wt.% in a plastic vacuum desiccator for 4 days at 80°C. TOC was measured using Elementar Soli TOC equipment, and NMR measurements were performed on the Geospec2 Rock Core Analyzer.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized Berea Sandstone (sandstone) and carbonate samples. Measurements included TOC percentages, NMR T2 distributions at 0, 20, and 200 psi, and capillary pressure curves from both porous plate and centrifuge methods.

## Key Findings

- Average porosity for all samples was approximately 19-20%. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 46]])
- Permeability of Berea sandstone ranged from 150-250 mD, while carbonate permeability was between 50-150 mD. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 47]])
- Application of oleic acid resulted in a significant increase in the hydrophobicity of both rock types, leading to faster brine discharge rates. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 57]])
- Carbonate rocks were found to be more affected by wettibility alteration than sandstone due to the interaction between calcite and organic acids. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 58]])
- At a capillary pressure of 20 psi, there was an average reduction in water saturation (Sw) of 19% for sandstone and 50% for carbonate after treatment with organic acid. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 58]])
- Contact angle measurements showed that even minimal concentrations of organic acid (e.g., 0.5 wt.%) significantly increased contact angles, indicating a shift toward oil-wet behavior. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 39]])
- Increasing organic acid concentration had no significant influence on capillary pressure curves, but it significantly influenced contact angles. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 59]])
- TOC results showed low oleic acid adsorption in both carbonate and sandstone regardless of concentration due to the neutral form of oleic acid. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 59]])
- Carbonate rocks showed slightly higher adsorption levels than sandstone due to interactions between calcite and organic acid compared to silica. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 59]])
- At 20 psi, it requires only 20 psi to expel approximately 50% of the brine in carbonate and 20% in sandstone; however, displacement becomes harder beyond this point. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 60]])
- NMR T2 distribution for Berea Sandstone showed a shift to the left at 20 psi, indicating that water in large dominant pores was partially displaced. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 60]])
- In carbonate samples, a capillary pressure of 20 psi was sufficient to displace all brine from macropores (pores > 200 ms T2). ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 61]])
- At 200 psi, approximately half of the discharged water in sandstone is attributed to the expulsion from larger pores at 20 psi, while the other half is from smaller pores (1-7 μm) after 200 psi. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 61]])
- Cleaning samples with methanol and toluene can inadvertently increase water wetness, potentially leading to inaccurate contact angle measurements. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 64]])
- A trace of organic acid may enhance the gas (CO2/H2) storage capacity of sandstone reservoirs by up to 33% and carbonate reservoirs by up to 50%. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 51]])

## Limitations

The study notes that standard cleaning procedures with methanol and toluene can inadvertently alter the wettibility index, potentially leading to inaccurate results in contact angle measurements.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The author suggests that storing gas in depleted hydrocarbon reserves may be more efficient due to the potential for organic acids to enhance storage capacity. They also emphasize the importance of accounting for contact angles when calculating capillary pressure for reservoir simulation.
