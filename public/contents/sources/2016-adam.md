# RELATIVE PERMEABILITY OF SMART WATER FOR CARBONATE RESERVOIRS: EXPERIMENTAL AND MODELING INVESTIGATION

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the petrophysical properties and relative permeability of carbonate reservoir rocks using smart water flooding. The study utilizes CT scanning to evaluate porosity, thin-section petrography to characterize mineralogy and pore connectivity, and core flooding experiments to measure absolute permeability in samples saturated with formation brine and seawater.

---

## Research Problem

There is a lack of understanding regarding the mechanisms behind smart water flooding (low salinity/modified ion) in carbonate rocks, leading to inconsistent results between experimental interpretations and simulation models. Specifically, there is a lack of reliable relative permeability data for history matching these processes.

## Objectives

- Investigate the advantages and demerits of current approaches in experimental and modeling investigation of smart water flooding.
- Perform unsteady state core flooding experiments with specific criteria to emphasize Smart water flooding mechanisms in carbonate reservoir rock.
- Obtain reliable relative permeability of Smart water brines data from designed unsteady state core flooding tests for use as constraints in transport flow simulation.
- Build a simulation model that can generate relative permeability of Smart water based on predetermined parameters from the core flooding experiment by coupling transport flow and geochemical models.
- Evaluate different modeling approaches (e.g., Jerauld et al., Korrani et al.) for representing salt-dependent properties.
- Investigate the impact of specific ions (Ca2+, Mg2+) and pH on wettibility alteration in carbonate systems.

## Methods

The study utilized a series of core flooding experiments on Indiana limestone samples. Core properties (porosity, permeability) were established via steady-state flow tests. Brine desaturation was performed using Ultra-Centrifuge and Porous-Plate methods. Samples were aged with UTMN dead oil at 100°C and 2500 psig for two weeks. Unsteady state core flooding was conducted under conditions of 100°C, 2500 psi overburden, and 1500 psi pore pressure. Effluent analysis included IC-Metrohm (ion chromatography) and ICP-MS to measure cations (Na+, Mg2+, Ca2+) and anions (Cl-, SO42-, HCO3-). Modeling was performed using the PHREEQC software with the IPhreeqc module to couple Buckley-Leverett flow equations with geochemical reaction kinetics.

## Data and Materials

The study involves at least 3 distinct experimental sets (Exp-1, Exp-2, Exp-3) involving the injection of fluids into porous media. Data includes pressure drop measurements (dP), oil recovery rates, and chemical concentrations for six specific ions (Na+, Cl-, Ca2+, Mg2+, SO42-, HCO3-) measured in parts per million (ppm).

## Key Findings

- Smart water flooding can provide an additional oil recovery of up to 30% in laboratory scale by modifying salinity or specific ions. ([[2016-adam|ADAM, 2016, p. 15]])
- IFT reduction is not the main contributor to smart water flooding as it was found that even when pH increased, it did not always correspond with incremental oil production. ([[2016-adam|ADAM, 2016, p. 23]])
- Clay migration/fines mobilization can lead to both direct and indirect mobilization of oil by altering pore paths or acting as surfactants. ([[2016-adam|ADAM, 2016, p. 25]])
- Wettibility alteration is a primary mechanism, potentially triggered by double layer expansion, multi-ion exchange (MIE), or pH-induced desorption. ([[2016-adam|ADAM, 2016, p. 26]])
- Double layer expansion occurs when the concentration of electrolytes in the bulk water solution decreases, leading to an increase in the size of the diffuse layer. ([[2016-adam|ADAM, 2016, p. 28]])
- Multi-ion exchange (MIE) involves free multi-charged cations replacing double layer cations at clay surfaces, potentially making the rock more water-wet. ([[2016-adam|ADAM, 2016, p. 29]])
- Mineral dissolution (e.g., anhydrite) can lead to a shift in NMR results and indicate changes in pore network connectivity. ([[2016-adam|ADAM, 2016, p. 31]])
- Coupling the transport flow with a geochemical model allows for the representation of complex reactions like mineral-aqueous equilibrium during smart water injection. ([[2016-adam|ADAM, 2016, p. 20]])
- The study identifies three distinct experimental setups to compare standard vs. non-standard brine sequences for determining initial water saturation (Swi). ([[2016-adam|ADAM, 2016, p. 57]])
- Core set B and C were validated as similar using the Huppler ordering criterion, yielding F values of 0.025-0.026. ([[2016-adam|ADAM, 2016, p. 56]])
- The study identifies that some mechanisms like ion exchange (MIE) and mineral dissolution are critical for wettibility alteration in carbonate systems. ([[2016-adam|ADAM, 2016, p. 46]])
- A specific threshold model was identified where relative permeability is modeled as a function of salinity, with distinct curves for high and low salinity. ([[2016-adam|ADAM, 2016, p. 39]])
- The study highlights that the presence of certain ions (Ca2+, Mg2+) and pH are key drivers in carbonate-specific smart water flooding benefits. ([[2016-adam|ADAM, 2016, p. 47]])
- Average CT-number of set B is 2470, while average CT-number of set C is 2450, indicating similar porosity states in both limestone core sets. ([[2016-adam|ADAM, 2016, p. 61]])
- Thin section analysis of sample B-3 shows a grain-stone dominated structure with 20-25% pore distribution and good connectivity. ([[2016-adam|ADAM, 2016, p. 62]])

## Limitations

The JBN method assumes that capillary pressure and capillary end effects are negligible; however, specific flow rates were selected to minimize these impacts. The study notes that some discrepancies in relative permeability between experiments (e.g., Exp-3) may be due to variations in the establishment of initial water saturation ($S_{wi}$).

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Standardize coreflooding procedures across all experiments to minimize source errors.
- Implement in-situ saturation monitoring to evaluate capillary end effects.
- Conduct further investigation into complex surface complexation models, mineral kinetics, and local equilibrium in geochemical simulations.
