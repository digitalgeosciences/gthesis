# The Potential Capacity of Carbon Fly Ash Coated with Polyelectrolytes in Removing Cadmium from Water

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the use of carbon fly ash (FA) coated with polyelectrolytes (PDADMAC and PSS) as an adsorbent for removing cadmium (Cd(II)) from contaminated water. The study characterizes three types of adsorbents—raw, acid-treated, and polyelectrolyte-coated—and concludes that the PEFA adsorbent achieves high removal efficiency (~99%) under optimized conditions.

---

## Research Problem

The accumulation of heavy metals like cadmium in water sources poses significant environmental and health risks, requiring effective and cost-effective remediation methods using industrial waste products like fly ash.

## Objectives

- To prepare and characterize raw FA (RFA), acid treated FA and polyelectrolytes coated FA (PDADMAC-PSS FA).
- To demonstrate removal of Cd from contaminated water using polyelectrolytes coated FA under bench-scale condition.
- To study the effect of solution conditions such as pH, contact time, dosage, mixing rate, initial concentration of metal ion, and temperature on the adsorption efficiency.
- To evaluate the adsorption isotherms and kinetics under the optimum treatment conditions.
- Characterize the structural, elemental, and thermal properties of raw (RFA), acid-treated (AFA), and polyelectrolyte-coated (PEFA) fly ash.
- Evaluate the effect of various parameters (pH, contact time, adsorbent dose, mixing rate, initial concentration, temperature, and number of coating layers) on Cd(II) removal.

## Methods

The research involved preparing three types of adsorbents: raw fly ash (RFA), acid-treated fly ash (AFA) using 1M HNO3, and polyelectrolyte-coated fly ash (PEFA) via a layer-by-layer (LBL) assembly technique involving alternating exposure to PDADMAC and PSS. Characterization was performed using FESEM (TSCAN Mira 3), EDX, FTIR (TENSOR 27), XRD (D8-Advance), and TGA (Q600 SDT). Batch adsorption studies were conducted in 100 mL Erlenmeyer flasks to evaluate the effects of pH (4-10), contact time (5-120 min), dosage (1-6 g/L), mixing rate (50-250 rpm), and initial concentration. Analysis was performed using ICP-OES (Perkin-Elmer). Isotherm models (Langmuir, Freundlich, Scatchard) and kinetic models (pseudo-first-order, pseudo-second-order, intra-particle diffusion) were used to evaluate adsorption behavior.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized raw fly ash sourced from a local power plant in the Eastern Province of Saudi Arabia. Samples included RFA, AFA, and PEFA (5 and 20 layers). Batch experiments involved 100 mL flasks with 50 mL Cd(II) solutions at concentrations ranging from 1000-10,000 µg/L.

## Key Findings

- The removal efficiency of cadmium using polyelectrolyte-coated fly ash (PEFA) reached 98.6% under optimized conditions. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 14]])
- Optimum parameters for Cd(II) removal were: 4 g/L adsorbent dose, 15 min contact time, 150 rpm mixing rate, 2 mg/L initial concentration of Cd(II), and 5 layers of PE-coated FA. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 14]])
- Desorption experiments showed 55% and 35% desorption of Cd(II) ions from the 5 and 20 layers PE coated FA, respectively. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 15]])
- Scatchard plot analysis indicated the presence of multiple binding sites on the PEFA surface, confirming a good fit in the Freundlich model. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 15]])
- The dimensionless (RL) and Freundlich constant (n) values indicated a favorable sorption process between the adsorbents and Cd(II) ions. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 15]])
- Raw fly ash (RFA) showed an adsorption capacity for heavy metals like Cu, Cr, and Hg of 1.39 mg/g, 2.92 mg/g, and 2.82 mg/g respectively in previous studies. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 25]])
- Acid treatment (using HNO3) was shown to be an effective method for functionalizing the surface of fly ash to enhance its sorption capability. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 25]])
- The LBL technique allowed for the construction of up to 20 layers of polyelectrolytes (PDADMAC/PSS) on the fly ash surface. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 42]])
- RFA showed a hollow shape with heterogenous pore structures and an average diameter of 100 microns (mesoporous, 2 to 50 nm). ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 58]])
- EDX analysis of RFA showed 71.7% carbon, 16.6% oxygen, and trace amounts of Si, Cu, V, Al, and S. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 62]])
- Acid treatment (AFA) increased carbon content to 91.0% and removed significant heavy metal impurities. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 62]])
- XRF analysis showed RFA had high sulfur (51.44%) but no silicon, while AFA's sulfur increased to 86.25%. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 71]])
- FTIR confirmed the presence of C-S bonds (604 cm-1), C-C bonds (1367 cm-1), and C=O groups in RFA; PEFA showed a distinct C-N peak at 2386 cm-1 indicating polyelectrolyte coating. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 66]])
- XRD confirmed the presence of carbon, sulfur, alpha-quartz (low), beta-quartz (high in AFA/PEFA), and cristobalites. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 67]])
- TGA showed RFA had a 84% weight loss between 470-600°C, while PEFA showed a 97% weight loss between 580-670°C due to gas generation. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 68]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

1. Polyelectrolytes (PDADMAC-PSS) can be used to modify other adsorbents like carbon nanofibers or nanotubes. 2. PEFA should be tested for other pollutants (Pb, Cr, Mn, Cu). 3. Test for multi-pollutants. 4. Include particle size analysis of the modified adsorbent.
