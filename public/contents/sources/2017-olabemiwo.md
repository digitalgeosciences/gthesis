# The Potential Capacity of Carbon Fly Ash Coated with Polyelectrolytes in Removing Cadmium from Water

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the use of carbon fly ash (FA) modified with polyelectrolytes (PE) as an adsorbent to remove cadmium (Cd(II)) from contaminated water. The study evaluates various modification parameters, including coating layers and chemical treatments, concluding that PE-coated fly ash (specifically PEFA5) shows high removal efficiency and favorable thermodynamic properties for heavy metal remediation.

---

## Research Problem

Cadmium is a toxic heavy metal that poses significant risks to human health and the environment, requiring efficient and cost-effective removal methods from water sources. While fly ash (FA) is an available industrial waste with potential for use as an adsorbent, its efficiency can be enhanced through surface modification using polyelectrolytes.

## Objectives

- Modify the surface of fly ash with cationic poly diallyl dimethyl ammonium chloride (PDADMAC) and anionic polystyrene sulfonate (PSS)
- Characterize the physical and chemical properties of raw, acid-treated, and polyelectrolyte-coated fly ash
- Evaluate the effect of various parameters (pH, dosage, time, concentration, mixing rate, temperature, and number of layers) on cadmium removal efficiency
- Determine adsorption isotherm models and kinetic studies for the removal of Cd(II) ions
- To prepare and characterize raw FA (RFA), acid treated FA and polyelectrolytes coated FA (PDADMAC-PSS FA).
- To demonstrate removal of Cd from contaminated water using polyelectrolytes coated FA under bench-scale condition.
- To study the effect of solution conditions such as pH, contact time, dosage, mixing rate, initial concentration of metal ion, and temperature on the adsorption efficiency.
- To evaluate the adsorption isotherms and kinetics under the optimum treatment conditions.
- Modify the surface of raw fly ash (RFA) using acid treatment to remove impurities.
- Enhance the surface charge and removal capacity of fly ash by coating it with polyelectrolytes (PDADMAC and PSS) via Layer-by-Layer (LBL) deposition.
- Characterize the physical, chemical, and mineralogical properties of RFA, AFA, and PEFA.
- Evaluate the adsorption performance of different fly ash types for cadmium removal under varying conditions.
- To modify the surface of fly ash with polyelectrolytes (cationic PDADMAC and anionic PSS) to enhance its adsorption capacity.
- To evaluate the effect of various parameters (pH, contact time, adsorbent dosage, mixing rate, initial concentration, temperature, and number of coating layers) on the removal of Cd(II).
- To determine the thermodynamic properties of the sorption process.
- To model the adsorption kinetics and isotherms for the prepared adsorbents.
- To modify the surface of Fly Ash (FA) with cationic and anionic polyelectrolytes to enhance its adsorption capacity.
- To characterize the structural, elemental, and thermal properties of the polyelectrolyte-coated fly ash (PEFA).
- To evaluate the removal efficiency of Cd(II) ions from aqueous solutions using different types of modified fly ash.

## Methods

The study involved preparing three types of fly ash: Raw Fly Ash (RFA), Acid-treated Fly Ash (AFA), and Polyelectrolyte-coated Fly Ash (PEFA). Characterization was performed using XRD to identify mineral phases, TGA to assess thermal stability/purity, XRF for elemental composition, and FTIR for functional group identification. Batch adsorption experiments were conducted to determine the impact of pH (4-10), contact time (5 min - 2 hrs), adsorbent dosage (1-6 g/L), mixing rate (50-250 rpm), initial concentration (1-10 mg/L), and temperature (273-328 K). Thermodynamic parameters ($\Delta G^o$, $\Delta H^o$, $\Delta S^o$) were calculated using the Van't Hoff equation. Adsorption was modeled using Langmuir, Freundlich, Scatchard, and kinetic models (pseudo-first order, pseudo-second order, and intra-particle diffusion).

## Data and Materials

The study utilizes fly ash (FA) sourced from a local industrial power plant. Samples include raw FA, acid-treated FA, and FA coated with cationic polyelectrolyte PDADMAC and anionic polyelectrolyte PSS. Experimental parameters for cadmium removal include various pH levels, contact times, dosages, mixing rates, initial concentrations of Cd, and temperatures.

## Key Findings

- The removal of Cd(II) ions by RFA, AFA, and PEFA was found to be pH dependent, with the highest removal efficiency achieved at pH 9. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 14]])
- At optimal conditions (pH 9, 4 g/L dose, 15 min contact time, 150 rpm mixing rate, and 2 mg/L initial concentration), the PEFA adsorbent achieved a removal efficiency of 98.6%. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 14]])
- The desorption experiment showed that 55% of Cd(II) ions were desorbed from the 5-layer PEFA, while 35% was desorbed from the 20-layer PEFA. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 15]])
- The Freundlich constant (n) and dimensionless (RL) values indicated a favorable sorption process between the adsorbents and Cd(II) ions. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 15]])
- Scatchard plot analysis confirmed the presence of multiple binding sites on the PEFA surface, supporting the fit to the Freundlich model. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 17]])
- Raw fly ash (RFA) showed a 95% removal efficiency for cadmium at a contact time of 120 minutes, pH 7, and a mixing rate of 150 rpm. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 35]])
- Modified fly ash with complexing agents and alkaline treatment showed up to 97% removal of Cd(II) ions. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 37]])
- Fly ash was able to remove 97% of polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) from wastewater at a dosage of 5 g/L, pH 7, and 25°C. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 37]])
- Polyelectrolyte-coated carbon nanoparticles achieved a removal rate of nearly 99% for various heavy metals including Cd(II). ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 38]])
- The use of polyelectrolytes like poly(styrene sulfonate) and poly(allylamine hydrochloride) on porous supports can yield membranes with high water flux and selective ion transport. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 38]])
- RFA exhibited a hollow shape with heterogenous pore structures and an average diameter of 100 μm (2 to 50 nm pores). ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 48]])
- EDX analysis showed RFA contains 71.7% carbon, 16.6% oxygen, and trace amounts of Si (0.2%), Cu (6.0%), V (0.7%), Al (2.0%), and S (2.8%). ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 50]])
- Acid treatment (AFA) increased carbon content from 71.7% to 91.0% and silicon from 0.2% to 0.4%, while removing heavy metal impurities. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 50]])
- finding_page_note: The text notes that acid treatment removed trace metals like Cu, Cd, Mn, V, and Ni to prevent cross-contamination. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017]])
- Polyelectrolyte coating (PEFA) increased sulfur content from 2.8% in RFA to 5.0% in EDX analysis and up to 92.5% in XRF analysis due to the PSS component. ([[2017-olabemiwo|Olabemiwo, 2017, p. 51]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Polyelectrolytes (PDADMAC-PSS) can be used to modify other adsorbents like carbon nanofibers (CNF) and carbon nanotubes (CNT).
- PEFA should be tested for the removal of other heavy metals such as Pb, Cr, Mn, and Cu.
- PEFA should be tested for the removal of multiple pollutants simultaneously.
- Future studies should include the effect of adsorbent particle size on heavy metal removal.
