# INFLUENCE OF TREATED SEWAGE EFFLUENTS ON SAUDI COAST OF THE ARABIAN GULF FROM WATER QUALITY AND MICROBIAL ASPECTS

**Summary**: The thesis investigated the impact of treated sewage effluents on water quality and microbial characteristics in three coastal outfall locations (Al-Khobar, Dammam, and Rahima) along the Saudi Arabian coast of the Arabian Gulf. It concluded that while all outfalls were significant sources of inorganic nutrients like nitrate and phosphate, only the Dammam site showed significant signs of eutrophication due to higher chlorophyll a concentrations.

---

## Research Problem

The impact of treated sewage effluents on the water quality and microbial safety of coastal waters in the Arabian Gulf, specifically regarding nutrient enrichment and potential health risks from indicator bacteria like E. coli and total Coliforms.

## Objectives

- assess the water quality (nutrients and chlorophyll a) of the sewage outfall locations
- study the microbial diversity in relation to the effect of treated sewage effluents discharge in the coastal waters
- evaluate the risk of pathogenic bacterial contamination in the coastal waters using indicator bacteria
- Assess the influence of sewage effluents on water quality (nutrients, chlorophyll a) in Saudi Arabian coastal waters.
- Evaluate microbial aspects including heterotrophic plate counts and total Col1form counts at various distances from outfall points.
- Determine the relationship between bacterial counts and environmental factors like distance from discharge and nutrient levels.
- Investigate the influence of sewage effluents on water quality and microbial aspects of Saudi Arabian coastal waters.
- Analyze physico-chemical characteristics of effluent samples at three outfall locations and a control site (Half Moon Bay).
- Assess the concentration of nutrients, chlorophyll a, and bacterial counts in the vicinity of discharge points.
- To analyze nutrient concentrations (NO3, NO2, NH4, PO4) in surface waters at different outfall sites.
- To measure chlorophyll a concentrations as an indicator of primary productivity.
- To assess microbial aspects including Total Coliform counts and Heterotrophic plate counts across the study area.
- To compare water quality parameters between three treatment plant outfalls (Al-Khobar, Dammam, Rahima) and a control site (Half Moon Bay).

## Methods

In-situ measurements of temperature, pH, dissolved oxygen (DO), and salinity were conducted using specialized hand-held instruments. BOD was determined via a 5-day test; COD was measured using the titrimetric standard method. Nutrient concentrations (nitrate, nitrite, phosphate, ammonia) and silicate were analyzed using the Skalar San++ auto-analyzer. Chlorophyll a was quantified using a Turner Designs Trilogy fluorometer after extraction with acetone. Microbial analysis included heterotrophic plate counts on nutrient agar and total Coliform enumeration on Levine EMB agar. Bacterial identification of 50 isolates was performed using API 20 E and API 20 NE strips.

## Data and Materials

Water samples collected from 4 locations (3 outfall sites + 1 control site). Data included concentrations of nitrate (0.30-29.12 µmol/L), nitrite (0.17-10.66 µmol/L), ammonia (0.51-1.15 µmol/L), phosphate (0.35-25.00 µmol/L), and chlorophyll a. Bacterial counts ranged from 4,000 to 310,000 CFU/100ml for Total Coliforms.

## Key Findings

- All three outfalls were found to serve as important sources of inorganic nutrient into the coastal waters. ([[2015-yusuf|Yusuf, 2015, p. 14]])
- Significantly higher concentrations of nitrate and nitrite in Dammam and Rahima; ammonia in Al-Khobar and Dammam; and phosphate in all three outfall locations were recorded relative to the control. ([[2015-yusuf|Yusuf, 2015, p. 14]])
- Mean nitrate, nitrite, ammonia, and phosphate concentrations in sampling stations ranged from 0.30 to 29.12 µmol/L, 0.17 to 10.66 µmol/L, 0.51 to 1.15 µmol/L, and 0.35 to 25.00 µmol/L respectively. ([[2015-yusuf|Yusuf, 2015, p. 14]])
- Phosphate concentrations ranged from 0.62 to 64.76 µg/L. ([[2015-yusuf|Yusuf, 2015, p. 14]])
- Chlorophyll a concentrations were significantly high only in Dammam at levels indicative of poor water quality. ([[2015-yusuf|Yusuf, 2015, p. 14]])
- Total Coliform counts in sampling stations ranged from 4000 to 310000 CFU/100ml while heterotrophic plate counts ranged from 70 to 210000 CFU/ml. ([[2015-yusuf|Yusuf, 2015, p. 14]])
- The lowest bacteria counts in effluent samples were recorded in Rahima. ([[2015-yusuf|Yusuf, 2015, p. 14]])
- Microbiological quality of stations close to the outfalls is generally not safe for activities such as recreation and shell-fish harvesting based on international standards. ([[2015-yusuf|Yusuf, 2015, p. 14]])
- Average dissolved oxygen content in outfall locations was lower than the control site (Al-Khobar: 6.38±0.71 mg/L; Dammam: 4.51±0.81 mg/L; Rahima: 6.20±0.89 mg/L) compared to Half Moon Bay (7.74±0.71 mg/L). ([[2015-yusuf|Yusuf, 2015, p. 35]])
- Nutrient concentrations in outfall areas were significantly higher than control; specifically, nitrate ranged from 2.1-29.05 µmol/L and phosphate from 18.29-36.08 µmol/L. ([[2015-yusuf|Yusuf, 2015, p. 36]])
- Chlorophyll a concentrations were highest at the Dammam outfall (mean 35.04 µg/L) compared to Al-Khobar (1.39 µg/L) and Rahima (0.24 µg/L). ([[2015-yusuf|Yusuf, 2015, p. 39]])
- The study identified eight bacterial species: E. coli, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Vibrio cholera, Aeromonas hydrophila, Salmonella typhi, Shigella spp., Campylobacter jejuni, and Yersinia enterocolitica. ([[2015-yusuf|Yusuf, 2015]])

- Effluent samples from all three outfall locations were identified as significant sources of inorganic nutrients, specifically nitrate, nitrite, and phosphate, into the coastal waters. ([[2015-yusuf|Yusuf, 2015, p. 47]])
- Mean phosphate concentrations in effluent samples exceeded the PME prescribed limit of 10 µmol/L. ([[2015-yusuf|Yusuf, 2015, p. 47]])

## Limitations

The study could not sample effluent from the Al-khobar outlet because it was submerged under the tide and immediately mixed with seawater.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Adopt effective tertiary treatment (similar to Rahima) in all STPs to control microbial pollution.
- Prioritize regular maintenance of existing treatment plants to ensure full utilization of capacity for removing organic materials.
- Implement sewage treatment technologies that specifically reduce inorganic nutrient release.
- Establish regular monitoring of trophic state and pathogenic contamination levels of coastal waters.
- Control shellfish harvesting and recreational activities in areas near sewage outfalls to mitigate public health risks.
