# ECOTOXICITY OF ARABIAN LIGHT CRUDE OIL AND OIL BASED DRILLING MUD ON THE LOCAL ARABIAN KILLIFISH (Aphanius dispar) AND COMMERCIAL BRINE SHRIMP (Artemia sp.)

**Summary**: The thesis investigated the ecotoxicity of Arabian Light crude oil and its associated components (water-soluble fraction, solid phase) as well as oil-based drilling mud on two aquatic species: the local Arabian Killifish (Aphanius dispar) and commercial Brine Shrimp (Artemia sp.). The study concluded that while the local fish showed high tolerance to certain fractions due to low solubility of PAHs in seawater, the brine shrimp provided a more sensitive and feasible indicator for short-term toxicity testing.

---

## Research Problem

Oil spills and drilling activities in the Arabian Gulf pose significant risks to marine life. There is a need to identify suitable local and commercial bio-indicators to assess the acute toxicity of Arabian Light crude oil and oil-based mud (OBM) components, specifically focusing on the impact of polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs).

## Objectives

- Evaluate the feasibility of using the local Arabian Killifish (Aphanius dispar) in acute toxicity (LC50) studies using Arabian light crude oil and oil-based drilling mud.
- Evaluate the feasibility of using the commercial Brine Shrimp (Artemia sp.) in sublethal toxicity (EC50) studies using Arabian light crude oil and oil-based drilling mud.
- Evaluate the toxicity of the water-soluble fraction (WSF) and solid phase (SPP) of oil-based mud on commercial Brine Shrimp (Artemia sp.).
- Determine the cyst hatching success (EC50) of Brine Shrimp exposed to WSF of Arabian Light crude oil and SPP of oil-based mud.
- Quantify the concentration of various PAHs in the different oil and drilling mud fractions.

## Methods

The study utilized two marine organisms: local Arabian Killifish (Aphanius dispar) collected from Tarut Bay and commercial Brine Shrimp (Artemia sp.). Toxicity tests were conducted in a controlled growth chamber at 21°C. LC50 values for the fish were determined over a 96-hour exposure period, while LC50 and EC50 values for the brine shrimp were determined over a 48-hour period. The toxicants tested included Arabian Light Crude Oil (ALCO), its Water Soluble Fraction (WSF), oil-based mud (OBM) in both Suspended Particulate Phase (SPP) and Solid Phase (SP), and an oil dispersant (Surfatron). Chemical analysis of the samples was performed using GC-MS (Agilent Technologies 6890N) to determine PAH concentrations. Data analysis for LC50/EC50 values was conducted using SPSS by fitting two-parameter log-logistic functions.

## Data and Materials

The study analyzed Arabian Light crude oil, its water-soluble fraction (WSF), oil-based mud (OBM), and the solid phase of oil-based mud (SPP). Samples were tested against 10 individuals of Aphanius dispar per aquarium in triplicate for fish tests. Brine shrimp tests used 20 nauplii per well in multi-well plates.

## Key Findings

- The LC50 of Arabian Killifish (Aphanius dispar) exposed to CuSO4 as a positive control was 29.65 mgL-1. ([[2018-amiruddin|Amiruddin, 2018, p. 51]])
- The LC50 of local species Arabian killifish (Aphanius dispar) exposed to Arabian Light crude oil plus the oil dispersant Surfatron for 96 hours is 164.19 mgL-1. ([[2018-amiruddin|Amiruddin, 2018, p. 52]])
- The LC50 of Arabian Killifish (Aphanius dispar) exposed to the Solid Phase (SP) of oil-based mud for 96 hours is 137,565.4 mgL-1. ([[2018-amiruddin|Amiruddin, 2018, p. 55]])
- No mortality was observed in Arabian Killifish (Aphanius dispar) when exposed to Water Soluble Fraction (WSF) or Suspended Particulate Phase (SPP) of oil-based mud for 96 hours. ([[2018-amiruddin|Amiruddin, 2018, p. 56]])
- The LC50 of commercial Brine Shrimp (Artemia sp.) exposed to CuSO4 as a positive control is 10.23 mgL-1. ([[2018-amiruddin|Amiruddin, 2018, p. 58]])
- The LC50 of commercial Brine Shrimp (Artemia sp.) exposed to the Suspended Particulate Phase (SPP) of oil-based mud for 48 hours is 36.8%. ([[2018-amiruddin|Amiruddin, 2018, p. 59]])
- The concentration of total PAHs in WSF of Arabian light crude oil was 39.55 ng/ml and in SPP of oil-based mud was 345.58 ng/ml. ([[2018-amiruddin|Amiruddin, 2018, p. 17]])
- The concentration of total PAHs in Arabian Light crude oil was 27377.7 ng/ml, while it was only 39.55 ng/ml in the water-soluble fraction (WSF). ([[2018-amiruddin|Amiruddin, 2018, p. 51]])
- The concentration of total PAHs in the solid phase of oil-based mud (SPP) was 345.58 ng/ml. ([[2018-amiruddin|Amiruddin, 2018, p. 51]])
- The EC50 of the cysts of the commercial Brine Shrimp (Artemia sp.) exposed to the WSF of Arabian Light crude oil for 48 hours is 5.95%. ([[2018-amiruddin|Amiruddin, 2018, p. 54]])
- Methyl-Naphthalene had the highest concentration among other PAHs in both the WSF and SPP of oil-based mud. ([[2018-amiruddin|Amiruddin, 2018, p. 57]])

## Limitations

The study noted that LC50 values for some tests (e.g., Brine Shrimp exposed to WSF) could not be calculated because mortality exceeded 100% in the initial trials, and for others, no mortality occurred due to low PAH solubility in artificial seawater.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study concludes that Arabian Killifish (Aphanius dispar) can be used for short-term (96 h) toxicity tests using the Arabian Light Crude Oil plus the dispersant and the SP of oil-based mud. Brine Shrimp (Artemia sp.) are more feasible for short-term toxicity tests on Arabian Light crude oil and oil-based mud.
