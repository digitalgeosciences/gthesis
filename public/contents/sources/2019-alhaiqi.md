# GREEN REMEDIATION OF HYDROCARBON CONTAMINATED SOILS BY PHYTO-TECHNIQUES

**Summary**: The thesis investigated the ability of two plant species, Prosopis juliflora and Conocarpus erectus, to remediate sandy and limy soils contaminated with polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) including Phenanthrene, Pyrene, and Fluoranthene. The study concluded that both plants accelerated PAH dissipation, with Prosopis juliflora showing a higher capacity for absorption compared to Conocarpus erectus.

---

## Research Problem

The accumulation of toxic, carcinogenic, and mutagenic polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) in soil and groundwater poses significant environmental risks, especially in Saudi Arabia where groundwater is a primary water source. There is a need for cost-effective, green remediation techniques like phytoremediation to address these contaminants.

## Objectives

- To evaluate the ability of a green and environment-friendly approach to clean contaminated sites instead of conventional remediation methods.
- To determine which type of phytoremediation mechanism is more feasible to remediate petroleum hydrocarbons.
- Evaluate the ability of Prosopis juliflora and Conocarpus erectus to remediate hydrocarbons-polluted soils.
- Compare the remediation efficiency of these two plants across different soil types (sandy and limy).
- Assess the accumulation of PAHs in the roots, stems, and leaves of the selected plant species.

## Methods

The study used two types of soil (Sandy from KFUPM beach and Limy from KFUPM campus) spiked with three PAH compounds (Phenanthrene, Fluoranthene, and Pyrene) at three concentration levels (0.5, 1, and 2 ppm). Two plant species, Prosopis juliflora and Conocarpus erectus, were grown in these soils for 90 days. Soil samples were analyzed using XRF to determine mineral content. PAH extraction from soil and plant tissues was performed via sonication in a dichloromethane/hexane (1:1) mixture. The extracts were then analyzed using a Thermo Scientific Trace GC Ultra Gas Chromatography/Mass Spectroscopy (GC/MS) system with a J&W Fused Silica Capillary Column.

## Data and Materials

12 plants total (6 per species) were used in each experimental setup. Soil types included sandy and limy. PAH concentrations tested: Low (~2,400 µg/kg), Medium (~3,300 µg/kg), and High (~5,000-6,000 µg/kg). Sampling intervals: 10, 20, 30, 40, 70, and 90 days.

## Key Findings

- Both plants (Prosopis juliflora and Conocarpus erectus) accelerated the dissipation of Phenanthrene, Pyrene, and Fluoranthene from both sandy and limy soils. ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 14]])
- In sandy soil, a residual of less than 1% (99% removal) of Phenanthrene was observed for both plants at all concentration levels after 30 days. ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 39]])
- For Fluoranthene in sandy soils, the residual was less than 1% for all three concentration levels across both plant types. ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 15]])
- In limy soils, the residual of Fluoranthene was less than 15% (low), 10% (medium/high) for Prosopis, and less than 11% (low/medium), 4% (high) for Conocarpus. ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 15]])
- A residual of 3% Pyrene was detected in sandy soils for all three concentration levels and both plants. ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 15]])
- Accumulation of these compounds by the plants was higher in the roots than in the stems or leaves. ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 15]])
- Prosopis Juliflora showed a greater ability to absorb these chemicals from soil compared to Conocarpus Erectus. ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 15]])
- The primary mechanism for remediation in both plants was likely phytodegradation. ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 16]])
- Most removal of PAHs was observed within the first 30 days of the experiment. ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 67]])

## Limitations

The study used metallic containers without pores, which may have limited the interaction with the soil environment compared to real plots. The results are based on a specific set of three PAH compounds and two plant species.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Test different concentrations of PAHs.
- Evaluate phytoremediation potential for petroleum hydrocarbons and heavy metals.
- Evaluate the effect of microorganisms on the dissipation of PAHs.
- Use different cultivation methods, such as real plots or porous containers.
- Study the lipid content of the plants.
