# GREEN REMEDIATION OF HYDROCARBON CONTAMINATED SOILS BY PHYTO-TECHNIQUES

**Summary**: The thesis investigated the ability of two plant species, Prosopis juliflora and Conocarpus erectus, to remediate hydrocarbon-polluted soils (specifically PAHs like Phenanthrene, Pyrene, and Fluoranthene) in both sandy and limy soil types. The study concluded that both plants accelerated PAH degradation, with Prosopies juliflora showing a higher capacity for absorption compared to Conocarpus.

---

## Research Problem

The accumulation of persistent organic pollutants like Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons (PAHs) in soil and groundwater poses significant environmental risks to human health and ecosystems. Traditional remediation methods are often costly or energy-intensive, necessitating the development of cost-effective, green alternatives such as phytoremediation using hardy species suitable for arid regions.

## Objectives

- Demonstrate the ability of Prosopis juliflora and Conocarpus erectus to remediate hydrocarbons-polluted soils.
- Evaluate the removal and degradation of specific PAHs (Phenanthrene, Fluoranthene, and Pyrene) from sandy and limy soil types.
- Assess the accumulation of these compounds within the plant tissues (roots, stems, and leaves).
- To evaluate the ability of a green and environment-friendly approach to clean contaminated sites instead of conventional remediation methods.
- To determine which type of phytoremediation mechanism is more feasible to remediate petroleum hydrocarbons.
- Evaluate the remediation efficiency of Prosopis juliflora and Conocarpus erectus for three PAH compounds.
- Compare the performance of these plants across two distinct soil types: Sandy and Limy.
- Quantify the accumulation of PAHs in plant roots over a 90-day period.
- Investigate and evaluate the ability of Prosopis juliflora and Conocarpus erectus to remediate sandy and limy soils contaminated by PAHs (Phenanthrene, Pyrene, and Fluoranthene).
- Evaluate the concentration of these compounds in different parts of the plants (roots, stems, and leaves) over a period of 90 days.
- Demonstrate the ability of Prosopis juliflora to remediate hydrocarbon-polluted soils.
- Demonstrate the ability of Conocarpus erectus to remediate hydrocarbon-polluted soils.
- Evaluate the removal efficiency of phenanthrene, fluoranthene, and pyrene in different soil types (sandy and limy).

## Methods

The study utilized two soil types (Sandy from KFUPM beach and Limy from KFUPM campus) characterized via XRF. Soils were spiked with Phenanthrene, Fluoranthene, and Pyrene at three concentration levels (0.5, 1, and 2 ppm). Plants (Prosopis juliflora and Conocarpus erectus) were grown in these soils for 90 days. Soil samples were extracted using an ultrasonic bath (EPA3550C method) with a dichloromethane/hexane solvent mix. Plant tissues were harvested at 30, 60, and 90 days, separated into roots, stems, and leaves, and processed via ultrasonication and extraction before analysis.

## Data and Materials

The study involved two plant species: Prosopis juliflora and Conocarpus erectus. Two soil types were used: sandy soil and limy soil. Three specific PAH compounds were monitored: Phenanthrene, Fluoranthene, and Pyrene. The experiment included multiple concentration levels (low, medium, high) for the contaminants.

## Key Findings

- Both plants (Prosopis juliflora and Conocarpus erectus) accelerated the dissipation of Phenanthrene, Pyrene, and Fluoranthene from both soil types. ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 14]])
- Most of the dissipation was observed in samples collected after 30 days compared to those at 60 and 90 days. ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 15]])
- Residual phenanthrene was less than 1% and 4% in sandy soil after 30 days for Prosopis and Conocarpus respectively, and less than 5% in limy soils for both plants. ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 15]])
- In limy soils, the residual of fluoranthene was less than 15% (low), 10% (medium/high) for Prosopis, and less than 11% (low/medium), 4% (high) for Conocarpus. ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 15]])
- Residual pyrene in sandy soils was less than 3% for all three levels and both plants after 30 days. ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 15]])
- In limy soils, the residual of Pyrene was less than 19% (low) and 11% (medium/high) for Prosopis; less than 15% (low/medium) and 6% (high) for Conocarpus. ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 15]])
- Accumulation of these compounds was higher in the roots than in the stems or leaves. ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 15]])
- Prosopis Juliflora showed a greater ability to absorb these chemicals from soil compared to Conocarpus Erectus. ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 15]])
- Conocarpus was found to have high concentrations of hydrocarbons in its leaves (4349-1754 mg/kg) compared to stems (589-671 mg/kg) and soil (878-126 mg/kg). ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 23]])
- Prosopis juliflora showed a reduction of 26% of TPH in its rhizosphere soil compared to other plants. ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 24]])
- The study identified that the optimal range for Log Kow (octanol-water partition coefficient) for organic petroleum hydrocarbons to be transpired by plants is between 0.5 and 3.0. ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 20]])
- Phenanthrene removal from sandy soil by both P. juliflora and C. erectus reached >99% at all concentration levels after 30, 60, and 90 days. ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 56]])
- Pyrene removal from sandy soil exceeded 97% for both plant species across all initial concentration levels by the end of the study. ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 61]])
- Fluoranthene removal from limy soil reached >90% in P. juliflora and >99% in C. erectus at high initial concentrations after 390 days. ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 67]])
- The highest accumulation of Pyrene in roots was observed in Prosopis plants contaminated with high initial concentration, reaching over 440 µg/kg after 90 days. ([[2019-alhaiqi|Alhaiqi, 2019, p. 62]])

## Limitations

The study noted that slow plant growth was attributed to nutrient shortages and limited irrigation rather than contaminant toxicity. Differences in results between certain samples were potentially due to variations in rhizosphere soil volume or individual plant characteristics.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Try different concentrations of PAHs.
- Evaluate phytoremediation potential for Petroleum hydrocarbons and Heavy metals.
- Evaluate the effect of microorganisms on the dissipation of PAHs.
- Use different cultivation methods, such as using different containers or real plots instead of metallic ones without pores.
- Study lipid content in these plants.
