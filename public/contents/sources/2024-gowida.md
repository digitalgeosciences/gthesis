# AQUEOUS-BASED FOAM FLUIDS FOR UNDERBALANCED DRILLING APPLICATIONS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the development and characterization of an eco-friendly aqueous-based foam system using Ammonium Alcohol Ether Sulfate (AAES) for underbalanced drilling (UBD). It evaluates the impact of pressure, temperature, polymer additives (PAC, CMC, X-gum), and clay solids (Saudi bentonite) on foam stability, viscosity, and hydrocarbon tolerance.

---

## Research Problem

Existing drilling foams often lack sufficient stability and compatibility in the harsh downhole conditions of underbalanced drilling (UBD), specifically regarding high-salinity brine, alkaline pH levels, and hydrocarbon contamination.

## Objectives

- Screen a set of foaming agents to evaluate their compatibility with high saline water and mildly alkaline environments.
- Perform full characterization of bulk foam properties at ambient and elevated pressure/temperature conditions for selected surfactants.
- Evaluate the impact of additives (polymers and local Saudi clay solids) on foam stability, rheology, and hydrocarbon tolerance.
- Compare the performance of developed formulations against a commercial drilling foam blend.
- Introduce a new eco-friendly foaming agent, Ammonium Alcohol Ether Sulfate (AAES), for UBD applications.
- Evaluate the impact of gas pressure and temperature on AA1S foam stability and viscosity.

## Methods

The study utilized a DFA 100 setup for foam characterization, including surface tension measurements using an Attension Theta Optical Tensiometer. Foam stability was assessed by measuring half-life and volume retention under varying pressures (14.7 to 1000 psi) and temperatures (25 to 90°C). Rheological properties, specifically apparent viscosity and yield point, were measured across a range of shear rates (100 to 1000 s^-1). The impact of additives was tested by comparing AAES-based foams with and without PAC, CMC, X-gum, and various clay solids (bentonite, vermiculite, perlite). Hydrocarbon tolerance was evaluated by measuring foam drainage rates in the presence of oil.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized an AAES surfactant system. Experimental conditions included pressures of 14.7 psi, 500 psi, and 1000 psi; temperatures ranging from 25°C to 90°C; and various additives (PAC, CMC, X-gum, bentonite, vermiculite, perlite). Measurement parameters included foam half-life, bubble density (bubbles/mm^2), mean bubble size (µm), and viscosity (cP).

## Key Findings

- Ammonium Alcohol Ether Sulfate (AAES) was identified as the only surfactant that remained clear and stable in both synthetic seawater and mildly alkaline environments (pH 9.5). ([[2024-gowida|Gowida, 2024, p. 38]])
- Gas pressure variations significantly impacted foam stability and viscosity up to 60°C, with temperature becoming the dominant factor beyond this threshold. ([[2024-gowida|Gowida, 2024, p. 17]])
- Polymers (PAC, CMC, Xanthan gum) significantly enhanced foam stability and viscosity, with PAC providing the most significant improvement. ([[2024-gowida|Gowida, 2024, p. 15]])
- Saudi bentonite clay solids improved both foam stability and viscosity. ([[2024-gowida|Gowida, 2024, p. 15]])
- A blend of PAC and Saudi bentonite outperformed commercial blends, yielding higher viscosity, stability, and yield point. ([[2024-gowida|Gowida, 2024, p. 15]])
- The inclusion of PAC or bentonite increased foam resistance to hydrocarbon contamination. ([[2024-gowida|Gowida, 2024, p. 15]])
- Synthetic seawater (SW) was prepared with a total salinity of 67.70 g/L, including Na2SO4, NaHCO3, CaCl2·2H2O, MgCl2·6H2O, and NaCl. ([[2024-gowida|Gowida, 2024, p. 32]])
- The required foam quality (FQ) for stable systems is typically between 52% and 95%. ([[2024-gowida|Gowida, 2024, p. 16]])
- AAES surfactant showed optimal stability at a concentration of 0.5 wt.%, where surface tension was significantly reduced without the excessive formation of micelles. ([[2024-gowida|Gowida, 2024, p. 60]])
- Increasing pressure from 14.7 psi to 1000 psi improved foam stability, with half-life increasing from 97 minutes at 14.7 psi to over 350 minutes at 1000 psi. ([[2024-gowida|Gowida, 2024, p. 63]])
- Higher pressure (1000 psi) led to a reduction in the amount of gas required for full foam height by 20% compared to 14.7 psi, and increased initial bubble density from 35 to 80 bubbles/mm^2. ([[2024-gowida|Gowida, 2024, p. 65]])
- At high pressures (500 and 1000 psi), foam structure showed greater uniformity and reduced bubble coarsening compared to low pressure (14.7 psi). ([[2024-gowida|Gowida, 2024, p. 66]])
- Temperature above 60°C significantly decreased foam stability, with half-life dropping from over 300 minutes at 60°C to 25 minutes at 90°C. ([[2024-gowida|Gowida, 2024, p. 73]])
- The addition of PAC resulted in a significant increase in viscosity (up to 238% at high shear rates) and a threefold increase in foam half-life compared to the base AAES foam. ([[2024-gowida|Gowida, 2024, p. 86]])
- Bentonite successfully enhanced foam stability, with some concentrations providing over 14 times more stability than the base foam. ([[2024-gowida|Gowida, 2024, p. 91]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Investigate the use of weighted foams for broader pressure ranges.
- Study combined effects of different surfactants to optimize foam bulk properties.
- Explore influence of formation types (e.g., drilled cuttings) on foam stability.
- Investigate corrosion inhibitors' effect on foaming properties.
- Conduct simulation-based evaluations in simulated drilling environments.
