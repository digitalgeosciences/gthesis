# Quantitative Assessment of Disinfection By-products Formed by Chlorine, Chlorine Dioxide and Ozone in Saudi Arabia Drinking Water

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the formation of disinfection by-products (DBPs) such as trihalomethanes, chlorite, and bromate in Saudi Arabian drinking water. It evaluates how different disinfectants (chlorine, chlorine dioxide, and ozone) interact with blended desalinated and raw groundwater under varying dosages, temperatures, and reaction times.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the challenge of managing hazardous disinfection by-products (DBPs) that form during the treatment of blended water (desalinated seawater mixed with raw groundwater) in Saudi Arabia. Specifically, it evaluates how different disinfectants (chlorine, chlorine dioxide, and ozone) and varying operational conditions (dosage, temperature, contact time) affect the concentration of TTHMs, chlorite, and bromate.

## Objectives

- Quantify Trihalomethanes (THMs) formation using chlorine as a disinfectant under varying conditions.
- Assess Chlorite formation resulting from chlorine dioxide disinfection.
- Evaluate Bromate formation during the ozonation process.
- Investigate the impact of blending ratios, disinfectant dosages, temperature, and reaction time on DBP concentrations.
- Investigate the effect of different disinfectants (chlorine, chlorine dioxide, and ozone) on desalinated water blended with raw groundwater at different ratios.
- Quantify the formation of trihalomethanes (THMs) during chlorination.
- Assess the formation of chlorite and chlorate during chlorine dioxide treatment.
- Evaluate bromate formation during ozonation of bromide-containing waters.
- Study the effect of source water quality on the formation of by-products.
- Study the effect of blending ratios on the levels of disinfection by-products.
- Determine the optimum disinfection condition to achieve the minimum DBP.
- Investigate the fate of DBPs formed when using three disinfectants (Cl2, ClO2, and O3) in blended water.
- Quantify by-products under different treatment conditions: disinfectant dosage, temperature, and reaction time.
- Investigate the correlation between the type of by-products formed and the quality of the source water (desalinated seawater and groundwater).
- To evaluate the effect of different disinfectants on the quality of blended water.
- To quantify the formation of disinfection by-products from chlorination, chlorine dioxide, and ozonation.

## Methods

The study involved three parts corresponding to the three disinfectants used. Analytical techniques included Ion Chromatography (IC) for measuring chlorite, Bromide determination, and potentially other ions; Gas Chromatography (GC) for quantifying trihalomethanes (TTHMs); and UV-Vis Spectroscopy for monitoring chemical concentrations. The experiments were conducted on blended water samples with varying ratios (1:2, 1:5, and 1:10). Parameters such as temperature (25°C, 35°C, and 45°C) and contact times (5, 10, and 20 minutes for ozone; 24 and 48 hours for chlorine/chlorine dioxide) were varied to assess their impact on DBP formation.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized blended water samples consisting of desalinated seawater and raw groundwater at ratios of 1:2, 1:5, and 1:10 (v/v). Three disinfectants were tested: Chlorine (Cl2), Chlorine Dioxide (ClO2), and Ozone (O3) at dosages ranging from 1 to 5 ppm. Analytical data included TTHM concentrations (Chloroform, Dibromochloromethane, Dibromochloromethane, Bromoform), Chlorite levels, Bromate levels, and Bromide content.

## Key Findings

- Trihalomethanes (TTHMs) formation increased from 0 to 247 ppb as chlorine dosage increased from 1 to 5 ppm. ([[2011-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2011, p. 9]])
- The blending ratio of 1:10 (raw: desalinated) showed the lowest TTHMs formation at all tested chlorine dosages (1, 2, and 5 ppm). ([[2011-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2011, p. 9]])
- TTHM formation increased with both increasing reaction time and higher temperatures. ([[2011-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2011, p. 9]])
- Chlorite levels were not detected at a blending ratio of 1:10 during chlorine dioxide disinfection. ([[2011-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2011, p. 9]])
- Bromate formation increased with ozone dosages from 2 to 5 ppm, temperature increases from 25°C to 45°C, and reaction times from 5 to 20 minutes. ([[2011-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2011, p. 9]])
- Significant high levels of bromate were observed in water samples with a blending ratio of 1:2. ([[2011-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2011, p. 9]])
- Bromate is a known class 2B carcinogen, and its formation during ozonation of bromide-containing water is a significant concern in Saudi Arabia due to high bromide content in groundwater. ([[2011-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2011, p. 21]])
- The USEPA recommends that the combined residuals of chlorite, chlorate, and chlorine dioxide not exceed 1 mg/l in distribution systems. ([[2011-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2011, p. 20]])
- Bromide concentration can lead to a shift in trihalomethane speciation from predominantly chloroform at low bromide levels to bromoform at high bromide concentrations (e.g., >70 μg/l). ([[2011-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2011, p. 27]])
- In the presence of bromide, total trihalomethane concentrations can nearly double as bromide concentrations increase from <70 μg/l to approximately 2,000 μg/l. ([[2011-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2011, p. 28]])
- Bromate formation during ozonation of water with as low as 50 μg/l of bromide can produce bromate at concentrations exceeding regulatory thresholds. ([[2011-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2011, p. 33]])
- Chlorine dioxide treatment results in the conversion of up to 60% of the applied ClO2 dose into chlorite ion and up to 10% into chlorate ion. ([[2011-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2011, p. 31]])
- Bromoform (CHBr3) and dibromochloromethane (CHBr2Cl) were the dominant TTHM components in all chlorination samples, with bromoform exceeding 80% of total TTHMs due to high bromide levels in brackish groundwater. ([[2011-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2011, p. 43]])
- TTHM levels remained below the maximum allowable limit of 80 ppb when using a 1:10 blending ratio at chlorine dosages of 1, 2, and 5 ppm at 25°C. ([[2011-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2011, p. 46]])
- Temperature significantly increased TTHM formation; for example, levels rose from ~29 ppb to 138.5 ppb when temperature increased from 25°C to 45°C in a 1:10 blend. ([[2011-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2011, p. 46]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

Lower ozone doses (e.g., 2 ppm) are recommended to keep bromate concentrations within drinking water standards; a 1:10 blending ratio is identified as ideal for controlling TTHM formation during chlorination.
