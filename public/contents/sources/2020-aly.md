# RADIAL ACIDIZING OF CARBONATE BLOCKS WITH EMULSIFIED ACID

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the relationship between linear and radial acidizing of carbonate blocks using a newly developed Pickering emulsified acid system. It evaluates wormhole initiation, propagation, and breakthrough in local Saudi limestone rocks to establish predictive models for radial behavior based on linear coreflood data.

---

## Research Problem

Linear (1D) acidizing experiments are common but do not accurately represent the 3D radial flow environment of actual reservoirs; furthermore, most existing studies use Indiana limestone and non-retarded acid systems rather than local Saudi carbonate rocks with emulsified acids.

## Objectives

- Develop a stable Pickering emulsified acid system using nano-clays as the emulsifying agent.
- To study the acidizing of linear and radial geometries and analyze the wormhole structures qualitatively and quantitatively.
- To establish a relation between the linear and radial acidizing geometries at the same conditions.
- To compare the wormhole development behavior for emulsified acid at low and high temperatures and develop a relation between them to be able to predict the radial behavior at high temperatures from linear experiments.
- Evaluate the effect of corrosion inhibitors on the stability and safety of the emulsified acid.
- Characterize the physical properties (rheology, morphology, particle size) of the developed emulsified acid.

## Methods

The study involved developing an emulsified acid (15%wt. HCl, 70:30 acid-to-diesel ratio) using nano-clays (NC-1). Stability was tested at 90°C. Corrosion tests were performed on steel coupons in a pressure cell for 6 hours at 90°C. Rheology was measured using an Anton-Paar MCR 302 HPHT rheometer and fitted to the Carreau-Yasuda model. Morphology and particle size were analyzed via microscopy and Fritsch Wet Dispersion Unit. Radial acidizing was performed on three limestone blocks (ASW-001, ASW-002, ASW-003) using a poly-axial stress frame at SDCR. Linear coreflooding was conducted at KFUPM. CT scanning (Toshiba Alexion TSX-032A and high-energy scanners at BAM) and digital rock analysis (PerGeos®) were used to model wormhole networks.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized carbonate rock samples from five Jurassic formations (Dhruma, Tuwaiq Mountain, Hanifa, Jubaila, or Arab). Core permeability ranged between 8 and 19 mD (max 28.6 mD) with an average porosity of 13.7%. Three sets of linear experiments were conducted for plain HCl, Low Temperature (LT) EA, and High Temperature (HT) EA.

## Key Findings

- A stable emulsified acid system was developed using 15%wt. HCl in an acid-to-diesel ratio of 70:30 with a nano-clay emulsifier. ([[2020-aly|Aly, 2020, p. 22]])
- The study established that linear corefloods are not representative of the actual reservoir case where flow is radial around the wellbore, as wormhole initiation and propagation differ between systems. ([[2020-aly|Aly, 2020, p. 63]])
- High-energy CT scanning provided an accuracy of up to 0.29mm for visualizing wormhole structures in large blocks compared to standard scanners. ([[2020-aly|Aly, 2020, p. 56]])
- Previous studies showed that using emulsified acids results in lower values for pore volume to breakthrough (PVBT) compared to plain HCl, with a difference of approximately 25%. ([[2020-aly|Aly, 2020, p. 43]])
- The study identified that the use of Pickering emulsions (nano-clay) provides more stable systems and is more environmentally friendly than surfactant-based emulsifiers. ([[2020-aly|Aly, 2020, p. 33]])
- The NC-1 emulsifier produced a stable emulsion at both room temperature and 90°C, while other candidates (NC-2, NC-3, NC-4) failed. ([[2020-aly|Aly, 2020, p. 58]])
- Emulsifying 15%wt. HCl reduced the corrosion rate from 0.3471 lb/ft² to 0.0479 lb/ft², a reduction of 86.2%. ([[2020-aly|Aly, 2020, p. 61]])
- Adding 3%vol. corrosion inhibitor (CI-401) further reduced the corrosion rate to 0.0059 lb/ft², which is only 1.7% of the rate for plain HCl. ([[2020-aly|Aly, 2020, p. 61]])
- The emulsified acid was confirmed as a water-in-oil (w/o) type via both dispersion and electrical conductivity tests (conductivity < 20 μS/cm). ([[2020-aly|Aly, 2020, p. 65]])
- Particle size analysis showed that the emulsified acid droplets ranged between 0.05 and 24 μm, with a peak at 2.32 μm. ([[2020-aly|Aly, 2020, p. 91]])
- The emulsified acid was confirmed as a shear-thinning fluid, successfully fitted to the Carreau-Yasuda model across temperatures of 25°C, 40°C, 60°C, and 90°C. ([[2020-aly|Aly, 2020, p. 93]])
- Block ASW-001 showed a permeability of 4.625 mD with an average porosity of 13.67%. ([[2020-aly|Aly, 2020, p. 85]])
- The linear core samples exhibited higher permeability (average ~12.5 mD) compared to the blocks (~3.5 mD), resulting in a calculated permeability anisotropy of k_h/k_v = 0.28. ([[2020-aly|Aly, 2020, p. 106]])
- In block ASW-001, wormhole breakthrough occurred on the southern face at approximately 6.5 inches from the top. ([[2020-aly|Aly, 2020, p. 117]])
- The emulsified acid (EA) system was found to be a shear-thinning fluid following the Carreau-Yasuda model with droplets ranging from 0.5 to 20 $\mu$m. ([[2020-aly|Aly, 2020, p. 153]])

## Limitations

Only two radial experiments achieved breakthrough, necessitating the use of assumptions to represent the optimum case in some calculations. The study assumes that high temperature results for blocks will not differ significantly from low temperature ones due to the stability of the EA system.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Conduct an additional radial experiment at a higher injection rate (e.g., 600 cc/min) to better match PVBT curves.
- Incorporate CT scan data for radial experiments into the linear-radial relation analysis.
- Perform numerical simulations for both regimes to simulate different rates or populate results to reservoir dimensions.
