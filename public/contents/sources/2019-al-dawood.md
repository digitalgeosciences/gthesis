# Rock Physics Modeling and Inversion in Glacial Ordovician Reservoirs

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the use of post-stack seismic data to characterize reservoir fluids in Ordovician reservoirs by applying rock physics modeling and AVO analysis. It compares two inversion workflows: one using actual well logs and another using synthetic wells generated from forward modeling to predict porosity and gas saturation.

---

## Research Problem

Geophysicists face challenges in characterizing fluid types in Ordovician reservoir channels due to a lack of well control and poor quality of pre-stack seismic gathers, making pre-stack inversion difficult.

## Objectives

- Investigate the use of post-stack seismic data directly to characterize reservoir fluids using rock physics and AVO analysis.
- Perform rock physics modeling using Gassmann's fluid substitution equation to determine feasibility and pay zones.
- Generate inversion analyses to predict porosity and gas saturation.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of synthetic well inversions in areas with no local well control.

## Methods

The study is divided into two parts. Part 1 uses well log cross-plots (AI vs. Vp/Vs, AI vs. Porosity) to perform rock physics modeling and determine pay zones via Gassmann's fluid substitution. Part 2 assumes no local wells exist; it creates synthetic seismograms through forward modeling of P-wave velocity and density, followed by inversion analysis on these synthetics. Techniques included spectral decomposition (15, 20, 25 Hz) to map channels, wavelet extraction (multi-well, filtered, and statistical), and model-based pre-stack inversion using a multi-well approach for AI, density, and Vp/Vs.

## Data and Materials

3D seismic volume covering approximately 3400 km2; data from five angle stacks (0 to 50 degrees); logs from six exploration wells (including sonic, density, and mineral content).

## Key Findings

- The study determined that AI inversion can differentiate between porous and tight zones, but Vp/Vs inversion is required to identify the rock's fluid type. ([[2019-al-dawood|Al-Dawood, 2019, p. 45]])
- A synthetic well's AVA curve was classified as Class IV (amplitude decreases with offset in a low shear impedance reservoir) for well C. ([[2019-al-dawood|Al-Dawood, 2019, p. 51]])
- The average tuning thickness inside the channels, calculated from dominant frequency and velocity, is approximately 140 ft. ([[2019-al-dawood|Al-Dawood, 2019, p. 59]])
- A multi-well wavelet extracted from filtered seismic yielded a predictability of 6.5%, while the best extraction from original volume gave 52%. ([[2019-al-dawood|Al-Dawood, 2019, p. 67]])
- Porosity derived from neural network guided inversion showed a matching value of approximately 78% compared to actual well logs. ([[2019-al-dawood|Al-Dawood, 2019, p. 99]])
- Gas saturation derived from neural network guided inversion matched the log at 56%. ([[2019-al-dawood|Al-Dawood, 2019, p. 101]])
- Synthetic logs generated by forward modeling showed less than 10% error when compared to real well logs. ([[2019-al-dawood|Al-Dawood, 2019, p. 104]])

## Limitations

The seismic data was noisy and suffered from phase inconsistency, leading to low predictability values in wavelet extraction. Pre-stack inversion is limited by the lack of high-quality pre-stack gathers.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Reprocess seismic data gather from field tapes using an AVO-friendly processing workflow to improve signal-to-noise ratio.
- Apply synthetic inversion on areas with better seismic quality where fluid prediction is validated by real well logs.
