# SURFACE-ACTIVE-COMPONENT-BASED METHODOLOGY TO SCREEN FOR WETTABILITY ALTERATION IN CARBONATES

**Summary**: The thesis develops a screening methodology using an HPLC system equipped with a UV-vis detector to evaluate the effectiveness of different brine formulations (SWME* and NaI) in removing surface-active components from limestone. The study concludes that NaI brine is highly effective at reversing oil-wet conditions induced by Salicylic Acid, making it a viable candidate for carbonate reservoir treatment.

---

## Research Problem

The lack of a simple, rapid laboratory method to screen and select optimal aqueous formulations (like low-salinity or smart water) for promoting wettability alteration in carbonate reservoirs, which are typically oil-wet due to the presence of surface-active compounds like asphaltenes and carboxylic acids.

## Objectives

- Identify suitable surface-active model compounds (SAMC) simulating Carboxylic Acid and Asphaltenes for use in laboratory experiments.
- Develop a methodology using an HPLC system with UV-vis detection to quantify the release of SAMCs from rock surfaces during flooding.
- Evaluate different brine formulations (SWME, SWME+NaI, and DI+NaI) to determine their effectiveness in removing surface-active compounds.
- Quantify wettability alteration on carbonate surfaces using contact angle measurements before and after conditioning.
- Screen various model compounds (Stearic acid, Citric acid, Mandelic acid, Gallic acid, Salicylic acid, and Quercetin) to identify suitable surrogates for crude oil components like asphaltenes.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of different brine formulations (SWME* and NaI) in reversing wettability changes.

## Methods

The study utilized a modified HPLC system with a UV-vis detector to monitor the release of surface-active model compounds (SAMCs). Limestone powder was conditioned with varying concentrations of Salicylic acid and Quercetin. The powdered rock was packed into column tubes, and different fluids (DI water, SWME* brine, NaI brine, and a mixture) were pumped through at 1 ml/min. The concentration of the SAMC in the effluent was monitored via UV-vis absorbance. Contact angle measurements using a Rame-hart goniometer were performed on calcite chips to validate the wettability changes observed during the chemical screening.

## Data and Materials

200g of Indiana limestone; 6 types of surface-active model compounds (Mandelic Acid, Stearic Acid, Salicylic Acid, Citric Acid, Gallic Acid, Quercetin); three brine formulations (SWME, SWME+NaI, DI+NaI) with specific ionic concentrations as detailed in the study's tables.

## Key Findings

- Contact angle experiments confirmed that native carbonate was water-wet, while conditioning with SAMC increased the contact angle to oil-wet. ([[2020-opoku|Opoku, 2020, p. 18]])
- Conditioning with different brines successfully reduced the contact angle of SAMC-conditioned surfaces from oil-wet back to water-wet. ([[2020-opoku|Opoku, 2020, p. 18]])
- Note: The specific reduction in contact angles was not quantified numerically in the abstract, but confirmed as a transition from oil-wet to water-wet. ([[2020-opoku|Opoku, 2020]])
- Spectral analysis showed that NaI released more SAMC compared to pure SWME brine or a combination of SWME+NaI brine. ([[2020-opoku|Opoku, 2020, p. 18]])
- The high recovery of surface-active material was attributed to the presence of Iodide ions in the brine. ([[2020-opoku|Opoku, 2020, p. 18]])
- The least recovery of SAMC was observed in SWME brine. ([[2020-opoku|Opoku, 2020, p. 18]])
- The presence of Mg2+, SO42-, and I- ions in the SWME+NaI brine resulted in competition among the ions, which lowered the effect of I- to recover more SAMC. ([[2020-opoku|Opoku, 2020, p. 18]])
- Mandelic Acid (MA) showed a maximum absorption wavelength (λmax) at 200 nm with a retention time of less than one minute. ([[2020-opoku|Opoku, 2020, p. 63]])
- Stearic Acid (SA) exhibited a peak intensity at λmax = 200 nm and was successfully detected via the HPLC-UV system. ([[2020-opoku|Opoku, 2020, p. 67]])
- Gallic Acid (GA) showed an absorption wavelength of interest between 264nm and 320nm with a λmax of 264nm. ([[2020-opoku|Opoku, 2020, p. 70]])
- Salicylic acid was selected as the primary model compound because its wavelength (λmax = 295 nm) is distinct from the background absorption of various brines. ([[2020-opoku|Opoku, 2020, p. 75]])
- Quercetin was rejected for screening because its spectrum became distorted and difficult to identify after being conditioned to limestone powder. ([[2020-opoku|Opoku, 2020, p. 78]])
- A concentration of 2.86% wt Salicylic Acid resulted in a maximum intensity of 3.0x10^6 μAu, while 2.0% wt yielded 2.17x10^6 μAu. ([[2020-opoku|Opoku, 2020, p. 94]])
- NaI brine showed the highest recovery of Salicylic acid (105% and 84% respectively) compared to SWME* or the mixture in both tested concentrations. ([[2020-opoku|Opoku, 2020, p. 114]])
- The Brine Enhancement Factor (BEF) for NaI was significantly higher than other brines, reaching 5.0 and 5.1 respectively for the two test cases. ([[2020-opoku|Opoku, 2020, p. 118]])

## Limitations

The 6%wt Salicylic Acid concentration was excluded from analysis because it exceeded the UV-vis detector's intensity limit (4x10^6 μAu), resulting in folded signals that made quantification difficult.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future work should establish a correlation between the percentage of Salicylic acid released and the actual volume of oil recovered in core flooding or field-scale tests. The study also suggests using ferulic acid as an alternative model compound and exploring microfluidic flow cells with carbonate coatings.
