# BENTHIC FORAMINIFERAL TAXONOMY, DISTRIBUTION AND ECOLOGY IN THE ARABIAN GULF

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the taxonomy, distribution, and ecology of benthic foraminifera in the Arabian Gulf, specifically focusing on morphological abnormalities caused by epibiont interactions. It also describes several new species (Pseudonubeculina arabica and Pseudotriloculina hottingeri) and evaluates the use of deformity rates as a proxy for environmental stress in hypersaline environments like Salwa Bay.

---

## Research Problem

The lack of detailed taxonomic studies and accurate biodiversity data for benthic foraminifera in the Arabian Gulf, which are critical for understanding paleoenvironmental changes and monitoring modern impacts of human activity on these unique hypersaline ecosystems.

## Objectives

- Conduct qualitative and quantitative analysis of benthic foraminifera assemblages from shallow intertidal to deep offshore environments in the Saudi Arabian parts of the Arabian Gulf.
- Identify and document the distribution of foraminiferal biofacies and dead assemblages with respect to water depth and substrate parameters.
- Investigate the impact of epibiont-basibiont relationships on the development and ontogeny of basibionts.
- Describe new species and genera (e.g., Pseudonubeculina arabica, Pseudotriloculina hottingeri) to improve taxonomic clarity.
- Investigate the role of epibiont-basibiont interactions in causing morphological abnormalities in host foraminifera.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of deformity rates as a proxy for pollution biomonitoring in naturally stressed environments like Salwa Bay.

## Methods

Samples were collected from various locations in the Arabian Gulf using a van Veen grab and then sieved (63 µm) and dried. Foraminifera were identified using SEM, light microscopy, and QEMSCAN/EDX to verify mineralogy. A modified resin embedding technique was used for cross-sections. Environmental parameters including temperature, salinity, TPH, and heavy metals were analyzed via GC-FID, ICP-OES, and ion chromatography. Grain-size analysis was performed using laser diffraction (Microtract s3500) and processed with GRADISTAT software. Statistical analyses included diversity indices (Shannon-Weiner, dominance, richness) calculated in PAST.

## Data and Materials

Over 3500 specimens were picked from various sites; 120 benthic foraminiferal species were identified across 24 genera in the Saudi Arabian study area. Samples from Murray's Pool included data from six distinct subenvironments. Data on salinity, temperature, and sediment composition (TOC, heavy metals) were collected to correlate with faunal distribution.

## Key Findings

- Benthic foraminifera are major carbonate producers in modern Arabian Gulf waters and their fossil remains comprise a large portion of petroleum-bearing carbonate rock in Eastern Saudi Arabia. ([[2017-amao|Amao, 2017, p. 19]])
- In Salwa Bay, benthic foraminiferal species were dominated by porcelaneous taxa (85.1%), followed by hyaline (14.9%). ([[2017-amao|Amao, 2017, p. 20]])
- Peneroplis pertusus, P. planatus, and C. hemprichii account for approximately 48% of the assemblage in Salwa Bay. ([[2017-amao|Amao, 2017, p. 20]])
- Approximately 43% of the most common taxa in Salwa Bay (e.g., Peneroplis, Coscinospira) exhibit mild to severe deformities such as fusion of two adults or double tests. ([[2017-amao|Amao, 2017, p. 20]])
- The study identified a new species, Pseudotriloculina hottingeri n. sp., which has a depth range of 0.02–17 m and tolerates salinity of 36–52 ppt. ([[2017-amao|Amao, 2017, p. 20]])
- The new species Pseudonubeculina arabica is characterized by a uniserial chamber arrangement, coarsely agglutinated bilamellar test wall with white high-Mg calcite cement, and a terminal slit-like aperture. ([[2017-amao|Amao, 2017, p. 64]])
- A total of 224 benthic foraminiferal species and subspecies were identified belonging to 63 genera, 34 families, 22 superfamilies and six orders along the Iranian coast. ([[2017-amao|Amao, 2017, p. 142]])
- The most common species on the Iranian coast include Nonion sp. 1 (5.5%) and Asterorotalia dentata (5.0%). ([[2017-amao|Amao, 2017, p. 21]])
- The study found that the epibiont Cymbaloporetta sp. shows no preference for host selection, attaching to any available basibiont. ([[2017-amao|Amao, 2017, p. 47]])
- Morphological test abnormalities caused by an epibiont in an unpolluted environment have implications for the use of such abnormalities as indicators for pollution biomonitoring. ([[2017-amao|Amao, 2017, p. 47]])
- Morphological abnormalities in host foraminifera (e.g., Agglutinella soriformis, Adelosina carinatastriata) were found at a rate of 1.94%, which is higher than the typical background value of 1% for undisturbed populations. ([[2017-amao|Amao, 2017, p. 58]])
- Pseudotriloculina hottingeri was found in 20 out of 78 sampling stations, showing a restricted distribution in the Arabian Gulf with a depth range of 0.02–17 m and salinity tolerance of 36–52 ppt. ([[2017-amao|Amao, 2017, p. 73]])
- Approximately 43% of the most common taxa in Salwa Bay exhibited mild to severe deformities, with Peneroplis and Coscinospira accounting for 75.5% of all observed test deformities. ([[2017-amao|Amao, 2017, p. 96]])
- The average salinity in Salwa Bay was 52.6 ± 0.4 ppt, with a temperature of 27.6 ± 0.4°C. ([[2017-amao|Amao, 2017, p. 93]])
- The mean grain size for all sampled sites in Salwa Bay fell within the sand fraction (1.1–1.8 ϧ), with 90% of samples characterized as moderately sorted, medium-grained sand. ([[2017-amao|Amao, 2017, p. 94]])

## Limitations

The study notes that foraminiferal diversity is still largely under-documented in the Arabian Gulf and that some species are only tentatively identified due to a lack of taxonomic guides. Additionally, it highlights that deformity rates may not be reliable indicators of pollution in naturally stressed environments like Salwa Bay.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future studies are needed to separate test abnormalities caused by epibionts from those attributable to different types of pollution and to better constrain the possible parasitic vs. epibiont-relationship of Cymbaloporetta sp. Systematic study of the area based on additional samples is also recommended.
