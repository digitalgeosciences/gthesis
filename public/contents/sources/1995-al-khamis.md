# Evaluation of Correlations for Natural Gas Compressibility Factors

**Summary**: The thesis evaluates and compares nine commonly used empirical correlations for calculating the Natural Gas compressibility factor (Z-factor) using 5,940 data points from the Standing-Katz chart. It concludes that the Dranchuk and Abou-Kassem correlation provides the best fit for original data, while the Hall and Yarborough correlation performs best with smoothed data.

---

## Research Problem

The need for accurate and efficient methods to estimate the Natural Gas compressibility factor (Z-factor) in petroleum calculations, specifically addressing inconsistencies in existing charts and the limitations of various empirical correlations at low reduced temperatures.

## Objectives

- Evaluate and compare nine commonly used correlations for calculating Z-factors using error and graphical analysis.
- Validate and smooth data from the Standing-Katz chart to ensure consistency with established theories.
- Construct 3-D plots of each correlation to provide a better understanding of their representation.
- Evaluate the statistical accuracy of nine different Z-factor correlations.
- Compare direct and iterative correlation methods for natural gas compressibility.
- Identify which correlations provide the most reliable data across the widest range of pressure (Pr) and temperature (Tr).

## Methods

The study utilized 5,940 Z-factor values from the Handbook of Natural Gas Engineering. Data were first analyzed for typographical errors and then smoothed specifically for the $T_r$ values of 1.05 and 1.10. Evaluation was performed using two methods: error analysis (calculating average absolute relative errors across various pseudo-reduced temperatures and pressures) and graphical analysis (constructing 3-D plots to compare correlation shapes against the actual function). Several specific correlations were analyzed, including those by Leung, Papay, Gopal, Burnett, Papp, Hankinson et al., Hall and Yarborough, Dranchuk et al., and Dranchuk and Abou-Kassem.

## Data and Materials

The study utilizes 'Standing and Katz Natural Gas Z Factor Data' as the primary dataset for validation. The data includes variables such as pseudoreduced pressure (P/Pc), pseudoreduced temperature (T/Tc), and gas compressibility factor (Z).

## Key Findings

- The Dranchuk and Abou-Kassem correlation was found to give the least average absolute relative error with respect to original data. ([[1995-al-khamis|Al-Khamis, 1995, p. 14]])
- The Hall and Yarborough correlation gave the least average absolute relative error with respect to smoothed data. ([[1995-al-khamis|Al-Khamis, 1995, p. 14]])
- Both Dranchuk and Abou-Kassem and Hall and Yarborough correlations were found to describe the shape of the actual function very closely. ([[1995-al-khamis|Al-Khamis, 1995, p. 14]])
- Data for $T_r$ values of 1.05 and 1.10 showed significant deviations when expressed in terms of reduced gas density, likely due to errors in original data or the failure of Kay's rule. ([[1995-al-khamis|Al-Khamis, 1995, p. 44]])
- The difference between Standing-Katz and Brown et al. charts was highest at $T_r = 1.05$, with a calculated difference of 0.855 for the lowest curve. ([[1995-al-khamis|Al-Khamis, 1995, p. 45]])
- Hankinson et al. correlation failed to converge using Newton-Raphson iteration for nine specific data points at $T_r = 1.05$. ([[1995-al-khamis|Al-Khamis, 1995, p. 30]])
- The Dranchuk and Abou-Kassem correlation provided the lowest average absolute relative error for both original and smoothed data. ([[1995-al-khamis|Al-Khamis, 1995, p. 77]])
- The Gopal correlation was found to give the least absolute average relative error for the complete range of the data among direct correlations, despite having clear discontinuities between its thirteen segments. ([[1995-al-khamis|Al-Khamis, 1995, p. 77]])
- Papay's correlation showed the highest error and failed to accurately represent the actual function graphically. ([[1995-al-khamis|Al-Khamis, 1995, p. 77]])
- The Burnett correlation was found to work only for a very limited range of data compared to other correlations. ([[1995-al-khamis|Al-Khamis, 1995, p. 77]])
- Hankinson et al. correlation showed the highest average absolute relative error among iterative correlations. ([[1995-al-khamis|Al-Khamis, 1995, p. 78]])
- The Hall and Yarborough, Dranchuk et al., and Dranchuk & Abou-Kassem correlations were identified as the only ones capable of covering the full range of the Standing and Katz chart with reasonable accuracy. ([[1995-al-khamis|Al-Khamis, 1995, p. 77]])
- The correlation coefficient (R) represents the degree of success in reducing standard deviation by regression analysis, with values between 0 and 1. ([[1995-al-khamis|Al-Khamis, 1995, p. 93]])
- The Coefficient of Determination ($R^2$) is defined as the square of the correlation coefficient, indicating the strength of the relationship between independent and dependent variables. ([[1995-al-khamis|Al-Khamis, 1995, p. 93]])
- Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) serves as a measure of the closeness of correlation prediction to measured values; lower values indicate better fits. ([[1995-al-khamis|Al-Khamis, 1995, p. 91]])

## Limitations

The Burnett correlation is limited to a very narrow range; the Gopal correlation, while accurate in some areas, suffers from significant discontinuities between its thirteen segments.
