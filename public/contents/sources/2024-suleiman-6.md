# APPLICATION OF CYCLIC EXOTHERMIC AND ENDOTHERMIC THERMOCHEMICAL FLUIDS FOR TIGHT RESERVOIRS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of cyclic endothermic and exothermic thermochemical fluid treatments on the mechanical properties and breakdown pressure of tight rock samples (Eagle Ford shale and organic-rich carbonates). It concludes that the endothermic-exothermic sequence is more effective at reducing brittleness index and Young's modulus, thereby significantly lowering breakdown pressures compared to the reverse sequence.

---

## Research Problem

The challenge of fracturing and improving flow in tight formations (like Eagle Ford shale) where low natural permeability makes conventional stimulation difficult. The study specifically addresses how induced thermal shock from thermochemical fluids can create micro-fractures and reduce rock stiffness to lower breakdown pressures.

## Objectives

- Investigates the effect of sequence thermochemical treatment on the mechanical properties of the shale and organic-rich carbonate
- Examines the potential reduction in the breakdown pressure under ambient conditions, revealing the reduction in tensile stress of the rock.
- Investurges the possibility of Total Organic Carbon Content maturation in shale rock through cyclic thermochemical treatment.
- Evaluate the impact of endothermic-exothermic and exothermic-endothermic treatment sequences on the mechanical properties (Young's modulus, Poisson's ratio, brittleness index) of tight samples.
- Assess the reduction in breakdown pressure of rock samples after thermochemical treatment.
- Quantify the change in hardness (intrinsic specific energy) of samples following thermal shock.

## Methods

The study utilized two types of rock samples: Eagle Ford shale (EF1.1, EF1.2, EF1.3) and organic-rich carbonate (J1, J2, J3). Samples were subjected to 'Sequence #1' (Endo-Exo) and 'Sequence #2' (Exo-Endo) using thermochemical fluids. Temperature was monitored via a logging device during the cycles. Mechanical properties like Young's modulus and Poisson's ratio were derived from sonic tests. Breakdown pressure was measured by pumping de-ionized water into drilled holes at 0.1ml/min. Hardness was determined via scratch tests to calculate intrinsic specific energy (ISE). TOC and mass changes were analyzed using rock-eval pyrolysis. Micro-CT scans were performed in different orientations to visualize internal fractures.

## Data and Materials

Samples included 3 core samples of Eagle Ford shale (initially 8-inch long, 1.5-inch diameter) and 3 samples of Organic-rich Jordan Carbonate with ~16% TOC. Experimental data included sonic wave velocities (Vp, Vs), breakdown pressure measurements, scratch test results for 7 points per sample, and Rock-Eval pyrolysis analysis.

## Key Findings

- Breakdown pressure was reduced by 79.28% in shale samples and 82.58% in organic-rich carbonate samples when the cyclic endothermic-exothermic thermochemical treatment was applied. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 17]])
- TOC reduction of 0.94% and 0.78% was observed in shale and carbonate samples respectively, suggesting potential for supporting TOC maturation. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 18]])
- Note: The text on page 18 mentions 'TOC reduction' but the abstract/summary implies this is a positive indicator of maturity or transformation during the process. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024]])
- The endothermic-exothermic sequence reduced the brittleness index by 29.61% in Eagle Ford shale and 13.79% in organic-rich carbonate samples. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 89]])
- Cyclic thermochemical treatment reduced Young's modulus by 29.38% in Eagle Ford samples and 15.56% in organic-rich carbonate samples. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 89]])
- The endothermic-exothermic sequence (EF1.2) resulted in a breakdown pressure of 631.57 psi, compared to 3048.35 psi for the untreated sample (EF1.1), representing a 79.28% reduction. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 78]])
- Average hardness for EF1.2 (en-ex) was 85.4 MPa, significantly lower than the 108.05 MPa of the base sample (EF1.1). ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 81]])

## Limitations

Micro-CT scans had limited resolution, making it difficult to visualize small micro-fractures in some samples. The study noted that results could be affected by the non-uniformity of rock sampling locations and the anisotropic nature of rocks.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Implement better thermal insulation to minimize heat/fluid loss during treatment.
- Increase temperature differentials to accelerate TOC maturation.
- Maintain higher temperatures for longer durations to promote kerogen cracking.
- Conduct more than three cycles to further reduce breakdown pressure in high-strength rocks.
- Perform tensile strength tests and COMSOL modeling for deformation studies.
