# Investigation of Condensate Removal in Tight Reservoirs Using Thermochemical Fluids

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the use of thermochemical fluids to mitigate condensate banking and improve gas production in tight reservoirs, including shale formations. Through coreflooding experiments, NMR measurements, and CMG-GEM simulations, it concludes that thermochemical treatment significantly reduces capillary pressure, lowers condensate viscosity, and creates micro-fractures, leading to superior recovery compared to conventional gas injection.

---

## Research Problem

The accumulation of condensate liquid in tight reservoirs (shale, sandstone, and carbonate) causes significant production decline (up to 80%) due to increased flow resistance and capillary forces. Existing methods like gas injection are less effective than thermochemical treatments which provide simultaneous heat, pressure, and chemical effects.

## Objectives

- Evaluate a novel thermochemical fluid treatment for mitigating condensate banking in various rock types (sandstone, carbonate, shale).
- Quantify the impact of generated heat and pressure from thermochemical reactions on condensate phase behavior.
- Compare thermochemical treatment against conventional methods like gas injection and solvent treatment.
- Perform field-scale simulations to assess long-term production sustainability.
- To introduce a novel treatment to eliminate the gas condensation in gas condensate reservoirs using thermoredynamic fluids.
- To develop a new approach for removing the water and emulsion blockages from tight formations.

## Methods

The study employed a dual approach: laboratory experiments and field-scale simulation. Experimental methods included coreflooding to measure flow properties, rate transient analysis (RTA) to evaluate productivity indices, and NMR measurements to confirm the creation of micro-fractures and changes in pore structure. Field-scale simulations were conducted using the CMG-GEM simulator, incorporating real gas compositions and reservoir conditions. The simulation was performed in two stages: condensate development (analyzing the impact of production rates and bottomhole pressures) and thermochemical treatment (modeling in-situ heat/pressure generation, viscosity reduction, and capillary pressure reduction).

## Data and Materials

Core samples from three sandstone formations (Scioto, Kentucky, and Bandera), a carbonate formation, and a shale formation; thermochemical fluids (ammonium chloride and sodium nitrite); gas types for comparison (decane, methane, nitrogen).

## Key Findings

- Thermochemical treatment can enhance gas productivity by 62% in tight rocks. ([[2020-mohammed|Mohammed, 2020, p. 22]])
- Treatment reduced capillary pressure by 47% and increased absolute open flow (AOF) by approximately 450%. ([[2020-mohammed|Mohammed, 2020, p. 22]])
- Cumulative condensate recoveries were 72.97%, 68.09%, and 43.37% for sandstone, carbonate, and shale samples, respectively. ([[2020-mohammed|Mohammed, 2020, p. 206]])
- Thermochemical treatment induced permanent fractures in the rock matrix, confirmed by NMR and permeability measurements. ([[2020-mohammed|Mohammed, 2020, p. 22]])
- Absolute permeability increased by more than 200 times following thermochemical injection. ([[2020-mohammed|Mohammed, 2020, p. 22]])
- Capillary pressure (Pc) decreased by 55.57% due to thermochemical treatment. ([[2020-mohammed|Mohammed, 2020, p. 14]])
- 80% of water was removed by injecting two pore volumes of nitrogen gas at 180 °C. ([[2020-mohammed|Mohammed, 2020, p. 14]])
- More than 60% of the trapped condensate was removed using thermochemical fluids in Scioto rock samples. ([[2020-mohammed|Mohammed, 2020, p. 14]])
- Four cycles of chemical injection remove 62.5% of the condensate bank in Kentucky rock samples. ([[2020-mohammed|Mohammed, 2020, p. 14]])
- 72.9% of the condensate liquid was removed using four cycles of chemical injection in Bandera sandstone. ([[2020-mohammed|Mohammed, 2020, p. 14]])
- Thermochemical reactions generated in-situ pressure up to 1300 psi and temperature increases sufficient to move the system above the dew point curve, converting liquid condensate into gas. ([[2020-mohammed|Mohammed, 2020, p. 60]])
- The thermochemical treatment reduced capillary forces by 25% to 36%, leading to a significant increase in the capillary number (Nc) from 7x10^-4 to 6x10^-2. ([[2020-mohammed|Mohammed, 2020, p. 87]])
- A reduction of 41% in condensate volume was observed when increasing pressure from 2000 to 3500 psi using the WinProp simulation. ([[2020-mohammed|Mohammed, 2020, p. 86]])
- The study found that a 63% of original condensate in place was recovered by injecting 2.1 pore volumes of thermochemical fluids in coreflooding tests. ([[2020-mohammed|Mohammed, 2020]])

## Limitations

The study was conducted at a laboratory scale using core samples; however, it notes that field-scale application would require careful consideration of well integrity due to high generated pressures/temperatures.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study suggests that while three cycles of thermochemical injection are sufficient for sandstone and carbonate formations, shale formations may require a greater number of stimulation cycles and longer soaking periods due to their inherent tightness.
