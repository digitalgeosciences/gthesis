# The Use of Geophysical Methods in Geothermal Exploration of the Wadi Al Lith Field in Western Saudi Arabia

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the geothermal potential of the Wadi Al Lith field in Western Saudi Arabia by integrating land gravity, magnetic, and electromagnetic (MT, TEM) data. It develops a multi-geoscientific workflow to characterize subsurface structures, identify heat sources, and map tectonic features like dikes and fault zones that facilitate thermal fluid circulation.

---

## Research Problem

The identification of viable geothermal reservoirs in Western Saudi Arabia requires detailed knowledge of subsurface structures (faults, dikes, basement depth) which are difficult to map using single geophysical methods alone due to noise or lack of resolution at specific depths.

## Objectives

- Utilize geophysical data (potential field methods and EM) to characterize the prospective geothermal field of Wadi Al Lith.
- Highlight the importance of gravity and magnetic methods in geothermal exploration.
- Integrate multi-geophysical and geoscientific datasets to develop a robust workflow for subsurface characterization.
- Determine the depth of the Curie isotherm and estimate surface heat flow using airborne magnetic data.
- Identify potential sites for drilling exploratory wells based on integrated data models.
- Integrate land gravity and magnetic data with EM (MT/TEM) data to characterize the subsurface structure of Wadi Al Lith.

## Methods

The research employed a multi-method geophysical approach: (1) Gravity analysis included 2D modeling of profiles, 3D gravity inversion using Oasis montaj VOXI Earth Modeling, and curvature analysis to identify structural boundaries. (2) Magnetic data were analyzed via spectral analysis (spectral peak and centroid methods) to estimate the Curie isotherm depth and used for lineament density mapping. (3) Electromagnetic techniques included Magnetotelluric (MT) and Transient-electromagnetic (TEM) surveys to map resistivity and validate gravity/magnetic results. (4) A 1D geotherm was constructed using a three-layered model (upper crust, lower crust, mantle) based on assumed thermal conductivities and radiogenic heat production.

## Data and Materials

Data includes: land gravity surveys (using CG-5 gravitometer), airborne magnetic data (large area coverage), magnetotelluric (MT) soundings (e.g., MT11, MT13), transient electromagnetic (TEM) soundings, seismic profiles, well logs, and surface geological maps of the Wadi Al Lith region.

## Key Findings

- The Wadi Al Lith area is characterized by high geothermal gradient values (> 19°C/km) and heat flow between 47.7 and 87.8 mW/m². ([[2024-chavanidis|Chavanidis, 2024, p. 36]])
- The Curie isotherm in the study area is estimated to be shallower than 29.6 km based on airborne magnetic data. ([[2024-chavanidis|Chavanidis, 2024, p. 36]])
- The heat source of the prospective geothermal field is located at an approximate depth of 5-6 km, as determined by MT analysis. ([[2024-chavanidis|Chavanidis, 2024, p. 37]])
- average reservoir temperature of 136°C and a heat flow of around 183 mW/m² were reported for the Ain Al Harrah hot spring using silica and Na-K-Ca geothermometers. ([[2024-chavanidis|Chavanidis, 2024]])

- The Curie isotherm depth, derived from aeromagnetic data, was successfully validated by the large resistivity contrast observed in MT soundings at consistent depths. ([[2024-chavanidis|Chavanidis, 2024, p. 50]])
- Lineament analysis of potential field data identified structural trends that correspond to known features and provide a basis for identifying faults as conduits for geothermal fluids. ([[2024-chavanidis|Chavanidis, 2024, p. 49]])
- The Dammam Dome study showed that gravity inversion revealed two distinct vertical bodies (A and B) with significant density contrasts, confirming the impact of intraplate stresses from the Arabian-Eurasian collision. ([[2024-chavanidis|Chavanidis, 2024, p. 64]])
- The integration of MT cross-sections as a priori information for gravity modeling significantly reduced RMS errors and improved the accuracy of identifying fractured vs. fresh basement zones. ([[2024-chavanidis|Chavanidis, 2024, p. 100]])
- Two specific MT soundings (MT11, MT13) showed consistent behavior with slight curve splitting at periods > 0.1s, indicating a non-layered subsurface model. ([[2024-chavanidis|Chavanidis, 2024, p. 84]])
- The Curie isotherm depth was estimated at 14.8 km using spectral analysis of airborne magnetic data. ([[2024-chavanidis|Chavanidis, 2024, p. 103]])
- A surface heat flow value of 109.8 mW/m2 was determined for the study area, supporting its classification as a high to very high favorable area for geothermal energy. ([[2024-chavanidis|Chavanidis, 2024, p. 104]])
- The 3D gravity model identified a low-density zone with an E-W trend in the southern part of the study area, interpreted as a fracture zone providing pathways for thermal fluids. ([[2024-chavanidis|Chavanidis, 2024, p. 136]])
- TEM soundings (TEM 1-3) confirmed a fractured zone with low resistivity (under 10 Ohm.m) at depths below 120 m. ([[2024-chavanidis|Chavanidis, 2024, p. 130]])
- The study identified dikes as secondary pathways for geothermal fluids, potentially bringing thermal water to shallower depths. ([[2024-chavanidis|Chavanidis, 2024, p. 135]])

## Limitations

The study notes that the interpretation of gravity data is sensitive to noise from urban environments and high temperatures; also, the lack of specific density values for local formations required the use of regional literature values (e.g., Langheim et al.).

## Recommendations and Future Work

Conduct higher resolution seismic and non--seismic surveys (3D seismic, surface CSEM, micro-gravity); perform detailed geological and geochemical studies of local groundwater; drill exploratory boreholes to ground-truth the estimated surface heat flow.
