# Saturation-Height Modelling of Oil and Gas-Capped Reservoirs

**Summary**: The thesis evaluates the use of saturation-height modeling to estimate water saturation and hydrocarbon volumes in oil and gas-capped carbonate reservoirs. It utilizes a multi-step workflow involving core analysis, hydraulic flow unit (HFU) identification via flow zone indicators (FZI), and Leverett J-function normalization to create a robust petrophysical model.

---

## Research Problem

Determining accurate hydrocarbon volumes in carbonate reservoirs where resistivity logs are often limited by shoulder effects and thin bed problems, necessitating a robust method to link petrophysical rock types (or hydraulic flow units) to log data for consistent saturation estimation across both cored and un-cored intervals.

## Methods

The study utilizes a multi-step workflow: 1. Establishing a geological framework using sequence-stratigraphic principles, well logs, core data, and seismic data. 2. Defining a structural framework including top maps and fault identification. 3. Identifying sedimentary facies through core description, log signatures, and thin-section petrography to link them to petrophysical rock types. 4. Determining fluid interfaces (FWL, FOL, OWC, GOC). 5. Modeling permeability in non-cored wells by correlating core data to well logs. 6. Determining rock-fluid properties (IFT and contact angles) and fluid properties (densities). 7. Defining reservoir quality groups based on pore throat radius proxies (sqrt(K)/phi). 8. Normalizing capillary pressure data using the J-function approach.

## Data and Materials

Data includes wireline log suites (Gamma Ray, Density, Neutron, Sonic, Resistivity) for three wells in a carbonate reservoir; core plug data from two wells used for calibration of petrophysical models; and mercury injection capillary pressure (MICP) data to characterize pore systems.

## Key Findings

- Capillary pressure is a function of pore geometry, surface tension, and interfacial tension. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 17]])
- The Leverett J-function is used to normalize capillary pressure curves by accounting for variations in permeability and porosity. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 55]])
- Several curve fitting functions are available for capillary pressure data, including Simple Non-Linear, Logarithmic, Simple Exponential, Advanced Exponential, Hyperbolic, Lambda, and Thomeer functions. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 42]])
- Petrophysical rock types (e.g., Hydraulic Flow Units) are essential for predicting permeability in uncored wells. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 139]])
- The distinction between Free Water Level (FWL), Initial Oil Water Contact (IOWC), and Producing Oil Water Contact (POWC) is critical for reservoir modeling. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 33]])
- The gas oil contact (GOC) was located at -4862 feet in the reservoir section. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 25]])
- The oil water contact (OWC) lies at -4930 feet, with a field-wide average free water level (FWL) of -4937 feet. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 25]])
- A field-wide average free oil level (FOL) was interpreted at -4867 feet based on pressure elevation analysis. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 25]])
- Petrophysical rock types were defined using RQI and FZI to group rocks with similar flow characteristics into five distinct units. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 131]])
- Mercury injection capillary pressure curves for different petrophysical rock types showed distinct clusters, indicating unique pore throat distributions. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 137]])
- The study identified that vuggy porosity in oomoldic limestone causes a significant deviation from the standard lithology lines on sonic transit time plots. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 107]])
- The Archie 'm' factor was determined to be 1.9 based on special core analysis. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 26]])
- Neutron porosity of shale beds was found to range from 0.35 to 0.40. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 26]])
- The advanced exponential function is used to generate the J-function versus Sw curves, where a non-linear equation for each HFU can replace the Swir term to account for variations in rock quality. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 30]])
- Saturation-height models (red curve) compared favorably with Archie water saturation (blue curve), showing only minor differences of five to ten units in specific intervals. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 31]])

## Limitations

Mercury injection data was not available at high pressure to characterize micro-pores; however, since the reservoir had a sufficient buoyancy force to overcome capillary entry pressures of micro-pores, the J-function method remained applicable.
