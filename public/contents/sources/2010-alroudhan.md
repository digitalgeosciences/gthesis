# Saturation-Height Modelling of Oil and Gas-Capped Reservoirs

**Summary**: The thesis explores various methodologies for saturation height modeling in oil and gas-capped reservoirs, focusing on the integration of core data (capillary pressure) with well log data to accurately determine water saturation. It details several mathematical models, including Thomeer, Skelt-Harrison, and Cuddy methods, specifically applied to carbonate systems.

---

## Research Problem

The challenge of accurately estimating water saturation and hydrocarbon volume in carbonate reservoirs where standard log-based calculations (Archie's equation) may be limited by issues like the shoulder effect or thin bed problems, especially in complex systems with both oil and gas caps.

## Objectives

- Establish a multi-disciplinary workflow to integrate core data, well logs, and fluid analysis.
- Determine petrophysical rock types (PRTs) or hydraulic flow units (HFUs) from core data and correlate them to well logs.
- Develop predictive relationships between log responses and petrophysical properties for use in non-cored wells.
- Calculate permeability and determine saturation height functions for each identified hydraulic unit.
- Populate 3D geological models with accurate hydrocarbon saturations.
- Review the existing saturation height modeling methodologies.

## Methods

The methodology involves establishing a geological framework using sequence-stratigraphic principles (based on seismic, log, and core data) to define sedimentary facies. These are then linked to petrophysical rock types. Core analysis includes mercury injection capillary pressure (MICP) to determine pore throat distributions and petrography to identify mineralogy and diagenesis. Capillary pressure data is corrected for laboratory effects (stress, clay-bound water), normalized using the J-function approach, and fitted to various mathematical models (e.g., Thomeer, Lambda, or exponential functions). These are then used to calculate water saturation profiles in a 3D model based on height above Free Water Level (FWL) and Free Oil Level (FOL).

## Data and Materials

The thesis utilizes core samples for mercury injection capillary pressure measurements, thin sections for petrography, and wireline log data (resistivity, porosity, neutron) to establish correlations between rock properties and well logs. A carbonate reservoir with a small oil column is used as the primary case study.

## Key Findings

- The average free oil level (FOL) was determined at -4867 feet, while the average field-wide free water level (FWL) was established at -4937 feet. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 25]])
- The best estimate for the Archie 'm' factor in the reservoir is 1.9, with a corresponding Rw of 0.095 at 170°F. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 150]])
- Hydraulic units (HU) were successfully identified and predicted from log data; for example, HU1 represents the best reservoir quality while HU5 represents the worst. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 159]])
- The saturation height model results compared favorably with resistivity-derived saturations, showing only minor variations in specific intervals. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 176]])
- Petrophysical rock types were identified using a combination of core data and log responses to ensure consistency across the reservoir. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 18]])
- Saturation height modeling requires distinct calculation of buoyancy forces for gas-oil systems using specific gas-oil interfacial tensions and contact angles, rather than just oil-water parameters. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 43]])
- A dynamic modeling layout in a spreadsheet format can successfully manage up to seven rock types and four different capillary pressure datums simultaneously. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 44]])
- The 'square-root of permeability divided by porosity' (Flow Zone Indicator) is an effective proxy for reservoir pore throat radius when defining rock quality groups. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 49]])
- Thomeer hyperbolic functions are highly effective for fitting capillary pressure data as they relate parameters directly to porosity and permeability. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 87]])
- The J-function approach is used to normalize capillary pressure curves, allowing the calculation of water saturation from log-derived properties like porosity and permeability. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 50]])
- The Thomeer parameter (BVnw) can be used to fit capillary pressure curves using a hyperbolic equation with parameters A and B. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 93]])
- Leverett's J-function normalizes capillary pressure to account for changes in porosity and permeability, allowing data from different rocks to collapse into a single curve. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 94]])
- The Skelt-Harrison method uses four coefficients (a, b, c, d) where 'a' shifts the curve along the saturation axis, 'b' along the pressure/height axis, 'c' controls curvature, and 'd' accounts for free water level. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 99]])
- The Cuddy method is independent of permeability as it uses bulk volume water (BVW) which is a function of height alone. ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 103]])
- Petrophysical rock types in carbonates are categorized into three classes by Lucia based on pore size: Class 1 (>100 microns), Class 2 (20-100 microns), and Class 3 (<20 microns). ([[2010-alroudhan|AlRoudhan, 2010, p. 128]])

## Limitations

The thesis notes that standard models often fail in oil-wet or mixed-wet reservoirs unless specific imbibition-type capillary pressure curves are used; however, the study focuses on a carbonate reservoir with limited complexity regarding wettability.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The relationship between hydraulic flow units and textural-based geological classifications should be further studied to assist in assigning HFUs to geological facies mapped between wells.
