# PERFORMANCE EVALUATION OF GREEN SURFACTANT FOR EOR PROCESSES

**Summary**: The thesis evaluates the performance of green surfactants, specifically rhamnolipid, alkyl polyglycoside (APG), and lecithin, for enhanced oil recovery (EOR) in sandstone reservoirs. It investigates their phase behavior, fluid properties like interfacial tension (IFT), and core-flooding results, concluding that rhamnolipid and certain APG formulations show significant potential for EOR.

---

## Research Problem

The environmental impact of traditional synthetic surfactants used in chemical enhanced oil recovery (EOR) has led to a need for safer, 'green' alternatives like biosurfactants (e.g., rhamnolipid) and other biodegradable surfactants (e.g., APG, lecithin).

## Objectives

- To determine the ability of the biosurfactant rhamnolipid; the green surfactants APG and lecithin; and the cosolvent 1-butanol to reduce the IFT to effectively low levels for enhanced oil recovery needs in various formulated surfactant blends.
- To find the optimal surfactant-cosurfactant-cosolvent formulation for maximum performance in EOR processes.
- To discover the potential of the most effective surfactant formulations to recover an economically significant amount of residual oil via core-flooding experiments using Berea sandstone cores.
- To optimize our surfactant formulations by simulating the previously performed core-flood experiments using a commercial simulator that has been optimized to take into account chemical EOR techniques.
- Evaluate the phase behavior of different green surfactant formulations under varying salinity conditions.
- Measure fluid properties including density, viscosity, and interfacial tension (IFT) for the selected formulations.

## Methods

The study involved: 1. Phase behavior analysis via salinity scans to identify microemulsion formation and optimal salinity. 2. Fluid property measurements including density (using a DMA 4500 densitometer), viscosity (using a U-tube viscometer in a thermostatic bath), and IFT (using an M6500 spinning drop tensiometer). 3. Core-flooding experiments using Berea sandstone cores, involving brine saturation, oil flooding, waterflooding, surfactant flooding, and post-flushing. 4. Reservoir simulation using Eclipse to model the core flood results in a 3D Cartesian grid.

## Data and Materials

The study used Arabian crude oil (30° API, 11 EACN), rhamnolipid (90% purity), APG Glucopon® 600 CSUP (50-75% purity), 1-butanol (99%), lecithin (97%), and NaCl for brine preparation. Core-flooding was performed on Berea sandstone cores.

## Key Findings

- The first formulation (rhamnolipid/butanol) achieved approximately 23% tertiary recovery and 62% of the OOIP in core flooding experiments. ([[2019-alghamdi|AlGhamdi, 2019, p. 18]])
- The second formulation performed poorly, with incremental oil recovery from surfactant flooding reaching only around 10%, and a total recovery of 55%. ([[2019-alghamdi|AlGhamdi, 2019, p. 18]])
- The third formulation (rhamnolipid/lecithin) performed best among the three in experiments, with a tertiary recovery of 24% and nearly 70% total recovery. ([[2019-alghamdi|AlGhamdi, 2019, p. 18]])
- Simulation results for the three formulations were 26%, 12%, and 24% for tertiary oil recoveries, and 63%, 55%, and 67% for total oil recoveries, matching experimental values well. ([[2019-alghamdi|AlGhamdi, 2019, p. 18]])
- Rhamnolipid was shown to reduce the surface tension of water from 72 mN/m to 29 mN/m or less in some studies. ([[2019-alghamdi|AlGhamdi, 2019, p. 30]])
- Rhamnolipid achieved an IFT of about 0.05 dynes/cm in carbonate reservoir experiments. ([[2019-alghamdi|AlGhamdi, 2019, p. 42]])
- APG-alcohol formulations were shown to reach ultra-low IFT values as low as 0.001 mN/m. ([[2019-alghamdi|AlGhamdi, 2019, p. 45]])
- A study using lecithin found it could recover 25% of the original oil in place (OOIP) in sand packs. ([[2019-alghamdi|AlGhamdi, 2019, p. 48]])
- The Rhamnolipid/butanol formulation (RB) developed a distinct microemulsion in all samples, with an optimal salinity of 10%. ([[2019-alghamdi|AlGhamdi, 2019, p. 70]])
- The Rhamnolipid/APG/butanol formulation (RAB) showed a stable microemulsion for salinities above 3%, with an optimum at 7%. ([[2019-alghamdi|AlGhamdi, 2019, p. 73]])
- The Rhamnolipid/lecithin formulation (RL) produced a stable microemulsion across the 1-10% salinity range, with an optimum of 5%. ([[2019-alghamdi|AlGhamdi, 2019, p. 75]])
- Crude oil viscosity was reduced from 19.8 cP at 25°C to 8.2 cP at 52°C. ([[2019-alghamdi|AlGhamdi, 2019, p. 77]])
- The IFT between crude oil and brine was measured at 23 dynes/cm. ([[2019-alghamdi|AlGhamdi, 2019, p. 78]])
- The Rhamnolipid/APG/butanol (RAB) formulation reached an IFT minimum of approximately 0.2 dyne/cm at 0.02 wt.% concentration. ([[2019-alghamdi|AlGhamdi, 2019, p. 101]])
- The Rhamnolipid/lecithin (RL) formulation achieved a very low IFT of approximately 0.02 dyne/cm at 0.02 wt.% concentration. ([[2019-alghamdi|AlGhamdi, 2019, p. 103]])

## Limitations

The study used NaCl brine instead of real reservoir brine and did not account for surfactant adsorption or specific Saudi Arabian reservoir conditions (temperature/pressure).

## Recommendations and Future Work

Use real reservoir brine; test formulations in actual Saudi Arabian reservoirs; test in carbonate reservoirs; conduct surfactant adsorption studies; perform sensitivity analyses on simulation models.
