# Microseismic Modeling of Hydraulic Fracturing and Analysis of Location and Detectability Uncertainties

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the uncertainties in microseismic monitoring for hydraulic fracturing, focusing on signal processing techniques and hypocenter location accuracy. It evaluates various filtering methods (Butterworth), signal enhancement operators (Cross Product 'M' and Transformed Spectrogram 'S'), and iterative relocation algorithms (Geiger's method) to improve source localization.

---

## Research Problem

Microseismic event location uncertainty is a significant challenge in interpreting hydraulic fracturing data; inaccurate velocity models (especially those not accounting for anisotropy) and poor signal-to-noise ratios can lead to 'fuzzy' clouds instead of clear fracture trends, hindering the evaluation of stimulated reservoir volume.

## Objectives

- Evaluate different microseismic monitoring configurations (surface array, downhole array, and hybrid systems).
- Analyze the impact of velocity structure complexity and anisotropy on hypocenter location accuracy.
- Quantify uncertainties in microseismic event detection and localization using forward modeling.
- Calibrate velocity models using perforation shot data to improve location precision.
- Understand different sources of uncertainties associated with passive seismic data processing and estimation of hypocenters.
- Evaluate the impact of velocity model accuracy on the determination of arrival times and hypocenter locations.
- Analyze the effects of anisotropy (specifically Transverse Isotropy) on microseismic location estimation.
- Assess the role of signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and acquisition geometry in data quality.
- Evaluate the impact of different monitoring configurations (surface-only, downhole-only, and combined) on microseismic location accuracy.
- Assess the effect of velocity model complexity (1D coarse vs. 1D fine vs. 3D coarse) on hypocenter localization.
- Quantify the influence of signal-to-noise ratio and sensor spacing on detection and location precision.
- Evaluate the impact of different signal processing techniques on microseismic event detection.
- Analyze the effect of various filtering methods (Butterworth band-pass) on P and S wave identification.
- Implement and compare signal enhancement operators (M and S) for improving signal-to-noise ratio (SNR).
- Assess the accuracy of hypocenter location using iterative algorithms like Geiger's method.
- Calibrate velocity models using perforation shot data to minimize arrival time misfit.
- Evaluate the impact of isotropic vs. anisotropic velocity models on microseismic hypocenter locations.
- Quantify the effects of different monitoring geometries (vertical vs. horizontal) on location uncertainty.
- Assess the sensitivity of hypocenter locations to specific variations in P-wave and S-wave velocities.
- Analyze the impact of geological complexity (lithology changes from shale to sandstone) on ray path modeling.
- Evaluate the impact of microseismic location uncertainties on interpreted fracture geometries.
- Analyze the role of acquisition geometry and velocity model accuracy in improving microseismic event location.
- Investigate signal enhancement techniques for better detection of microseismic events.

## Methods

The study employs a forward modeling approach using Fermat's principle-based ray tracing (Cat3D) and the Discrete Wave Number method to simulate seismic wave propagation. A hypocenter inversion algorithm, Hypo3D, utilizing a shrinking grid technique, was used to relocate events. Three distinct velocity models were constructed: Model A (1D coarse, 7 layers), Model B (1D fine, 62 layers), and Model C (3D coarse, 7 layers). The study compares these models against three monitoring configurations: surface-only, downhole-only, and a combined system.

## Data and Materials

The study utilizes microseismic data from multiple hydraulic fracturing stages in the Qusaiba Member. It includes data from three monitoring configurations: a surface array, a downhole array, and a hybrid system. The analysis involves 10-12 specific fracture stages (e.g., stages 5-10) to compare isotropic vs. anisotropic velocity models.

## Key Findings

- Downhole monitoring arrays provide superior control on vertical components compared to surface arrays in certain geometries. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 100]])
- The use of a hybrid monitoring system (surface and downhole) provides the best balance for capturing both horizontal and vertical components. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 102]])
- Uncertainties in velocity structure significantly impact hypocenter location; even small changes in P or S wave speeds (e.g., 5-10%) lead to measurable relocation errors. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 104]])
- Anisotropic models (VTI, HTI, TTI) significantly improve the accuracy of perforation shot locations compared to isotropic models. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 95]])
- Signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) varies across different monitoring systems, impacting the detectability of events in high-noise environments. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 53]])
- The STA/LTA algorithm is used to identify and flag microseismic events by comparing short-term and long-term signal averages. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 71]])
- Microseismic data provides critical information for optimizing hydraulic stimulation operations and estimating stimulated rock volume. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 20]])
- Velocity model and acquisition geometry settings are the main sources of uncertainty in microseismic data interpretation. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 20]])
- Transverse Isotropy (TI) velocity model calibration is a suitable solution to correct for anisotropy effects on hypocenter location estimation. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 20]])
- The Qusaiba Member contains up to 75 m of hot shale with total organic carbon as high as 14 percent. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 32]])
- A cutoff value of 150 API gamma-ray units distinguishes potential source shale from non-source shale in the Qusaiba Member. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 34]])
- Microseismic events are typically small earthquakes with magnitudes between -4 and +2 on the Richter scale. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 28]])
- The inclusion of surface receivers in the inversion process significantly improves location accuracy, with average errors in hypocentral coordinates falling below 20m for both vertical (Δz) and radial (Δr) directions in models A and C. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 69]])
- Errors are notably larger when using only borehole receivers or when utilizing the very fine Model B, indicating that high-resolution velocity models without sufficient spatial diversity may not improve localization as much as diverse sensor geometry. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 69]])
- Note: The text mentions a significant difference in Vp/Vs ratio at the boundaries of the target formation (interfaces 3 and 4), which is a primary cause for errors in time origin estimates. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015]])

## Limitations

The study was limited by the small number of high-quality perforation shots available, with only Stage 9 providing sufficiently clear data for robust velocity model calibration. Additionally, surface data was excluded from some analyses due to extreme noise levels.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The thesis suggests that a full 3D Earth model, ideally evolving in time (4D), is necessary to achieve accurate hypocenter locations as hydraulic fracturing alters the rock's dynamic and elastic properties.
