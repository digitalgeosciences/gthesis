# Microseismic Modeling of Hydraulic Fracturing and Analysis of Location and Detectability Uncertainties

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of velocity model inaccuracies and monitoring geometry on the precision of microseismic event locations during hydraulic fracturing. It concludes that while vertical monitoring minimizes vertical uncertainty, accurate velocity modeling is critical to mitigate errors caused by lithological complexity.

---

## Research Problem

Microseismic location uncertainty is a major challenge in interpreting unconventional reservoir data; specifically, inaccurate velocity models and complex anisotropy in formations like the Qusaiba hot-shale can lead to significant errors in determining fracture geometry.

## Objectives

- Evaluate different sources of uncertainty associated with passive seismic data processing and estimation of hypocenters.
- Assess the impact of velocity model complexity (1D vs 3D, isotropic vs anisotropic) on hypocenter location accuracy.
- Assess the effect of acquisition geometry on signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and event detectability.
- Develop a workflow for refining microseismic data through spectral analysis, signal enhancement, and velocity model calibration.
- Evaluate different forward modeling techniques (Fermat's ray tracing, discrete wavenumber) for microseismic signal calculation.
- Quantify the effects of monitoring geometry (surface-only, borehole-only, combined) on location uncertainty.

## Methods

The study employs forward modeling using Fermat's ray tracing (Cat3D) and discrete wavenumber methods to simulate microseismic signals. Hypocenter inversion is performed using the Geiger method (a Gauss-Newton based iterative approach). Signal processing includes Butterworth bandpass filtering (35-200 Hz), cross-product operators (M), and transformed spectrogram analysis (S) for signal enhancement. Velocity models are calibrated using perforation shots, transitioning from isotropic to transverse isotropic (TI) models incorporating Thomsen parameters ($\epsilon$, $\delta$, $\gamma$) derived from VSP and sonic logs.

## Data and Materials

The study utilizes microseismic data from a multi-stage horizontal well stimulation. Data includes 12 surface seismic lines, a 7x7 surface patch grid, and multiple borehole arrays (shallow and deep). Calibration data consists of perforation shots from at least 9 stages.

## Key Findings

- The Qusaiba hot-shale is an organic-rich shale interval of the Qalibah formation, with TOC as high as 14% and a thickness of up to 75m. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 32]])
- A cutoff value of 150 API gamma-ray units distinguishes potential source shale from non-source shale in the Qusaiba Member. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 34]])
- The study identifies velocity model and acquisition geometry as the primary sources of uncertainty in microseismic data interpretation. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 20]])
- Transverse Isotropy (TI) velocity model calibration is identified as the most suitable solution to correct for anisotropy effects on hypocenter location estimation. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 20]])
- Signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) is a critical factor; signals falling below background noise levels cannot be measured, impacting detectability. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 41]])
- The use of a cross-product operator for signal enhancement was shown to improve the identification of P and S waves in noisy data. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 68]])
- An inaccurate velocity model can lead to hypocenter location errors of several hundred feet. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 47]])
- The survey included a grid of 37 shallow wells providing coverage for signals propagating between 20m and 182m depths. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 50]])
- The inclusion of surface receivers in the inversion process significantly reduces hypocenter location errors compared to using only borehole sensors, particularly in the horizontal dimensions. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 69]])
- A 3D coarse model (Model C) provided more accurate results than a 1D fine model (Model B), which showed high error values (e.g., $\Delta r$ of 506m for some cases). ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 70]])
- The use of an anisotropic initial velocity model significantly improved the fit between observed and modeled times compared to isotropic models. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 94]])
- High values of $\gamma$ (gamma) in the target zone, as identified by advanced sonic tools, were found to be a primary cause for large horizontal shifts when using isotropic models. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 110]])
- A manual waveform pick correction on just two events resulted in a location shift of over 170m, highlighting the sensitivity of hypocenter locations to picking errors. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 99]])
- Vertical monitoring configurations provide superior control on vertical components with lower uncertainty compared to horizontal or hybrid setups. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 120]])
- Model #1 (higher Vp/Vs ratio due to +5% P-wave and -5% S-wave) shifted hypocenters closer to the monitoring well, while Model #2 (lower Vp/Vs ratio due to +10% S-wave) shifted them further away. ([[2015-own|Own, 2015, p. 126]])

## Limitations

The study notes that the number of available perforation shots was limited (only Stage 9 provided high SNR, clear data). Additionally, surface data was excluded from some parts of the analysis due to high ambient noise and low SNR caused by nearby equipment.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future research should incorporate more surface data into the processing workflow despite current noise challenges. The study also suggests that a full 3D model evolving in time (4D) would be ideal for accurate hypocenter location.
