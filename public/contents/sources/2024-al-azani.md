# EXPERIMENTAL EVALUATION OF OIL RECOVERY FROM CARBONATE ROCKS BY GEMINI SURFACTANTS

**Summary**: The thesis evaluates the oil recovery performance of in-house synthesized ethoxylated quaternary ammonium cationic Gemini (GS) and betaine-type zwitterionic surfactants (ZIS) in carbonate rocks under high temperature and high salinity (HTHS) conditions. Results show that both GS and ZIS significantly improved oil recovery by reducing interfacial tension and altering rock wettability compared to a standard surfactant formulation.

---

## Research Problem

Carbonate reservoirs in the Middle East present challenges for oil recovery due to high heterogeneity, oil-wet nature, and harsh conditions (high temperature/salinity). There is a need for effective surfactants that can withstand these conditions and improve recovery through wettability alteration.

## Objectives

- Evaluate surfactants synthesized in-house as agents for EOR in carbonate reservoirs including Middle East reservoirs through coreflooding experiments.
- Establish trends between oil recovery and the different influencing parameters including injection rate and permeability.
- Investigate the synergistic effect of different methods/additive with the in-house synthesized Gemini surfactant.
- Evaluate the performance of in-house synthesized Gemini and zwitterionic surfactants as single components in carbonate rocks.
- Assess the impact of these surfactants on oil/water interfacial tension (IFT) and rock wettability.
- Compare the performance of GS and ZIS against a standard surfactant formulation (SF).

## Methods

The study utilized coreflooding experiments in a VINCI Technologies CFS700 system at 100°C and 3,000-4,000 psi. Rock samples from the Dam formation were characterized using XRD, QEMSCAN, and micro-CT scanning. Coreflooding involved a two-stage process: seawater (SW) flooding followed by GS solution flooding (2,500 ppm). NMR measurements (using an Oxford Geospec2 instrument) were performed at various stages to track oil displacement. HPLC with ELSD was used for effluent analysis to determine surfactant concentration and dynamic retention. Contact angle measurements were conducted using a Siemens' Attension Theta Optical Tensiometer.

## Data and Materials

The study used four Indiana limestone samples (IL-53, IL-22, IL-4, IL-17) with varying properties; three specific carbonate cores were analyzed for permeability effects (IMP-M2, IL-22, IHP-HP1). Brines included synthetic seawater (SW) at 57,745 ppm and formation water (FW) at 213,768 ppm. Crude oil was sourced from a Middle Eastern carbonate reservoir.

## Key Findings

- Both GS and ZIS surfactants achieved approximately 11–12% recovery of initial oil in the core (OOIP) when injected post-seawater flooding on Indiana limestone. ([[2024-al-azani|Al-Azani, 2024, p. 22]])
- Adding a one-pore-volume slug of standard acrylamide copolymer at 2000 ppm increased oil recovery to around 16–17% OOIP for both GS and ZIS surfactants. ([[2024-al-azani|Al-Azani, 2024, p. 22]])
- The recovery factor by surfactant flooding was higher at lower injection rates (0.10 and 0.25 cc/min), with 0.25 cc/min being the optimum rate tested for balancing recovery and retention. ([[2024-al-azani|Al-Azani, 2024, p. 23]])
- Surfactant floods led to additional recoveries of 31%, 23%, and 19% for low (10 mD), medium (100 mD), and high-permeability (>1,000 mD) samples, respectively. ([[2024-al-azani|Al-Azani, 2024, p. 23]])
- NMR analysis confirmed that dynamic retention was highest in the lowest permeability rocks and lowest in the highest permeability rock. ([[2024-al-azani|Al-Azani, 2024, p. 23]])
- Coreflooding on a Saudi Arabian outcrop carbonate plug with GS surfactant in seawater resulted in an additional oil recovery of around 23% OOIP with a dynamic retention of 0.37 mg/g-rock. ([[2024-al-azani|Al-Azani, 2024, p. 24]])
- Chemical flooding with GS/LowSal water yielded 26.3% OOIP extra oil recovery, with a dynamic retention of 0.35 mg/g-rock. ([[2024-al-azani|Al-Azani, 2024, p. 24]])
- Incorporating DTPA into the process led to the largest additional oil recovery among all tested solutions, approximately 37% OOIP, with a dynamic retention of 0.41 mg/g-rock. ([[2024-al-azani|Al-Azani, 2024, p. 24]])
- Adding methylene blue with the GS slowed down the recovery process, requiring a larger volume of chemical solution. ([[2024-al-azani|Al-Azani, 2024, p. 24]])
- The GS surfactant reduced oil/water IFT to less than 1.0 mN/m across a temperature range of 25-80 °C. ([[2024-al-azani|Al-Azani, 2024, p. 96]])
- The ZIS surfactant reduced oil/water IFT to less than 1.0 mN/m at 2,500 ppm concentration. ([[2024-al-azani|Al-Azani, 2024, p. 96]])
- GS treatment resulted in a contact angle increase from ~26° to ~65°, indicating a shift toward intermediate-wet conditions. ([[2024-al-azani|Al-Azani, 2024, p. 95]])
- ZIS treatment resulted in a contact angle of approximately 75.7°, shifting the rock surface to an intermediate-wet state. ([[2024-al-azani|Al-Azani, 2024, p. 95]])
- The standard formulation (SF) showed only a minor change in contact angle (to ~45°), indicating weak wettibility alteration. ([[2024-al-azani|Al-Azani, 2024, p. 95]])
- Seawater flooding produced an initial oil recovery of 54.3% to 56.9% OOIP across all samples. ([[2024-al-azani|Al-Azani, 2024, p. 97]])

## Limitations

The study notes that some surfactant may partition into the crude oil, which is not accounted for as dynamic retention. Additionally, it mentions that while high-permeability rocks were studied, the full range of carbonate heterogeneity was limited to three specific samples.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Perform static adsorption tests under more realistic reservoir conditions (with crude oil).
- Optimize the performance of DTPA and Methylene blue.
- Study the synergistic impact of polymers with GS.
- Utilize advanced imaging like dynamic micro-CT or MRI to visualize flow behavior.
