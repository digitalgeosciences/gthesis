# SEQUENCE STRATIGRAPHIC ANALYSIS AND POROSITY EVALUATION OF A FIELD USING 3D SEISMIC AND WELL LOGS: AN EXAMPLE FROM THE NIGER DELTA, NIGERIA

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the sequence stratigraphic evolution and reservoir potential of the Otu field in the onshore Niger Delta using 3D seismic data and wireline logs from 13 wells. It identifies three major sequences bounded by four erosional unconformities (SB1-SB4) and evaluates porosity and reservoir quality across various sand bodies.

---

## Research Problem

The need to understand the complex relationship between structural elements and stratigraphic components in the Otu field, where previous data on these relationships were not well defined, to improve prediction of reservoir quality and porosity distribution.

## Objectives

- Identify different facies and their depositional environments
- Establish the tectono-stratigraphic evolution and structural styles of Otu field
- Establish sequence stratigraphic model within the study area
- Evaluate the porosity and predict reservoir quality in field scale
- Understand the sequence stratigraphic evolution of the Otu field.
- Build a sequence stratigraphic model for the Otu field using 3D seismic data and well logs.

## Methods

The study utilized 3D seismic data (421 sq. km coverage, 25m bin spacing) and wireline logs (Gamma Ray, Resistivity, Density, Neutron) from 13 wells. Lithostratigraphy was determined using a GR cut-off of 35 API. Facies were identified based on GR log patterns (Blocky, Coarsening upward, Serrated, Bell-shaped). Sequence stratigraphy followed the Hunt and Tucker model to identify MFSs and SBs. Seismic geomorphology and attributes like spectral decomposition and coherence were used to map channel systems and structural features. Porosity was evaluated by cross-plotting density and neutron logs using Techlog software.

## Data and Materials

Data included 3D seismic volumes, wireline logs from thirteen (13) wells in the Otu field, and lithostratigraphic data for the Agbada formation. Five specific reservoir units (A, B, I, J, K) were evaluated based on porosity measurements.

## Key Findings

- The subsurface facies reveal three sequences bounded by four sequence boundaries (SB1 to SB4) interpreted as erosional unconformities. ([[2015-sanuade|Sanuade, 2015, p. 17]])
- Four sequence boundaries (SB1 to SB4) and three maximum flooding surfaces (MFS1, MFS2, MFS3) were identified as key stratigraphic markers. ([[2015-sanuade|Sanuade, 2015, p. 73]])
- Fourteen reservoir sands (Sand A to Sand N) were identified in Otu field; Sand A is the shallowest and Sand N is the deepest. ([[2015-sanuade|Sanuade, 2015, p. 46]])
- page_number_reference_for_finding_13_wells_used_in_study_is_page_17 ([[2015-sanuade|Sanuade, 2015]])
- Serrated log patterns were interpreted as tidal flat deposits, while blocky patterns were interpreted as fluvial channel deposits. ([[2015-sanuade|Sanuade, 2015, p. 58]])
- The Agbada formation is approximately 3,700 m thick and contains a paralic sequence of interbedded sands and shales. ([[2015-sanuade|Sanuade, 2015, p. 26]])
- A sedimentary system with three distinct sequences was identified, where LSTs are fluvial channel sands, FSSTs are clinoform-like deltaic wedges, and TSTs consist mainly of marine shales. ([[2015-sanuade|Sanuade, 2015, p. 18]])
- Spectral decomposition at 10Hz/40Hz revealed a meandering channel belt in the NE-SW direction on SB-1. ([[2015-sanuade|Sanuade, 2015, p. 89]])
- The study identified three major listric faults (F3, F6, and F7) and several other structural features impacting reservoir continuity. ([[2015-sanuade|Sanuade, 2015, p. 66]])
- Lithofacies analysis revealed four types: Shaly Sandstone (coarsening upward), Sandstone (blocky), Mudrock (retrogradational), and Heterolith (bell-shaped). ([[2015-sanuade|Sanuade, 2015, p. 67]])
- Sequence one is interpreted as a transitional environment; sequence two as shallow marine; and sequence three as transitioning from transitional to shallow marine. ([[2015-sanuade|Sanuade, 2015, p. 100]])
- The study identified five potential reservoirs (A, B, I, J, K) with average effective porosities ranging from 0.220 to 0.313. ([[2015-sanuade|Sanuade, 2015, p. 99]])
- Reservoir I exhibited the highest quality with an average effective porosity of 0.298 (range 0.298-0.329), classified as 'excellent'. ([[2015-sanuade|Sanuade, 2015, p. 100]])
- Structural analysis identified three major listric normal faults (F3, F6, and F7) trending NW-SE, with F6 providing a significant hanging wall structural closure. ([[2015-sanuade|Sanuade, 2015, p. 84]])
- Seismic geomorphology revealed that channel belts are associated with continental to transitional environments, while individual channels are linked to shallow marine deposits. ([[2015-sanuade|Sanuade, 2015, p. 105]])

## Limitations

No core samples were available from the Otu field, and porosity evaluation was not possible for five wells (Otu 6, 8, 19, 46, 56) due to missing log data.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The author recommends obtaining additional biostratigraphic, core, and permeability data to improve the reliability of interpretations in the field.
