# New Insights into Asphaltene Precipitation/Deposition and Mitigation in Reservoir Rocks

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of rock mineralogy and well operations on asphaltene behavior, specifically focusing on how contact with minerals like kaolinite, illite, and chlorite alters asphaltene structure and remediation efficiency. It utilizes zeta-potential measurements to evaluate surface chemistry and various spectroscopic techniques (XRD, Raman, FTIR) to characterize the structural changes in asphaltenes after interaction with different mineral types.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the problem of asphaltene-induced flow assurance issues and wettibility alterations caused by interactions between crude oil components and iron minerals in reservoir rocks. Specifically, it explores how changes in pH and ionic composition during operations like acidizing or seawater injection affect the surface charge of these minerals, which determines their potential to act as sites for asphaltene adsorption.

## Objectives

- To establish the concept of potential determining minerals in the asphaltene problem.
- To investigate the applicability of sacrificial fluid as an asphaltene mitigation strategy.
- Review and compare various thermodynamic models for predicting asphaltene precipitation.
- Evaluate different experimental techniques used to quantify and visualize asphaltene behavior.
- Analyze the impact of gas injection and other factors on asphaltene stability.
- Investigate mitigation strategies for asphaltene-related issues in production.

## Methods

Asphaltenes were extracted from five crude oils (Arab Extra Light, Arab Light, Arab Medium, Arab Heavy) and subjected to a 6-week aging process with various rock minerals (Barea Grey, Bandera Brown, Guelph Dolomite, Indiana Limestone, Desert Pink). Characterization included XRF for heavy metal content, Rock-Eval analysis for organic sulfur/carbon and hydrogen index, CHNS for elemental composition, FTIR and Raman spectroscopy for functional groups and aromatic sheet dimensions, and XRD to determine crystallite parameters (dm, dr, La, etc.). Zeta-potential was measured across various pH levels and salt concentrations to assess clay mineral surface charge. Remediation efficiency was quantified by measuring the density increase of xylene after 24 hours of exposure to asphaltene samples.

## Data and Materials

5 crude oil samples (Arab Extra Light, Arab Light, Arab Medium, Arab Heavy) from Berri, Ghawar, Khursaniya, Qatif, and Safaniya fields. 5 rock mineral types (Barea Grey, Bandera Brown, Guelph Dolomite, Indiana Limestone, Desert Pink). Zeta-potential measurements for 4 clay minerals (kaolinite, montmorillonite, illite, chlorite) in various salt solutions.

## Key Findings

- The investigation of ion-specific rock-fluid interactions offers great potential in finding a solution to the asphaltene problem by identifying minerals that contribute most to deposition. ([[2023-suleiman|Suleiman, 2023, p. 33]])
- Sacrificial fluids can mitigate asphaltene adsorption by coating the rock surface and disengaging the interaction between asphaltenes and minerals. ([[2023-suleiman|Suleiman, 2023, p. 39]])
- An optimal concentration exists for treatment fluids where asphaltene interaction is effectively mitigated. ([[2023-suleiman|Suleiman, 2023, p. 34]])
- Surface energy evaluation is a critical tool to screen fluids for use in asphaltene mitigation. ([[2023-suleiman|Suleiman, 2023, p. 34]])
- The method of implementation (squeeze operation vs. continuous injection) is critical to the success of remediation. ([[2023-suleiman|Suleiman, 2023, p. 34]])
- Asphaltene is a polyaromatic molecule with heteroatoms and aliphatic chains, where hydrogen content typically ranges from 82 ± 3% and carbon from 8.1 ± 0.7%. ([[2023-suleiman|Suleiman, 2023, p. 48]])
- A constant hydrogen/carbon ratio of approximately 1.2 is observed across different crude oil asphaltene samples. ([[2023-suleiman|Suleiman, 2023, p. 49]])
- Heteroatoms (Oxygen, Sulfur, Nitrogen) are found in concentrations of 0.3-4.9%, 0.3-10%, and 0.6-3.3% respectively. ([[2023-suleiman|Suleiman, 2023, p. 49]])
- The PC-SAFT model is more robust than cubic equations of state (like PR or SRK) for predicting asphaltene precipitation across varying precipitant concentrations and solvent types. ([[2023-suleiman|Suleiman, 2023, p. 74]])
- CPA models provide reliable predictions for both injection and non-injection cases, while PC-SAFT requires improvement for specific injection scenarios to increase robustness. ([[2023-suleiman|Suleiman, 2023, p. 64]])
- The colloidal approach assumes asphaltene is a colloid stabilized by resins; if the resin content falls below a critical level, precipitation occurs. ([[2023-suleiman|Suleiman, 2023, p. 66]])
- Asphaltene stability can be quantified using indices like Colloidal Instability Index (CII), where values ≥0.9 indicate unstable crude and ≤0.7 indicate stable crude. ([[2023-suleiman|Suleiman, 2023, p. 70]])
- The presence of heteroatoms in asphaltene molecules significantly influences their aggregation behavior and electrical charge, affecting deposition control. ([[2023-suleiman|Suleiman, 2023, p. 75]])
- Asphaltene precipitation is not an instantaneous process but a transient one requiring time for stabilization, evidenced by changes in contact angle during pressure depletion. ([[2023-suleiman|Suleiman, 2023, p. 77]])
- The gravimetry technique's accuracy is limited by the selection of pressure steps and the concentration measurement method, while it requires large volumes of fluid. ([[2023-suleiman|Suleiman, 2023, p. 78]])

## Limitations

The study notes that while 5 wt.% EDTA is a standard, it may not be sufficient to control the precipitation of certain minerals like hematite in all scenarios. Additionally, the complexity of determining optimal ion-engineered fluid compositions for different mineral types makes it difficult to ensure consistent results across various formations.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future research should focus on rock-fluid interaction perspectives, specifically how contacting minerals' surface chemistry and charge influence asphaltene stability. Development of more efficient production management strategies and field trials for mitigation techniques like chemical washes or specialized solvents are recommended.
