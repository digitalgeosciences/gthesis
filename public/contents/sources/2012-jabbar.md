# EFFECT OF BRINE COMPOSITION ON WETTABILITY ALTERATION OF CARBONATE ROCKS IN THE PRESENCE OF POLAR COMPOUNDS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of brine salinity and specific ion concentrations (SO42-, Ca2+, Mg2+) on the wettability of carbonate rocks in the presence of polar compounds (short and long chain carboxylic acids). Using contact angle measurements and surface characterization, it concludes that sulfate and magnesium ions are key drivers for shifting carbonate surfaces toward a more water-wet state.

---

## Research Problem

The challenge of improving oil recovery in carbonate reservoirs, which are typically oil-wet or neutral-wet due to the adsorption of polar compounds (carboxylic acids) on the rock surface. Specifically, it seeks to identify which ions in brine (SO42-, Ca2+, Mg2+) and what concentrations are effective for wettability alteration.

## Objectives

- Identify the potential determining ions in the brine that can alter the wettability of carbonate rock surfaces to a more water-wet condition.
- Understand rock/fluids interactions responsible for the wettability alteration of carbonate rocks.
- Evaluate the effect of acid chain length on the initial wettibility of calcite crystals.
- Determine the impact of brine salinity and specific ion concentrations (SO4, Ca, Mg) on the desorption of carboxylic acids.
- Compare the effectiveness of Arabian Gulf seawater versus modified brines in altering carbonate rock wettability.
- Evaluate the effect of different brine compositions on the contact angle of calcite and carbonate rocks.

## Methods

The study utilized calcite crystals (Iceland Spar) and carbonate rocks from the Saudi Arab-D outcrop. Two types of model oils were prepared using heptanoic acid (short chain) and stearic acid (long chain) in toluene, both with a total acid number (TAN) of 2. Brines were synthesized to vary concentrations of Na+, Ca2+, Mg2+, and SO42-. Wettibility was evaluated using the sessile drop technique with an NRL contact angle Goniometer (model DSA-100). Surface characterization included XRD for mineralogy and SEM-EDX for surface analysis after treatment with various brines.

## Data and Materials

Calcite crystals, carbonate rocks from Saudi Arab-D outcrop; two types of model oils (heptanoic acid/toluene and stearic acid/toluene) both with TAN=2; a suite of 10 different brine formulations (SWME, SWME*, SWME*0S, SWME*2S, SWME*4S, SWME*0Ca, SWME*2Ca, SWME*4Ca, SWME*0Mg, SWME*2Mg, SWME*4Mg).

## Key Findings

- Long chain fatty acid (Stearic Acid) shows stronger adsorption onto the calcite surface from the oil phase compared to short chain (Heptanoic Acid), as confirmed by measured contact angles. ([[2012-jabbar|JABBAR, 2012, p. 13]])
- Twice diluted Arabian Gulf seawater was found to be a less efficient EOR fluid for wettability alteration compared to Arabian Gulf seawater. ([[2012-jabbar|JABBAR, 2012, p. 13]])
- Significant wettability alteration was observed for the twice diluted Arabian Gulf seawater with higher concentrations of SO42- and Mg2+. ([[2012-jabbar|JABBAR, 2012, p. 13]])
- The presence of sulfate (SO42-) is a key driver; it can act as a wettability modifier alone, without any expensive surfactant present. ([[2012-jabbar|JABBAR, 2012, p. 30]])
- Magnesium ions (Mg2+) are important potential determining ions; they provide a more stable water film and require higher capillary pressure to rupture the water film as temperature increases. ([[2012-jabbar|JABBAR, 2012, p. 31]])
- At temperatures greater than 90-100°C, Mg2+ ions are able to substitute Ca2+ ions from the chalk surface, releasing the Ca2+ and R-COO- compound from the surface. ([[2012-jabbar|JABBAR, 2012, p. 31]])
- The study confirmed that the concentration of active ions (SO42-, Ca2+, Mg2+) is critical for wettibility alteration; a decrease in these concentrations led to a drastic decrease in change of oil saturation. ([[2012-jabbar|JABBAR, 2012, p. 33]])
- Stearic acid (SA) resulted in higher contact angles than heptanoic acid (HA), confirming that longer carbon chains increase oil-wetness. ([[2012-jabbar|JABBAR, 2012, p. 58]])
- The addition of sulfate ions significantly reduced the contact angle on calcite crystals, with SWME*4S yielding a lower value (21.1°) than SWME*2S (22.6%). ([[2012-jabbar|JABBAR, 2012, p. 63]])
- Calcium ions in the presence of sulfate and magnesium were found to reduce contact angles on calcite crystals, with SWME*4Ca resulting in a contact angle of 31.3°. ([[2012-jabbar|JABBAR, 2012, p. 65]])
- Magnesium ion concentration had an insignificant effect on the wettibility of calcite crystals (e.g., 37.0° to 38.2° for different concentrations). ([[2012-jabbar|JABBAR, 2012, p. 66]])
- On carbonate rock surfaces, magnesium ions did show a significant impact, with higher concentrations reducing the contact angle from 38.5° to 29.6%. ([[2012-jabbar|JABBAR, 2012, p. 74]])
- Dilution of Arabian Gulf seawater (SWME) resulted in an increase in contact angle compared to full-strength SWME, indicating that higher ion concentrations are necessary for effective wettibility alteration. ([[2012-jabbar|JABBAR, 2012, p. 83]])
- Long chain fatty acid (SA) altered calcite wettability to neutral-wet, while short chain fatty acid (HA) resulted in a less water-wet state. ([[2012-jabbar|JABBAR, 2012, p. 106]])
- Arabian Gulf seawater (SWME) was more effective in changing the wettibility of calcite and carbonate surfaces toward water-wet compared to twice diluted Arabian Gulf seawater (SWME*). ([[2012-jabbar|JABBAR, 2012, p. 107]])

## Limitations

The study notes that some measurements were difficult to capture when contact angles fell below 12° due to drop instability, leading to higher uncertainty in those specific data points.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Perform contact angle measurements at higher temperature and pressure to mimic reservoir conditions; test on dolomite samples; use actual crude oil from specific reservoirs; vary brine pH levels.
