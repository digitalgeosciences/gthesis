# Evaluation of Empirically Derived PVT Properties for Oil Field Brines

**Summary**: The thesis evaluates existing empirical correlations for predicting the physical properties of oilfield brines, specifically gas solubility and viscosity. It also develops new correlation models based on a collection of 422 data points from various published sources to improve accuracy in reservoir engineering calculations.

---

## Research Problem

Existing correlations for brine gas solubility and viscosity often use complex coefficients or are based on limited datasets (like methane-only models), leading to poor accuracy when applied to diverse oilfield conditions. There is a need for simpler, more robust empirical models that account for temperature, pressure, and salinity.

## Objectives

- Evaluate significant existing correlations for brine-gas solubility and brine viscosity.
- Develop new correlation models for predicting physical properties of oilfield waters using a compiled dataset of 422 points.
- Perform statistical comparison between newly developed and existing correlations to determine effectiveness.
- Evaluate existing brine PVT correlations using a combined experimental dataset.
- Develop new empirical models for gas solubility in brine.
- Compare the accuracy of newly developed models against established industry correlations (e.g., McCain, Jones, Fabuss & Korosi).

## Methods

The study utilizes data acquisition from published literature in chemical, mechanical, and petroleum engineering fields. It focuses on NaCl brine solutions as the primary model for oil_field brines. The methodology involves: 1) Compiling a dataset of pure water-gas solubility (70 points) and brine-gas solubility (56 points). 2) Developing new mathematical models for gas solubility and viscosity based on these datasets. 3) Performing statistical analysis, including mean percentage relative error, standard deviation, and coefficient of determination, to compare the performance of newly developed correlations against existing ones like those by Dodson & Standing, McKetta & Katz, and others.

## Data and Materials

The study utilizes a combined dataset of 271 data points for brine viscosity (from Fabuss & Korosi, Kestin et al., and Ersharghi et al.) and a dataset of 25 points for pure water viscosity. Gas solubility data from multiple sources were used to evaluate existing correlations like McCain and Jones.

## Key Findings

- The study identified that gas solubility in brine decreases as total solid content increases. ([[1996-mehmood|Mehmood, 1996, p. 33]])
- Pure water viscosity was found to decrease by approximately half as reservoir pressure dropped from 12,000 to 1,000 psia. ([[1996-mehmood|Mehmood, 1996, p. 28]])
- The study noted that the effect of pressure on brine viscosity is negligible for most practical purposes compared to temperature. ([[1996-mehmood|Mehmood, 1996]])

- The indirect approach (predicting water properties first, then applying a salinity correction factor) provides more reliable estimates and easier comparison with pure water than the direct approach. ([[1996-mehmood|Mehmood, 1996, p. 51]])
- A new gas solubility model developed in this study achieved an average absolute error of 3.86% and a standard deviation of 4.49 when using a salinity correction factor. ([[1996-mehmood|Mehmood, 1996, p. 50]])
- The modified Bingham viscosity model for pure water yielded a correlation coefficient of 0.999, an absolute error of 0.36%, and a standard deviation of 0.44. ([[1996-mehmood|Mehmood, 1996, p. 51]])
- The new brine viscosity model developed in this study achieved an average error of 6.29% with a standard deviation of .8, outperforming several existing models including those by Numbere et al. ([[1996-mehmood|Mehmood, 1996, p. 52]])
- Existing correlations using only methane to predict natural gas solubility are incapable of predicting the solubility of natural gas accurately in oilfield applications. ([[1996-mehmood|Mehmood, 1996, p. 68]])
- The effect of pressure on the estimation of brine viscosity can be safely neglected for standard oilfield operating conditions. ([[1996-mehmood|Mehmood, 1996, p. 71]])

## Limitations

At low pressure ranges (200-6000 psia), all correlations, including those in this study, show larger errors because the solubility of different gas components is not well characterized at low pressures.
