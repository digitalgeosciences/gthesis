# THE IMPACT OF ROCK TYPES ON ELECTRICAL PROPERTIES AND RESERVOIR FLUID SATURATION

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of rock types and multimodal pore systems on electrical properties (m and n exponents) and their subsequent effect on water saturation calculations in two reservoirs, Reservoir D and Reservoir F. By utilizing a hydraulic units approach to categorize rocks based on geological and petrophysical similarities, the study demonstrates that this method provides more accurate water saturation estimates compared to standard average-well methods.

---

## Research Problem

Standard petrophysical evaluations often use constant Archie parameters (m, n) for entire reservoirs, which can lead to inaccurate water saturation estimates because these values vary significantly based on rock type, pore geometry, and mineralogy. This is particularly problematic in reservoirs with heterogeneous lithologies or distinct hydraulic units.

## Objectives

- Investigate the variation of electrical parameters with rock type or hydraulic units.
- Assess the impact of using constant or variable electrical parameters to compute water saturation using Archie's equation on reserves estimation.
- Determine the impact of rock types on electrical properties (cementation exponent m and saturation exponent n).
- Evaluate the effectiveness of the hydraulic units technique in categorizing rocks with similar petrophysical properties.
- Compare water saturation results between standard well-synthesis methods and the hydraulic units approach for Reservoir D and Reservoir F.
- Quantify the potential increase in oil-in-place estimation using the hydraulic units method.

## Methods

The study utilized a multi-step workflow: 1) Core sample collection and preparation (cleaning with Toluene/Methanol, drying at 85°C). 2) Petrographic analysis via thin section (TS) and SEM-EDS to identify minerals and pore geometry. 3) Mineralogical analysis using XRD (bulk rock and clay fraction). 4) Physical property measurement including porosity, permeability (Klinkenberg), and mercury injection capillary pressure (MICP) to determine pore throat size distribution. 5. Hydraulic unit determination using the RQI/PHIZ method (Amaefule et al., 1993). 6. Determination of cementation factor (m) and saturation exponent (n) through laboratory measurements of core samples in brine-saturated conditions. 7. Comparison of water saturation results between a 'constant parameter' model and a 'variable parameter' model based on hydraulic units using re-processed open-hole logs.

## Data and Materials

The study analyzed 10 core samples from two reservoir intervals (Reservoir D and Reservoir F). Samples included: 1030, 1047, 1054, 1075, 1077, 1094, and 1110. Data included mineral weight percentages from XRD, pore throat distributions from mercury injection, and electrical properties (m, n) derived from core plug tests.

## Key Findings

- The use of hydraulic unit-based parameters resulted in a 5.3% decrease in water saturation in Reservoir-D compared to using single-value average electrical parameters. ([[2011-al-nasser|Al-Nasser, 2011, p. 11]])
- The use of hydraulic unit-based parameters resulted in a minor increase of 1% in water saturation across Reservoir-F. ([[2011-al-nasser|Al-Nasser, 2011, p. 12]])
- Gas permeability for samples in Hydraulic Unit 1 (HU1) ranged from 33 to 213 mD with an average of 77mD. ([[2011-al-nasser|Al-Nasser, 2011, p. 43]])
- Hydraulic unit 1 is characterized as Bioclastic Peloidal Grainstone, where pore geometry shows bimodality and is dominated by macro pores. ([[2011-al-nasser|Al-Nasser, 2011, p. 43]])
- The saturation exponent (n) varies from 1.7 for strongly water-wet to 3.0 for strongly oil-wet formations. ([[2011-al-nasser|Al-Nasser, 2011, p. 15]])
- Hydraulic unit 2 is characterized by a mean gas permeability of 4.3 mD, with values ranging from 1.6 to 9.3 mD, and is significantly impacted by dolomite cementation. ([[2011-al-nasser|Al-Nasser, 2011, p. 44]])
- Hydraulic unit 3 shows gas permeabilities ranging from 1.1 to 6.8 mD with an average of 2.8 mD, dominated by micro pores. ([[2011-al-nasser|Al-Nasser, 2011, p. 46]])
- XRD analysis showed that calcite is the most abundant mineral in Reservoir D (average 66%), while lower calcite content correlates with higher dolomite values. ([[2011-al-nasser|Al-Nasser, 2011, p. 47]])
- Cementation exponents (m) at ambient conditions were calculated between 1.91 and 2.07, while overburden conditions yielded m values between 2.06 and 2.21. ([[2011-al-nasser|Al-Nasser, 2011, p. 48]])
- The average cementation exponent (m) at ambient condition was 1.99, and the composite value at overburden condition was 2.11. ([[2011-al-nasser|Al-Nasser, 2011, p. 50]])
- Saturation exponents (n) for various samples ranged from 2.14 to 2.34 across different core plugs. ([[2011-al-nasser|Al-Nasser, 2011, p. 56]])
- The use of the hydraulic units approach in Reservoir D resulted in a 5.3% reduction in water saturation, equivalent to a 5.3% increase in oil-in-place estimation compared to standard methods. ([[2011-al-nasser|Al-Nasser, 2011, p. 67]])
- In Reservoir F, the hydraulic units approach showed only a minor difference of 1.1% in water saturation estimation compared to average well calculations. ([[2011-al-nasser|Al-Nasser, 2011, p. 67]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Use the hydraulic units methodology to calculate water saturation when variable m and n are suspected.
- Utilize hydraulic units to differentiate rock types based on their direct relationship to petrophysical properties like permeability.
- Implement systematic rock classification using the HU method to improve Sw estimation by assigning electrical parameters to specific unit characters.
- Use hydraulic unit classification to correlate reservoirs for initial indications of fluid flow across an area.
- Understand each hydraulic unit's character to inform completion strategies.
