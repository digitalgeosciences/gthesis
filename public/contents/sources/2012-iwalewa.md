# Groundwater Management Approach at KFUPM Campus Using Numerical Simulation

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the sustainability of the Umm Er Radhuma (UER) aquifer at the KFUPM campus by developing and calibrating groundwater flow and solute transport models. It evaluates three pumping scenarios to predict future water levels and Total Dissolved Solids (TDS) concentrations, concluding that 'Alternative Scheme II' is the most sustainable approach.

---

## Research Problem

Rapid development and population growth at KFUPM have led to increased groundwater consumption, potentially causing significant drawdown and saline intrusion in the Umm Er Radhuma aquifer. A systematic management approach is needed to balance water demand with sustainable extraction limits.

## Objectives

- To define the geological and hydrogeological settings of Umm Er Radhuma (UER) aquifer in the study area.
- To investigate the groundwater quality in terms of major ionic compositions.
- To investigate the historical changes in groundwater levels of Umm Er Radhuma aquifer system in the area.
- To develop a groundwater flow model and transport model for the aquifer system in the area, and to use this model to predict the future changes in groundwater levels and quality under three long-term pumping scenarios.
- To investigate possible environmental and health problems with respect to the hydrochemistry of the groundwater in the study area.
- Develop a 3D numerical model of the Umm Er Radhuma (UER) aquifer using MODFLOW to simulate groundwater flow.

## Methods

The study combined field sampling, laboratory analysis, and numerical modeling. Groundwater samples were collected from eight active wells in January 2012. Hydrochemical parameters (pH, EC, TDS) were measured immediately, while metals were analyzed using ICP-OES (EPA method 200.7). Ion chromatography was used for nitrate, chloride, and sulfate. Water quality classification was performed using Piper, Durov, and Stiff diagrams in Rockworks software. Numerical modeling included a Groundwater Flow Model and a Solute Transport Model to simulate three management scenarios over a 20-year period (2011-2030).

## Data and Materials

The study uses data from KFUPM Maintenance Department and Research Institute, including pumping records for 12 wells (one excluded), historical TDS values, and local hydrogeological parameters. The model domain covers approximately 4.8 km² with a grid of up to 52x69 cells in the transient state.

## Key Findings

- Groundwater pumping increased from 2.4 MCM in 1967 to 13.9 MCM in 2010, representing a 480% increase. ([[2012-iwalewa|Iwalewa, 2012, p. 15]])
- The average decline in water level due to pumping between 1967 and 2010 was approximately 8.5 m. ([[2012-iwalewa|Iwalewa, 2012, p. 15]])
- The Umm Er Radhuma (UER) aquifer is a karstified fractured bedrock aquifer with transmissivity ranging from 1 x 10^-4 to 1 x 10^1 m^2/s and hydraulic conductivity between 1 x 10^-7 and 1 x 10^-2 m/s. ([[2012-iwalewa|Iwalewa, 2012, p. 48]])
- Groundwater samples were classified as alkaline water with a prevailing sulfate-chloride signature, where Na+ and K+ are the dominant cations. ([[2012-iwalewa|Iwalewa, 2012, p. 16]])
- the order of abundance of the anions is Cl -> SO4^2- -> HCO3-. ([[2012-iwalewa|Iwalewa, 2012]])

- The Umm Er Radhuma (UER) aquifer shows average thickness of 85 m and hydraulic conductivities ranging from 50 to 350 m/day. ([[2012-iwalewa|Iwalewa, 2012, p. 54]])
- Steady-state calibration for the UER aquifer yielded a mean absolute error of 0.055 m and root mean square of 0.066 m, confirming high accuracy. ([[2012-iwalewa|Iwalewa, 2012, p. 80]])
- The steady-state water balance showed an inflow of 16,149 m3/day from the southwest and a well discharge of 6,541 m3/day. ([[2012-iwalewa|Iwalewa, 2012, p. 85]])
- Transient simulation (1967–2010) showed an average drawdown of 8.5 m in the UER aquifer. ([[2012-iwalewa|Iwalewa, 2012, p. 88]])
- The solute transport model for 1967 predicted TDS values between 2,450 and 3,120 mg/L (average 2,850 mg/L). ([[2012-iwalewa|Iwalewa, 2012, p. 108]])
- Simulated TDS at the end of 2010 ranged from 3,900 to 4,700 mg/L with an average of 4,200 mg/L. ([[2012-iwalewa|Iwalewa, 2012, p. 108]])
- Alternative Scheme I (high demand) resulted in a predicted drawdown of 5.4 m over the 20-year period (2011–2030). ([[2012-iwalewa|Iwalewa, 2012, p. 106]])
- Alternative Scheme II (conservative) resulted in an average drawdown of only 3 m over the 20-year period. ([[2012-iwalewa|Iwalewa, 2012, p. 108]])
- Alternative Scheme III (current status) predicted a drawdown of 4.1 m over the 20-year period. ([[2012-iwalewa|Iwalewa, 2012, p. 113]])

## Limitations

The study notes that some well data were excluded due to lack of records, and certain parameters like vertical conductivity were assumed as one-tenth of horizontal values based on previous studies.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Alternative II (conservative measures: 300 L/capita/day and a 20% reduction in pumping for irrigation) is the best management strategy. It is recommended to investigate water conservation methods, such as using treated wastewater or replacing grass with granite. New wells should only be located in the south of the study area; any new well in the northeast is strongly discouraged.
