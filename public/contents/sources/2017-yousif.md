# SEDIMENTOLOGY AND RESERVOIR CHARACTERISTICS OF MIDDLE JURASSIC DHRUMA FORMATION, OUTCROP APPROACH, CENTRAL SAUDI ARABIA

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the lithofacies, paleoenvironment, stratigraphic architecture, and reservoir characteristics of the Middle Jurassic Dhruma Formation in central Saudi Arabia. Using an outcrop approach involving petrography, stable isotope analysis, and 3D geostatistical modeling (Petrel), the study characterizes the D5 and D6 Members to improve understanding of reservoir heterogeneity.

---

## Research Problem

Subsurface data in carbonate reservoirs like the Dhruma Formation are often limited by large inter-well spacing and low resolution of seismic data, making it difficult to fully understand complex internal architectures and reservoir qualities. This study uses a high-resolution outcrop analog in central Saudi Arabia to overcome these limitations.

## Methods

The study utilized a multi-scale approach: 1) Field investigation involving outcrop mapping, photo mosaic creation, and sample collection. 2) Sedimentological analysis using thin-section petrography (Olympics microscope), XRD (JSX-1000S Element Eye machine), XRF, and SEM (GEOL JSM.5900LV). 3) Petrophysical measurements of porosity and permeability on ~70 core plugs prepared with a Core Milling Machine (MSC). 4) Statistical analysis including calculation of Coefficient of Variation (Cv), Lorenz Coefficient (Lc), and Dykstra-Parsons Coefficient (VDP). 5) Geostatistical modeling using Petrel software involving variogram analysis, kriging, and sequential simulation.

## Data and Materials

The study analyzed outcrop sections of the Dhruma Formation in the Khashm Mi’dad area, central Saudi Arabia. The section has a total thickness of approximately 58 meters and extends laterally for over 680 meters. Data includes lithofacies counts (9 types), stratigraphic cycles (approx. 53), and petrophysical measurements (porosity/permeability) across the D5 and D6 Members.

## Key Findings

- Identified 9 distinct lithofacies (LFT1 to LFT9) in the D5 and D6 Members of the Dhruma Formation. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 86]])
- Lithofacies include skeletal peletal spiculitic wackestone (LFT1), peloidal echinoderm packstone (LFT2), fissile shale (LFT3), and oolitic grainstone (LFT6). ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 86]])
- The Dhruma Formation exhibits a variety of pore types, including moldic porosity from meteoric dissolution and intercrystalline porosity. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 100]])
- XRD data showed dominant calcite minerals with minor quartz in the studied carbonate units. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 110]])
- XRF analysis revealed a distribution of elements including traces of Magnesium in the Dhruma Formation. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017]])

- Nine lithofacies types (LFTs) were identified, with fissile shale (LFT3) being the most dominant at 36%, followed by skeletal pelatal spiculitic wackestone (15%) and skeletal foraminiferal peloidal packstone (12%). ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 18]])
- The lithofacies were grouped into five major carbonate environments ranging from distal-to-proximal carbonate ramp settings. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 18]])
- Detailed stratigraphic analysis revealed approximately 53 cycles and cycle sets of 5th to 6th order magnitude, with parasequences averaging 1.5 meters in thickness. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 19]])
- The study identified four high-frequency sequences (HFSs) with thicknesses ranging from 1 to 14 meters, grouped into a single 3rd order depositional sequence. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 19]])
- Stable isotope ratios of carbon and oxygen were found to be depleted in transgressive-dominated intervals compared to regressive-dominated parts; an abrupt shift in δ18O indicated a subaerial exposure surface above a thick unit of cross-bedded peloidal skeletal oolitic grainstone. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 19]])
- The Jurassic maximum flooding surface (MFSJ30) was identified within the thick transgressive shale/mudstone of the D5 Member. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 19]])
- Four primary pore types were identified: fracture, intraparticle, moldic, and intercrystalline; fracture and intraparticle are the dominant types. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 19]])
- Moldic and intraparticle porosity are predominant in the upper part of the section equivalent to the Lower Fadhili reservoir. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 19]])
- Factors enhancing reservoir quality include dissolution and dolomitization, while cementation, grain compaction, and stylolites reduce it. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 19]])

## Limitations

The study notes that the 3D model shows a 'layer cake' appearance in low resolution due to grid limitations and the influence of thicker beds on the representation of thin layers. Additionally, it mentions that some small layers were hidden during the upscaling process.

## Recommendations and Future Work

1) Apply integrated approaches to remaining Dhruma members (D1-D4). 2) Conduct regional microfacies analysis for better correlation between surface and subsurface. 3) Perform detailed diagenetic analysis within a sequence stratigraphic framework. 4) Analyze micro-porosity specifically as many high porosity readings are linked to it. 5) Integrate LiDAR data with photo mosaics to enhance the 3D lithofacies model.
