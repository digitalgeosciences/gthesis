# SEDIMENTOLOGY AND RESERVOIR CHARACTERISTICS OF MIDDLE JURASSIC DHRUMA FORMATION, OUTCROP APPROACH, CENTRAL SAUDI ARABIA

**Summary**: The thesis characterizes the lithofacies, paleoenvironment, and reservoir properties of the Middle Jurassic Dhruma Formation in central Saudi Arabia using an outcrop approach. It integrates petrography, diagenetic analysis, and 3D geostatistical modeling to evaluate how primary textures and secondary alterations like meteoric dissolution and cementation impact porosity and permeability.

---

## Research Problem

The high heterogeneity of carbonate facies in the Dhruma Formation makes it difficult to predict reservoir architecture and quality; this study aims to improve characterization through detailed lithofacies analysis and stratigraphic mapping of the D5 and D6 Members.

## Objectives

- Describe the detailed microfacacies and interpret their depositional environment.
- Establish high-resolution stratigraphic framework.
- Evaluate reservoir characteristics (porosity, permeability) and their impact on quality.
- Construct a static model for lithofacies and petrophysical properties.
- Characterize the lithofacies and paleoenvironment of the Dhurma Formation.
- Determine high-resolution stratigraphic architecture of the Dhruma Formation.

## Methods

The study utilized field investigations at outcrops near Khashm Mi’dad to collect over 130 samples. Laboratory analysis included thin-section petrography (using OLYMPIC Microscope), XRD (at RI facilities), XRF (using JSX-1000S machine), and SEM (using GEOL JSM.5900LV). Petrophysical measurements of porosity and permeability were conducted on approximately 70 core plugs using a Helium Porosimeter and Hassler holder assembly. Geostatistical modeling was performed in Petrel software, involving variogram analysis and sequential indicator simulation (SIS) for facies and sequential Gaussian simulation (SGS) for petrophysical properties.

## Data and Materials

Analysis of well-exposed outcrops of the Dhruma Formation in the Khashm Mi’dad area, central Saudi Arabia; identification of 9 lithofacies types (LFT1-LFT9); analysis of approximately 53 cycles and cycle sets; measurement of porosity and permeability across various facies.

## Key Findings

- Nine lithofacies types (LFTs) were identified, with fissile shale (LFT3) being the most dominant at 36%, followed by skeletal peletal spiculitic wackestone (LFT1) and skeletal foraminiferal peloidal packstone (LFT8) at 15% and 12% respectively. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 18]])
- The lithofacies were grouped into five major carbonate environments ranging from distal-to-proximal carbonate ramp settings. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 18]])
- Detailed stratigraphic analysis revealed approximately 53 cycles and cycle sets of 5th to 6th order magnitude, with parasequences averaging 1.5 meters in thickness. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 19]])
- High-frequency sequences (HFSs) were identified as four units ranging from 1 to 14 meters thick, grouped into a single 3rd order depositional sequence. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 19]])
- Abrupt shifts in δ18O values indicated subaerial exposure surfaces above thick amalgamated units of cross-bedded peloidal skeletal oolitic grainstone and oolitic grainstone. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 19]])
- The Jurassic maximum flooding surface (MFSJ30) was identified within the thick transgressive shale/mudstone of the D5 Member. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 19]])
- Dominant pore types were found to be fracture and intraparticle porosities, with moldic and intraparticle porosity being dominant in the upper part equivalent to the Lower Fadhili reservoir. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 19]])
- Factors enhancing reservoir quality included dissolution and dolomitization, while cementation, grain compaction, and stylolites reduced it. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 19]])
- Nine hydraulic flow units (HFUs) were established with Flow Zone Indicators (FZI) ranging from 0.0938 to 1.5. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 20]])
- A positive correlation was found between carbon/oxygen stable isotope signatures and the porosity log along the vertical stratigraphic section. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 20]])
- Lithofacies LFT5 and LFT6 are characterized by high-energy conditions, with LFT5 showing ooids (~60%) and skeletal grains (~40%). ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 75]])
- The Dhruma Formation shows a clear distinction between the D5 Member (transgressive) and D6 Member (regressive). ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 90]])
- A significant shift in δ18O values was observed in the thick amalgamated unit of oolitic grainstone, indicating a subaerial exposure surface. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 100]])
- The primary pore types identified are fracture and intraparticle porosity; moldic and intraparticle porosity dominate the upper section (Lower Fadhili equivalent). ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 100]])
- Lithofacies LFT8 and LFT9 represent lagoonal environments, with LFT8 showing a 'hard ground' barrier for fluid movement. ([[2017-yousif|Yousif, 2017, p. 105]])

## Limitations

The study notes that high porosity readings may be related to micro-porosity, which requires further detailed analysis. Additionally, the 3D models show limitations in visualizing thin layers due to grid resolution and the influence of thicker beds.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Apply integrated approaches to remaining members (D1-D4).
- Perform regional microfacies analysis for better correlation between surface outcrops and subsurface equivalents.
- Conduct detailed diagenetic analysis within a sequence stratigraphic framework.
- Perform detailed micro-porosity analysis.
- Utilize geomechanical and geochemical tools for reservoir zonation.
- Integrate LiDAR data with high-resolution photo mosaics to enhance 3D lithofacies models.
