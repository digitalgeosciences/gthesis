# Impacts of Injection Rate, Pore Pressure and Rock Permeability on Wormhole Generation during CO2 sequestration in Limestone Aquifers

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of rock permeability, injection rate, and pore pressure on wormhole formation during CO2-saturated brine injection in carbonate rocks. Using Indiana limestone samples with varying permeabilities (3-22 mD), it concludes that lower permeability leads to more extensive wormhole development and greater degradation of mechanical properties like Young's modulus.

---

## Research Problem

Understanding how specific parameters (injection rate, pore pressure, and initial rock permeability) influence the development of wormhole structures and subsequent changes in petrophysical and geomechanical properties during CO2 sequestration in carbonate reservoirs.

## Objectives

- Ascertain the effects of injection parameters on wormhole generation
- Understand the effect of the injection rate
- Assess the effect of pore pressure on wormhole generation and subsequent changes in petrophysical properties (porosity, permeability) and geomechanical properties (Young's Modulus, Poisson's ratio).
- Determine the effect of initial rock permeability on wormhole development and mechanical degradation.
- Determination of the effects of a generated wormhole on the mechanical and petrophysical characteristics of rocks
- Evaluate the impact of injection rates on wormhole morphology and petrophysical properties.

## Methods

The study utilized Indiana limestone samples (1.5 in diameter, 3 in length) with average porosity of 15.7% and permeability of 2.6 mD. Coreflooding experiments were conducted at 60°C with a brine salinity of 120,000 ppm mixed with 30% CO2. Six injection rates (10, 5, 2, 1, 0.5, and 0.25 cm3/min) were tested to determine optimal flow for wormhole formation. Four pore pressure levels (1500, 2000, 3000, and 4000 psi) were evaluated. Micro-CT scanning (resolution of 49 μm) was used to visualize and quantify wormhole volumes. Mechanical properties including dynamic Young's Modulus (YM), Poisson's ratio, and surface hardness were measured using the Autolab 500 and Autoscan instruments.

## Data and Materials

6 Indiana limestone samples; brine solution (120,000 ppm); CO2 gas; coreflooding setup with 1L accumulator; Autolab 500 for geomechanics; Micro-CT scanner (ZEISS VERSA XRM - 500).

## Key Findings

- An injection rate of 1 cm3/min resulted in the lowest Pore Volume Breakthrough Time (PVBT) among tested rates (0.25 to 5 cm3/min). ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 16]])
- Higher pore pressure correlated with increased mechanical damage, evidenced by reductions in surface hardness and Young's modulus. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 16]])
- The experiment with the lowest permeability core (3 mD) yielded the lowest PVBT, while higher permeability led to less significant deterioration due to lower dissolution effects. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 16]])
- A linear relationship was found between wormhole size and porosity change (R² = 0.9849). ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 52]])
- The optimal velocity for CO2-saturated brine was determined to be 0.4 cm/min (equivalent to an injection rate of 0.75 cm3/min) using the modified Buijse and Glasbergen model. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 36]])
- Permeability doubled in the 10 cm3/min experiment and nearly quadrupled in the 5 cm3/min experiment (rising from 2.5 mD to 8.5 mD). ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 52]])
- XRD analysis revealed that the sample composition was nearly 100% calcite. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 45]])
- Higher flow rates (10 and 5 cm3/min) produced ramified/branched wormhole structures, while lower flow rates resulted in dominant, single-channel wormholes. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 53]])
- The largest wormhole volume was observed at the 5 cm3/min experiment (383 mm³), with others ranging from 106 to 184 mm³. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 53]])
- An optimal injection rate of 0.75 cm3/min was determined via the Buijse & Glasbergen model, though a poor correlation existed for velocities below this optimum. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 63]])
- A significantly lower optimal Damköhler number of 0.01 was found for carbonic acid compared to standard acids due to low reaction kinetics. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 64]])
- No clear relationship was found between the degree of porosity change and injection pressure, as both were primarily driven by wormhole volume. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 82]])
- The 2000 psi experiment yielded the largest wormhole (184.72 mm³) and a more complex morphology compared to other pressures. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 83]])
- The highest reduction in Young's Modulus (YM) occurred at the 4000 psi pore pressure, while the least was at 1500 psi. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 86]])
- A direct relationship was observed between increasing pore pressure and the reduction in surface hardness (highest at 4000 psi). ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 124]])

## Limitations

The Buijse & Glasbergen model showed poor correlation for velocities below the optimum, likely because it was originally developed for strong acids like HCl rather than weak acids like carbonic acid.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future research should focus on refining models for weak acids and selecting specific reservoir locations to balance wormhole growth with rock integrity. High flow rates are recommended near wellbores to maintain stability while lower flow rates may be better in the reservoir interior to improve injectivity.
