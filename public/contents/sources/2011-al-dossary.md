# INVESTIGATION OF POOR CEMENTING BEHIND OIL WELL CASING ACROSS ARAB-C AND D FORMATIONS IN UTHMANIYAH

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the causes of poor cementation between the Arab-C and Arab-D formations in the Uthmanyia field, which led to water communication between high-pressure and low-pressure zones. It evaluates field practices, analyzes cement samples under simulated downhole conditions (high pressure/temperature), and proposes solutions for improving zonal isolation.

---

## Research Problem

The investigation of poor cementing behind oil well casings/liners, specifically focusing on the communication between the Arab-C and Arab-D formations caused by cement failure in a sour (CO2 and H2S) and high-pressure environment.

## Objectives

- Review current cement practices at the field and identify the main cause of cement failure.
- Investigate experimentally the actual mix of cement slurry and design cement mix to solve the cement failure.
- Evaluate the quality of cement between Arab-C and D formations using CBL logs.
- Investigate the impact of sour gas (CO2, H2S) on cement slurry integrity.
- Analyze the effect of Arab-C water and high pressure on cement degradation.
- Evaluate the impact of Arab-C water on cement permeability and mechanical properties.

## Methods

The study involved a two-phase approach: Phase I included reviewing field data (completion reports, cased hole logs, and field practices) and experimental testing of cement samples exposed to Arab-C water under simulated downhole conditions (high pressure/temperature). Experimental tests included permeability measurements, mechanical property analysis, TGA (Thermogravimetric Analysis), and XRF/EDX for chemical characterization. Phase II involved analyzing the results to propose modifications to cement systems or field practices.

## Data and Materials

Analysis of data from multiple wells (Well-A, Well-B, Well-C, Well-D, Well-E) in the Uthmanyia field; experimental testing of various cement samples (including 12 different sample types as shown in Figure 30-41) and chemical analysis of cement after exposure to Arab-C water for 3 and 6 months.

## Key Findings

- The primary cause of communication was identified as the inability of cement to maintain hydrostatic pressure during its initial set, allowing high-pressure fluids from Arab-C to enter the wellbore. ([[2011-al-dossary|Al-Dossary, 2011, p. 15]])
- Arab-C water contains only a low concentration (3%) of gases that are potentially corrosive or harmful to cement, suggesting chemical degradation by the water itself was not the primary driver. ([[2011-al-dossary|Al-Dossary, 2011, p. 12]])
- The use of a top packer in some wells led to an isolation of hydrostatic pressure for fluids above the cement, contributing to communication issues. ([[2011-al-dossary|Al-Dossary, 2011, p. 12]])
- Cement samples exposed to Arab-C water for 3 and 6 months showed that while chemical composition remained relatively stable, mechanical properties were affected by environmental conditions. ([[2011-al-dossary|Al-Dossary, 2011, p. 90]])
- The study confirmed that cement slurry must maintain a sufficient hydrostatic pressure gradient during the transition time (from liquid to solid state) to prevent gas or water migration. ([[2011-al-dossary|Al-Dossary, 2011, p. 19]])
- A 'gas tightness factor' was identified as a metric to characterize the ability of cement to resist gas migration, with values between 5 and 20 being considered acceptable. ([[2011-al-dossary|Al-Dossary, 2011, p. 33]])
- Well-A showed poor cement behind the liner with a continuous water channel, confirming communication between Arab-C and D zones (Page 64). ([[2011-al-dossary|Al-Dossary, 2011, p. 64]])
- Well-B also exhibited poor cement across Arab-C and D zones and a continuous water channel, indicating cross-flow confirmed by GR logs (Page 70). ([[2011-al-dossary|Al-Dossary, 2011, p. 70]])
- Well-E showed severe cement contamination causing communication problems between Arab-C and D zones (Page 73). ([[2011-al-dossary|Al-Dossary, 2011, p. 73]])
- Cement degradation due to carbonation in high pressure/high temperature environments can lead to a volume increase of up to 6%, potentially causing cracks (Page 74). ([[2011-al-dossary|Al-Dossary, 2011, p. 74]])
- Cement remained impermeable during brine injection at 700 psi for 15 minutes after exposure to Arab-C water. ([[2011-al-dossary|Al-Dossary, 2011, p. 101]])
- TGA analysis showed cement lost approximately 13% of mass due to moisture evaporation between 0 and 150°C. ([[2011-al-dossary|Al-Dossary, 2011, p. 102]])
- EDXRF results showed that cement samples after initial curing mainly consist of 60% CaO and 19% SiO2 by weight. ([[2011-al-dossary|Al-Dossary, 2011, p. 102]])
- After 6 months of exposure to Arab-C water, the sample mass decreased by 21.38% in total during TGA testing. ([[2011-al-dossary|Al-Dossary, 2011, p. 106]])
- No major change in cement integrity or chemical composition was observed after long-term (6 month) exposure to Arab-C water. ([[2011-al-dossary|Al-Dossary, 2011, p. 106]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

The thesis suggests potential solutions including a redesign of the cement system (e.g., using specialized additives or different slurry designs) and modifications to field practices such as improved casing centralization and better mud cake removal.
