# PREDICTION OF WATER SATURATION FROM WELL LOGS USING ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE TECHNIQUES

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the use of machine learning techniques, specifically Artificial Neural Networks (ANN), to predict water saturation from well logs in the Prinos basin, Greece. It compares these AI methods against traditional petrophysical models like Archie's equation and Simandoux to overcome limitations such as missing parameters or complex lithologies.

---

## Research Problem

Traditional petrophysical models for estimating water saturation (e.g., Archie, Waxman-Smits) often require specific parameters that are not always available or are difficult to determine accurately in complex formations like shaly sands and carbonates.

## Objectives

- Evaluate the accuracy of machine learning techniques (ANN, ANFIS, FN, SVM, RF) for predicting water saturation from well logs.
- Compare AI-based prediction methods against traditional petrophysical models (Archie, Simandoux, Indonesia, and dual-water).
- Develop a method to extract equations from optimized ANN models to provide a more direct relationship between inputs and outputs.
- Develop a machine learning model (ANN) to predict water saturation from well log data.
- Optimize the parameters of the ANN model including training functions and activation functions.
- Derive an empirical equation from the optimized ANN model for practical use.

## Methods

The study involved collecting 364 data points (Vshale, porosity, permeability, and deep resistivity) from Well-1. Data were preprocessed by removing non-real values and outliers (outside ±3 standard deviations). An Artificial Neural Network (ANN) was trained using a 70/30 split for training and testing. Parameters such as the number of layers, neurons per layer, learning functions (e.g., Bayesian regularization backpropagation), and activation functions (e.g., Logarithmic sigmoidal) were optimized. A final empirical equation was derived from the weights and biases of the optimized model.

## Data and Materials

A dataset of 252 entries derived from core data collected from five wells in the Prinos basin, Greece. Input parameters include well logs for resistivity, gamma ray (GR), bulk formation density, sonic porosity, and neutron porosity.

## Key Findings

- Machine learning models like ANN can solve mapping problems by finding close approximations of correlations between input and output data without the need for complex manual parameter adjustments. ([[2023-gad|Gad, 2023, p. 20]])
- SVM is capable of handling complex problems by transforming input datasets into higher-dimensional feature spaces to perform optimal hyperplanes. ([[2023-gad|Gad, 2023, p. 18]])
- Random Forests (RF) provide an effective way of handling missing data and can produce accurate predictions without extensive hyper-parameter tuning. ([[2023-gad|Gad, 2023, p. 18]])
- ANFIS showed more capability for the accurate prediction compared to other methods in previous studies (e.g., Ahmed Farid Ibrahim et al., 2022). ([[2023-gad|Gad, 2023, p. 19]])
- The optimal ANN model configuration was identified as a single hidden layer with five neurons using the Bayesian regularization backpropagation training function and Logarithmic sigmoidal activation function. ([[2023-gad|Gad, 2023, p. 33]])
- The training data showed an average absolute percent error (AAPE) of 0.90% and a correlation coefficient (R) of 0.999. ([[2023-gad|Gad, 2023, p. 39]])
- The testing data showed an AAPE of 1.08% and a correlation coefficient (R) of 0.9999. ([[2023-gad|Gad, 2023, p. 40]])
- The derived empirical equation yielded a correlation coefficient of 0.91 and an AAPE of 6% when validated with unseen data. ([[2023-gad|Gad, 2023, p. 42]])

## Limitations

Traditional models like Archie's equation are limited by the need for specific constants (a, m, n) and are less accurate in shaly formations where clay increases conductivity.
