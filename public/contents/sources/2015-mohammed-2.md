# Optimization of In-Situ Generated CO2 Using Chelating Agents for EOR from Carbonate Reservoirs

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the optimization of in-situ generated CO2 using chelating agents (EDTA and GLDA) to enhance oil recovery (EOR) in carbonate reservoirs, specifically focusing on the Arab-D formation. It evaluates how different concentrations and pH levels affect chemical stability, precipitation, and permeability enhancement.

---

## Research Problem

The primary challenge is finding an effective method for generating CO2 in-situ to improve mobility and reduce damage in heterogeneous carbonate reservoirs (like the Arab-D) while ensuring the chemicals used remain stable and do not cause scale precipitation or formation damage at high temperatures and salinities.

## Objectives

- To investigate the in-situ Carbon dioxide generation using chelating agents after seawater flooding.
- To study the effect of chelating agent concentration on optimizing the in-situ carbon dioxide generation in carbonates.
- To study the effect of in-situ carbon dioxide generation on increasing oil recovery.
- To better understand the mechanism leading to incremental oil recovery using EDTA and GLDA in carbonate reservoirs.
- Evaluate the compatibility of chelating agents (EDTA and GLDA) with sea water under various pH levels and concentrations.
- Investigate the impact of in-situ CO2 generation on permeability and porosity in carbonate reservoirs.

## Methods

The research involved core flooding experiments using Indiana limestone samples under reservoir conditions. Zeta potential measurements were conducted to assess rock surface charge and wettability alteration. Interfacial tension (IFT) was measured between crude oil and various chelating agent solutions using the spinning-drop method. XRD and XRF analyses were performed on crushed limestone to determine mineralogical and elemental composition. Compatibility tests between chelating agents and seawater were conducted across a range of pH values.

## Data and Materials

The study involved multiple core samples from carbonate reservoirs, crude oil, seawater, and chelating agent solutions (EDTA and GLDA). Experimental data included concentration ranges of 3wt% to 10wt%, pH levels ranging from 4 to 12, and various measurement points for pressure drop, oil recovery (%OOIP), and ion concentrations.

## Key Findings

- Chelating agent solutions prepared with synthetic Arabian Gulf seawater changed limestone surface charge to more negative zeta potential values, leading to a more water-wet condition. ([[2015-mohammed|Mohammed, 2015, p. 15]])
- The presence of crude oil in the solution resulted in more negative zeta potential values, improving wettability alteration and increasing oil recovery. ([[2015-mohammed|Mohammed, 2015, p. 15]])
- Chelating agent solutions showed lower IFT values compared to deionized water (DIW) and seawater (SW), facilitating better flow conditions. ([[2015-mohammed|Mohammed, 2015, p. 15]])
- Increasing the chelating agent concentration led to lower IFT values. ([[2015-mohammed|Mohammed, 2015, p. 15]])
- No CO2 was observed during EDTA flooding at pH 6, whereas GLDA flooding resulted in in-situ CO2 generation that increased with GLDA concentration. ([[2015-mohammed|Mohammed, 2015, p. 16]])
- A concentration of 3wt% for both EDTA and GLDA was identified as optimal to maximize oil recovery without causing severe rock dissolution. ([[2015-mohammed|Mohammed, 2015, p. 112]])
- The best sequential scenario for enhancing oil recovery was flooding with 3wt.% followed by 5wt.% for both GLDA and EDTA. ([[2015-mohammed|Mohammed, 2015, p. 16]])
- EDTA at a concentration of 10 wt.% and pH of 11 showed significant permeability improvement in Indiana limestone, with a ratio of approximately 2.13. ([[2015-mohammed|Mohammed, 2015, p. 55]])
- GLDA was found to be stable in seawater across a wide range of pH values (up to 4) and at high temperatures (85°C for 1 day). ([[2015-mohammed|Mohammed, 2015, p. 64]])
- EDTA is not compatible with sea water or diluted sea water at low pH values less than 5, showing significant precipitation. ([[2015-mohammed|Mohammed, 2015, p. 62]])
- In-situ CO2 generation using H3HEDTA (pH=2.5) resulted in a 34% incremental recovery of oil over sea water flooding. ([[2015-mohammed|Mohammed, 2015, p. 47]])
- The Indiana limestone core showed high calcite content (~97% CaCO3) and low illite content (<3 wt%). ([[2015-mohammed|Mohammed, 2015, p. 66]])
- A higher ratio of CO2 to water (greater than 2) led to more significant permeability enhancement and less formation damage. ([[2015-mohammed|Mohammed, 2015, p. 57]])
- The study confirmed that a concentration of 10 wt.% EDTA at pH 11 provides the best balance for preventing precipitation while enhancing permeability in carbonate rocks. ([[2015-mohammed|Mohammed, 2015, p. 62]])
- GLDA was found to be more powerful than EDTA in generating CO2 and improving recovery, with a 83.76% OOIP recovery compared to 54% for EDTA in similar conditions. ([[2015-mohammed|Mohammed, 2015, p. 92]])

## Limitations

High concentrations of GLDA (above 10wt%) led to significant precipitation and severe rock dissolution, creating wormholes that could lead to bypassed oil and reduced ultimate recovery.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Use micro-CT scans during core flooding to visualize in-situ CO2 generation.
- Develop 3D reservoir simulations to estimate oil recovery from limestone reservoirs due to in-situ CO2 generation.
- Investigate other types of chelating agents for carbonate formation EOR.
