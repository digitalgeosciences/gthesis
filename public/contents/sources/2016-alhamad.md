# EVALUATION OF NANOPARTICLES FOR ENHANCED OIL RECOVERY IN HIGH SALINITY AND HIGH TEMPERATURE CARBONATE RESERVOIRS

**Summary**: The thesis evaluates the use of zeolite nanoparticles and PVP as additives in nanofluids to enhance oil recovery (EOR) in high salinity and temperature environments. The study utilizes zeta potential, IFT, contact angle measurements, and core flooding experiments to determine optimal concentrations for improving wettability and reducing interfacial tension.

---

## Research Problem

Nanoparticles are potential EOR agents, but their stability in saline environments (like seawater) is a major challenge due to precipitation caused by divalent salts. This study addresses this by testing zeolite nanoparticles stabilized with different surfactants for use in carbonate reservoirs under high temperature and pressure conditions.

## Objectives

- Identify potential nanoparticles that can enhance oil recovery at high-temperature and high-salinity conditions with minimum surface adsorption.
- Investigate the effect of nanoparticles on interfacial tension between brine and oil phases.
- Investigate the impact of different nanoparticles on oil recovery using core-flooding.
- Determine the size distribution of prepared zeolite nanoparticles using SEM.
- Evaluate the stability of nanoparticles in various aqueous solutions (deionized water, NaCl brines, and seawater).
- Identify the impact of different salts on nanoparticle stability via zeta potential measurements.

## Methods

The study utilized SEM to determine particle size distribution. Centrifugation was used to remove larger particles (above 100nm). Turbiscan was employed to monitor dispersion stability. Zeta potential was measured using the Zetapals instrument to assess electrostatic repulsion/attraction. A variety of surfactants (PEG, Tergitol, Triton, CTAB, Solsperse, Pluronic, Synperonic, and PVP) were tested at 1 wt% concentration in seawater. Coreflooding experiments were designed to compare nanoparticle-enhanced fluids against standard seawater flooding under reservoir conditions.

## Data and Materials

3 Indiana limestone core samples (LS1, LS2, LS3) with porosities of 16.03%, 16.84%, and 17.5% and gas permeabilities of 328.6 mD, 410.6 mD, and 210 mD; synthetic seawater (67 kppm); crude oil (density 0.8651 at 25°C); natural zeolite minerals (Al2O3: 6.61%, SiO2: 44.38%, P2O5: 0.62%, K2O: 0.99%, CaO: 3.44%, TiO2: 0.34%, Fe2O3: 1.99%); various surfactants at 1% concentration.

## Key Findings

- Stability tests showed that precipitation of zeolite nanoparticles occurred in less than 1 hour when dispersed in seawater without stabilizers. ([[2016-alhamad|AlHamad, 2016, p. 14]])
- Zeolite nanoparticles showed good stability in NaCl solution at a concentration of 0.14 wt%, but destabilized at higher concentrations. ([[2016-alhamad|AlHamad, 2016, p. 14]])
- Divalent salts (MgCl2 and CaCl2) caused zeolite nanoparticles to destabilize even at very low concentrations. ([[2016-alhamad|AlHamad, 2016, p. 14]])
- Polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP) was identified as the best surfactant to stabilize Zeolite nanoparticles in seawater. ([[2016-alhamad|AlHamad, 2016, p. 14]])
- The addition of PVP-stabilized zeolite nanoparticles reduced interfacial tension (IFT) values and altered wettability to more water-wet. ([[2016-alhamad|AlHamad, 2016, p. 15]])
- A mixture of Al2O3 (0.05 wt%) and SiO2 (0.01 wt%) showed an incremental oil recovery factor of 23.724% under reservoir conditions. ([[2016-alhamad|AlHamad, 2016, p. 24]])
- The study found that smaller nanoparticle sizes (e.g., 7 nm) lead to higher oil recovery and more significant wettability changes. ([[2016-alhamad|AlHamad, 2016, p. 29]])
- Lower flow rates (e.g., 0.2 cc/min) were recommended for maximizing the impact of nanoparticles on oil recovery. ([[2016-alhamad|AlHamad, 2016, p. 29]])
- Centrifugation for 35 minutes was found sufficient to reduce particle size to a range of 50-70 nm, suitable for reservoir application. ([[2016-alhamad|AlHamad, 2016, p. 51]])
- Nanoparticles showed good stability in deionized water (stable for >5 days) but precipitated in less than 1 hour when added to seawater. ([[2016-alhamad|AlHamad, 2016, p. 54]])
- Stability in NaCl solutions was dependent on concentration: stable for 3 days at <6.5 kppm, but precipitated in under 6 hours at concentrations above 12.5 kppm. ([[2016-alhamad|AlHamad, 2016, p. 55]])
- Divalent cation salts (CaCl2 and MgCl2) caused rapid precipitation of nanoparticles in less than 1 hour regardless of concentration, due to lower critical salt concentrations (CSC). ([[2016-alhamad|AlHamad, 2016, p. 55]])
- Polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP) at a concentration of 1 wt% was identified as the most effective surfactant for maintaining nanoparticle dispersion in seawater for up to 9 hours. ([[2016-alhamad|AlHamad, 2016, p. 62]])
- With 1 wt% PVP, nanoparticles remained stable with incipient stability (zeta potential between -10 and -20 mV) up to a concentration of 0.05 wt%. ([[2016-alhamad|AlHamad, 2016, p. 63]])
- At nanoparticle concentrations exceeding 0.05 wt% in the presence of PVP, zeta potential values dropped significantly (e.g., -2.78 mV at 0.1 wt%), indicating rapid aggregation. ([[2016-alhamad|AlHamad, 2016, p. 64]])
