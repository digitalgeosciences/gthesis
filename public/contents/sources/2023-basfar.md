# Removal of Barite Settling While Drilling High-Pressure High Temperature Wells

**Summary**: The thesis investigates methods to mitigate barite sag in high-pressure, high-temperature (HPHT) drilling fluids by incorporating alternative weighting agents like ilmenite and Micromax. The study evaluates these additives in both water-based (WBM) and oil-based muds (OBM), concluding that specific concentrations of ilmenite and Micromax effectively maintain suspension and stability under extreme conditions.

---

## Research Problem

Barite settling (sag) in water-based drilling fluids under HPHT conditions leads to inconsistent mud density, poor hole cleaning, and potential well control issues. Existing solutions like using high concentrations of certain additives can be costly or less effective than the proposed combinations of ilmenite or laponite.

## Objectives

- Prevention of barite sagging using a combination of different weighting agents (barite/ilmenite) for both water-based and other oil-based drilling fluid at HPHT conditions.
- Designing protocol for different drilling fluids with Barite for WBM and OBM at HPHT conditions.
- Evaluate the effect of adding ilmenite to barite-weighted drilling fluid on density, pH, and rheological properties.
- Assess the impact of ilmenite on static and dynamic sag factors under HPHT conditions.
- Investigate the use of synthetic clay (laponite) as an anti-sagging agent in water-based mud.
- Evaluate the effect of laponite on drilling fluid rheology, filtration performance, and sag tendency.

## Methods

The study utilized several experimental procedures: 1. Material Characterization: Barite and Micromax were analyzed using X-ray fluorescence (XRF) for elemental composition, Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) for morphology, and laser diffraction to determine particle size distribution. 2. Sag Tests: Static sag tests were conducted at 350°F and 500 psi in vertical and inclined (45°) positions for 24 hours; dynamic sag tests were performed using a viscometer sag shoe test (VSST) at 120°F for 30 minutes. 3. Rheology: Measured via an OFITE 77-HPHT Viscometer to determine plastic viscosity, yield point, and gel strength. 4. Viscoelasticity: Evaluated using an Anton Paar rheometer to measure storage (G') and loss (G'') moduli. 5. Filtration: HPHT filtration tests were conducted on a 10-micron ceramic filter discs at 350°F and 500 psi.

## Data and Materials

The study involved several oil-based mud (OBM) formulations containing varying concentrations of ilmenite (20, 40, 60, 80 wt.%) and Micromax (0, 15, 30 wt.%). Measurement parameters included density (ppg), electrical stability (V), yield point (lb/100 ft²), plastic viscosity (cP), gel strength, filtration volume (cm³), and filter cake thickness (mm).

## Key Findings

- Adding 40% and 50% of ilmenite was sufficient to prevent the barite settling of OBM and WBM respectively. ([[2023-basfar|Basfar, 2023, p. 19]])
- Micromax was more efficient than ilmenite, with barite remaining in suspension at only 30% and 25% Micromax concentrations. ([[2023-basfar|Basfar, 2023, p. 19]])
- Barite sagging in WBM was prevented by using laponite at a concentration of 0.75 lb/bbl. ([[2023-basfar|Basfar, 2023, p. 19]])
- Ilmenite-weighted fluids showed no significant drop in rheological properties under HPHT conditions compared to the base fluid (without ilmenite). ([[2023-basfar|Basfar, 2023, p. 32]])
- A sag factor of 0.51 was achieved for ilmenite-blended fluids, indicating successful prevention of solids sag. ([[2023-basfar|Basfar, 2023, p. 32]])
- Micromax particles (average size < 1 µm) provided superior suspension compared to barite due to their very small particle size. ([[2023-basfar|Basfar, 2023, p. 38]])
- Adding ilmenite increased the drilling fluid density from 14.95 ppg to 15.90 ppg (at 50 wt.%) due to its higher specific gravity compared to barite. ([[2023-basfar|Basfar, 2023, p. 84]])
- The base fluid (100% barite) exhibited poor performance with a sag factor of 0.6 in inclined conditions and a VSST of 2.1, both exceeding acceptable limits. ([[2023-basfar|Basfar, 2023, p. 85]])
- Adding 25 wt.% ilmenite reduced the dynamic sag (VSST) to 0.52, while 50 wt.% ilmenite reduced it further to 0.05, both falling within the acceptable range (<1.0). ([[2023-basfar|Basfar, 2023, p. 85]])
- The addition of 50 wt.% ilmenite maintained rheological properties at 250°F, with only a 18% drop in PV and 27% in YP, compared to the 42-51% drop seen in the base fluid. ([[2023-basfar|Basfar, 2023, p. 69]])
- Laponite at 0.75 lb/bbl reduced the static sag factor to 0.502 (vertical) and 0.51 (inclined), bringing them into the safe range of 0.50-0.53. ([[2023-basfar|Basfar, 2023, p. 101]])
- Laponite at 0.75 lb/bbl reduced the dynamic sag (VSST) to 0.17, indicating no significant solids settlement. ([[2023-basfar|Basfar, 2023, p. 101]])
- The addition of laponite increased the yield point from 33 lb/100 ft2 to 48 lb/100 ft2 at 0.75 lb/bbl, improving hole cleaning and suspension. ([[2023-basfar|Basfar, 2023, p. 105]])
- Laponite improved filtration performance by reducing the total filtrate volume by 15% (to 5.6 cm3) and filter cake thickness by 20% (to 2.95 mm). ([[2023-basfar|Basfar, 2023, p. 110]])
- The average particle size (D50) of ilmenite was 1.66 µm, significantly smaller than the barite's D50 of 16.64 µm. ([[2023-basfar|Basfar, 2023, p. 56]])

## Limitations

The study notes that while blended weighting agents improve filtration, removing the resulting composite filter cake (barite/ilmenite or barite/Micromax) requires specialized fluid formulations to avoid formation damage.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study recommends using 25 wt.% ilmerite as an optimal balance between performance and cost. It also suggests that while laponite is effective, its concentration must be balanced with other additives to manage gel strength.
