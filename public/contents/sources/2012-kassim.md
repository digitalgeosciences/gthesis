# THE ROLE OF POTENTIAL DETERMINING IONS IN CARBONATE ROCK-SEAWATER INTERACTIONS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the role of specific ions (Ca2+, Mg2+, SO4 2-, and Cl-) as potential determining ions in carbonate rock-seawater interactions. Using both precipitated calcium carbonate (PCC) and limestone from the Uthmaniyah Arab-D field, it evaluates how these ions affect surface charge (zeta potential) and adsorption behavior under different temperatures and crude oil presence.

---

## Research Problem

The impact of specific ion concentrations in seawater on the surface chemistry and wettability of carbonate rocks is complex due to competing effects of mineral dissolution and ion adsorption. This study specifically addresses how Ca2+, Mg2+, SO4 2-, and Cl- act as potential determining ions in these systems.

## Objectives

- Experimentally investigate the interactions between Ca2+, Mg2+, SO4 2-, and Cl- in Arabian Gulf seawater with crushed Arab-D reservoir rock.
- Identify optimum parameters of the seawater that will trigger the adsorption/desorption of these ions to potentially cause wettibility alteration.
- Measure the zeta potential of limestone in various modified seawater brines to identify potential determining ions.
- Evaluate the impact of temperature (25°C and 90°C) on surface charge and ion adsorption.
- Assess the influence of crude oil presence on the adsorption behavior of key ions.
- Identify which specific ions are responsible for determining the surface charge in carbonate systems.

## Methods

The study utilized two types of samples: a limestone powder from the Uthmaniyah Arab-D field and precipitated calcium carbonate (PCC). Characterization was performed using XRD, XRF, and SEM-EDX. 16 different synthetic brines were prepared by varying concentrations of Ca2+, Mg2+, SO4 2-, and Cl-. Adsorption experiments were conducted for both PCC and limestone in the presence and absence of crude oil. Analytical techniques included ICP-OES (Spectro Ciros Vision) for Ca2+ and Mg2+ measurement, and Ion Chromatography (IC, ICS-3000) for SO4 2- and Cl-. Zeta potential was measured using a Zeta Potential Analyzer (Zetapals) and a Zeta Meter 3.0. Statistical analysis of the results was performed using ANOVA to determine the significance of individual factors and their interactions.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized limestone samples (73% dolomite) and precipitated calcium carbonate (PCC) as model rocks. 16 different modified seawater brines were used for zeta potential measurements. Experimental data included adsorption densities of ions in mg/g across various concentration levels of Ca2+, Mg2+, SO4 2-, and Cl-.

## Key Findings

- Zeta potential measurements confirmed that the carbonate rock is negatively charged in Arabian Gulf seawater, with surface charge varying based on brine composition, crude oil presence, and temperature. ([[2012-kassim|KASSIM, 2012, p. 19]])
- The interaction of Ca2+, Mg2+, SO4 2-, and Cl- with carbonate rocks depends on rock type, brine composition, crude oil presence, and temperature. ([[2012-kassim|KASSIM, 2012, p. 19]])
- Sulfate (SO4 2-) acts as a catalyst in the mechanism of wettibility alteration; its adsorption onto the surface lowers positive charge, allowing Ca2+ to adsorb and displace carboxylic material. ([[2012-kassim|KASSIM, 2012, p. 32]])
- At high temperatures, Mg2+ is able to substitute/displace Ca2+ linked to the carboxylic groups on the carbonate surface due to increased reactivity from dehydration. ([[2012-kassim|KASSIM, 2012, p. 32]])
- The presence of non-active ions like Na+ and Cl- in high concentrations can hinder the access of active ions (Ca2+, Mg2+, SO4 2-) to the carbonate surface, thereby reducing wettibility alteration effectiveness. ([[2012-kassim|KASSIM, 2012, p. 35]])
- Sulfate ion concentration was found to be a critical factor; for example, 15% higher recovery was obtained with seawater containing SO4 2- compared to seawater without it in limestone. ([[2012-kassim|KASSIM, 2012, p. 34]])
- XRD analysis of limestone powder revealed a composition of 25% calcite, 73% dolomite, and 2% anhydrite. ([[2012-kassim|KASSIM, 2012, p. 41]])
- The zeta potential (ZP) of PCC was found to be 6.45 mV at the isoelectric point (IEP). ([[2012-kassim|KASSIM, 2012, p. 43]])
- Zeta potential of PCC decreased with an increase in temperature from 25°C to 90°C, attributed to increased Ca2+ solubility. ([[2012-kassim|KASSIM, 2012]])

- At 25°C in the presence of crude oil, increasing calcium ion concentration significantly triggered the adsorption of calcium ions onto limestone. ([[2012-kassim|KASSIM, 2012, p. 98]])
- At 25°C in the presence of crude oil, the highest desorption of chloride ion was observed at low sulfate ion concentrations (C1) and lowest at high sulfate concentration (C2). ([[2012-kassim|KASSIM, 2012, p. 103]])
- At 90°C in the absence of crude oil, increasing calcium ion concentration significantly reduced sulfate desorption and may trigger its adsorption. ([[2012-kassim|KASSIM, 2012, p. 99]])
- At 90°C in the absence of crude oil, magnesium adsorption was enhanced by increasing sulfate and chloride ions or decreasing calcium ions. ([[2012-kassim|KASSIM, 2012, p. 96]])
- At 90°C in the presence of crude oil, the optimum way to enhance magnesium ion adsorption is to simultaneously decrease the calcium ions and increase either magnesium or sulfate ions. ([[2012-kassim|KASSIM, 2012, p. 108]])
