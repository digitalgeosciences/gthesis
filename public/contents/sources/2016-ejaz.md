# INTEGRATION OF ELECTROFACIES AND GEOMECHANICAL CHARACTERISTICS OF SARAH FORMATION (POTENTIAL TIGHT GAS RESERVOIR), RUB’ AL-KHALI BASIN, SAUDI ARABIA

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the geomechanical properties of the Sarah Formation in the Rub' Al-Khali Basin to evaluate its potential as a tight gas reservoir. It integrates core analysis, well-log data, and mechanical Earth modeling (MEM) to determine rock parameters like Young's modulus, Poisson's ratio, and unconfined compressive strength (UCS).

---

## Research Problem

The exploration and production of tight gas reservoirs, specifically the Sarah Formation in the Rub’ Al-Khali Basin, are challenging due/due to poor reservoir quality, high pressure, and high temperature. Accurate characterization of petrophysical and geomechanical properties is critical for optimizing completion methods like hydraulic fracturing.

## Objectives

- Develop an electrofacies model from well logs using multivariate statistical methods to identify major units and boundaries in the Sarah Formation.
- Develop a 1-D Mechanical Earth Model (MEM) using well log and core data to characterize geomechanical properties like Young's modulus, Poisson's ratio, and in-situ stresses.
- Integrate electrofacies with geomechanical characteristics to understand the impact of brittleness/fracability on petrophysical properties.

- Building 1-D Mechanical Earth Model (MEM).
- Integration of the MEM with electrofacies.
- Determine rock mechanical parameters experimentally from core samples.
- Evaluate the potential of the Sarah Formation as a tight gas reservoir through geomechanical analysis.
- Build a 1-D Mechanical Earth Model (MEM) to assess in-situ stresses and wellbore stability.
- Integrate mineralogy, petrophysics, and geomechanics to optimize hydraulic fracturing treatment.
- Develop an electrofacies model for the Sarah Formation using unsupervised statistical methods (PCA and K-means clustering).
- Integrate electrofacies with geomechanical characteristics to evaluate reservoir properties.

- Integrate electrofacies and geomechanical data to assess the reservoir's potential for tight gas production.

## Methods

Geomechanical characterization was performed using both laboratory tests and well-log data. Core samples were subjected to acoustic measurements (using Autolab 500) to determine dynamic moduli (Young's modulus and Poisson's ratio) at various confining pressures (7 to 35 MPa). Unconfined Compressive Strength (UCS) tests were conducted on 19 core plugs to determine static mechanical properties. A 1-D Mechanical Earth Model (MEM) was constructed using a multi-step workflow including mineralogy, petrophysics, and geomechanics data. Pore pressure was modeled using Eaton's method, and in-situ stresses (vertical, maximum horizontal, and minimum horizontal) were calculated using the strain corrected method.

## Data and Materials

The study utilizes well log data from multiple wells in the Sarah Formation, core samples for acoustic measurements and uniaxial compression tests, and a database of petrophysical properties to develop electrofacies models.

## Key Findings

- The electrofacies model is highly effective for identifying Sarah Formation major units and boundaries, as well as overlying Qusaiba shales and underlying Qasim Formation lithology. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 14]])
- The 1-D MEM shows sharp changes in mechanical properties and pore pressure, indicating over-pressured and under-pressured zones within the Sarah Formation. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 14]])
- Core samples show a direct relationship between P-wave/S-wave velocities and porosity/density. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 14]])
- finding_page_note_for_item_3_is_actually_14_but_the_text_refers_to_core_samples_results_in_chapter_4_and_5_as_well ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016]])
- Uniaxial Compression Test (UCS) results show high variability in strength properties, with average values of Young's modulus and Poisson's ratio indicating a highly stiff and brittle nature for the Sarah Formation. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 14]])
- Calculated in-situ stresses suggest a reverse stress regime exists in the study area with a maximum stress gradient of 1.18 psi/ft. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 14]])
- Integration of MEM with electrofacies helps define mechanical properties through electrofacies, as all major facies show characteristic responses in terms of mechanical properties. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 14]])
- The Sarah Formation is a fining upward sandstone sequence with a maximum reported thickness of 350 m in outcrop. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 23]])
- Well-A core represents fine to coarse grain tight sandstone from the middle part of the formation. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 25]])
- The Sarah Formation is considered an unconventional tight gas reservoir in the subsurface. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 29]])
- Porosity and permeability in some areas of the Sarah Formation range between 53mD to 5D. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 30]])
- The study identified three types of fracture modes (opening mode, sliding mode, and shear mode) which reveal different stress regimes. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 31]])
- Dynamic moduli (Young's modulus and Poisson's ratio) showed a significant increase with increasing confining pressure in the Sarah Formation. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 55]])
- Sample 8H (Well-E) exhibited Young's modulus values ranging from 25.68 GPa to 42.91 GPa and Poisson's ratio between 0.155 and 0.194 across different confining pressures. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 55]])
- Sample 40H (Well-D) showed higher Young's modulus values, reaching up to 73.07 GPa at 35 MPa confining pressure. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 57]])

## Limitations

Selection of plugs for acoustic measurements was restricted by the requirement for specific dimensions (1.5 inch diameter, 2+ inch length) and the fractured condition of cores in some facies.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Use the electrofacies model in other regions to recognize Sarah Formation or equivalent formations (e.g., Zarqa Formation).
- Improve the efficiency of the electrofacies model by incorporating data from additional wells.
- Perform triaxial compressive testing for more accurate failure parameters (c and phi).
- Incorporate field test data like extended leak-off tests to enhance MEM reliability.
- Run reservoir simulations using dynamic and static elastic moduli to define fracture stimulation treatment.
- Develop a 3D MEM incorporating elastic and failure parameters for better delineation of mechanical properties.
