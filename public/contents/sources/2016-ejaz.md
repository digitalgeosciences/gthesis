# INTEGRATION OF ELECTROFACIES AND GEOMECHANICAL CHARACTERISTICS OF SARAH FORMATION (POTENTIAL TIGHT GAS RESERVOIR), RUB’ AL-KHALI BASIN, SAUDI ARABIA

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the Sarah Formation in the Rub’ al-Khali Basin as a potential tight gas reservoir. It integrates electrofacies analysis using unsupervised machine learning (PCA and K-means clustering) with geomechanical modeling (MEM) to characterize rock properties and define safe drilling windows.

---

## Research Problem

Tight gas reservoirs, specifically the Sarah Formation, have low porosity and permeability, making them difficult to develop without accurate knowledge of lithofacies associations and geomechanical properties to optimize hydraulic fracturing designs.

## Objectives

- Develop an electrofacies model for the Sarah Formation using multi-well log data.
- Perform a 1-D Mechanical Earth Model (MEM) to determine in-situ stresses and rock mechanical properties.
- Integration of the MEM with electrofacies.
- Integrate electrofacies with geomechanical parameters to characterize the reservoir's behavior.
- Determine safe mud weight windows for drilling operations.

## Methods

The study utilized core and well log data from six wells. Electrofacies were developed using Interactive Petrophysics software applying multivariate statistical methods to identify distinct lithofacies. Geomechanical properties (Young's modulus, Poisson's ratio, UCS) were determined through laboratory testing of 46 plugs (ultrasonic velocity measurements via Autolab 500 and uniaxial compression tests). These results were used to calibrate log-derived dynamic parameters to build a 1-D Mechanical Earth Model (MEM). Finally, the MEM was integrated with electrofacies to identify geomechanical characteristics across the reservoir.

## Data and Materials

Data included 19 core plug samples from 6 wells, well log suites (including sonic, gamma ray, and lithology logs), and a 1000 ft stratigraphic section for Well-C including Qusaiba Member, Baq’a Sandstone/Shale, Sarah Formation, and Quwarah Member.

## Key Findings

- The electrofacies model is highly effective in identifying Sarah Formation major units, boundaries, and overlying Qusaiba shales. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 14]])
- The 1-D MEM shows sharp changes in mechanical properties and pore pressure, indicating over-pressured and under-pressured zones within the Sarah Formation. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 14]])
- P-wave and S-wave velocities in the Sarah Formation show a direct relationship with porosity and density. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 99]])

- The Sarah Formation is characterized by high stiffness and brittle nature, with average values of Young's modulus and Poisson's ratio indicating these properties. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 99]])
- Calculated in-situ stresses suggest a reverse stress regime with a maximum stress gradient of 1.18 psi/ft. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 14]])
- Sample 8H (Well-E) showed Young's modulus ranging from 25.68 GPa to 42.91 GPa and Poisson's ratio between 0.155 and 0.194 across varying confining pressures. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 55]])
- Sample 40H (Well-D) showed Young's modulus ranging from 70.6 GPa to 73.07 GPa and Poisson's ratio between 0.219 and 0.228. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 57]])
- Uniaxial compression tests on samples W5 and W6 both showed a failure at approximately 77.7 MPa (UCS). ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 63]])
- A reverse stress regime exists in the study area with a maximum stress gradient of 1.18 psi/ft. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 99]])
- The recommended safe mud weight for the Sarah Formation ranges from 11,000 psi to 29,000 psi. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 78]])
- The electrofacies model successfully identified three distinct units: Baq’a Sandstone Member, Baq’a Shale Member, and Sarah Formation. ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 98]])
- The electrofacies model showed high correlation with elastic parameters (Young's modulus, Poisson's ratio) and strength parameters (UCS). ([[2016-ejaz|Ejaz, 2016, p. 95]])

## Limitations

Selection of plugs for acoustic measurements was restricted by the requirement for specific dimensions (1.5" diameter, >2" length) and the fractured condition of cores; not all facies could be represented in every test.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Use the electrofacies model in other regions to recognize Sarah Formation or equivalent formations like Zarqa Formation.
- Perform triaxial compressive testing for more accurate failure parameters (c and Ϧ).
- Utilize field test data such as extended leak-off tests to enhance MEM reliability.
- Run reservoir simulations using dynamic/static elastic moduli to design fracture stimulation.
