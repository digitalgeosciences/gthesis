# Microfacies Analysis of the Bajocian—Bathonian Dhruma Carbonates, Central Saudi Arabia

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the carbonate microfacies and reservoir properties of the Middle Jurassic Dhruma Formation in Central Saudi Arabia. Using a combination of field sedimentology, thin-section petrography, XRD, and core analysis, the study identifies eight distinct microfacies types across three outcrop sections to characterize the depositional environment as a carbonate ramp.

---

## Research Problem

Characterizing the complex carbonate systems of the Dhruma Formation (Bajocian-Bathonian) in Central Saudi Arabia to understand how mineralogy, microfacies, and diagenetic processes influence reservoir properties like porosity and permeability.

## Objectives

- Identify distinct microfacies within the Dhruma Formation (D2 to D4 Units).
- Determine the mineralogical composition of different microfacies using XRD and petrography.
- Interpret depositional environments for the identified microfacies.
- Evaluate the distribution of porosity and permeability across various microfacies.
- Construct a detailed analysis of the sedimentology in the study area through sedimentary observations including sedimentary structure and texture, and other key features during the fieldwork.
- Identify the microfacies of the D2–D4 Units of the Dhruma Formation and decipher their depositional environment.
- Integrate microfacies analysis and depositional environment with the petrophysical properties (porosity and permeability) to determine the reservoir potentials of the identified microfacies.
- Examine the carbonate microfacies of the Dhruma Formation using field observation and laboratory analysis.
- Quantify the abundance of different microfacies types across three outcrop sections.
- Determine the porosity and permeability values for various microfacies to assess reservoir quality.
- Establish a depositional environmental model based on integrated petrographic and sedimentological data.
- Identify and characterize distinct microfacies within the Dhruma Formation.
- Quantify the mineralogical composition of the carbonate units using XRD and petrography.
- Analyze the distribution and abundance of different microfacies across study sections.
- Perform biofacies analysis to interpret paleoenvironmental settings.
- Evaluate the relationship between microfacies, mineralogy, and reservoir properties (porosity and permeability).
- Identify and describe different microfacies types within the D2, D3, and D4 units of the Dhruma Formation.
- Determine the depositional environments for these microfacies using biofacies analysis.
- Evaluate petrophysical properties (porosity and permeability) across different microfacies associations.
- Compare the local carbonate ramp settings with other analogous systems, such as those in Oman.

## Methods

The study utilized a multi-step workflow: 1) Fieldwork included reconnaissance to select outcrops (Wadi, Road-cut, and Hafirat Nisah), followed by detailed sedimentological description of beds and collection of over 100 samples. 2) Laboratory work involved slabbing and etching samples with 10% HCl for analysis under an Olympus SZX7 stereomicroscope. 3) SEM (JEOL 5900 LV-Oxford X-MaxN) was used to determine mineralogical composition, cement types, and micropores (<4 µm). 4) Thin sections were prepared, stained with Alizarin Red-S, and analyzed under an Olympus BX51 polarized microscope. 5) XRD (Rigaku Ultima IV) was used to quantify carbonate minerals (calcite, dolomite, aragonite). 6) Core analysis using the AP-608 Automated Permeameter-Porosimeter measured porosity and permeability at 500 psi.

## Data and Materials

Analysis of D2, D3, and D4 units within the Dhruma Formation at the Hafirat Nisah area. Data includes thin sections for petrography, SEM images for mineralogy/pore analysis, and helium porosity-permeability plots for three distinct microfacies associations (lagoon, shoal complex, open marine).

## Key Findings

- Identified 8 distinct microfacies: MF1 (Peloidal Skeletal Wackestone), MF2 (Skeletal Peloidal Packstone), MF3 (Skeletal Oolitic Packstone), MF4 (Peloidal Grainstone), MF5 (Oolitic Grainstone), MF6 (Skeletal Floatstone), MF7 (Burrowed Wackestone), and MF8 (Mudstone). ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 17]])
- Calcite is the dominant mineral in the study area, accounting for over 90% of the composition, followed by dolomite (less than 10%) and silica minerals. ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 12]])
- The highest porosity and permeability values, indicating high-quality reservoir potential, were found in the burrowed wackestone (MF7) due to dedolomitization processes. ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 17]])
- Micritization, dolomitization, and cementation processes significantly reduced porosity and permeability values in mudstone (MF8) and peloidal skeletal wackestone (MF1). ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 17]])
- Oolitic grainstones (MF5) showed evidence of dissolution of ooid grains, which can enhance porosity. ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 13]])
- The Dhruma Formation is interpreted as a carbonate ramp system with environments ranging from lagoon to open marine settings. ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 17]])
- The Dhruma Formation is comprised of three distinct members (Lower, Middle, and Upper) or seven informal units (D1—D7). ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 18]])
- Microfacies analysis identified several types: ooid-bearing limestone with bioclasts/peloids, packstone with bioclasts/peloids, packstone with peloids, grain_stone with nodules, grain_stone (oolitic), float-lime, and mud_lime. ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 18]])
- Microfacies are divided into three groups: coastal lagoon (ooid-bearing limestone, packstone with bioclasts/peloids), shallow marine (packstone with peloids, grain_stone with nodules, grain_stone oolitic), and open marine (float-lime, ooid-bearing limestone, mud_lime). ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 18]])
- The most porous facies is the 'ooid-bearing limestone' which underwent dissolution processes. ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 19]])
- Cementation and dissolution reduced porosity and permeability in mud_lime and packstone with bioclasts/peloids. ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 19]])
- The Dhruma Formation spans the Bajocian to Bathonian ages (170—166.1 m.y.). ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 21]])
- Identified 8 distinct microfacies types (MF1-MF8) across the Dhruma Formation, with MF7 (Burrowed Wackestone) showing the highest permeability of 11.0 mD and MF4 (Peloidal Grainstone) having a permeability of 10.23 mD. ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 64]])
- The Dhruma Formation is characterized as a carbonate ramp, with facies ranging from lagoonal to open marine settings. ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 43]])
- MF1 (Peloidal Skeletal Wackestone) contains approximately 92.75% calcite and shows no observable porosity under microscope. ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 67]])

## Limitations

Limited information on sedimentary structures including burrows and lamination was recorded from the field work.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The author recommends using micro-Computed Tomography Scan (micro-CT) and mercury injection capillary pressure (MICP) to better understand the vertical and lateral continuity of burrows regarding porosity and permeability. Additionally, further diagenetic studies are needed for D3 and D4 units.
