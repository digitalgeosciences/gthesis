# Microfacies Analysis of the Bajocian—Bathonian Dhruma Carbonates, Central Saudi Arabia

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the microfacies and depositional environments of the Bajocian-Bathonian Dhruma Formation in Central Saudi Arabia. By integrating field observations, petrographic analysis (thin sections), SEM, XRD, and porosity-permeability measurements, the study identifies eight distinct microfacies across lagoon, shoal complex, and open marine settings.

---

## Research Problem

Characterizing the sedimentary architecture and reservoir potential of the Bajocian-Bathonian Dhruma carbonate ramp in Central Saudi Arabia, specifically identifying how diagenetic processes like dolomitization and cementation affect porosity and permeability across different microfacies.

## Objectives

- Construct a detailed analysis of the sedimentology in the study area through sedimentary observations including sedimentary structure and texture, and other key features during the field work.
- Identify the microfacies of the D2–D4 Units of the Dhruma Formation and decipher their depositional environment.
- Integrate microfacies analysis and depositional environment with the petrophysical properties (porosity and permeability) to determine the reservoir potentials of the identified microfacies.
- Identify and characterize distinct microfacies within the D2, D3, and D4 units of the Dhruma Formation.
- Quantify mineralogical composition using XRD and petrographic analysis to validate sedimentary observations.
- Evaluate the impact of diagenetic processes (e.g., dolomitization, calcitization) on porosity and permeability.

## Methods

The study utilized a multi-step workflow integrating field and lab work. Fieldwork involved selecting outcrops in the Darma' Quadrangle, performing detailed sedimentological descriptions, and collecting 110 carbonate samples from three sections (S1, S2, S3). Laboratory analysis included: 1) Petrography using Olympus SZX7 and BX51 microscopes to identify grain types, textures, and sedimentary structures; 2) SEM (JEOL 5900 LV-Oxford X-MaxN) to determine mineralogical composition, cement, and micropores (<4 µm); 3) XRD (Rigaku Ultima IV) to quantify carbonate minerals like calcite and dolomite; 4) Porosity and permeability measurements using the Coretest System Automated Permeater-Porosimeter (AP-608) with helium gas at 500psi. Microfacies were classified using the modified Dunham classification, while pore types were categorized using the Choquette and Pray (1970) system.

## Data and Materials

Analysis of 3 outcrop sections (Section 1: D2-D3; Section 2: lower D4; Section 3: upper D4). Data includes 80+ XRD samples, 69 core plugs for porosity/permeability measurements, and hundreds of thin sections for biofacies analysis.

## Key Findings

- Identified eight distinct microfacies: peloidal skeletal wackestone, skeletal peloidal packstone, skeletal oolitic packstone, peloidal grainstone, oolitic grainstone, skeletal floatstone, burrowed wackestone, and mudstone. ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 17]])
- The lagoonal setting is represented by peloidal skeletal wackestone and skeletal peloidal packstone. ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 17]])
- Skeletal oolitic packstone, peloidal grainstone, and oolitic grainstone were deposited in the shoal complex. ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 17]])
- The open marine setting is characterized by skeletal floatstone, burrowed wackestone, and mudstone. ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 17]])
- The highest values of porosity and permeability (highest quality reservoir) were found in the burrowed wackestone due to dedolomitization processes. ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 17]])
- Micritization, dolomitization, and cementation in mudstone and peloidal skeletal wackestone reduced porosity and permeability values. ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 17]])
- XRD analysis showed calcite as the dominant mineral (over 90%), followed by dolomite (less than 10%) and silica minerals (quartz and kaolinite). ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 65]])
- D4 Unit shows burrows with average diameters of 1 to 2 cm, significantly developed in the upper and lower parts. ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 39]])
- Thalassinoides trace fossils were commonly found in section 2, with tunnel-shaped structures having diameters of 2 to 3 centimeters. ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 40]])
- Identified 8 distinct microfacies (MF1-MF8), with Skeletal Peloidal Packstone and Burrowed Wackestone being the most abundant in the study area. ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 67]])
- D3 Unit is dominated by calcite (>90%) with low dolomite content (<5%), while D4 Unit shows increasing dolomite (up to 8% average) and higher heterogeneity. ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 83]])
- Porosity values for the studied samples range from 1% to 30.2% with an average of 12.5%, while permeability ranges from 0 to 8 mD (average 0.22 mD). ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 90]])
- High porosity in the D4 Unit is specifically attributed to calcitization/dedolomitization of dolomite rhombs in burrowed wackestone and moldic pores in oolitic packstones. ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 103]])
- Microfacies MF5 (Oolitic Grainstone) shows high porosity due to the dissolution of radial-fabric ooids, with average calcitization levels at 3%. ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 75]])
- Dolomite in the D4 Unit is characterized as non-fabric preserved (NFP) dolomite, which can be a factor for vertical and lateral heterogeneity. ([[2018-ismanto|Ismanto, 2018, p. 104]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

The author suggests using micro-Computed Tomography Scan and mercury injection capillary pressure (MICP) to better understand the continuity of burrows regarding porosity/permeability, and further diagenetic studies to understand post-depositional controls on reservoir quality.
