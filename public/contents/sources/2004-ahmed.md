# LABORATORY STUDY ON PRECIPITATION OF CALCIUM SULPHATE IN BEREA SANDSTONE CORES

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the kinetics of calcium sulphate (CaSO4) precipitation in Berea sandstone cores under varying temperature, pressure, and flow rate conditions. A kinetic model was developed to predict the formation of scale in porous media based on experimental core flooding data.

---

## Research Problem

Scale deposition from incompatible water mixing (e.g., seawater and formation water) causes significant permeability reduction in oil fields; however, there is a lack of specific reaction kinetics data for CaSO4 precipitation in porous media to accurately predict this behavior.

## Objectives

- Measure the amount and rate of scale deposition in porous rock from flowing supersaturated brine.
- Generate sufficient kinetic data to develop an empirical model for CaSO4 scale formation considering kinetics and hydrodynamic parameters.
- Evaluate the impact of temperature, pressure, flow rate, and supersaturation on precipitation.
- Determine the reaction rate of CaSO4 precipitation in Berea sandstone cores.
- Evaluate the influence of temperature, pressure, concentration (scaling index), and flow rate on the reaction rate constant.
- Identify the most appropriate kinetic rate law from existing literature for CaSO4 precipitation.

## Methods

The study utilized core flooding experiments where synthetic brines (CaCl2 and Na2SO4) were injected into Berea sandstone cores. The experimental setup included a Hassler-type core holder, dual syringe pumps for simultaneous injection, and a back pressure regulator. Conductivity was monitored to determine steady-state concentration. SEM and EDS were used to characterize the precipitated crystals. Kinetic rate constants (k) were calculated using various models from literature (Equations 4.18-4.23). A final model was developed by combining Arrhenius kinetics with flow-rate dependencies.

## Data and Materials

21 core flooding runs were performed on Berea sandstone cores (average porosity ~22%, average permeability ~200 mD). Three types of synthetic brines were prepared with varying scaling indices (SI = 1.235, 2.834, and 3.890). Analytical measurements included conductivity, pressure drop, and calcium concentration via atomic absorption.

## Key Findings

- The scaling index (S.I.) for the worst-case scenario was found to be 1.098 at a temperature of 95°C with a mixing ratio of 0.5:0.5, indicating high scaling potential. ([[2004-ahmed|Ahmed, 2004, p. 49]])
- The rate of CaSO4 precipitation increased significantly with higher temperatures and higher flood velocities. ([[2004-ahmed|Ahmed, 2004, p. 15]])
- Pressure had only a slight effect on the formation of CaSO4 scale. ([[2004-ahmed|Ahmed, 2004, p. 15]])
- The concentration of Ca2+ in the effluent was monitored to determine the rate of precipitation; for example, Run #8 showed specific trends in both Ca++ and conductivity. ([[2004-ahmed|Ahmed, 2004, p. 57]])
- SEM and EDS analysis confirmed the presence of calcium sulphate (gypsum/anhydrite) at both the inlet and outlet faces of the core. ([[2004-ahmed|Ahmed, 2004, p. 62]])
- The reaction rate constant (k) for CaSO4 precipitation was found to follow the Arrhenius equation with an activation energy of 22.9 kJ/mol. ([[2004-ahmed|Ahmed, 2004, p. 111]])
- A steady-state concentration of 2000 ppm of Ca++ was reached in Run #8, resulting in a reduction of 180 ppm across the core. ([[2004-ahmed|Ahmed, 2004, p. 74]])
- The average loss in porosity due to CaSO4 precipitation in Run #8 was estimated at 8% with a corresponding permeability loss of over 23%. ([[2004-ahmed|Ahmed, 2004, p. 79]])
- The reaction rate constant (k) showed no significant sensitivity to the scaling index (supersaturation level). ([[2004-ahmed|Ahmed, 2004, p. 93]])
- The reaction rate constant (k) increased nearly linearly with the total brine flow rate through the core. ([[2004-ahmed|Ahmed, 2004, p. 111]])
- Equation 4.22 (k = q / (C_Ca * C_So4 / Ksp - 1)) was identified as the most appropriate kinetic law for CaSO4 precipitation. ([[2004-ahmed|Ahmed, 2004, p. 109]])
- The final developed model (Equation 6.3/6.5) yielded a mean absolute percentage error of 11.16% in predicting rate constants across various conditions. ([[2004-ahmed|Ahmed, 2004, p. 102]])

## Limitations

The thesis notes that many existing models are based on thermodynamics alone and lack kinetic data to predict scale formation over time or in porous media. Additionally, some previous studies used only single-form solutions which did not account for the interaction of multiple minerals.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Use in-line ion analyzers for better effluent analysis.
- Incorporate variations in porosity and permeability into the reaction model.
- Utilize real oil field brines (e.g., mixing produced water and sea water).
- Test other core materials such as limestone and dolomite.
