# STUDY OF VISCOELASTICITY AND IFT AS A MICROSCOPIC OIL RECOVERY MECHANISM IN SANDSTONE FORMATION

**Summary**: The thesis investigates and compares the effectiveness of polymer viscoelasticity versus interfacial tension (IFT) reduction as microscopic oil recovery mechanisms in water-wet sandstone. Using a controlled experimental design with two fluids of equal viscosity but different properties, it concludes that while both methods reduce residual oil saturation, the viscoelastic polymer is more effective at low flux rates.

---

## Research Problem

Determining whether polymer viscoelasticity can serve as a viable replacement or complement for surfactant-based IFT reduction in mobilizing capillary-trapped residual oil in water-wet sandstone reservoirs, especially under field-relevant low flux conditions.

## Objectives

- Compare the relative roles of interfacial tension (IFT) reduction and polymer viscoelasticity on residual oil recovery.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of surfactant-glycerol solutions (IFT reduction only) versus synthetic polymer solutions (viscoelasticity but no IFT reduction).
- Generate capillary desaturation curves (CDC) to identify dominant microscopic recovery mechanisms.
- Assess the impact of varying flux rates on the mobilization of trapped oil.
- Compare the relative effectiveness of IFT reduction and polymer viscoelasticity in enhancing oil recovery in water-wet sandstone.
- Identify the dominant mechanism responsible for mobilizing residual oil trapped by capillary forces.

## Methods

The study utilized a controlled experimental design where two fluids were prepared with matched viscosities (100 cP). A surfactant-glycerol solution was formulated to provide IFT reduction without viscoelasticity, and a synthetic polymer (HPAM 3630) was used to provide viscoelasticity without significant IFT reduction. Core samples of Bentheimer sandstone were characterized for porosity and permeability using helium porosimetry and liquid permeability measurements. Interfacial tension (IFT) was measured using a KRÜSS spinning drop tensiometer and an Anton Paar Density Meter. Wettibility was assessed via contact angle measurements on sandstone chips. Coreflooding experiments were conducted at various flux rates (0.05 to 32 ft/day). NMR (Nuclear Magnetic Resonance) analysis was performed using an Oxford Instruments system to evaluate pore-scale fluid distribution and T2 relaxation times.

## Data and Materials

Two types of EOR fluids: 1) Surfactant-glycerol solution (CTAB + Glycerol), 2) Synthetic polymer solution (HPAM 3630 at 2000 ppm). Samples were tested in water-wet Bentheimer core samples with high permeability. Flux rates ranged from low to high, specifically highlighting results at 2 ft/day and 3 ft/day.

## Key Findings

- Viscoelastic polymer flooding provided higher incremental oil recovery compared to surfactant-glycerol flooding, particularly at lower flux rates. ([[2024-jameel|Jameel, 2024, p. 17]])
- The onset of rapid oil mobilization (desaturation) occurred earlier during polymer flooding at a flux rate of 2 ft/day, compared to 3 ft/day in the surfactant-glycerol flood. ([[2024-jameel|Jameel, 2024, p. 17]])
- Viscoelasticity is a rheological phenomenon with a dependency on time and flux rate, whereas IFT reduction is an interfacial phenomenon less influenced by flux rate. ([[2024-jameel|Jameel, 2024, p. 16]])
- The critical capillary number (Ncrit) marks the point where residual oil saturation (Sor) starts to decrease significantly as viscous forces begin to overcome capillary forces. ([[2024-jameel|Jameel, 2024, p. 41]])
- Polymer solutions with higher Deborah numbers were found more effective in mobilizing residual oil due to pronounced elastic behavior. ([[2024-jameel|Jameel, 2024, p. 36]])
- Extensional rheology is a more robust tool than the Deborah number for predicting polymer performance in EOR because it better accounts for flow through pore throats. ([[2024-jameel|Jameel, 2024, p. 48]])
- The surfactant-glycerol solution was designed to reduce IFT without exhibiting viscoelasticity, while the synthetic polymer solution had no IFT reduction capability but exhibited viscoelasticity. ([[2024-jameel|Jameel, 2024, p. 17]])
- The surfactant-glycerol solution achieved an IFT of 8.15 mN/m, significantly lower than the polymer-based system's IFT of 16.4 mN/m, despite both having a viscosity of 100 cP. ([[2024-jameel|Jameel, 2024, p. 74]])
- The surfactant-glycerol solution showed an intermediate wettability (contact angle of 25.3°), while the polymer solution also exhibited intermediate wettability (24°). ([[2024-jameel|Jameel, 2024, p. 80]])
- At a low flux rate of 0.05 ft/day, the polymer flood achieved an incremental oil recovery of 6.2%, compared to only 2.5% for the surfactant flood. ([[2024-jameel|Jameel, 2024, p. 82]])
- The critical capillary number (Nc) for the viscoelastic polymer flood was significantly lower (2.79e-5) than that of the surfactant glycerol flood (1.82e-4). ([[2024-jameel|Jameel, 2024, p. 93]])
- NMR analysis showed that the viscoelastic polymer successfully mobilized oil from both macropores and micropores, as evidenced by a significant increase in cumulative porosity across all T2 relaxation times. ([[2024-jameel|Jameel, 2024, p. 87]])
- The surfactant-glycerol solution's IFT was significantly lower in high-salinity (10xEU) brine compared to standard European Brine (EU). ([[2024-jameel|Jameel, 2024, p. 71]])
- The viscoelastic polymer flood exhibited additional recovery compared to the surfactant glycerol flood, which did not show additional recovery in the tested conditions. ([[2024-jameel|Jameel, 2024, p. 93]])
- Viscoelastic properties of the polymer flood enhance elongational flow and improve pore-scale displacement efficiency even at relatively low capillary numbers. ([[2024-jameel|Jameel, 2024, p. 93]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future research should focus on further elucidating the mechanisms of IFT reduction and polymer viscoelasticity at the pore scale, developing advanced modeling techniques, and investigating the integration of these methods with other EOR techniques like CO2 injection.
