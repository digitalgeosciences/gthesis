# Incorporating the Effects of Gas Slippage and Non-Darcy Flow in the Measurement of Permeability using Pressure Pulse Decay Technique

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of gas slippage and non-Darcy flow (NDF) on pressure pulse decay (PPD) measurements to determine permeability, Klinkenberg constant, and Forchheimer constants. It develops a numerical simulator and compares it with analytical solutions and experimental data to evaluate how these effects influence estimation accuracy.

---

## Research Problem

Neglecting gas slippage (Klinkenberg effect) and non-Darcy flow (Forchheimer effect) during the analysis of pressure pulse decay data can lead to significant errors in determining the true permeability of low-permeability rocks, especially when these effects are large enough to be significant but are often ignored or only approximately accounted for.

## Objectives

- Develop a mathematical model and numerical simulator that incorporates both gas slippage and non-Darcy flow effects.
- Develop a computer program using non-linear regression to analyze experimental pressure pulse decay data.
- Determine permeability, Klinkenberg constant (b), and Forchheimer coefficient ($\beta$) from experimental data.
- Evaluate the accuracy of various analytical solutions (e.g., Jones' method) compared to the developed simulator.
- Evaluate the impact of gas slippage and non-Darcy flow on the estimation of permeability and Klinkenberg constants from PPD data.
- Compare the results of the developed simulation with analytical solutions (e.g., Jones method) and experimental data.

## Methods

The study utilizes a mathematical model based on the mass balance for porous media, incorporating the Klinkenberg relation and the Forchheimer equation. A numerical scheme using the finite difference technique is implemented in a computer simulator (Appendix C) to solve the governing partial differential equations. Experimental data from pressure pulse decay tests are analyzed using a non-linear regression program (utilizing the DRNLIN subroutine from MSIMSL library). The study also compares results against several established analytical methods, such as those by Jones and others.

## Data and Materials

The study uses synthetic data generated from a numerical simulator for permeabilities ranging from 0.01 md to 1.0 md. Experimental data were collected from two specific core samples: H4-275-3 (porosity 0.1359, permeability ~9.5 md) and H4-113-3 (porosity 0.0634, permeability ~0.0134 md).

## Key Findings

- The simulation results showed that because gas slippage and non-Darcy flow have opposite effects on the pressure pulse decay, it is not reliable to determine permeability (k), Klinkenberg constant (b), and Forchheimer coefficient ($\beta$) simultaneously from a single test. ([[2000-khan|Khan, 2000, p. 18]])
- Separate tests must be designed and performed where the data are influenced by gas-slippage and non-Darcy flow effects separately to accurately determine k, b, and $\beta$. ([[2000-khan|Khan, 2000, p. 18]])
- The study confirms that the Klinkenberg effect is significant in low permeability media (e.g., clay and silt) where it can lead to large errors in gas-phase velocity calculations. ([[2000-khan|Khan, 2000, p. 37]])
- Forchheimer's equation describes the relationship between pressure gradient and velocity, with the non-Darcy coefficient ($\beta$) being a measure of inertial effects. ([[2000-khan|Khan, 2000, p. 22]])
- The Klinkenberg constant (b) is related to gas slippage at the fluid-solid boundary, which becomes significant at low pressures or in small pores. ([[2000-khan|Khan, 2000, p. 23]])
- Gas slippage and non-Darcy flow effects act in opposite directions on PPD curves; slippage causes faster pressure decay (leading to overestimation of k), while non-Darcy flow causes slower decay (leading to underestimation of k). ([[2000-khan|Khan, 2000, p. 73]])
- Ignoring the effect of gas slippage in a high-slippage scenario can lead to an error in permeability estimation as high as +150% (e.g., from 0.01 md to 0.024 md). ([[2000-khan|Khan, 2000, p. 86]])
- Neglecting non-Darcy flow effects in a scenario where they are significant can lead to an underestimation of permeability by approximately 25%. ([[2000-khan|Khan, 2000, p. 86]])
- The developed data analysis program showed high accuracy (error < 0.1%) for determining k and b when slippage was the dominant effect. ([[2000-khan|Khan, 2000, p. 90]])
- For core sample H4-113-3, the determination of permeability and Klinkenberg constant using the developed program yielded k = 0.0134 md and b = 55.46 psi. ([[2000-khan|Khan, 2000, p. 101]])
- The Jones method for determining k and b from experimental data showed significant scatter due to noisy derivatives in the pressure pulse decay data. ([[2000-khan|Khan, 2000, p. 113]])
- The Jones method for core sample H4-113-3 resulted in a permeability of 0.0138 md and a Klinkenberg constant of 46.13 psi. ([[2000-khan|Khan, 2000, p. 114]])
- It is not reliable to find the values of Permeability (k), Klinkenberg constant (b) and Forchheimer constant ($\beta$) simultaneously from a single pressure pulse decay test due to their opposing effects. ([[2000-khan|Khan, 2000, p. 127]])
- The Jones method is found to be unreliable for very tight core samples (< 0.1 md), specifically for those with permeability < 0.01 md, because early time data cannot be analyzed. ([[2000-khan|Khan, 2000, p. 128]])

## Limitations

The study notes that it is not reliable to find the values of k, b, and B simultaneously from a single PPD test because the effects of gas-slippage and non-Darcy flow act in opposite directions. Additionally, the Jones method is limited by noise in pressure derivative data for certain core samples.

## Recommendations and Future Work

To avoid errors in permeability estimation, it is necessary to measure initial upstream pressure with minimum error. The study suggests using specific experimental conditions where only one effect (slippage or non-Darcy) dominates to ensure accurate parameter determination.
