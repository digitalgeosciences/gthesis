# BIODEGRADATION OF PETROLEUM HYDROCARBONS USING INDIGENOUS HYDROCARBONS-DEGRADING BACTERIA FROM HEAVILY CONTAMINATED SOILS

**Summary**: The thesis investigated the biodegradation of petroleum hydrocarbons in heavily contaminated soils from the Eastern Region of Saudi Arabia. It focused on isolating indigenous bacterial strains and evaluating their ability to reduce total organic carbon (TOC) under varying temperature and pH conditions, concluding that higher temperatures and neutral-to-alkaline pH levels significantly enhance degradation efficiency.

---

## Research Problem

Soil contamination by total petroleum hydrocarbons (TPHs) from industrial activities in the Eastern Region of Saudi Arabia poses significant environmental risks. Existing physical, chemical, and thermal remediation methods are often costly or less effective than biological treatments using indigenous bacteria adapted to high hydrocarbon concentrations.

## Objectives

- Isolate indigenous bacteria that have degrading capability in the heavily contaminated soil.
- Determine the optimum temperature and pH for the biodegradation process.
- Identify the biodegradation enzymes used by the isolated bacteria.

## Methods

Soil samples (1 kg) were collected from two sites near Alhasa City. Soil characterization included pH measurement (mixed with distilled water at 1:2 ratio, agitated at 100 rpm for 5 days). TPH analysis was performed using GC-FID. Bacteria were isolated by incubating soil in nutrient broth and then on crude oil agar plates. Bacterial counts were determined via colony counting after serial dilution. Degradation tests involved inoculating Bushnell Haas broth with crude oil and bacterial cultures, monitored over four weeks at three temperatures (10°C, 25°C, 37°C) and three pH levels (4, 7, 10). TOC and DO were measured weekly. Enzyme screening for monooxygenases and dioxygenases was conducted using fluorescence assays with specific substrates like benzyl phenyl sulfide and phenylacetone.

## Data and Materials

2 soil samples from Al Jafurah and Al Uqayr areas; Bushnell Haas broth (magnesium sulphate, calcium chloride, dipotassium hydrogen phosphate, ammonium nitrate, ferrous chloride); crude oil as a carbon source; 100g of contaminated soil per sample.

## Key Findings

- The study achieved an average efficiency of 38% for the reduction of total organic carbon (TOC) using indigenous bacterial strains over a five-week period. ([[2024-almutawa|AlMutawa, 2024, p. 12]])
- Soil pH in the sampled areas was found to be between 7.0 and 7.2. ([[2024-almutawa|AlMutawa, 2024, p. 27]])
- Biodegradation efficiency significantly increased as temperature rose from 10°C to 37°C, with the most rapid TOC reduction occurring at 37°C. ([[2024-almutawa|AlMutawa, 2024, p. 32]])
- At 37°C, a rapid reduction in TOC levels was evident at pH 7 and 10, compared to pH 4, indicating higher bacterial activity at neutral to alkaline conditions. ([[2024-almutawa|AlMutawa, 2024, p. 33]])
- The slowest degradation of TOC was observed at 10°C across all pH levels. ([[2024-almutawa|AlMutawa, 2024, p. 35]])
- Heatmap analysis confirmed that the lowest TOC concentrations (highest biodegradation) occurred at 37°C and pH 7. ([[2024-almutawa|AlMutawa, 2024, p. 36]])
- The study found that indigenous bacteria were more efficient in degrading contaminated soil than a microbial consortium, with individual strains achieving 73.6% and 69.3% removal rates compared to 20-30% for consortia. ([[2024-almutawa|AlMutawa, 2024, p. 22]])
- Monooxygenases were identified as critical enzymes that convert hydrophobic compounds into more hydrophilic forms (alcohols, aldehydes, or ketones) to facilitate further degradation. ([[2024-almutawa|AlMutawa, 2024, p. 40]])

## Limitations

The study notes a gap in the literature regarding the comparative efficiency of degradation under optimal conditions and how environmental factors like temperature and even pH influence the interaction between indigenous bacteria and other soil organisms.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future research should include additional environmental factors such as salinity, moisture content, nutrient availability, and the presence of other microbial species. There is also a need to study the dynamics of microbial communities and increase global funding for multi-disciplinary collaboration in bioremediation.
