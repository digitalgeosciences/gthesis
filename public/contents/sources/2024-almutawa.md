# BIODEGRADATION OF PETROLEUM HYDROCARBONS USING INDIGENOUS HYDROCARBONS-DEGRADING BACTERIA FROM HEAVILY CONTAMINATED SOILS

**Summary**: The thesis investigated the biodegradation of petroleum hydrocarbons in heavily contaminated soils from the Eastern Province of Saudi Arabia. It evaluated the impact of temperature and pH on the degradation efficiency of indigenous bacterial strains, concluding that higher temperatures (37°C) and neutral to alkaline pH levels (7-10) significantly enhance TOC reduction.

---

## Research Problem

Industrial activities in the Eastern Region of Saudi Arabia, specifically in the 2nd and 3rd Dammam Industrial City and surrounding oil field sites, have led to significant soil contamination by total petroleum hydrocarbons (TPHs). Existing physical, chemical, and thermal remediation methods are often costly or impractical for large-scale use.

## Objectives

- Isolate indigenous bacteria that have degrading capability in the heavily contaminated soil.
- Determine the optimum temperature and pH for the biodegradation process.
- Identify the biodegradation enzymes used by the isolated bacteria.
- Evaluate the extent of environmental degradation of petroleum hydrocarbons by isolating and identifying bacterial species.
- Assess the impact of temperature and pH on the degradation efficiency of indigenous bacteria.
- Identify and characterize monooxygenase and dioxygenase enzymes in the isolated strains.
- Evaluate the extent of environmental degradation of petroleum hydrocarbons.
- Isolate and identify bacterial species capable of efficiently reducing total organic carbon (TOC) in contaminated soil.
- Determine the influence of temperature on hydrocarbon removal efficiency.
- Assess the impact of pH levels on microbial metabolic activities and enzymatic functions.

## Methods

Soil samples (1 kg) were collected from Al Jafurah and Al Uqayr areas. Soil characterization included pH measurement (mixed with distilled water at 1:2 ratio, agitated for 5 days) and TPH analysis using GC-FID. Bacteria were isolated from contaminated soil using nutrient broth and crude oil agar plates over a 10-day period. Bacterial counts were performed via colony counting after serial dilutions. Degradation tests involved incubating cultures in Bushnell Haas broth with 2 ml of crude oil at three temperatures (37°C, 25°C, 10°C) and three pH levels (4, 7, 10). TOC and DO were monitored weekly for four weeks. Enzyme screening for monooxygenases involved fluorescence measurement using specific reagents like Peroxy Green 1 and PTDH.

## Data and Materials

Soil samples collected from 2nd and 3rd Dammam Industrial City and oil field sites in the Eastern Region. Crude oil was used as the sole carbon source for bacterial growth. Analysis included monitoring TOC levels across various temperature (10, 20, 30, 37°C) and pH ranges.

## Key Findings

- The study achieved an average efficiency of 38% for the reduction of total organic carbon (TOC) using indigenous bacterial strains. ([[2024-almutawa|AlMutawa, 2024, p. 12]])
- Indigenous bacteria can utilize petroleum hydrocarbons as sources of carbon and energy, making them viable candidates for bioremediation. ([[2024-almutawa|AlMutawa, 2024, p. 15]])
- Monooxygenases and dioxygenases are the key enzymes responsible for initiating the breakdown of hydrocarbons by introducing oxygen atoms into hydrocarbon molecules. ([[2024-almutawa|AlMutawa, 2024, p. 15]])
- Previous studies showed that Actinomycetes could reduce TPH content by 86.3% from initial contamination under specific conditions (20-25°C, pH 7-8). ([[2024-almutawa|AlMutawa, 2024, p. 19]])
- Maximum bacterial activity for certain species was observed in the temperature range of 20-25°C and within a pH range of 7-8. ([[2024-almutawa|AlMutawa, 2024, p. 19]])
- Indigenous hydrocarbon-degrading bacteria were more efficient in biodegradation than a microbial consortium, with individual bacteria achieving removal rates of 73.6% and 69.3%. ([[2024-almutawa|AlMutawa, 2024, p. 22]])
- At 37°C and pH 7, indigenous hydrocarbon soil bacteria achieved an average reduction of approximately 38% in TOC within a 28-day period. ([[2024-almutawa|AlMutawa, 2024, p. 38]])
- TOC degradation was significantly higher at 37°C compared to 20°C and 10°C, showing a strong temperature dependency of bacterial efficacy. ([[2024-almutawa|AlMutawa, 2024, p. 34]])
- The lowest TOC levels (highest removal) were observed at 10 °C and pH 4, where significant concentrations remained under these conditions. ([[2024-almutawa|AlMutawa, 2024, p. 37]])
- Soil pH of the samples from the Eastern Region was found to be between 7.0 and 7.2. ([[2024-almutawa|AlMutawa, 2024, p. 27]])
- Temperature significantly impacts hydrocarbon removal; higher temperatures were found to increase the capacity of indigenous bacteria to degrade hydrocarbons, with degradation rates increasing exponentially as temperature rose. ([[2024-almutawa|AlMutawa, 2024, p. 39]])
- pH levels are a critical determinant; neutral to alkaline pH levels (specifically around 7.5) stimulated higher bacterial growth and enzymatic activity, leading to more efficient hydrocarbon degradation. ([[2024-almutawa|AlMutawa, 2024, p. 39]])
- Extreme acidity or alkalinity can inhibit microbial activity or lead to microbial death due to the disruption of hydrogen bonds and ionic interactions within enzyme structures. ([[2024-almutawa|AlMutawa, 2024, p. 39]])
- The cell membrane integrity of microorganisms is affected by pH; extreme values increase permeability, leading to leakage of essential ions. ([[2024-almutawa|AlMutawa, 2024]])

## Limitations

The complex interactions between microorganisms, hydrocarbons, and the environment are not yet fully understood.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Broaden research scope to include additional environmental factors such as salinity, moisture content, nutrient availability, and presence of other microbial species.
- Investigate the dynamics of microbial communities involved in biodegradation processes, including diverse bacterial species and their interactions.
- Increase global funding for research on bacterial biodegradation to support both basic research and practical applications.
- Promote partnerships between public institutions, private enterprises, and non-governmental organizations to share resources and perspectives.
