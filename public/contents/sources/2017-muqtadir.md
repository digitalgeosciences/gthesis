# Role of Fracturing Fluid on the Breakdown Pressure of Tight Sandstone Rocks

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of fracturing fluid types and saturating fluids on the breakdown pressure and geomechanical properties of tight sandstone rocks. Using a custom-designed fracturing cell, the study demonstrates that higher viscosity fluids lead to higher breakdown pressures and larger fracture dimensions, while brine saturation significantly reduces rock strength.

---

## Research Problem

There is significant uncertainty regarding how the type of fracturing fluid used affects the breakdown pressure of tight sandstone rocks, which can lead to inefficient hydraulic fracturing designs and non-economical production.

## Objectives

- Develop an efficient experimental setup to determine the breakdown pressure of tight sandstone rocks
- Study the effect of the type of fracturing fluid on breakdown pressure
- Investigate the geomechanical properties of tight sandstone rocks under different conditions
- Determine geomechanical properties (Young's Modulus, Poisson's Ratio) for dry, brine-saturated, and oil-saturated samples.
- Develop a fracturing cell setup to measure breakdown pressure under different fluid conditions.
- Evaluate the effect of various fracturing fluids (varying in viscosity) on breakdown pressure and fracture geometry.

## Methods

Geomechanical properties were determined using ultrasonic tests (NER AutoLab 1500) for dynamic properties and Brazilian disc tests for indirect tensile strength. Core analysis included NMR to determine pore systems and CT scans to visualize fracture behavior and internal features. A custom fracturing cell, modified from an aging cell, was used to measure breakdown pressure under constant confining pressure (100 psi). Fracturing fluids including Brine, Linear Gels (25 & 40 ppt), Crosslinked Gels (25 & 40 ppt), and a GLDA/Guar blend were prepared and their rheology measured using a Grace m3600. Post-test analysis involved CT scanning to quantify fracture height and width.

## Data and Materials

The study used 38 core plugs from the Scioto tight sandstone outcrop: 23 were used for fracturing tests (2 in. diameter, 2 in. length with a 0.25-in. central hole) and 15 were used for geomechanical tests (1.5 in. diameter, 3 in. length).

## Key Findings

- The study identified that the breakdown pressure of tight sandstone is influenced by the type of fracturing fluid used, as evidenced by two cases in the Qasim formation where different fluids resulted in significantly different breakdown pressures (19,287 psi vs 23,088 psi). ([[2017-muqtadir|Muqtadir, 2017, p. 56]])
- The study notes that for tight gas reservoirs, Saudi Aramco defines the criteria as permeability less than 1 mD and porosity less than 12%. ([[2017-muqtadir|Muqtadir, 2017, p. 32]])
- A reduction in Young's modulus of up to 50% and an increase in Poisson's ratio of 100% were observed in sandstone when water saturation ranged from 20% to 90%. ([[2017-muqtadir|Muqtadir, 2017, p. 62]])
- The study highlights that while slickwater is cost-effective and environmentally friendly, its low viscosity makes it an inefficient proppant carrier for certain applications. ([[2017-muqtadir|Muqtadir, 2017, p. 40]])
- Cross-linked gels are noted to be effective in high-temperature environments (up to 300 F) and deep reservoirs (>15,000 ft), which are common in Saudi Arabian tight gas formations like Unayzah and Sarah. ([[2017-muqtadir|Muqtadir, 2017, p. 52]])
- Average porosity of the tight sandstone cores was 13.31% with an average permeability of 1.3 mD. ([[2017-muqtadir|Muqtadir, 2017, p. 96]])
- NMR analysis revealed two pore systems (micropore and mesopore) with a mean porosity of 14.8%. ([[2017-muqtadir|Muqtadir, 2017, p. 99]])
- Brine saturation caused a 25% reduction in UCS and a 23% reduction in static Young's Modulus compared to dry samples. ([[2017-muqtadir|Muqtadir, 2017, p. 129]])
- Oil saturation resulted in a 10% reduction in UCS and a 13% reduction in static Young's Modulus. ([[2017-muqtadir|Muqtadir, 2017, p. 129]])
- Average tensile strength of dry rocks was 786 psi, reduced to 447 psi for brine-saturated samples (a 43% reduction). ([[2017-muqtadir|Muqtadir, 2017, p. 140]])
- A strong power relationship exists between fracturing fluid viscosity and breakdown pressure; average breakdown pressures ranged from 570 psi (Brine) to 1937 psi (40 ppt Crosslinked Gel). ([[2017-muqtadir|Muqtadir, 2017, p. 175]])
- Higher viscosity fluids resulted in larger fracture widths and heights, with crosslinked gels producing the largest dimensions. ([[2017-muqtadir|Muqtadir, 2017, p. 175]])
- Saturated samples showed a significant reduction in breakdown pressure: -38% for brine-saturated and -8% for oil-saturated compared to dry samples. ([[2017-muqtadir|Muqtadir, 2017, p. 182]])

## Limitations

The study notes that many existing studies on breakdown pressure use only one type of fracturing fluid or were performed on non-sandstone rocks, limiting the applicability of those results to tight sandstone systems.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Perform studies on samples with intermediate saturation levels.
- Extend research to lower permeability sandstones, carbonates, and shales.
- Utilize Acoustic Emission (AE) to monitor deformation, initiation, and propagation.
