# POROSITY CLASSIFICATION AND RESERVOIR FLOW UNITS RANKING IN THE KHARTAM MEMBER, KHUFF FORMATION (PERMO-TRIASSIC): OUTCROP APPROACH, CENTRAL SAUDI ARABIA

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the porosity and flow unit characteristics of the Upper Khartam Member of the Khuff Formation in central Saudi Arabia using an outcrop approach. It utilizes thin section petrography, Petrographic Image Analysis (PIA), and SEM to classify pore types by geometry and size, ultimately ranking reservoir quality based on these parameters.

---

## Research Problem

The high complexity of carbonate reservoirs and limited data within the inter-well space make it difficult to characterize flow units and identify their lateral and vertical variations. This study uses an outcrop approach to overcome low resolution in well data and provide a higher resolution for characterizing pore geometry, size, and connectivity.

## Objectives

- Identify the geometrical characteristics for each pore type and make the proper subdivision for the individual pore type when any geometrical difference is noticed.
- Establish the porosity-permeability relationship and determine the heterogeneity in the porosity and permeability.
- Integrate the porosity-permeability data to identify and rank the flow units at the outcrop scale.
- Determine the reservoir flow units ranking.
- Identify geometrical characteristics for each pore type and make proper subdivisions based on geometric differences.
- Rank flow units in the Upper Khartam Member based on petrophysical properties and diagenetic history.

## Methods

The study employed an outcrop approach in the At Tarafiyah area. Field work included mapping, identifying reservoir units (grainy layers), and determining their lateral extent and vertical thickness. Laboratory analysis involved core plug measurements for porosity and permeability using the water saturation method. Thin section petrography was used to identify grain types, cement, and matrix. Petrographic Image Analysis (PIA) was performed using JMicroVision software to quantify pore size, aspect ratio, and roundness factor. Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) was used to characterize microporosity.

## Data and Materials

The study analyzed four vertical sections (ranging from 6.5m to 9.5m thick) in the Upper Khartam Member at the At Tarafiyah area. Samples were collected for thin section analysis, SEM, and XRD across multiple lithofacies including well-sorted oolitic grainstone, cross-bedded oolitic fossiliferous grainstone, graded mudstone to packstone, and dolostone.

## Key Findings

- The study revealed two distinguished geometries of the moldic porosity (oomoldic and skelmoldic) and two different geometrical-sizes of the vuggy porosity (vuggy-1 and vuggy-2). ([[2014-mohammed|Mohammed, 2014, p. 17]])
- Oomoldic and vuggy-1 porosities are ranked to be the best porosity types in terms of size and their contribution in the overall porosity. ([[2014-mohammed|Mohammed, 2014, p. 18]])
- The combination of oomoldic and vuggy-1 form the optimum reservoir quality. ([[2014-mohammed|Mohammed, 2014, p. 18]])
- Coefficients of variance indicated major heterogeneity in the porosity and permeability, which can be attributed to the variation within the individual pore type, presence of micropore system and the mixture of the pore types within the individual facies. ([[2014-mohammed|Mohammed, 2014, p. 18]])
- Generally, poor correlation coefficient was obtained between porosity and permeability when the lithofacies are combined; however, this correlation improved significantly when porosity and permeability were plotted based on the individual facies group. ([[2014-mohammed|Mohammed, 2014, p. 18]])
- The cross-bedded oolitic skeletal grainstone facies has the highest flow units' quality where moldic and vuggy porosities are dominant. ([[2014-mohammed|Mohammed, 2014, p. 20]])
- In the vertical sections, the flow units boundaries perfectly coincide with the facies boundaries in most cases. ([[2014-mohammed|Mohammed, 2014, p. 20]])
- Well-sorted oolitic grainstone facies showed total core porosity of 25-35% and permeability of 10-23 mD; moldic porosity was the dominant type (16-28%). ([[2014-mohammed|Mohammed, 2014, p. 95]])
- Cross-bedded oolitic fossiliferous grainstone facies showed total core porosity of 25-35% and permeability of 10-23 mD; moldic, vuggy, intergranular, and intragranular porosities were present. ([[2014-mohammed|Mohammed, 2014, p. 99]])
- Graded mudstone to packstone facies had total core porosity of 5-10% and permeability of 0.3-1 mD; vuggy porosity was the dominant type. ([[2014-mohammed|Mohammed, 2014, p. 108]])
- Dolostone facies showed a total measured core porosity of approximately 10% and permeability of about 0.4 mD, with intercrystalline and intracrystalline porosities as common types. ([[2014-mohammed|Mohammed, 2014, p. 111]])
- Moldic pores in the well-sorted oolitic grainstone were identified as having an average aspect ratio near 1.5 (equidimensional) and a roundness factor of approximately 2 (rounded). ([[2014-mohammed|Mohammed, 2014, p. 96]])
- Vuggy porosity in the graded mudstone to packstone facies was characterized by small, scattered, disconnected pores with an average aspect ratio of 2. ([[2014-mohammed|Mohammed, 2014, p. 108]])
- Intercrystalline porosity in the dolostone facies was found to be larger and more angular/elongated than intracrystalline porosity. ([[2014-mohammed|Mohammed, 2014, p. 112]])
- Micro-fractures were identified as rare but potentially contributing to enhanced porosity and permeability when not filled with calcite cement. ([[2014-mohammed|Mohammed, 2014, p. 93]])
