# INFLUENCE OF NANOADDITIVES ON THE RHEOLOGY AND THERMAL PROPERTIES OF VISCOELASTIC SURFACTANT USED IN MATRIX ACIDIZING

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the influence of carbon nanotubes (CNT) and nanoclays (NC) as nanoadditives on the rheological and thermal properties of viscoelastic surfactants (VES) used in matrix acidizing. The study concludes that CNT-included samples exhibit superior thermal endurance and distinct chemical signatures compared to standard formulations.

---

## Research Problem

The research addresses the need for stable and effective viscosity-enhancing agents in harsh environments (high temperature and salinity) where standard visco-elastic surfactants (VES) may fail. Specifically, it explores how nanoadditives like CNTs and NCs can stabilize these systems and improve their performance in complex brine formulations.

## Objectives

- To compare and contrast the rheology and thermal properties of the commercial and locally synthesized VES products.
- To study influence of salinity (mono, di and tr-valent counter-ions) on the rheology of select Viscoelastic Surfactant (VES).
- To study the thermal stability of the select products under the influence of salinity and nanoparticles.
- To evaluate the efficiency of the VES in the presence of Carbon Nanotubes (CNT) and Nano-clays (NC).
- Evaluate the rheological properties of commercial vs. local VES formulations.
- To investigate the impact of carbon nanotubes (CNT) and nanoclays (NC) on the viscosity and stability of VES in various salt concentrations.

## Methods

The research involved preparing multi-component brines with high total dissolved solids (TDS 67,000 ppm). Viscoelastic surfactants (VES) were mixed with either carbon nanotubes (CNT) or nanoclays (NC) and subjected to ultrasound cavitation using a Vibra Cell Ultrasonicator for 30 minutes at 60% amplitude and 60°C. Rheological properties were measured using a TA HR-3 Discovery Rheometer at 65°C and 95°C, while viscosity was also measured using a KV 3000 viscometer and an Ostwald tube. Thermal stability was analyzed via Thermogravimetric Analysis (TGA) using an SDT Q1000 instrument. Chemical characterization of the functional groups in the samples was performed using 1H and 13C NMR spectroscopy on a Joel Instruments 500MHz system.

## Data and Materials

The study used DIVE.vt.5.HT surfactant, COOH-functionalized single-walled carbon nanotubes (CNT) with 1-2nm diameter, and organo-nanoclay platelets (NC). Multi-component brine was prepared using NaCl, Na2SO4, NaHCO3, CaCl2·2H2O, and MgCl2·6H2O to reach a TDS of approximately 67.5 g/L.

## Key Findings

- CNT-included samples showed the highest thermal endurance with regards to both solvent losses at 220°C for the divalent ions, making them most suitable for high-temperature formation applications. ([[2014-alao|Alao, 2014, p. 18]])
- The addition of zinc oxide nanoparticles resulted in a manifold increase in shear viscosity at a strain of 5% and caused the elastic modulus to become dominant over the viscous modulus. ([[2014-alao|Alao, 2014, p. 34]])
- A marked chemical-shift band evolved from NMR data distinguishing the compositions at different temperatures, where low-temperature samples showed strong alkylic character while high-temperature equivalents showed susceptible unsaturated bonds. ([[2014-alao|Alao, 2014, p. 18]])
- The coreflooding experiment with gelled acid/NC mix indicated wormholes that traveled halfway and a high pressure response, indicating good diversion potential at 65°C. ([[2014-alao|Alao, 2014, p. 19]])
- The gelled acid/CNT mix showed a breakthrough response in CT scans, implying they are less effective for diversion at 65°C compared to the NC mixture. ([[2014-alao|Alao, 2014, p. 19]])
- The Higiro et al. model successfully predicted the viscosity of both local and commercial VES, showing that 10,000cp could be achieved with approximately 4.5wt% for commercial and 8.24wt% for local products. ([[2014-alao|Alao, 2014, p. 57]])
- Multi-component brine (containing CaCl2, MgCl2, and FeCl3) resulted in higher zero-shear viscosity (up to 1200 Pas) compared to single-component brines at both 65°C and 95°C. ([[2014-alao|Alao, 2014, p. 59]])
- The addition of 0.1wt% CNT significantly increased the zero-shear viscosity and elastic modulus in high-temperature (95°C) environments compared to non-included batches. ([[2014-alao|Alao, 2014, p. 63]])
- TGA analysis showed that CNT-included formulations exhibited higher thermal stability, with only 35% mass loss as free solvent up to 220°C, making them suitable for high-temperature reservoirs. ([[2014-alao|Alao, 2014, p. 88]])
- H NMR spectra revealed that aged CNT-included samples showed the highest relative number of protons available for bonding (32.37 for R-H) compared to other formulations. ([[2014-alao|Alao, 2014, p. 100]])
- Commercial VES required less amount (4.5wt%) to reach a viscosity of at least 10,000cp at 30°C compared to local VES (8.3wt%). ([[2014-alao|Alao, 2014, p. 110]])
- C-13 NMR indicated an alkene band for the commercial product that was absent in the local formula after aging at 130°C. ([[2014-alao|Alao, 2014, p. 110]])
- Multi-component brine showed better rheology over single-component brine. ([[2014-alao|Alao, 2014, p. 110]])
- Viscosity fell by two orders of magnitude for Ca2+ loadings and four orders for Mg2+ loadings in certain formulations. ([[2014-alao|Alao, 2014, p. 110]])
- At 65°C, NC-modified surfactant showed superior properties using SW for Mg2+, while ordinary surfactants performed better with DW for Ca2+. ([[2014-alao|Alao, 2014, p. 110]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Fine-tune the chemistry of synthesized VES to compete with commercial equivalents.
- Investigate gel break-up in the presence of formation water, hydrocarbons, and mutual solvents.
- Test different corrosion inhibitors for Fe3+ protection.
- Conduct more coreflooding tests at higher temperatures.
