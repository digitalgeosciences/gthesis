# Wormhole Characterization Using Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR)

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the characterization of wormholes created by different acid systems (HCl, Emulsified Acid, and two types of Chelating Agents) in Indiana Limestone. It utilizes coreflooding experiments combined with micro-CT scanning and XRD analysis to evaluate the effectiveness and morphology of the resulting wormholes.

---

## Research Problem

Traditional methods like CT scans and differential pressure measurements are used to characterize wormholes, but there is a need for additional tools like NMR to provide better insights into pore size distribution and the impact of various acid systems (HCl, emulsified acid, and chelating agents) on carbonate reservoir connectivity.

## Objectives

- Validate NMR as a wormhole characterization technique by correlating its results with CT scan and coreflooding differential pressure data.
- Determine the performance of four different stimulation fluids (HCl, emulsified acid, and two chelating agents) in generating conductive wormholes through similar core plug samples.
- Evaluate the performance of Chelating Agent I and Chelating Agent II at different injection rates.
- Compare the morphology and extent of wormholes produced by various acid systems using micro-CT imaging.
- Analyze the mineralogical composition of dissolved materials to understand chemical interactions.
- Determine the impact of lithology on the effectiveness of different stimulation fluids.

## Methods

The study employed coreflooding experiments on Indiana Limestone samples. Core plugs were subjected to various acid systems (15% HCl, Emulsified Acid, Chelating Agent I, and Chelating Agent II) at varying injection rates (0.5, 1, 2, and 3 cm3/min). Micro-CT scans were performed before and after acidizing to visualize wormhole growth and volume. XRD was used to analyze the mineralogical composition of solids recovered from core holders. Petrophysical properties like porosity and permeability were measured for each core plug.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized 12 specific core plugs (numbered 2, 4, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12, 25, 25B, and 26) from the Indiana Limestone formation. Experimental conditions included a temperature of 200°F and pressures of 1,500 psi (confining) and 1,000 psi (back pressure).

## Key Findings

- NMR was able to accurately analyze pore size distribution and identify changes in interconnectivity between different pore systems while detecting formation damage. ([[2014-al-duailej|Al-Duailej, 2014, p. 13]])
- Emulsified acid showed the best performance across various injection rates and core plug samples compared to other acids. ([[2014-al-duailej|Al-Duailej, 2014, p. 13]])
- Chelating Agent I's performance was dependent on injection rate, pH value, and lithology. ([[2014-al-duailej|Al-Duailej, 2014, p. 13]])
- Chelating Agent II showed dependence on pH value and lithology, while HCl showed a clear dependence on lithology. ([[2014-al-duailej|Al-Duailej, 2014, p. 15]])
- HCl at an injection rate of 0.5 cm3/min resulted in high consumed PV (64) and was considered ineffective due to potential plugging by precipitated minerals. ([[2014-al-duailej|Al-Duailej, 2014, p. 29]])
- HCl at a higher injection rate of 1 cm3/min achieved breakthrough but with a very high and ineffective consumed PV. ([[2014-al-duailej|Al-Duailej, 2014, p. 29]])
- Emulsified acid showed immediate pressure drops indicating the completion of breakthrough and low PVBT across all tested injection rates (0.5, 1, and 2 cm3/min). ([[2014-al-duailej|Al-Duailej, 2014, p. 47]])
- CT scan images confirmed that emulsified acid followed paths of least density to create highly conductive wormholes. ([[2014-al-duailej|Al-Duailej, 2014, p. 47]])
- Chelating Agent I at an injection rate of 0.5 cm3/min did not achieve breakthrough and showed evidence of face dissolution. ([[2014-al-duailej|Al-Duailej, 2014, p. 61]])
- Chelating Agent I at 2.0 cm3/min achieved a breakthrough after consuming 7.8 PVBT, indicating the generation of a broad wormhole. ([[2014-al-duailej|Al-Duailej, 2014, p. 68]])
- Chelating Agent I at 3.0 cm3/min produced a narrower wormhole than that achieved at 2.0 cm3/min, with a breakthrough at 5.1 PVBT. ([[2014-al-duailej|Al-Duailej, 2014, p. 71]])
- Chelating Agent II at  were observed to have a much higher success rate in creating wormholes compared to Chelating Agent I at similar rates. ([[2014-al-duailej|Al-Duailej, 2014, p. 76]])
- XRD analysis of material from Chelating Agent I (at 0.5 and 1 cm3/min) showed it consisted of 69% Calcite and 31% Dolomite. ([[2014-al-duailej|Al-Duailej, 2014, p. 65]])
- Emulsified acid consumed only 0.51 PVBT in sample 26B, showing high efficiency compared to HCl which consumed 4.93 PVBT on a similar lithology. ([[2014-al-duailej|Al-Duailej, 2014, p. 84]])
- Chelating Agent II at 1 cm3/min achieved breakthrough after consuming 1.2 PVBT in sample 12, potentially aided by an undetected fracture. ([[2014-al-duailej|Al-Duailej, 2014, p. 76]])

## Limitations

Some core plug samples were not fully saturated due to the presence of hydrocarbons or precipitates, which could affect NMR results. One sample with Chelating Agent I showed a result that was not conclusive because the core plug was broken from the side.
