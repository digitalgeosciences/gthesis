# Effect of Saturation on Acoustic of Carbonate Rock

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the acoustic properties of carbonate rocks in the Dam formation, specifically focusing on how P-wave and S-wave velocities respond to changes in fluid saturation and confining pressure. It compares several empirical models (Gassmann, Khaksar, and a proposed polynomial model) to determine the most accurate method for predicting rock velocity under varying reservoir conditions.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the difficulty of accurately modeling P-wave and S-wave velocities in carbonate rocks under different saturation levels and confining pressures. Existing models like Gassmann's often fail to provide high accuracy for saturated samples, necessitating more robust empirical relationships for better reservoir characterization.

## Objectives

- Capture petrophysical parameters (macro-porosity, porosity types, and mineralogy) from thin sections of the rock samples and provide full petrographic description
- Determine the porosity, permeability, and density of rock samples at ambient condition
- Measure porosity and permeability as a function of confining pressure
- Determine pore size distribution, pore surface area, and the mean distance between pores
- Determine the compressional and shear velocities in dry and fully saturated samples as a function of confining pressure
- Develop empirical relationships between acoustics (Vp, Vs) at various fluid saturations and core porosity for carbonate reservoir rocks.

## Methods

The study involved field investigations to collect over 80 outcrop samples from the Dam Formation. Laboratory work included core drilling (vertical and horizontal), cutting into segments using a high-speed rotary saw, and grinding end-faces with an End-Face Grinder. Petrographic analysis was performed on 31 thin sections using binocular microscopy. Petrophysical measurements included porosity, permeability, and density at ambient conditions and under varying confining pressures. Acoustic data analysis involved measuring P-wave (Vp) and S-wave (Vs) velocities in dry and fully saturated samples across a range of confining pressures.

## Data and Materials

80+ outcrop samples from the Dam Formation; 31 thin sections for petrographic study; core segments cut into pieces ranging from 0.9 to 3.5 inches; measurements taken under varying confining pressures (up to 50 MPa in literature, specific range used in study not explicitly quantified but implied as a standard laboratory program).

## Key Findings

- The Dam Formation in the Al-Lidam area contains four facies types: mudstone, packstone, wackestone, and grainstone. ([[2012-elhaj|Elhaj, 2012, p. 13]])
- Measured porosity values for the Dam Formation range from 52% to 55% (Arabic text) or average 38% (English abstract), with permeability ranging from 0.39 mD to 18 D. ([[2012-elhaj|Elhaj, 2012, p. 13]])
- P-wave and S-wave velocities increase with confining pressure under dry conditions, but P-wave velocity increases while S-wave velocity decreases or remains flatter under saturated conditions. ([[2012-elhaj|Elhaj, 2012, p. 76]])
- The P-wave velocity increased and the S-wave velocity decreased with confining pressure under saturated condition. ([[2012-elhaj|Elhaj, 2012, p. 13]])
- A regression equation of the form V = AP^2 + BP + C provides a good fit to the measured velocity compared with other studies. ([[2012-elhaj|Elhaj, 2012, p. 13]])
- The Dam Formation is approximately 41 m thick and consists of marls, clays, and limestone with minor sandstones. ([[2012-elhaj|Elhaj, 2012, p. 27]])
- The Dam formation contains four primary textures: Mudstone (7-15% porosity), Wackestone (25-27%), Packstone (25-34%), and Grainstone (22-40%). ([[2012-elhaj|Elhaj, 2012, p. 42]])
- Grainstone texture exhibits the highest average porosity (36.3%) and largest pore sizes (up to 800 microns). ([[2012-elhaj|Elhaj, 2012, p. 50]])
- Porosity measurements from Helium Porosimeter and AP-608 showed a difference of less than 3% between the two devices. ([[2012-elhaj|Elhaj, 2012, p. 53]])
- Permeability values in the Dam formation range significantly from 0.39 mD to 18,748 mD, with an average of 3,375 mD. ([[2012-elhaj|Elhaj, 2012, p. 57]])
- The average porosity for Grainstone was found to be 36.3% with a corresponding average permeability of 3201.8 mD. ([[2012-elhaj|Elhaj, 2012, p. 62]])
- Four carbonate facies were identified based on thin section description and Dunham's classification: Mudstone (porosity 7-15%), Wackestone (25-27%), Packstone (25-34%), and Grainstone (22-40%). ([[2012-elhaj|Elhaj, 2012, p. 96]])
- Grainstone samples were the most common and exhibited the highest porosity values. ([[2012-elhaj|Elhaj, 2012, p. 96]])
- Measured porosity via Helium Porosimeter ranged from 24.36% to 54.22% (avg 37.87%), while AP-608 measurements were between 24.97% and 55.73% (avg 38.5%). ([[2012-elhaj|Elhaj, 2012, p. 97]])
- Dam formation samples showed a wide range of permeability from 0.39 mD to 18,748.2 mD, with an average of 3,375.3 mD. ([[2012-elhaj|Elhaj, 2012, p. 97]])

## Limitations

The proposed polynomial equations are not applicable for extrapolating beyond the experimental limit (P > 25MPa) because prediction error increases as pressure increases.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Study the connectivity between pores and gross geometrical classification of pore size using mercury porosimetry.
- Use XRD for more detailed petrographic description.
- Study the attenuation of P- and S-waves versus temperature (T) for samples saturated with heavy oil at constant reservoir confining pressure.
- Measure Vp and Vs vs. temperature (T) for samples saturated with heavy oil at constant reservoir confining pressure.
- Measure Vp and Vs vs. confining pressure for samples saturated with light oil at constant reservoir temperature.
