# IDENTIFICATION OF AQUIFER STORAGE AND RECOVERY POTENTIAL SITES IN CENTRAL – EAST REGION OF SAUDI ARABIA

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the identification of Aquifer Storage and Recovery (ASR) potential sites in the central-east region of Saudi Arabia. It utilizes an integrated approach combining GIS, remote sensing, and Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) to evaluate thematic layers such as aquifer geometry, geomorphology, and groundwater chemistry.

---

## Research Problem

The rapid depletion of groundwater resources due
to industrial, agricultural, and domestic demands in arid regions like Saudi Arabia necessitates the identification of suitable sites for Aquifer Storage and Recovery (ASR) to ensure sustainable water management.

## Objectives

- To identify the potential locations for ASR in the central–east Region of Saudi Arabia.
- To help decision-makers and water planners to explore alternative sustainable ways for enhancing and managing the groundwater resources.
- Identify potential sites for Aquifer Storage and Recovery (ASR) using GIS, remote sensing, and MCDA-WOA methods.
- Evaluate the suitability of different aquifers (Neogene, Dammam, and UER) for ASR implementation.
- Identify potential sites for Aquifer Storage and Recovery (ASR) in the central-east region of Saudi Arabia.
- Evaluate various hydrogeological parameters including aquifer thickness, transmissivity, and groundwater salinity.
- Integrate multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) with GIS and remote sensing to rank suitability for artificial recharge.
- Identify potential sites for Aquifer Storage and Recovery (ASR) in the Central and Eastern regions of Saudi Arabia
- Evaluate groundwater resources and suitability for storage using remote sensing and GIS techniques

## Methods

The study utilizes a Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) integrated with Geographic Information Systems (GIS) and Remote Sensing (RS). The methodology involves: 1. Generating thematic layers including aquifer system, geomorphology, slope, soil texture, lineaments, land use/cover, aquifer depth, aquifer thickness, aquifer transmissivity, and groundwater salinity. 2. Weight assignment using the Weighted Overlay Analysis (WOA) method where each parameter is assigned a weight based on its influence on ASR suitability. 3. Proximity analysis to determine the distance of potential sites from water sources like desalination plants and sewage treatment facilities.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized maps and data for three primary aquifers: Neogene, Dammam, and UER. Parameters included thickness measurements (e.g., Neogene < 65-345m; Dammam < 170-450m; UER < 40-500m), transmissivity values (Neogene < 1604-3455 m2/day; Dammam < 35-2497 m2/day; UER < 138-950 m2/day), and salinity levels. Land cover data included categories like Scrub, Bare ground, and Built Area.

## Key Findings

- The study area covers approximately 350,000 km’s between latitude 26° 05' 59.99" N and longitude 51° 00' 0.00" E. ([[2022-pambudi|Pambudi, 2022, p. 19]])
- The average annual precipitation in the study area is approximately ± 74 mm/year. ([[2022-pambudi|Pambudi, 2022, p. 20]])
- Total water consumption in the three major provinces (Riyadh, Qasim, and Eastern Provinces) is about 1920 million m³ / year. ([[2022-pambudi|Pambudi, 2022, p. 20]])
- The study identifies four categories of artificial recharge zones: Good, Moderate, Low, and Very low. ([[2022-pambudi|Pambudi, 2022, p. 14]])
- High priority ASR potential sites are those categorized as 'Good' and located in close proximity to desalination plants or sewage treatment plants. ([[2022-pambudi|Pambudi, 2022, p. 14]])
- The study identifies three primary aquifers for ASR: Neogene (depth <4-30m), Dammam (depth 9-80m), and UER (depth 100-700m). ([[2022-pambudi|Pambudi, 2022, p. 51]])
- The land cover map shows that bare ground covers 51% and scrub/shrub covers 45% of the study area. ([[2022-pambudi|Pambudi, 2022, p. 50]])
- Slope values in the research region range from 0 to 15%, categorized into five groups including flat (0-2%), gentle (2-4%), moderate (4-8%), moderate to steep (8-15%), and steep (>15%). ([[2022-pambudi|Pambudi, 2022, p. 33]])
- The Neogene aquifer depth varies from <4m to 30m, with the most shallow parts in the middle of the study area. ([[2022-pambudi|Pambudi, 2022, p. 51]])
- Dammam aquifer depth varies from <9m to 80m, increasing continuously from northwest to southeast. ([[2022-pambudi|Pambudi, 2022, p. 52]])
- UER aquifer depth ranges from <100m to 700m, with the deepest parts in the southeast region. ([[2022-pambudi|Pambudi, 2022, p. 52]])
- The Neogene aquifer thickness varies from less than 65 m in the western part to 295-345 m in the eastern region. ([[2022-pambudi|Pambudi, 2022, p. 54]])
- Dammam aquifer thickness ranges from < 170 m to 450 m, with the largest values (310-450 m) located in the central and southeast parts. ([[2022-pambudi|Pambudi, 2022, p. 54]])
- UER aquifer thickness varies from < 40 m to 500 m, with the maximum of 360-500 m occurring in the northeast portion. ([[2022-pambudi|Pambudi, 2022, p. 54]])
- Neogene aquifer transmissivity ranges from < 1604 to 3455 m2/day, with highest values (2530-3450 m2/day) in the southeast. ([[2022-pambudi|Pambudi, 2022, p. 56]])
