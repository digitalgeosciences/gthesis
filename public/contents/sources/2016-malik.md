# MICROPALEONTOLOGY AND SEQUENCE STRATIGRAPHY OF MIDDLE JURASSIC D4-D5 MEMBERS OF DHRUMA FORMATION, CENTRAL SAUDI ARABIA

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the D4 and D5 members of the Dhruma Formation in Central Saudi Arabia using sedimentology, micropaleontology, and sequence stratigraphy. By analyzing 85 samples through thin sections, XRD, XRF, and SEM, the study identifies 10 lithofacies and establishes a sequence stratigraphic framework to determine that the D5 member was deposited in an open marine subtidal environment.

---

## Research Problem

The lack of detailed micropaleontological studies on the shale/marl units (D4-D5 members) of the Dhruma Formation, which limits the high-resolution sequence stratigraphy and understanding of depositional environments for these specific facies in the Middle Jurassic of Central Saudi Arabia.

## Objectives

- Field trips to the area for detailed sampling and outcrop description of designated location.
- Collection of samples from both hard limestone and soft muddy/mary layers.
- Extraction of foraminifera and other microfossils using soap disaggregation and acid leaching methods.
- Conduct thin section and geochemical studies (XRF, XRD, SEM) for sedimentology.
- Formalize a full description of the micro-fauna present in the samples.
- Build a depositional model based on microfacies and biofacies.

## Methods

The study utilized a multi-pronged approach: 1) Fieldwork included detailed outcrop description, lithostratigraphic logging, and collection of 85 samples from a ~70m section. 2) Laboratory sedimentology involved cutting slabs for petrographic analysis (thin sections) to identify rock types, textures, and mineralogy, supplemented by XRF for elemental composition, XRD for mineralogical details, and SEM for high-magnification texture analysis. 3) Micropaleontology utilized two extraction methods: soap disaggregation for muddy samples and an acetic acid leaching method (80% acetic acid/20% water) for hard limestone samples to extract foraminifera. 4) Data integration of lithofacies, biofacies, and micropaleontological counts were used to define sequence boundaries and depositional environments.

## Data and Materials

The study analyzed the D4 and D5 members of the Dhruma Formation in Central Saudi Arabia. Data included thin sections from various lithofacies, XRF and XRD results for multiple samples (e.g., Samples 16D, 38, 02A, 16E, 34, 60, 49, 65, 04, 07, 36A, 11A, 42, 03A, 40, 64, 68, 18B), and a collection of 35 for1aminifera species across 19 genera.

## Key Findings

- The study identified 10 distinct lithofacies ranging from deep water muds to shallow water oolitic grainstones. ([[2016-malik|Malik, 2016, p. 53]])
- A total of 35 species belonging to 19 different genera were identified and documented in the micropaleontological analysis. ([[2016-malik|Malik, 2016, p. 17]])
- The D4 member (Uwaynid) contains well-bedded mudstone limestones at the base transitioning to grainstone limestone towards the top. ([[2016-malik|Malik, 2016, p. 34]])
- The D5 member (Barrah) contains well-bedded yellow fossiliferous limestones at the base, followed by green marls and a bioclastic limestone with several paleo-hardground beds rich in ammonite fauna. ([[2016-malik|Malik, 2016, p. 34]])
- XRD results for multiple samples showed calcite as the major mineral component with no traces of dolomite (e.g., Figures 4.4, 4.6, 4.8). ([[2016-malik|Malik, 2016, p. 10]])

- Identified 10 distinct lithofacies ranging from mudstones to oolitic grainstones (Cgdp, Esp, Og, Bw-p, Fp-g, Em-w, Bog, Fw, Spgdp, Ms). ([[2016-malik|Malik, 2016, p. 55]])
- XRD analysis confirmed that the majority of lithofacies are composed of calcite (CaCO3) with no significant traces of dolomite. ([[2016-malik|Malik, 2016, p. 57]])
- XRF analysis showed high calcium content in all studied facies, with minor amounts of Fe, Mn, Si, and Cr. ([[2016-malik|Malik, 2016, p. 59]])
- The Og facies (Oolitic grainstone) contains over 80% non-skeletal grains (ooids) in some samples. ([[2016-malik|Malik, 2016, p. 63]])
- The Fp-g facies shows a high concentration of foraminifera, with sample 65 showing abundant recovery of large and small fossils. ([[2016-malik|Malik, 2016, p. 104]])
- Identified 35 foraminiferal species belonging to 19 different genera within the Dhruma Formation. ([[2016-malik|Malik, 2016, p. 87]])
- The study identified 11 distinct foraminiferal biozones based on first and last occurrences of specific species. ([[2016-malik|Malik, 2016, p. 128]])
- Identified 18 high-frequency cycles (12 fining upward, 6 coarsening upward) which were grouped into 3 fourth-order sequences. ([[2016-malik|Malik, 2016, p. 126]])
- The depositional environment is interpreted as a subtidal setting on the middle portion of a shallow open marine ramp. ([[2016-malik|Malik, 2016, p. 124]])

## Limitations

Limitations include the non-availability of ammonite data and poor microfossil recovery in certain units (like the lower D5 member) due to high weathering and friability of the rock.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future work should extend the study to lower unstudied members of the formation, perform reservoir characterization for potential subsurface evaluation, and conduct water depth calibrations for the identified microfossils.
