# Sand Consolidation by Catalyzed Low-Temperature Oxidation

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the use of catalyzed low-temperature oxidation (LTO) of asphaltenes to consolidate sand and prevent sand production. It evaluates the impact of ferric nitrate concentration, temperature, and oxidation time on the resulting sand's compressive strength, permeability, and stability against aromatic solvents.

---

## Research Problem

Sand production from poorly consolidated formations causes significant damage to wellbore equipment; while LTO is a viable consolidation method, its reaction time can be long for field applications. The study aims to reduce this reaction time using catalysts (ferric nitrate) and higher temperatures.

## Objectives

- Investigate the catalytic effect of metallic additives, specifically ferric nitrate Fe(NO3)3, in accelerating the low-temperature-oxidation phase of the consolidation process.
- Determine the optimal concentration of ferric nitrate to enhance compressive strength while maintaining permeability.
- Evaluate the impact of different oxidation temperatures and durations on the stability and quality of the consolidated sand.

## Methods

The study involved preparing a sand pack (primarily quartz) from the Arabian Gulf coast, which was washed with acid and distilled water. Two fluids were used: an asphaltene-in-toluene solution (40 wt% concentration) and aqueous solutions of ferric nitrate at 3%, 6%, and 12% concentrations. The oxidation process occurred in a custom-built steel consolidation cell under high pressure and temperature. Permeability was measured using a vacuum pump and injection system, while compressive strength was assessed via scratch tests and stability tests involving soaking in toluene, mud acid, and crude oil.

## Data and Materials

Sand samples (primarily quartz) from the Arabian Gulf coast; 3 types of ferric nitrate solutions (3%, 6%, 12% wt); asphaltene-in-toluene solution (40 wt%); consolidation cell (8.5 inches long, 1.121 inch ID).

## Key Findings

- The addition of ferric nitrate at a 3% concentration was identified as the optimal catalyst to accelerate the LTO process. ([[2020-hiba|HIBA, 2020, p. 51]])
- Permeability remained stable between 2.63 and 2.69 Darcies across all tested temperatures (70, 90, 135, and 180°C), showing minimal impact from the oxidation process. ([[2020-hiba|HIBA, 2020, p. 48]])
- Compressive strength increased significantly with temperature: 250 psi at 70°C, 1080 psi at 90°C (with catalyst), and over 1700 psi at 180°C. ([[2020-hiba|HIBA, 2020, p. 48]])
- Only samples treated at 180°C showed full stability against aromatic solvents (toluene). ([[2020-hiba|HIBA, 2020, p. 49]])
- The use of a 3% ferric nitrate solution at 180°C reduced the required oxidation time to 4 hours while maintaining high compressive strength and permeability. ([[2020-hiba|HIBA, 2020, p. 58]])
- Ferric nitrate did not cause significant permeability loss, unlike clay minerals which were used in previous studies. ([[2020-hiba|HIBA, 2020, p. 51]])

## Limitations

The study notes that while ferric nitrate improves strength, it does not improve stability at lower temperatures (70-90°C) without sufficient oxidation time or higher temperature energy.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study suggests that in-situ heating of the wellbore to 200°C using thermochemicals could facilitate LTO application without external heating systems.
