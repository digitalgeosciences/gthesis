# DEVELOPMENT OF AN ENVIRONMENT-FRIENDLY OIL-BASED MUD USING CANOLA OIL

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the development of an environmentally friendly oil-based mud (OBM) system using canola oil as the base oil. It evaluates the impact of lime concentration, viscosity additives, and filtration control agents on rheological properties and stability under high-temperature/high-pressure (HTHP) conditions.

---

## Research Problem

Conventional oil-based muds (OBMs) use No. 2 diesel or mineral oils which are highly toxic and environmentally hazardous, leading to strict regulations and high disposal costs in regions like Saudi Arabia. There is a need for sustainable, biodegradable, and non-toxic alternatives that meet technical requirements for drilling in challenging environments.

## Objectives

- To develop an environment-friendly OBM using canola oil that can serve as a potential alternative to the conventional diesel oil-based mud.
- To use commercially available chemical additives that would be carefully selected, and be petro-free components for the development of the environment friendly OBM system.
- Evaluate the suitability of canola oil as a base oil for OBM systems.
- Determine the optimal lime concentration to stabilize the mud system and control alkalinity.
- Optimize the concentration of viscosifiers and filtration control additives to maintain stability under HTHP conditions.
- Develop a final formulation that provides stable rheological properties (PV, YP, Gels) and low filtrate loss.

## Methods

The study involved formulating 15 different mud systems using canola oil as the base. A high-speed mixer was used to ensure proper emulsification of additives. The research followed a four-stage process: 1) Determining optimal lime concentration (0, 3, 5, 7, and 10 ppb). 2) Optimization of the formulation based on stability at room temperature and under hot-rolling conditions. 3) Testing for HTHP effects to identify additives causing rheological instability. 4) Final modification of additive concentrations (viscosifier and filtration control agent) to stabilize the system.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized canola oil (selected for its GRAS status and non-toxicity), various chemical additives (weighting agents like barite/hematite; thickeners like bentonite; filtration control agents like starch/polymer), and two distinct mud formulations (BHR and AHR). Samples were aged for 16 hours at high temperatures.

## Key Findings

- Canola oil was confirmed to meet standard specifications required as a base oil for OBM formulation, being non-toxic and environmentally friendly. ([[2011-sanmi|Sanmi, 2011, p. 11]])
- The developed canola-based OBM met all requirements and compared favorably with the synthetic-based mud currently used in offshore locations in Saudi Arabian waters. ([[2011-sanmi|Sanmi, 2011, p. 11]])
- Canola oil based OBM performed comparably to synthetic systems in terms of rheology, stability, quantity of additives used, and level of toxicity. ([[2011-sanmi|Sanmi, 2011, p. 11]])
- A typical well can generate 1000-1500 tonnes of cuttings with approximately 15% oil retention (150-225 tonnes of toxic oil) when using diesel-based muds. ([[2011-sanmi|Sanmi, 2011, p. 15]])
- The cost to dispose a barrel of oil-based mud/cuttings ranges from 10 to 40 USD, contributing significantly to total drilling costs. ([[2011-sanmi|Sanmi, 2011, p. 20]])
- Canola oil met the required specifications for base oil (API Gravity: 49.9, Flash Point: 185°F, Fire Point: 314°F) and is suitable as a base oil for OBM. ([[2011-sanmi|Sanmi, 2011, p. 43]])
- Mud systems with 0 ppb and 3 ppb lime were unstable after 72 hours at room temperature, showing separation of the liquid phase and solids. ([[2011-sanmi|Sanmi, 2011, p. 63]])
- The 5 ppb lime concentration was identified as the optimal amount for maintaining stable rheology; systems with 7 ppb and 10 ppb became congealed or lost properties after hot-rolling at 250°F. ([[2011-sanmi|Sanmi, 2011, p. 63]])
- At high temperatures, AHR dial readings were approximately four times higher than BHR readings for the 5 ppb lime system, indicating instability that required adjustment of additives. ([[2011-sanmi|Sanmi, 2011, p. 71]])
- The final formulation (Table 6.9) achieved a stable HTHP filtrate loss of 12 ml (6x2) at 250°F and an electrical stability of 625 Volts. ([[2011-sanmi|Sanmi, 2011, p. 81]])
- Reducing the viscosity additive to 2 ppb successfully managed the HTHP effects on rheology compared to the 3 ppb or 6 ppb concentrations. ([[2011-sanmi|Sanmi, 2011, p. 77]])
- Canola oil was successfully used as a base-oil for the formulation of an oil-based mud system. ([[2011-sanmi|Sanmi, 2011, p. 88]])
- The developed canola oil-based mud system exhibited zero level of toxicity. ([[2011-sanmi|Sanmi, 2011, p. 88]])
- Canola-BMS (O/W=90/10) showed a reduction in plastic viscosity from 59 cp (BHR) to 36 cp (AHR), indicating a beneficial thinning effect under HTHP conditions. ([[2011-sanmi|Sanmi, 2011, p. 86]])
- The canola oil-based mud system remained stable at both room temperature (BHR) and simulated down-hole conditions (AHR) with an electrical stability of 990 Volts. ([[2011-sanmi|Sanmi, 2011, p. 87]])

## Limitations

The study notes that the filtration control additive does not fully disperse in the liquid phase and is highly sensitive to temperature/pressure, but its exact chemical interaction complexity makes it difficult to isolate as a single cause for instability.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Research the formulation of canola oil-based mud at a reduced oil/water ratio (e.g., 80/20).
- Investigate alternative filtration control additives.
- Evaluate the effect of suitable thinners on the mud system.
- Perform standard toxicity tests such as LC50.
