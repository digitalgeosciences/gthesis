# Adsorption Evaluation of Cross-linked Polymers for Water Treatment

**Summary**: The thesis evaluates the efficiency of synthesized cross-linked polymers (CPZDA, HCDAP, and HCPZA) as adsorbents for removing heavy metals (Ni, Co, Cd, and Cr) from water. The study investigates the impact of pH, temperature, and contact time on adsorption performance and characterizes the polymers using techniques like FTIR, TGA, and SEM-EDS.

---

## Research Problem

Heavy metal contamination in water bodies poses significant risks to human health and the environment. Existing remediation technologies like phytoremediation, electrocoagulation, and ion exchange have limitations or high costs, necessitating the development of more efficient and durable adsorbent materials like cross-linked polymers.

## Objectives

- To demonstrate removal of Nickel (Ni), Cobalt (Co), Cadmium (Cd), and Chromium (Cr) from contaminated water using the synthesized Cross-linked polymers.
- To determine optimum conditions (i.e. kinetic parameters) that include adsorbent loading, temperature, medium pH, contact time and concentration of heavy metals.
- To monitor adsorption chemistry (i.e. kinetics) under the identified optimum conditions.
- To test the removal capacity of heavy metals by the synthesized polymers from real wastewater under the optimum treatment conditions.
- Synthesize cross-linked polyzwitterion (CPZ) using N,N-diallyl-N-sulfopropylammoniopropylphosphonic acid and a cross-linker with TBHP initiator.
- Develop cross-linked polyzwitterion/dianion (CPZDA) by treating CPZ with NaOH.
- Synthesize new hydrophobic cross-linked dianionic Polyelectrolytes (HCDAP) via cyclopolymerization of N,N-diallyl-N-aminomethylphosphonic acid and a crosslinker using AIBN initiator.
- Evaluate the adsorption capacity of these polymers for heavy metals such as Chromium, Cadmium, Cobalt, and Nickel.
- Synthesize cross-linked polymers (CPZDA, HCPZA, HCDAP) with various functional groups.
- Characterize the physical and chemical properties of the synthesized polymers using SEM-EDX, TGA, FT-IR, and BET analysis.
- Evaluate the adsorption behavior of these polymers for heavy metal removal under varying pH, contact time, and adsorbent dosage.
- Determine the kinetic parameters (pseudo-first order, pseudo-second order) and intraparticle diffusion coefficients for the adsorption process.
- Synthesize cross-linked polymers for water treatment applications.
- Characterize the structure, surface morphology, and elemental composition of the synthesized polymers using physical methods.
- Evaluate the adsorption capacity and kinetics of the polymers for removing heavy metals (Co, Ni).
- Determine the thermodynamic parameters of the adsorption process.
- Assess the reusability and performance of the resins in real-world industrial wastewater samples.
- Prepare cross-linked polyzwitterion/dianion (CPZDA) by treating CPZ with NaOH.
- Evaluate the adsorption performance of these materials for water treatment.

## Methods

The study involved synthesizing three types of cross-linked polymers: CPZDA, HCPZA, and HCDAP. Characterization included SEM-EDX to analyze surface morphology and elemental composition; TGA to determine thermal stability and weight loss stages; FT-IR to identify functional groups (sulfonate, phosphonate, C=O); and BET analysis for specific surface area and pore volume. Batch adsorption experiments were conducted with metal ions (Cd2+, Co2+, Cr3+, Ni2+) in aqueous media at varying pH levels (3-7), contact times (up to 120 min), and adsorbent dosages (10-50 mg). Analytical tools included a Thermo Scientific iCE 3000 AAS for concentration measurement and an ICP-MS for real wastewater samples. Kinetic modeling was performed using Lagergren's pseudo-first and second order models, along with the intraparticle diffusion model.

## Data and Materials

Chemicals used: Nickel (II), Cadmium (II), Cobalt (II), Chromium (III) (1000 mg/L stock solutions); HNO3, HCl, NaOH; reagents like AIBN, TBHP, DMSO, piperazine, allyl chloride. Synthesis yields were recorded as percentages (e.g., 49%, 85%, 81%). FTIR spectra were analyzed for various products.

## Key Findings

- The study successfully synthesized three types of cross-linked polymers: CPZDA (cross-linked polyzwitterion/dianion), HCDAP (hydrophobic cross-linked dianionic Polyelectrolytes), and HCPZA (hydrophobic cross-linked polyzwitterionic acid). ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 13]])
- The adsorption efficiency of the resins were evaluated for Ni2+, Co2+, Cd2+, and Cr3+ from water. ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 13]])
- Experimental data for heavy metal adsorption fitted Langmuir, Freundlich, and Temkin isotherms. ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 13]])
- The study determined optimum conditions including adsorbent loading, temperature, medium pH, contact time, and concentration of heavy metals. ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 20]])
- The adsorption activity of the polymers was tested under optimum treatment conditions for real wastewater. ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 13]])
- Monomer 2 was confirmed to have a purity of approximately 90% based on H-1 NMR signals at δ 3.11 and 3.40. ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 38]])
- The synthesis of CPZ (resin 3) yielded 5.1 g with a yield of 49%. ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 36]])
- Transformation of CPZ to CPZDA resulted in a grey colored powder weighing 5.2 g (85% yield). ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 36]])
- HCPZA synthesis from monomers 1, 2, 3, and SO2 yielded 13.2 g with an 81% yield. ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 39]])
- The synthesis of HCDAP (from monomers 1-3 and SO2) resulted in a resin weighing 11.3 g with an 85% yield. ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 39]])
- Adsorption capacity for cross-linked chitosan grafted with amido-carboxylic functional group was reported as 318 mg/g for Cu(II) and Cr(VI). ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 32]])
- Cross-linked polyaminocarboxylates CAPE 6 showed an adsorption efficiency of 77.5% at initial concentration of 200 ppb for Cu2+. ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 33]])
- Cross-linked polyaminocarboxylates CAPE 9 showed an adsorption efficiency of 99.4% at initial concentration of 200 ppb for Cu2+. ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 33]])
- A study reported that the use of Peanut hull and Peanut hull pellets achieved more than 75% removal of copper within first 20 min, and 92% at 50 minutes. ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 32]])
- Activated Carbon (AC) showed a 90% removal rate for Cobalt ions. ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 32]])

## Limitations

Studies at pH > 7 were not performed to avoid metal ion precipitation.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Conduct further investigation on the efficiency of the polymers using a column system.
- Perform XPS studies to determine the state of metal species in solution.
- Investigate different hydrophobes of ionic liquids for commercial viability.
- Test a prototype sheet or column in a university water facility.
