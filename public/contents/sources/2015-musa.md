# Adsorption Evaluation of Cross-linked Polymers for Water Treatment

**Summary**: The thesis evaluates the efficiency of newly synthesized cross-linked polymers (CPZDA and HCDAP) as adsorbents for removing heavy metals (Ni, Co, Cd, and Cr) from water. The study characterizes these materials using techniques like SEM-EDX, TGA, and FT-IR to determine their suitability for industrial wastewater treatment.

---

## Research Problem

The contamination of groundwater and surface water by toxic heavy metals (Cd, Co, Cr, Ni) due to industrialization and urbanization requires the development of efficient, cost-effective, and environmentally friendly adsorbent materials for water purification.

## Objectives

- To demonstrate removal of Nickel (Ni), Cobalt (Co), Cadmium (Cd), and Chromium (Cr) from contaminated water using the synthesized Cross-linked polymers.
- To determine optimum conditions (i.e. kinetic parameters) that include adsorbent loading, temperature, medium pH, contact time and concentration of heavy metals.
- To monitor adsorption chemistry (i.e. kinetics) under the identified optimum conditions.
- To test the removal capacity of heavy metals by the synthesized polymers from real wastewater under the optimum treatment conditions.
- Synthesize and characterize three types of cross-linked polymers: CPZDA, HCDAP, and HCPZA.
- Evaluate the adsorption capacity and kinetics of these polymers for removing heavy metal ions (Ni, Co, Cr, Cd).

## Methods

The study involved the synthesis of cross-linked polyzwitterion (CPZ), which was converted to CPZDA. A new hydrophobic cross-linked dianionic polyelectrolyte (HCDAP) was synthesized via cyclopolymerization and subsequent treatment with NaOH. Characterization included BET surface area analysis, TGA for thermal stability, SEM-EDX for morphology and elemental composition, FT-IR for functional group identification, and Zeta potential analysis to assess electrostatic interactions. Adsorption experiments were conducted in batch systems varying pH (3-7), temperature (298K, 313K, 333K), contact time (up to 120 min), and adsorbent dose. Metal concentrations were measured using Flame Atomic Absorption Spectrometry (FAAS) and ICP-MS for real wastewater samples.

## Data and Materials

Three types of cross-linked polymers: CPZDA, HCDAP, and HCPZA. Metal ions tested: Ni(II), Co(II), Cr(III), Cd(II). Experimental parameters included pH range 3-7, contact times up to 120 minutes, and adsorbent dosages from 10 to 50 mg.

## Key Findings

- The synthesis of CPZDA resulted in a resin with both zwitterionic and dianionic moieties, providing abundant anionic functionalities for metal adsorption. ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 51]])
- HCDAP was synthesized as a hydrophobic cross-linked dianionic polyelectrolyte containing trimeric nitrogen and long chain (C18) hydrocarbons to capture organic contaminants and heavy metals. ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 51]])
- FT-IR spectroscopy confirmed the presence of specific functional groups in both CPZDA and HCDAP, with characteristic peaks at various wavenumbers (e.g., 3420 cm-1 for CPZDA). ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 51]])
- TGA analysis was used to determine the thermal stability of the resins over a range of 20-800 °C. ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 43]])
- SEM-EDX confirmed the elemental composition and surface morphology of the polymers before and after heavy metal loading (Cd, Co, Cr, Ni). ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 44]])
- Zeta potential analysis was utilized to evaluate electrostatic surface interactions in the adsorption process. ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 45]])
- The study evaluated the impact of pH (3-7) on the removal efficiency of heavy metals. ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 49]])
- Adsorption kinetics were modeled using Langmuir, Freundlich, and Temkin isotherms to evaluate the capacity and mechanism. ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 12]])
- CPZDA showed an outstanding removal of Ni(II) followed by Cr(III), Co(II), and Cd(II). ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 101]])
- HCDAP had a specific surface area of 26.3450 m2/g and a pore volume of 0.051 cm3/g. ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 53]])
- The maximum adsorption capacity for Ni(II) on CPZDA was determined to be 45.5 mg/g at pH 5.5 with a Langmuir model fit (R2 = 0.99). ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 86]])
- HCPZA showed an adsorption capacity of 76.9 mg/g for Co(II) at optimum pH 6. ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 101]])
- The activation energy (Ea) for Ni(II) on CPZDA was calculated as 15.8 kJ/mol, indicating a physical sorption process. ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 99]])
- The activation energy (Ea) for Co(II) on HCDAP was 5.7 kJ/mol, also suggesting physical sorption. ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 101]])
- Removal of Ni(II) followed the order: Ni(II) > Co(II) > Cr(III) > Cd(II), which correlated with the inverse order of hydrated radii and Pauling's electronegativity. ([[2015-musa|Musa, 2015, p. 59]])
