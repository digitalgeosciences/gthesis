# Phase behavior and microemulsion studies on a Saudi Arabian crude

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the phase behavior and interfacial tension (IFT) of various surfactants, including ethoxylated sulfonates, in a Saudi Arabian crude oil system under high temperature (90°C) and high salinity (20%) conditions. It identifies a 'secondary optimal salinity' for certain ethoxylated sulfonates, which is critical for designing effective microemulsion slugs in harsh reservoir environments.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the need for effective surfactants that can maintain low interfacial tension (IFT) in harsh reservoir conditions, specifically high-temperature and high-salinity environments typical of Saudi Arabian limestone reservoirs.

## Objectives

- Investigate the phase behavior of microemulsions using a student crude oil and pure alkanes.
- Formulate a microemulsion slug that provides high recovery efficiency under reservoir conditions (20% salinity, 90°C).
- Determine optimal salinities for various surfactant systems.
- Construct pseudo-ternary diagrams to delineate single and multiphase regions.
- Determine critical micelle concentrations (CMC) for different ethoxylated sulfonates.
- Investigate the effect of ethoxylation number on CMC and cloud point.

## Methods

The study utilized the spinning drop technique (using a UTSDI model 500) to measure interfacial tension between oil, brine, and microemulsion. Two-phase titration was used to determine surfactant concentration via complex formation with HTAB. Viscosity of the middle phase was measured using a Contraves Low Shear viscometer. Surface tension measurements were conducted using a Kruss K10 digital tensiometer to determine Critical Micelle Concentration (CMC). Ultraviolet light was used to distinguish between oil and microem phases in opaque crude oil samples.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized three surfactants: TRS10-410 (petroleum sulfonate), V2880 (ethoxylated sulfonate, EO=8), B1083 (ethoxylated sulfonate, EO=15), and Sapogenat T-150 (nonionic). Two oil types were used: n-dodecane (95% pure) and a Saudi Arabian crude oil. Cosurfactants included n-butanol, n-propanol, n-pentanol, and n-hexanol. Sodium chloride was used to simulate reservoir brine.

## Key Findings

- The study revealed the existence of a secondary optimal salinity for ethoxylated sulfonates at salinities higher than the primary optimal salinity (e.g., for EO=8, primary is 8% and secondary is 14%). ([[1987-fuseni|Fuseni, 1987, p. 23]])
- The secondary optimal salinity yields even lower interfacial tensions compared100% to the primary optimal salinity. ([[1987-fuseni|Fuseni, 1987, p. 24]])
- For B1083 (EO=15), a 20% salinity level resulted in a symmetric three-phase 'triangle', providing good IFT lowering and efficient displacement. ([[1987-fuseni|Fuseni, 1987, p. 24]])
- Petroleum sulfonate TRS10-410 showed an optimal salinity of 1.3 wt % for both dodecane and crude oil systems at room temperature. ([[1987-fuseni|Fuseni, 1987, p. 58]])
- The nonionic surfactant Sapogenat T-150 produced unacceptably high IFT values (above 40 dynes/cm) at 90°C, making it unsuitable for the study's goals. ([[1987-fuseni|Fuseni, 1987, p. 65]])
- Ethoxylated sulfonates V2880 and B1083 showed high tolerance to salt even at elevated temperatures (90°C). ([[1987-fuseni|Fuseni, 1987, p. 67]])
- The CMC for V2880 was determined to be 0.021 g/l and for B1083 as 0.043 g/l. ([[1987-fuseni|Fuseni, 1987, p. 71]])
- Ethoxylated sulfonates are more salt-tolerant and temperature-stable compared to ether phosphates, ether sulfates, or ether carboxymethylates. ([[1987-fuseni|Fuseni, 1987, p. 73]])
- The phase inversion temperature (PIT) for V2880 at 20% salinity was found to be 35°C, corresponding to an IFT of 8.4x10^-2 dynes/cm. ([[1987-fuseni|Fuseni, 1987, p. 76]])
- A secondary optimal salinity (SOS) was identified where a second minimum in IFT occurs at higher salt concentrations; for B1083, this occurred at approximately 14% salinity in dodecane and 20% in crude oil. ([[1987-fuseni|Fuseni, 1987, p. 79]])
- The secondary optimal salinity is attributed to the formation of a thin interfacial layer where over 90% of the surfactant accumulates, resulting in an oil-external microemulsion. ([[1987-fuseni|Fuseni, 1987, p. 81]])
- Increasing alcohol concentration from 0% to 2% n-butanol resulted in lower IFT for both crude oil and dodecane systems. ([[1987-fuseni|Fuseni, 1987, p. 83]])
- Higher chainlength alcohols (C4 and above) are preferred as cosurfactants, while those below C4 result in high interfacial tensions and undesirable phase behavior transitions. ([[1987-fuseni|Fuseni, 1987, p. 99]])
- B1083 showed a more robust performance at the secondary optimal salinity (20% NaCl) compared to V2880, with IFT values remaining low across a wide range of water-to-oil ratios. ([[1987-fuseni|Fuseni, 1987, p. 114]])

## Limitations

The study notes that while dodecane is used as an alkane model, it may not perfectly replicate the behavior of complex crude oil systems under all conditions, and the specific mechanism of the secondary minimum remains a subject for further investigation.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Further study the structure and mechanism of microemulsions at primary and secondary optimal salinities.
- Investigate ethoxylated sulfonates with higher EO numbers and their mixtures.
- Test different chain length alcohols as cosurfactants.
- Use a synthetic brine matching reservoir composition for phase behavior studies.
- Investigate the interaction of ethoxylated sulfonates with mobility control agents (polymers).
