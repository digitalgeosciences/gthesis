# EVALUATING THE MICROSCOPIC RECOVERY POTENTIAL OF LOW SALINITY VISCOELASTIC POLYMER FLOOD IN OIL-WET CARBONATE FORMATION

**Summary**: The thesis evaluates the recovery potential of a viscoelastic sulfonated polymer (AN-125 SH) compared to a biopolymer (xanthan gum) in an oil-wet carbonate reservoir. It utilizes core flooding experiments on outcrop samples from the Al-Lidam area to compare how different rheological behaviors—specifically shear-thinning and shear-thickening—impact residual oil recovery.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the challenge of recovering residual oil in oil-wet, heterogeneous carbonate reservoirs under high temperature and salinity conditions. It specifically investigates whether the viscoelastic properties (shear-thickening) of sulfonated polymers can provide a superior mobility and displacement advantage over standard biopolymers like xanthan gum.

## Objectives

- Evaluate the bulk rheology of SPAM polymer under reservoir temperature conditions (75 °C).
- Evaluate the in-situ rheology of SPAM polymer under carbonate formation conditions to track different in-situ flow regimes.
- Compare the oil recovery potential between biopolymers with lesser non-linear viscoelasticity and viscoelastic sulfonated polymers in heterogeneous non-water-wet carbonate formations at flux rates representing shear thinning and shear thickening.
- Characterize the rheological behavior of various sulfonated polymer concentrations and molecular weights in low salinity brine.
- Determine the impact of shear rate on the apparent viscosity of both synthetic and biopolymers in porous media.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of a viscoelastic polymer (AN-125 SH) versus a biopolymer (xanthan gum) in reducing residual oil saturation in an oil-wet carbonate reservoir.

## Methods

The study employs bulk rheology measurements using an Anton Paar MCR-702 rheometer (concentric cone and cylinder geometry) to characterize polymers in low salinity brine. Core flooding experiments were conducted on outcrop samples from the Al-Lidam area, which are stratigraphically equivalent to the Dam Formation. The core flood setup includes a syringe pump, pressure transducers, and an effluent collector. Analytical techniques include XRD for mineralogy characterization (showing dolomite dominance), SARA analysis for oil composition, and NMR (T2 relaxation) to characterize pore size distribution.

## Data and Materials

The study uses 8 core samples (F1-F21) from the Al-Lidam area with permeability ranging from 200 to 2000 mD. Two types of polymers were tested: AN-125 SH (at 2500 ppm) and xanthan gum (at 2000 ppm). The oil used is a light crude from Saudi Arabia (viscosity 4 cP at 75°C). Low salinity brine was prepared with specific concentrations of Na+, Ca2+, Mg2+, SO4-2, Cl-, and HCO-3.

## Key Findings

- Low salinity AN-125 SH injections lead to enhanced incremental residual oil recovery in heterogeneous formations with different pore sizes only at higher rates (20, 32.5 ft/day) where shear thickening was evident. ([[2023-dafaalla|Dafaalla, 2023, p. 18]])
- The study identifies two critical velocities: a second critical or critical transit velocity (Vcr) where HPAM solution changes from pseudoplastic to dilatant behavior, and a first critical velocity (Vce). ([[2023-dafaalla|Dafaalla, 2023, p. 40]])
- Viscoelastic polymers can reduce residual oil saturation by over 6% OOIP in some cases due to the 'pulling effect' of elastic nature. ([[2023-dafaalla|Dafaalla, 2023, p. 40]])
- The addition of n-VP to polymer solutions ensures their stability at 120°C in high-salinity brines containing divalent ions for over 180 days. ([[2023-dafaalla|Dafaalla, 2023, p. 43]])
- Low salinity polymer (LSP) can significantly enhance EOR efficiency in carbonate oil reservoirs, potentially providing up to 12.3% IOR when combined with high concentration of SO4-2 ions. ([[2023-dafaalla|Dafaalla, 2023, p. 49]])
- The core samples from the Al-Lidam area showed a dominance of dolomite as determined by XRD analysis. ([[2023-dafaalla|Dafaalla, 2023, p. 63]])
- Core sample F21 (permeability 1296 mD) and F2 (permeability 1375 mD) were used to establish in-situ rheology profiles for the two polymers. ([[2023-dafaalla|Dafaalla, 2023, p. 99]])
- The apparent viscosity of AN-125 SH exhibited shear-thinning at lower shear rates and transitioned to shear-thickening beyond a critical shear rate of 18.7 ft/day. ([[2023-dafaalla|Dafaalla, 2023, p. 101]])
- Xanthan gum (2000 ppm) showed only shear-thinning behavior, with its apparent viscosity profile intersecting the AN-125 SH profile at a flux rate of 31.5 ft/day. ([[2023-dafaalla|Dafaalla, 2023, p. 103]])
- NMR T2 relaxation results indicated a dual porosity system where 97% of core samples showed large pore sizes or fractures (relaxation times near 1000 ms). ([[2023-dafaalla|Dafaalla, 2023, p. 107]])
- The study identified that higher molecular weight (Mw) in AN-125 polymers leads to increased Newtonian viscosity and linear relaxation time, but the non-linear relaxation time remains relatively consistent across different Mw. ([[2023-dafaalla|Dafaalla, 2023, p. 79]])
- A concentration of 2500 ppm for AN-125 SH was selected as it provided a favorable viscosity ratio of 7 compared to the oil (4 cP) at 75°C. ([[2023-dafaalla|Dafaalla, 2023, p. 93]])
- Xanthan gum (2000 ppm) at a flux rate of 4 ft/day resulted in an oil recovery of 24.6% with a pressure gradient of 8.5 psi/ft. ([[2023-dafaalla|Dafaalla, 2023, p. 109]])
- The sulfonated polymer AN-125 SH (2500 ppm) at a flux rate of 32.5 ft/day achieved an oil recovery of 25% with a pressure gradient of 30 psi/ft. ([[2023-dafaalla|Dafaalla, 2023, p. 114]])
- Xanthan gum showed higher recovery than the viscoelastic polymer at low flux rates (4 ft/day) due to its higher viscosity in that regime. ([[2023-dafaalla|Dafaalla, 2023, p. 115]])

## Limitations

The study notes that some cores were fragile and sensitive to stress, leading to potential damage during high-pressure operations. Additionally, lower back pressures during certain phases may have resulted in suboptimal initial oil saturation levels.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Verify the oil wettability of the samples using Amott-Harvey cell.
- Explore mobilization of residual oil using high salinity viscoelastic polymers at high shear rates.
- Assess recovery capabilities in homogeneous core samples across both low and high salinity environments.
- Determine effectiveness at practical flooding rates.
- Investigate combined use of conformance control fluids and oil mobilization fluids.
