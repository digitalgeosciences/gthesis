# A New Dewpoint Pressure Correlation for Gas-Condensate Systems

**Summary**: The thesis develops a new empirical correlation to predict the dewpoint pressure of gas-condensate systems using multiple linear regression analysis. The model was developed and validated using 74 data sets from Middle East reservoirs, outperforming existing correlations like those by Organick and Golding or Nemeth and Kennedy.

---

## Research Problem

Laboratory determination of dewpoint pressure is often costly, cumbersome, or impossible due to limited sample volumes or lack of representative samples. Existing empirical correlations for predicting dewpoint pressure are limited in scope and may not accurately represent the diverse compositions of gas-condensate fluids in different regions, specifically the Middle East.

## Objectives

- Evaluate existing gas-condensate dewpoint pressure correlations using unpublished experimentally obtained PVT fluid data from Middle East reservoirs.
- Develop a new empirical dewpoint pressure correlation based on readily available field parameters (reservoir pressure, temperature, GOR, and specific gravities).
- Perform statistical error analysis to compare the accuracy of the new correlation against existing models.
- Validate the new correlation using independent experimental data sets.
- Develop a new empirical correlation for predicting dewpoint pressure in gas-condensate systems using multiple linear regression.
- Evaluate the accuracy of existing correlations (Organick and Golding, Nemeth and Kennedy) compared to the new model.

## Methods

The study utilized multiple linear regression analysis to develop a new empirical model. The methodology included: 1) Selection of independent variables (reservoir temperature, GOR, pseudoreduced properties). 2) Statistical evaluation of models using R-squared, t-tests, and standard deviation. 3) Graphical error analysis including crossplots, error distribution histograms, and sensitivity analysis. 4) Comparison against established correlations (Organick & Golding, Nemeth & Kennedy). 5) Validation of the new model against a separate set of experimental data.

## Data and Materials

74 unpublished data sets of gas-condensate systems from the Middle East region, including reservoir pressure/temperature, separator conditions, GOR, specific gravities, and experimentally determined dewpoint pressures from Constant Mass Expansion (CME) tests.

## Key Findings

- The new correlation developed in this study achieved a coefficient of determination (R-squared) of 0.897 and a correlation coefficient (r) of 0.9471, indicating that approximately 95% of the variation in dewpoint pressure is explained by the model. ([[2001-humoud|Humoud, 2001, p. 72]])
- The new model predicted dewpoint pressures with an average absolute relative error of 4.33% and a standard deviation of 3.93%. ([[2001-humoud|Humoud, 2001, p. 72]])
- Approximately 65% of the predicted dewpoint pressures fell within 5% average absolute error, and about 95% were within 10% average absolute error. ([[2001-humoud|Humoud, 2001, p. 74]])
- The t-test results confirmed that all independent variables in the model (reservoir temperature, pseudoreduced temperature, mass gas-oil ratio, pseudoreduced pressure, and heptanes-plus specific gravity) were statistically significant. ([[2001-humoud|Humoud, 2001, p. 73]])
- Reservoir temperature was identified as the most significant parameter in the model based on the highest t-test value. ([[2001-humoud|Humoud, 2001, p. 74]])
- The new correlation outperformed existing correlations (Organick & Golding and Nemeth & Kennedy) in terms of accuracy and consistency across various data points. ([[2001-humoud|Humoud, 2001, p. 70]])
- The Organick and Golding correlation resulted in an average absolute error of 32.6% and a maximum relative error of 53.4% for Middle East gas-condensate systems. ([[2001-humoud|Humoud, 2001, p. 89]])
- The Nemeth and Kennedy correlation yielded an average absolute error of 11.64% and a maximum relative error of 37.4%. ([[2001-humoud|Humoud, 2001, p. 89]])
- The new correlation outperformed existing ones with an average absolute error of 4.33%, a maximum relative error of 15.1%, and a root mean square error of 5.46%. ([[2001-humoud|Humoud, 2001, p. 90]])
- Standing's correlation for pseudocritical properties showed average absolute errors of 6% in temperature and 1.2% in pressure, but resulted in a 13.5% error when used to calculate dewpoint pressures. ([[2001-humoud|Humoud, 2001, p. 84]])
- New correlations for pseudocritical properties (Eqs 5.8 and 5.9) reduced average absolute errors to 1.8% for temperature and 1% for pressure, leading to a dewpoint prediction error of only 6.4%. ([[2001-humoud|Humoud, 2001, p. 85]])
- The new correlation showed almost constant average absolute error over different ranges of dewpoint pressure, unlike existing correlations which were influenced by the range. ([[2001-humoud|Humoud, 2001, p. 96]])
- The new correlation's accuracy was not affected by changes in reservoir temperature or acid gas concentration (H2S + CO2). ([[2001-humoud|Humoud, 2001, p. 98]])

## Limitations

Existing correlations are limited by the specific regions/fluids they were developed for; specifically, many older models were based on California or Louisiana samples and may not apply to Middle Eastern gas-condensate systems.
