# Surface charge behavior and adsorption of surfactants on carbonate rocks

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the surface charge behavior (zeta potential) and adsorption characteristics of three different surfactants (Sapogenat T-100, Triton X-102, and B1083) on carbonate rocks under varying pH, temperature, and salinity conditions. It concludes that Triton X-102 shows the best performance for surfactant flooding in carbonate reservoirs due to its lower adsorption and higher desorption compared to other tested surfactants.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the challenge of understanding how surface charge (zeta potential) relates to the adsorption behavior of different types of surfactants on carbonate rocks, which is critical for optimizing enhanced oil recovery (EOR) in carbonate reservoirs.

## Objectives

- Study the effect of surfactants on the surface charge behavior of carbonate rocks.
- Conduct dynamic adsorption tests to evaluate the impact of flow rate, temperature, and salinity on surfactant retention.
- Determine which type of surfactant has the least adsorption capacity on carbonate rocks.
- Evaluate the zeta potential of carbonate rock with varying concentrations of different surfactants in deionized water and brine.
- Determine the static adsorption isotherms of Sapogenat T-100, Triton X-102, and B1083 on crushed carbonate rocks.
- Compare zeta potential results with adsorption data to determine if surface charge can be used as a screening method for surfactant behavior.

## Methods

The study utilized zeta potential measurements to characterize the surface charge of carbonate rocks in various solutions. Static adsorption was evaluated using UV-260 spectrophotometry to measure concentration changes, and multiple linear regression was used to fit calibration curves. Dynamic adsorption tests were conducted on core samples at varying flow rates (e.g., 0.5 cc/min and 2 cc/min), temperatures (up to 107°C), and salinities (up to 57,000 ppm TDS) to determine breakthrough curves and desorption behavior.

## Data and Materials

The study used carbonate rock samples (crushed for static tests and core-sized for dynamic tests). Three surfactants were tested: Sapogenat T-100, Triton X-102, and B1083. Experimental conditions included pH ranges of 2-12, temperatures up to 107°C, and brine concentrations up to 57,000 ppm TDS.

## Key Findings

- The carbonate rock was found to have an isoelectric point of 4.2 in deionized water; at high brine salinities, the surface remained negatively charged across the entire studied pH range. ([[1992-rodriguez|Rodriguez, 1992, p. 90]])
- An increase in salinity produced an increase in the negativity of the zeta potential of the carbonate rock. ([[1992-rodriguez|Rodriguez, 1992, p. 51]])
- The nonionic surfactant Triton X-102 presented the lowest adsorption level and the highest desorption level at a temperature of 107 °C and a salinity of 57,000 ppm synthetic brine. ([[1992-rodriguez|Rodriguez, 1992, p. 21]])
- The specific surface area obtained for the rock chips was 0.317 m²/g. ([[1992-rodriguez|Rodriguez, 1992, p. 43]])
- The addition of anionic surfactant (B1083) increased the negativity of the zeta potential as concentration increased from 0.25% to 4.0%. ([[1992-rodriguez|Rodriguez, 1992, p. 51]])
- Nonionic surfactants showed a smaller change in zeta potential compared to anionic ones, with an effect primarily reducing surface charge toward zero. ([[1992-rodriguez|Rodriguez, 1992, p. 51]])
- Triton X-102 exhibited the lowest adsorption level and highest desorption level (over 30% recovery) at 107°C and 57,000 ppm TDS, making it a strong candidate for carbonate reservoir flooding. ([[1992-rodriguez|Rodriguez, 1992, p. 90]])
- The time required for adsorption equilibrium in dynamic tests was found to be inversely proportional to the surfactant concentration. ([[1992-rodriguez|Rodriguez, 1992, p. 90]])
- Dynamic adsorption results showed that Triton X-102's adsorption isotherm was altered by salinity, while B1083's isotherm shape remained consistent across different salinities. ([[1992-rodriguez|Rodriguez, 1992, p. 47]])
- Adsorption of both B1083 and Sapogenat T-100 increased markedly with temperature, though their adsorption isotherms maintained a Langmuir-type shape. ([[1992-rodriguez|Rodriguez, 1992, p. 47]])
- The zeta potential of nonionic surfactants (Triton X-102) was observed to decrease toward zero as concentration increased in deionized water at pH 8.0. ([[1992-rodriguez|Rodriguez, 1992, p. 39]])
- Sapogenat T-100 showed the highest adsorption among the tested surfactants, while Triton X-102 showed the lowest. ([[1992-rodriguez|Rodriguez, 1992, p. 79]])
- The zeta potential of B1083 showed a clear trend between surface charge and adsorption density at pH 8.0, unlike Sapogenat T-100 or Triton X-102. ([[1992-rodriguez|Rodriguez, 1992, p. 63]])

## Limitations

Zeta potential measurements were limited to 25,000 ppm due to equipment constraints and were performed in suspended solutions rather than directly with surfactant solutions.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Conduct interfacial tension studies for Triton X-102.
- Modify the Zeta meter system to allow measurements at higher ionic strengths and temperatures.
- Further research is needed to validate the use of zeta potential as a screening method for ionic surfactants.
