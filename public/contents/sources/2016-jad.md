# Formation Damage Quantification by Nuclear Magnetic Resonance

**Summary**: The thesis investigates formation damage in carbonate and sandstone cores caused by fines migration (triggered by HCl and fresh water) and viscoelastic surfactant (VES) adsorption. Using NMR, core flooding, and SEM analysis, the study quantifies permeability reduction and identifies specific pore sizes affected by these mechanisms.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the problem of formation damage in sandstone reservoirs, specifically how salinity contrasts between original reservoir fluids and injected fluids cause clay minerals like kaolinite, chlorite, and illite to swell or migrate, thereby reducing permeability and hindering production.

## Objectives

- Identify the damage location in sandstone cores using NMR technique.
- Compare the NMR results with the pressure drop and permeability measurement from core flooding system.
- Investigate the damage caused by fresh water and HCl on the sandstone cores.
- Identification of damage caused by VES in carbonate rocks using NMR.
- Identify the adsorption location in carbonate rocks from NMR-T2 distribution.
- Investigate the effect of viscosity on adsorption in carbonate rock using permeability measurement.

## Methods

The study involved core analysis using two types of sandstone: Berea (porosity 18.7%, permeability 100 mD) and Bandera Brown (porosity 25.4%, permeability 14.9 mD). Core plugs were saturated with compatible fluids (3% or 5% KCl) before being analyzed by NMR to establish a baseline T2 signal distribution. Damage was induced by flooding cores with deionized water or HCl at a rate of 5 cm3/min. Final permeability was measured after back-flushing. SEM-EDS was used to characterize the mineralogical composition and identify specific clay minerals (kaolinite, chlorite, illite) responsible for damage.

## Data and Materials

The study used sandstone core plugs (e.g., Bandera, Berea, Kentucky, and Scioto) and carbonate core plugs. Experimental fluids included brine (KCl), DIW, 15% HCl, and various concentrations of viscoelastic surfactants (VES). NMR T2 relaxation times were recorded for both damaged and undamaged samples.

## Key Findings

- Coreflooding experiments showed a permeability reduction of 84% in sandstone rocks after flooding with de-ionized water (DIW). ([[2016-jad|Jad, 2016, p. 15]])
- Permeability reduction of 7.9% was reported for samples flooded by HCl. ([[2016-jad|Jad, 2016, p. 15]])
- NMR results showed a clear change in the pore size distribution after the injection of HCl. ([[2016-jad|Jad, 2016, p. 16]])
- SEM images confirmed the migration of clay minerals in sandstone rocks. ([[2016-jad|Jad, 2016, p. 16]])
- The high viscosity VES system adsorbed more than the low viscosity system on the carbonate rocks. ([[2016-jad|Jad, 2016]])

- Acid injection (15 wt. % HCl) into Bandera sandstone caused permeability to drop from 4.1 mD to 0.85 mD, with a corresponding pressure drop increase from 400 to 1500 psi. ([[2016-jad|Jad, 2016, p. 44]])
- In carbonate cores, the amount of retained surfactant was found to be a function of the acid injection rate; only 20% of injected surfactant was removed by mutual solvent. ([[2016-jad|Jad, 2016, p. 45]])
- NMR T2 relaxation times for damaged samples showed a shift toward smaller values (e.g., from 0.72 s to 0.31 s), indicating the plugging of larger pores and reduction in pore size. ([[2016-jad|Jad, 2016, p. 49]])
- The permeability of sandstone followed a power law relationship with barite concentration: Sample 1 (k = 3.94t^-0.343) and Sample 2 (k = 2.701t^-0.403). ([[2016-jad|Jad, 2016, p. 49]])
- Viscosity of the VES system increased with NaCl concentration up to 15 wt.%, then decreased due to a shift toward a lamellar phase and screening of electrostatic interactions. ([[2016-jad|Jad, 2016, p. 66]])
- For CaCl2, viscosity also showed a significant decrease at 20% concentration compared to the general trend, attributed to the reduction of effective head group area. ([[2016-jad|Jad, 2016, p. 70]])
- The initial permeability of Berea sandstone samples BE3 and BE4 was measured at 97.2 mD and 101.6 mD, respectively. ([[2016-jad|Jad, 2016, p. 58]])
- The initial permeability of Bandera Brown sample BB4 was 16.5 mD. ([[2016-jad|Jad, 2016, p. 58]])
- Injection of deionized water into Berea core BE3 resulted in a pressure drop of 450 psi, attributed to the migration of kaolinite due to salinity contrast. ([[2016-jad|Jad, 2016, p. 59]])
