# EFFECT OF CO2 ON THE CAPILLARY PRESSURE CHARACTERISTICS OF SALINE AQUIFERS AND DEPLETED OIL RESERVOIRS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of CO2 and carbonated water on capillary pressure, wettibility, and porosity in Berea sandstone samples representing saline aquifers and depleted oil reservoirs. It concludes that exposure to carbonated water and cyclic CO2/water injection enhances rock wettability toward a more water-wet state, thereby increasing the residual trapping potential of CO2.

---

## Research Problem

The lack of accurate data regarding how CO2-induced wettability changes affect capillary pressure in both saline aquifers (water-wet) and depleted oil reservoirs (oil-wet), specifically addressing the limitations of using contact angle measurements which ignore surface roughness.

## Objectives

- Investigate wettability changes in depleted oil reservoirs and saline aquifers using capillary pressure curves.
- Compare water-wet and oil-wet Berea samples after exposure to live brine.
- Quantify initial and residual saturations of fluids during CO2 coreflooding cycles.
- Evaluate the potential mineral dissolution occurring within the live brine under supercritical conditions.
- Evaluate the effect of carbonated water on the wettibility and porosity of Berea sandstone samples.
- Investigate the impact of cyclic CO2/water injection (coreflooding) on residual trapping of CO2.

## Methods

The study utilized several experimental workflows: 1) Capillary Pressure Measurement: Samples were saturated with 3 wt.% NaCl and measured in a porous plate capillary pressure cell at steps of 20 psi from 20 to 200 psi. 2) Live Brine Aging: Samples were exposed to live brine (3 wt.% NaCl + CO2) in an aging cell at 75°C for one month under high pressure, followed by ICP analysis of the effluent. 3) X-ray Coreflooding: A cyclic process of injecting CO2 and water into Berea sandstone cores was performed using an X-ray coreflooding system to visualize fluid distribution and quantify saturations. 4) Characterization: XRD and micro-XRF were used for mineralogy/elemental analysis, and a helium porosimeter determined porosity and pore volume.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized Berea sandstone samples: four core plugs (1.5" x 3") for live brine aging (two water-wet, two oil-wet) and two larger cores (3" x 6") for X-ray coreflooding experiments.

## Key Findings

- The study identified that while contact angle measurements are common, they may be inaccurate due to surface roughness; therefore, direct capillary pressure curves were used to evaluate wett1ability and residual trapping. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 3]])
- Oil-wet Berea samples were successfully created using a carboxylic acid (oleic acid) treatment, confirmed by reduced water retention in capillary pressure measurements. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 32]])
- The study established that residual trapping of CO2 is a function of the relationship between capillary pressure and brine saturation. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 35]])
- Research showed that oil-wet samples had significantly lower residual trapping of CO2 compared to water-wet counterparts (8.7% for oil wet against 14.9% for water-wet) in certain studies. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 43]])
- The study utilized a tracer of sodium iodide (NaI) at 7 wt.% to distinguish between fluids during X-ray coreflooding. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 35]])
- XRD analysis of the Berea sandstone showed 97.1% quartz, 2.6% K-Feldspar, 0.1% pyrite, and 0.1% aragonite. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 56]])
- Water-wet samples (1 & 2) initially had residual water saturations of 26% and 32%, while oil-wet samples (3 & 4) had lower residual water saturations of 18% and 19%. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 58]])
- After oleic acid aging, the residual water saturation for oil-wet samples decreased to 18% and 19% (from higher values), confirming wettibility alteration. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 58]])
- Exposure to carbonated water increased porosity in both water-wet and oil-wet samples, with increases of 12.5% to 17.2% for water-wet and 16.7% to 26% for oil-wet. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 61]])
- XRF results showed a reduction in multivalent ions (Ca2+ and Al3+) after aging, corresponding to an increase in these ions in the effluent analyzed by ICP. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 61]])
- Residual CO2 in oil-wet samples was found to be higher than in water-wet samples during coreflooding experiments. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 43]])
- Carbonated water caused the rock surfaces to become more water-wet, a finding that contradicts some literature but aligns with studies focusing on low salinity and multicomponent ion exchange. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 65]])
- The removal of clay minerals (like K-Feldspar) by acidic brine led to the observed increase in porosity. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 66]])
- Multicomponent ion exchange where Na+ replaces Ca2+ and Al3+ expanded the electrical double layer, promoting hydrophilicity. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 67]])
- Cyclic injection of CO2/water (WAG-like) resulted in a more significant increase in residual trapping for oil-wet samples compared to water-wet ones. ([[2024-suleiman|Suleiman, 2024, p. 70]])

## Limitations

The study used a monovalent NaCl brine; the author suggests using divalent brines and actual crude oil instead of chemicals to better simulate reservoir conditions.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Use divalent brine for comparison of ion effects on residual saturation.
- Use formation water and seawater as fluids to investigate real-world reservoir conditions.
- Use crude oil instead of chemical agents (oleic acid) to form the mixed-wet state.
