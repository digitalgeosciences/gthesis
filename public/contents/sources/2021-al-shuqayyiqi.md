# EVALUATION OF FOAM SUPER-CRITICAL CO2 INJECTION IN THE COMPOSITE LIMESTONE CORE USING NEW MULTIPLE FRACTURE SYSTEM

**Summary**: The thesis evaluates the effectiveness of foam super-critical CO2 (SC-CO2) injection in a composite limestone core with multiple fractures to improve sweep efficiency and reduce gravity override. It integrates X-ray CT scans and NMR T2 diffusion to quantify oil recovery from individual plugs within the fractured system.

---

## Research Problem

The high mobility of supercritical CO2 leads to gas channeling and gravity segregation in carbonate reservoirs, especially those with complex fracture systems. This study addresses how foam (using AOS surfactant) can mitigate these issues at high pressure (3200 psi) and temperature (90 C).

## Objectives

- Investigate foam SC-CO2 injection for enhanced oil recovery in fractured limestone cores.
- Evaluate the effect of foam quality on sweep efficiency, SC-CO2 mobility, and gravity segregation.
- Integrate CT scans and NMR to estimate residual oil saturation and ultimate recovery in each plug of a composite core.

## Methods

The study utilized two types of limestone cores (Pink desert and Indiana) prepared as a 1.5-inch diameter, 6-inch long plugs or 2-inch segments for a composite model. Core flooding was performed at 3200 psi and 90 C using three injection modes: continuous SC-CO2 with surfactant, surfactant-alternating gas (SAG), and seawater flooding followed by the previous two. X-ray CT scans were taken at three stages (100% water saturation, initial oil/water saturation, and post-flooding) to analyze distribution. NMR T2 diffusion was used on hemicylindrical plugs to compare residual oil volumes.

## Data and Materials

6 core flooding experiments; 3 types of limestone cores (Pink desert and Indiana); 10 distinct core segments in the composite model; synthetic formation water and seawater; light dead crude oil (35 API); Alpha-Olefin Sulfonate (AOS) surfactant.

## Key Findings

- CO2 foam flooding with 80% quality reached ultimate recovery faster than 70% foam quality in the first set of experiments. ([[2021-al-shuqayyiqi|Al-Shuqayyiqi, 2021, p. 16]])
- SAG showed the ability to overcome the gravity override issue compared to co-injection methods. ([[2021-al-shuqayyiqi|Al-Shuqayyiqi, 2021, p. 16]])
- In Experiment 1 (70% foam), ultimate recovery was 86%, with a reduction in average CT number from 2241 to 2079. ([[2021-al-shuqayyiqi|Al-Shuqayyiqi, 2021, p. 30]])
- In Experiment 2 (80% foam), the core showed an ultimate recovery of 74% across three plugs, with residual oil saturations of 29.9%, 34.2%, and 22.3%. ([[2021-al-shuqayyiqi|Al-Shuqayyiqi, 2021, p. 31]])
- In Experiment 3 (SAG), the ultimate recovery was 78% over 3 PV, with NMR T2 diffusion showing nearly uniform oil saturation in both hemicylindrical plugs. ([[2021-al-shuqayyiqi|Al-Shuqayyiqi, 2021, p. 46]])
- In Experiment 4 (Seawater + 70% foam), the ultimate recovery was 70%, and CT numbers dropped from 2189 to 2050 in one segment. ([[2021-al-shuqayyiqi|Al-Shuqayyiqi, 2021, p. 50]])
- In Experiment 5 (Seawater + 80% foam), the ultimate recovery was 67%; results showed a mismatch between CT-based estimation and actual recovery due to fracture effects. ([[2021-al-shuqayyiqi|Al-Shuqayyiqi, 2021, p. 53]])
- In Experiment 6 (Seawater + SAG), the ultimate recovery was 72%, with an average CT number reduction from 2250 to 2158 in one segment. ([[2021-al-shuqayyiqi|Al-Shuqayyiqi, 2021, p. 57]])

## Limitations

The study notes that some previous research used high volumes of injected fluids (e.g., 139 PV) which are not practical for field application, and did not account for the behavior of fluids inside the core during the process.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Change flooding strategy from co-injection to pre-generated foam mode.
- Investigate phase behavior of injected gases and surfactants at a molecular level.
- Implement in-situ CT scanning during core flooding to trace flood fronts.
- Evaluate different surfactants with multiple concentrations.
