# Diagenesis of Jauf sandstone in Hawiyah area

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the diagenetic evolution of the Jauf Formation (Lower Devonian) in the Hawiyah area, focusing on how processes like compaction, cementation, and dissolution affect reservoir quality. It concludes that while early calcite and quartz cements reduced primary porosity, subsequent dissolution of feldspar and carbonate minerals created significant secondary porosity, which is critical for gas production.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the complex diagenetic history of the Jauf Formation sandstone in the Hawiyah area, specifically how early-stage cementation (calcite, clay) and later processes like quartz overgrowth and significant chemical dissolution influenced the development of porosity and permeability in this Devonian reservoir.

## Objectives

- Perform quantitative mineralogical analysis on core samples and outcrop samples using petrographic microscopy to determine composition and classify rock types.
- Analyze individual mineral cement phases using scanning electron microscopy (SEM) to identify the effect of cement and matrix phases on reservoir porosity and permeability.
- Identify clay minerals in pore spaces using x-ray diffraction (XRD).
- Determine the origin of cements.
- Establish a diagenetic model and paragenetic sequence of diagenetic processes.
- Characterize the mineralogical composition of the Jauf Formation sandstone.

## Methods

The study utilized thin-section petrography with a Leitz polarized microscope (Laborlux 11Pol) for mineralogy and porosity calculation. SEM-EDS was used to distinguish diagenetic from detrital features and analyze cement morphology/composition. XRD analysis was performed on separated clay fractions (less than 4 microns) using a JEOL x-ray diffractometer to identify minerals like illite and chlorite.

## Data and Materials

The study analyzed 50+ sandstone samples from two wells (Well A and Well B) in the Hawiyah area, including both surface/field samples and deep subsurface samples. Samples were evaluated for mineral content (quartz, feldspar, rock fragments), cement types, and dissolution features.

## Key Findings

- The sandstone reservoirs of Jauf Formation are mostly fine to medium-grained, moderately to well-sorted and texturally mature quartz arenites. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 54]])
- Average composition of the framework grains: 77% quartz, 2% feldspar, and 0.1% rock fragments (chert and shale). ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 56]])
- Early-calcite cement is poikilotopic and has occupied most of the pore spaces in some samples, reducing primary porosity. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 18]])
- Chlorite pore fillings and grain coatings reduced both porosity and permeability but helped retain initial porosity at depth by retarding quartz overgrowths. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 18]])
- Illite cement is most commonly found as hair-like growth coating grains and bridging pore throats, reducing permeability dramatically. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 18]])
- Invasions of acidic fluids resulted in partial dissolution of calcite cements and feldspar grains, creating secondary porosity in many samples. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 18]])
- Approximately 75% of the studied sandstones are fine to medium-grained; 37% are well-sorted; and 13% are poorly sorted. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 53]])
- 31% of samples are classified as 'Mature' (trace to <1% matrix), 39% as 'Submature' (1-5% matrix), and 30% as 'Immature' (7-35% matrix). ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 53]])
- Detrital quartz represents 55 to 96% of total rock components with an average of 77.5%. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 57]])
- Calcite is a major cement; highly cemented sandstones contain 16 to 30% calcite. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 64]])
- Poikilotopic calcite (early stage) can constitute up to 20% of the rock components, often occurring in areas with low compaction. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 93]])
- Illite is the most important clay mineral, appearing as pore-linings and forming dust lines between grains. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 70]])
- Chlorite occurs in two forms: grain coating (common) and pore-filling (less common). ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 71]])
- Quartz overgrowth is a dominant diagenetic process, typically ranging from 0 to 4% but reaching up to 8% in some samples. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 78]])
- Secondary porosity was created by the extensive dissolution of feldspar grains and calcite cements. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 100]])
