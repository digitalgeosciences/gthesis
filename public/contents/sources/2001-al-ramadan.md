# Diagenesis of Jauf sandstone in Hawiyah area

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the diagenetic evolution of the Jauf Formation sandstone in the Hawiyah area, focusing on how processes like compaction, cementation, and dissolution influenced reservoir quality. It concludes that secondary porosity, primarily from feldspar and calcite dissolution driven by acidic fluids from nearby shales, is a critical factor for maintaining reservoir quality at depth.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the impact of diagenetic processes—specifically compaction, multi-stage cementation (early/late calcite, chlorite, illite, and quartz overgrowths), and subsequent dissolution of minerals like feldspar and calcite—on the porosity and permeability of Jauf Formation sandstones in the Hawiyah area.

## Objectives

- Perform detailed quantitative mineralogical analysis on core and outcrop samples using petrographic microscopy to determine composition and classify rock types.
- Analyze individual mineral cement phases using SEM-EDS to identify the effects of specific cements and matrix phases on porosity and permeability.
- Identify clay minerals in pore spaces using XRD techniques.
- Determine the origin of various cements.
- Establish a diagenetic model and paragenetic sequence for the Jauf sandstone.
- Characterize the petrographic features (texture and mineralogy) of Jauf Formation sandstones from Well A and Well B.
- Identify and quantify authigenic cements including calcite, illite, and chlorite.
- Evaluate the impact of these minerals on reservoir quality.
- To characterize the mineralogical and textural properties of Jauf Formation sandstones from surface and subsurface samples.
- To identify and sequence the diagenetic events (compaction, cementation, dissolution) affecting the reservoir.
- To evaluate the extent and impact of secondary porosity development due to feldspar and calcite dissolution.
- Identify the primary mineralogical composition of the Jauf Formation sandstones.
- Determine the sequence of diagenetic events (compaction, cementation, dissolution) affecting the reservoir.
- Evaluate the role of specific authigenic minerals (chlorite, illite, calcite) on porosity and permeability.
- Investigate the origin and significance of secondary porosity in the sedimentary sequence.

## Methods

The study utilized three primary analytical methods: 1) Thin Section Petrography, where samples were impregnated with red/blue dyed epoxy to facilitate porosity identification under polarized light (Leitz Laborlux 11Pol microscope). 2) Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM-EDX), using a JEOL JSM-5800 LV system to observe cement morphology and composition. 3) X-Ray Diffraction (XRD), where clay fractions were separated from sandstone via water soaking and gravity settling, then analyzed in a JEOL X-ray diffractometer at 40 kV and 40 mA.

## Data and Materials

A total of 62 samples were collected from two wells (Well A and Well B) in the Hawiyah field. Additionally, surface samples were collected from three outcrops (L1, L2, and L3) in the Subbat El Wadi Member of the Jauf Formation in the Al-Jawf region.

## Key Findings

- Jauf Formation sandstone consists of fine to medium-grained, moderately to well-sorted, and texturally mature quartz arenites. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 18]])
- Framework grains average 77% quartz, 2% feldspar, and 0.1% rock fragments. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 18]])
- Major authigenic cements include calcite, chlorite, illite, and quartz; minor amounts of pyrite and illite/smectite are present. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 18]])
- Early-calcite cement is poikilotopic and occupied most of the pore spaces in some samples, reducing primary porosity. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 18]])
- Chlorite pore fillings and grain coatings reduced both porosity and permeability but helped retain initial porosity at depth by retarding quartz overgrowth development. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 18]])
- Illite cement is most commonly found as hair-like growth coating grains and bridging pore throats, reducing permeability dramatically. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 18]])
- Quartz overgrowths reduced both porosity and permeability significantly. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 18]])
- Late-calcite cement did not have a profound effect on the samples. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 18]])
- Invasions of acidic fluids resulted in partial dissolution of calcite cements and feldspar grains, creating secondary porosity. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 18]])
- The Jauf Formation sandstones are predominantly fine to medium-grained (75%), with average mineralogy of 77% quartz, 2% feldspar, and 0.1% rock fragments. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 53]])
- 49 sandstone samples were classified as quartz arenites, 2 as subarkosic arenites, 8 as feldspar graywackes, and 3 as quartzwackes based on QFL diagrams. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 54]])
- Poikilotopic calcite cement (10-17% by weight) indicates early-stage burial at shallow depths where minimal compaction occurred. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 65]])
- Late calcite cement occurs as isolated pore-filling and grain-replacing modes, often resulting in the destruction of original sedimentary features. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 65]])
- Illite is the most abundant clay mineral, appearing as high-birefringence coatings on grain surfaces (pore-linings) or as pore-bridging cement. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 70]])
- Chlorite exists in two forms: thin grain coatings (rims) and pore-filling rosettes/aggregates, with the absence of silt components confirming its authigenic origin. ([[2001-al-ramadan|Al-Ramadan, 2001, p. 70]])
