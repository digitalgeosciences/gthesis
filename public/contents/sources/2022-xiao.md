# Mechanism and influence of wettability alteration using Gemini surfactants in carbonate EOR

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the mechanisms and impacts of wettability alteration (WA) and interfacial tension (IFT) reduction on various types of residual oil in carbonate and sandstone reservoirs. It specifically evaluates how different EOR materials, such as Gemini surfactants and nanoparticles, influence these parameters to improve recovery from categories like oil films, dead-end oil, and trapped droplets.

---

## Research Problem

Carbonate formations are often oil-wet and naturally fractured, leading to significant bypassed oil. While surfactants are used for IFT reduction, the specific role of wettability alteration—especially using novel Gemini surfactants—in carbonate EOR is not fully understood, particularly regarding the influence of molecular structure and concentration.

## Objectives

- Determine the relative importance of wettability alteration (WA) over interfacial tension (IFT) reduction in carbonate rock enhanced oil recovery (EOR).
- Evaluate the wett ability alteration performance of gemini surfactants in carbonate rocks, considering factors like concentration and brine composition.
- Establish the relationship between the molecular structure of gemini surfactants and their physical properties.
- To evaluate the effectiveness of different surfactant types (cationic, anionic, nonionic, and zwitterionic) on carbonate rock wettability.
- To investigate the unique properties and potential of Gemini surfactants as high-performance wettibility modifiers in carbonate systems.
- To analyze the impact of nanoparticles (e.g., SiO2, TiO2, ZrO2) on carbonate rock wettability and their stability under high salinity/temperature.

## Methods

The study utilizes contact angle measurements using a Theta Attension Tensiometer (accuracy ± 0.1°) to assess wettability. Spontaneous imbibition tests in Amott cells are used to link wettability changes directly to oil recovery. SEM is employed for qualitative visualization of inter-granular fluid distribution and surface modifications. The spinning drop method (Kruss instrument) measures IFT between oil and water. Micro-CT scanning provides pore-scale images of rock plugs. FTIR spectroscopy identifies chemical bonding and functional groups on surfaces. TGA monitors mass changes to determine adsorption types. AFM is used to measure force-distance for surface composition analysis.

## Data and Materials

The study used filtered crude oil (API 31.07). Rock samples included Indiana limestone (porosity 14.6%) and Guelph dolomite. Several chemical formulations were tested, including five types of chelating agents at various concentrations (up to 3 wt%), a Gemini surfactant (GS8) at 0.05-0.1 wt%, and mixtures in synthetic seawater (SW) and other salt solutions (NaCl, NaHCO3, Na2SO4, MgCl2, CaCl2).

## Key Findings

- Gemini surfactants are found to reduce IFT more efficiently than conventional surfactants and can change oil-wet surfaces to water-wet conditions. ([[2022-xiao|Xiao, 2022, p. 25]])
- Surfactants with one phenyl group in the spacer performed best, changing oil-wet rock to water-wet conditions; unsaturation in the spacer and high salinity had a strong negative impact on performance. ([[2022-xiao|Xiao, 2022, p. 21]])
- The addition of chelating agents (e.g., DTPA) significantly improves the salt tolerance and wettability alteration performance of gemini surfactant solutions. ([[2022-xiao|Xiao, 2022, p. 21]])
- Spontaneous imbibition results indicate that when wettability alteration occurs, both enhancing WA and alleviating IFT reduction can increase and accelerate oil recovery. ([[2022-xiao|Xiao, 2022, p. 21]])
- Cationic surfactants are more effective than anionic or zwitterionic ones in modifying carbonate surfaces because they form ion pairs with adsorbed carboxylate groups. ([[2022-xiao|Xiao, 2022, p. 47]])
- Higher hydrophobicity in cationic surfactants facilitates ion-pair formation but can reduce the concentration of monomers, potentially impairing desorption efficiency. ([[2022-xiao|Xiao, 2022, p. 47]])
- An optimal salinity exists for cationic surfactants where increased salt reduces the solubility of surface-active materials and increases electrostatic repulsion. ([[2022-xiao|Xiao, 2022, p. 48]])
- Anionic surfactants are less effective in carbonate rocks due to their tendency to form a reversible double-layer structure via weaker hydrophobic interactions. ([[2022-xiao|Xiao, 2022, p. 53]])
- Zwitterionic surfactants show excellent tolerance toward high salinity and temperature but are limited by high cost. ([[2022-xiao|Xiao, 2022, p. 61]])
- Nonionic surfactants generally only alter oil-wet carbonate to intermediate-wet, except at high temperatures (90°C). ([[2022-xiao|Xiao, 2022, p. 67]])
- Gemini surfactants are approximately 3 orders of magnitude more efficient in reducing IFT and 2 orders of magnitude more effective in forming micelles than conventional surfactants. ([[2022-xiao|Xiao, 2022, p. 71]])

- Cationic surfactants provide strong wettability alteration (WA) but weak IFT reduction, while anionic surfactants offer weak WA but strong IFT reduction. ([[2022-xiao|Xiao, 2022, p. 103]])
- Oil film thickness and mobility are governed by the work of adhesion; reducing IFT and moving toward water-wet conditions both reduce this work, facilitating oil removal. ([[2022-xiao|Xiao, 2022, p. 112]])
- Dead-end oil is difficult to mobilize due to poor connectivity; however, it can be mobilized by foams or viscoelastic polymers that create vortices. ([[2022-xiao|Xiao, 2022, p. 113]])

## Limitations

The thesis notes that it is difficult to study IFT and wettability separately because most EOR materials change both simultaneously. Additionally, experimental results are often limited by the lack of identical natural cores and varying environmental conditions (pH, salinity, temperature).

## Recommendations and Future Work

Further investigation of Gemini surfactants on carbonate rock wettibility is needed due to their promising results in other systems.
