# Factors Affecting Mercury Capillary Pressure Measurements

**Summary**: The thesis investigates factors affecting mercury capillary pressure measurements in Berea sandstone and carbonate rocks, specifically focusing on sample size, epoxy coating, and equilibrium time. It evaluates these factors' impact on permeability estimation using Katz and Thompson’s method versus Swanson’s correlation, and explores the use of J-functions for data correlation.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the variability in mercury capillary pressure curves caused by experimental conditions (sample size, coating, equilibrium) and how these variations affect the accuracy of derived reservoir properties like permeability and pore throat distribution in both sandstone and carbonate rocks.

## Objectives

- Evaluate the influence of sample size on the capillary pressure saturation relation.
- Evaluate the influence of equilibrium time.
- Evaluate the effect of epoxy coating.
- Determine parameters influencing the forms of capillary pressure curves beyond those studied earlier.
- Use capillary pressure data to calculate permeability and compare with previous studies.
- Correlate experimental data using J-function or an extended J-function.

## Methods

Mercury injection experiments were conducted on core samples (Berea, Bentheimer, and carbonate) of varying lengths (2 cm, 4 cm, and 6 cm). A blank run was performed to establish a pressure-volume correction curve. Injection and withdrawal processes were conducted at both slow (equilibrium) and rapid rates. Epoxy coating was applied to some samples to minimize surface effects. Pore throat sorting (PTS) was calculated using the ratio of pressures at 25% and 75% mercury saturation. Permeability was calculated using the Katz and Thompson method, involving the identification of an inflection point in the volume-pressure curve to determine critical length and subsequent calculation of permeability from peak values.

## Data and Materials

The study includes multiple samples of Berea sandstone, Bentheimer sandstone, and carbonate rocks (including dolomite and limestone). Specific sample sizes (2 cm, 4 cm, 6 cm) and coating conditions (coated vs. uncoated) were tested for both sandstone and carbonate types.

## Key Findings

- Capillary pressure curves are insensitive to the sample size of sandstone samples regardless of equilibrium time and epoxy coating. ([[1991-ali|Ali, 1991, p. 22]])
- For coated carbonate rock samples, injection capillary pressure curves depend on sample size; as size increased, curves moved to lower saturation and displacement pressure decreased. ([[1991-ali|Ali, 1991, p. 22]])
- Katz and Thompson’s percolation theory gave a standard deviation error of 45% for permeability estimation, while Swanson’s correlation approach yielded an error of 72%. ([[1991-ali|Ali, 1991, p. 22]])
- Pore throat sorting (PTS) was used as a parameter in the extended J-function for sandstone rocks to improve correlation. ([[1991-ali|Ali, 1991, p. 22]])
- Critical length (L) was considered in the correlation function for carbonate samples with permeability ranging from 20 mD to 800 mD. ([[1991-ali|Ali, 1991, p. 22]])
- Approximately 80% of pore throats in both Berea and Bentheimer samples were in the range of 10 to 60 $\mu$m. ([[1991-ali|Ali, 1991, p. 53]])
- Berea samples had slightly larger pore throat diameters and more microporosity than Bentheimer samples. ([[1991-ali|Ali, 1991, p. 53]])
- Injection capillary pressure curves for Berea and Bentheimer samples were found to be essentially independent of sample size regardless of coating or equilibrium time. ([[1991-ali|Ali, 1991, p. 77]])
- Withdrawal capillary pressure curves showed that the amount of trapped mercury increased as the sample size increases, with rapid withdrawal rates leading to wider curves and higher residual saturation. ([[1991-ali|Ali, 1991, p. 87]])
- In carbonate rocks, sample size influenced the shape of the capillary pressure curves; larger pore throat samples resulted in injection curves shifting toward lower pressure ranges. ([[1991-ali|Ali, 1991, p. 89]])
- For Berea and Bentheimer sandstones, the displacement pressure remained consistent between slow and rapid injection rates because these rocks have high permeability and homogeneous pore sizes. ([[1991-ali|Ali, 1991, p. 97]])
- The study concluded that for carbonate rocks, porosity is the only variable that correlates with withdrawal efficiency. ([[1991-ali|Ali, 1991, p. 171]])
- Katz & Thompson's method provided a more realistic and reliable permeability estimation (45% deviation) compared to Swanson's correlation method (72% deviation). ([[1991-ali|Ali, 1991, p. 182]])
- Sample size did not significantly affect the injection capillary pressure curve for sandstone, but carbonate samples showed strong dependence on pore throat diameter and permeability. ([[1991-ali|Ali, 1991, p. 220]])
- For rapid withdrawal rates, residual saturation increased with sample size in sandstone, while unsaturated pore volumes remained constant across all sizes. ([[1991-ali|Ali, 1991, p. 220]])

## Limitations

The estimation of permeability using the Katz and Thompson method was found to have an estimated error of approximately a factor of 2, which aligns with previous literature for this specific methodology.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Conduct capillary pressure measurements under high pressure (~10,000 psi) to better understand mercury-air behavior in tight carbonate rocks.
- Increase the number of carbonate samples analyzed to better evaluate rock characteristics.
