# Evaluating Factors Controlling Sonic Velocity in Carbonate Factories

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the factors controlling sonic velocity in carbonate rocks, specifically focusing on how dolostone texture (fabric-preserving vs. non-fabric preserving), pore types, and crystal size influence acoustic properties. Using a dataset of 100 samples from five Saudi Arabian formations, the study concludes that while porosity is the primary driver of sonic velocity, rock texture and specific pore geometries significantly impact the variance in the porosity-velocity relationship.

---

## Research Problem

The complexity and heterogeneity of carbonate rocks due to diverse depositional environments and diagenetic processes make it difficult to accurately predict petrophysical properties like sonic velocity from simple models. This study addresses the need for a understanding how specific factors—pore types, mineralogy, and diagenesis—impact acoustic behavior in various carbonate 'factories' within Saudi Arabia.

## Objectives

- To examine the relationship between porosity/permeability, and sonic velocity in carbonate rocks of different carbonate factories and ages.
- To examine the relationship between pore type and size, and the sonic velocity in carbonate strata.
- To examine the role of mineralogy on the sonic velocity in terms of carbonate strata.
- To examine the role of dolomitization and dolomite texture on the sonic velocity for different carbonate factory sediments.
- To understand the impact of porosity, mineralogy, texture, and pore types on acoustic velocity.
- To understand how the original depositional texture and diagenetic pathway of the sediments control their elastic behavior.

## Methods

The study employed a multi-analytical approach: 1) Field work in the Riyadh area and other Saudi Arabian locations to collect samples from Hanifa, Jauf, Khuff, Rus, and Dam formations. 2) Thin-section petrography (with Alizarin Red S) to identify pore types and mineralogy. 3) XRD analysis using the X’Pert³ system for mineral quantification. 4) SEM analysis on representative samples to determine microstructural features. 5D. Porosity and permeability measurements using a pressure decay technique (AP-608 Automated Permeameter-Porosimeter). 6) Acoustic velocity measurement (P-wave and S-wave) using the NER 500 machine under varying confining pressures (5–40 MPa). 7. CT scan analysis using Toshiba Alexion TSX-032A and PerGeos software to quantify bioturbation intensity. 8. Rock physics modeling to link petrophysical properties with elastic parameters.

## Data and Materials

100 samples from five different formations (Jauf, Khuff, Hanifa, Rus, Dam) were analyzed; 27 specific samples were used for the detailed study of bioturbation in the Hanifa Formation. Measurements included porosity (1-45%), permeability (<0.01 to 2000 mD), and acoustic velocities (3-6.7 km/s).

## Key Findings

- Porosity is the primary controlling factor on sonic velocity, exhibiting a clear inverse relationship. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 21]])
- Samples with moldic and vuggy pores show significantly higher velocities than those with interparticle (intergranular/intercrystalline) and microporosity. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 21]])
- The presence of quartz and clay minerals results in a slight reduction in the velocity of both limestones and dolostones. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 21]])
- Bioturbation has an indirect relationship with velocity; specifically, samples with coarse-filled burrows have lower velocities than those with fine-filled bururs. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 21]])
- Dolomite texture and fabric significantly impact velocity, where fabric-preserved dolostones exhibit higher velocities than non-fabric preserved ones. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 21]])
- Using the equivalent pore aspect ratio (EPAR), a clear distinction was observed: moldic/vuggy pores have high EPAR values (>0.15), while interparticle and microporosity have low EPAR values. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 41]])
- In the Khuff Formation, dolomitization creates a connected pore system that increases permeability but decreases pore aspect ratio (and thus velocity) compared to other diagenetic features. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 43]])
- Blocky calcite cementation reduces EPAR from >0.4 to <0.1 for extensively infilled molds, significantly impacting the velocity-porosity relationship. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 43]])
- Porosity and permeability are inversely related to velocity (Vp and Vs) with high correlation coefficients (R2 = 0.7 for Vp vs porosity). ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 15]])
- The inclusion of DEM modeling results for different EPAR values showed that pore shape is a critical factor in explaining the variance in velocity at given porosities. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 41]])
- Porosity and permeability showed a general inverse relationship with VP and VS, but the correlation was stronger for porosity (R2 = 0.4-0.5) than for permeability (R2 = 0.2). ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 64]])
- Lithofacies and mineralogy showed no clear impact on acoustic velocity; the majority of samples (>90%) were dominated by calcite. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 66]])
- Eight pore types were identified: interparticle, moldic (oomolds, partially infilled, extensively infilled), vugs, framework, and micro-intercrystalline. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 59]])
- Samples with oomoldic or framework porosity showed the highest EPAR values (~0.4) and higher velocities at any given porosity compared to other pore types. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 68]])
- Micro-intercrystalline pores in dolostone samples resulted in lower velocity values compared to moldic and intragranular pores due to their low aspect ratio. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 72]])

## Limitations

Permeameter limitations required the exclusion of samples with permeability < 0.1 mD from certain correlation analyses.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study suggests that rock physics modeling using EPAR values can be used to differentiate between dolostone textures and improve the accuracy of porosity prediction from sonic logs and seismic data.
