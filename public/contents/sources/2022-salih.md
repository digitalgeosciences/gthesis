# Evaluating Factors Controlling Sonic Velocity in Carbonate Factories

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the factors controlling sonic velocity in carbonate rocks, specifically focusing on how porosity and rock texture (fabric-preserving vs. non-fabric preserving) influence acoustic properties. Using 100 outcrop samples from five Saudi Arabian formations, it demonstrates that while porosity is a primary driver of velocity, texture significantly influences the relationship between porosity and impedance.

---

## Research Problem

The primary challenge is the significant scatter in velocity-porosity relationships in carbonate reservoirs like the Khuff Formation. Understanding whether this variation is driven by mineralogical changes, lithofacies heterogeneity, or specific pore geometries (pore types) is essential for accurate seismic interpretation and reservoir characterization.

## Methods

The study involved collecting 100 samples from five formations: Qasr Member, Khuff Formation, Jubaila Formation, Rus Formation, and Dam Formation. Mineralogy was determined via XRD (X'Pert® system). Petrographic analysis included thin-section preparation and staining with blue epoxy and alizarin red to distinguish minerals. Crystal size and pore types were analyzed using ImageJ software on photomicrographs. Porosity was measured using a Helium Porosimeter, and permeability was measured using a PMI permeameter (pressure decay technique). Sonic velocities (Vp and Vs) were measured using an NER Autolab 500 machine at six different pressures (1 to 40 MPa). Rock physics modeling utilized Differential Effective Medium (DEM) theory to relate porosity and velocity by incorporating pore aspect ratios (EPAR).

## Data and Materials

100 outcrop dolostone samples from five formations; additional data from four other carbonate formations (totaling 200 samples in some contexts) were used to validate trends. Measurements included porosity (1-45%), permeability (<0.01 to 2000 mD), and velocities (VP: 3.04–6.77 km/s, VS: 1.64–3.68 km/s).

## Key Findings

- Both porosity and permeability have an inverse relationship with sonic velocity (VP and VS). ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 13]])
- In the Upper Khartam Member, calcite cementation of oomolds reduces pore aspect ratio significantly, resulting in only a slight increase in velocity despite a drastic decrease in porosity. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 13]])
- For the Hanifa Formation, velocity is more controlled by porosity (R2 = 0.7) than by permeability (R2 = 0.5). ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 15]])
- Quartz-rich samples in the Hanifa Formation exhibit lower velocities compared to quartz-poor samples. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 15]])
- Samples dominated by fractures and interparticle pores show the lowest compressional velocities (Vp). ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 15]])
- Porosity is the main controlling factor on sonic velocity, showing a clear inverse relationship. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 21]])
- Samples with moldic and vuggy pores show higher velocities than those with interparticle (intergranular/intercrystalline) or microporosity. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 21]])
- The presence of quartz and clay minerals slightly reduces the velocity of limestones and dolostones. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 21]])
- Bioturbation has an indirect relation with velocity; samples with coarse-filled burrows have lower velocities than those with fine-filled burrows. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 21]])
- Fabric-preserved dolostones exhibit higher velocities compared to non-fabric preserved dolostones. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 21]])
- A clear distinction between pore types is observed using Equivalent Pore Aspect Ratio (EPAR), where moldic and vuggy pores have high EPAR values (>0.15). ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 19]])
- Samples with higher permeabilities (>0.1 mD) mostly lie below the 5 km/s velocity line. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 102]])
- The inclusion of EPAR values in modeling shows that moldic, vuggy, and interparticle pores have higher EPAR than intercrystalline or micro-intercrystalline pores. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 19]])
- Excluding Dam Formation samples increases the correlation coefficient (R2) for the porosity-velocity relationship. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 145]])
- The Raymer-Hunt-Gardner (RHG) equation and Wyllie time average are compared against experimental data to evaluate accuracy in carbonate systems. ([[2022-salih|Salih, 2022, p. 148]])

## Limitations

The study notes that the exact physical reason for discrepancies between P-wave derived EPAR (EPARp) and S-wave derived EPAR (EPARs) remains unclear, potentially due to complex non-uniform pore geometries not fully captured by idealized DEM models.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The results suggest that sonic (well logging) and seismic data can provide useful tools to differentiate between various rock types based on pore types and diagenetic overprints in the Khuff Formation.
