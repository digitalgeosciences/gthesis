# The Effect of Clay Content and Type on Oil Recovery from Sandstone Cores Using High pH Chelating Agents

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of clay mineralogy and concentration on oil recovery in sandstone reservoirs, specifically focusing on mechanisms like fines migration, wettability alteration, and chemical interactions with chelating agents. It evaluates various chelating agents (EDTA, GLDA, HEDTA) and low-salinity water injection to determine their effectiveness in improving recovery and overcoming formation damage.

---

## Research Problem

The presence of various clay minerals (kaolinite, illite, chlorite, smectite) in sandstone reservoirs complicates oil recovery due to their ability to block pores, line surfaces, or bridge pore throats. The study addresses how these minerals interact with EDTA chelating agents and how different concentrations of the agent affect oil mobility and rock-fluid interactions.

## Objectives

- To evaluate the impact of different clay content on oil recovery using Na2EDTA chelating agent.
- To determine the optimum Na2EDTA concentration leading to the highest oil recovery from sandstone core samples in the presence of clays.
- To understand the mechanism leading to additional oil recovery using Na2EDTA chelating agents.
- To understand the role of iron (Fe) on oil recovery using Na2EDTA chelating agent.
- Evaluate the effect of clay minerals (kaolinite and illite) on oil recovery in sandstone cores.
- Investigate the impact of firing on clay mineralogy and its subsequent effect on oil recovery.

## Methods

The study employed XRD for mineral identification, XRF for elemental analysis, and SEM-EDS/FESEM-EDS to visualize clay distribution. FTIR and viscosity measurements were used to confirm the stability of Na4EDTA in seawater at 250°F. IFT was measured using a Tensiometer (Pendent Drop Technique) across various temperatures and EDTA concentrations. Zeta potential was measured using a Brookhaven ZetaPALS instrument on crushed rock powder. Core flooding experiments involved saturating core plugs with formation brine, aging them under pressure, and performing sequential injections of seawater and varying concentrations of Na4EDTA (1%, 3%, 5%, 7%) to monitor oil recovery, pressure drop, and effluent chemistry.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized Gray Berea sandstone (high clay content) and Gray Bandera sandstone (lower clay content). Core samples were analyzed for mineralogy (XRD), elemental composition (XRF), and morphology (SEM). Core flooding was performed on 10 core plugs with dimensions of approximately 2x1.5 inches, aged at 100°C and 2500 psi.

## Key Findings

- The 5wt% EDTA in seawater solution was found to be the optimum concentration which resulted in the highest recovery without affecting the rock integrity. ([[2015-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2015, p. 23]])
- Maximum oil recovered from Gray Berea sandstone with the 5wt% EDTA solution was slightly higher than 15.00% of OOIP. ([[2015-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2015, p. 23]])
- Oil recovery from Gray Bandera sandstone with 5wt% EDTA was approximately 6.30% of OOIP due to low permeability caused by high amounts of illite and kaolinite. ([[2015-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2015, p. 23]])
- Addition of 5wt% of EDTA in seawater reduced the IFT from 20.58 mN/m (seawater) to 5.29 mN/m (5wt% EDTA solution). ([[2015-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2015, p. 23]])
- Zeta potential of the 5wt% Na4EDTA/crushed rock samples at pH of 12.00 were -21.07 mV and -28.02 mV for Gray Berea and Gray Bandera respectively. ([[2015-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2015, p. 23]])
- Kaolinite, being a large-sized clay (up to 10 micrometers), is prone to pore filling at the entrance of pore throats. ([[2015-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2015, p. 39]])
- Chlorite has a high ability to line grain surfaces and reduce effective porosity. ([[2015-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2015, p. 39]])
- Illite can cause significant reduction in both porosity and permeability through pore bridging. ([[2015-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2015, p. 39]])
- Firing Berea sandstone at 1000°C converted all clay minerals (kaolinite and illite) to amorphous, fused alumino-silicates, resulting in an increase in oil recovery due to changes in pore geometry and rock/fluid interaction. ([[2015-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2015, p. 43]])
- The presence of kaolinite is not a requirement for the mechanism leading to increased oil recovery via low-salinity water; other clay types can also trigger mechanisms like pH increase and fines migration. ([[2015-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2015, p. 45]])
- Low salinity water injection results in an increase in oil recovery by potentially causing clay swelling or the creation of new flow channels through the migration of dispersed clay particles. ([[2015-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2015, p. 46]])
- HEDTA at a low pH of 4 has very low compatibility with illitic-Bandera sandstone compared to GLDA, as HEDTA tends to attack clay minerals more than carbonates. ([[2015-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2015, p. 47]])
- GLDA was compatible with all tested sandstone cores (up to 18% illite) and outperformed HEDTA and HCl in the stimulation of sandstone cores at pH 4. ([[2015-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2015, p. 47]])
- The mechanism for low-salinity water recovery may involve surfactant generation at elevated pH from the polar components of oil, reducing interfacial tension (IFT). ([[2015-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2015, p. 48]])
- Multi-component ion exchange between clay minerals and injected brine can lead to a significant decrease in Mg2+ concentration in produced water. ([[2015-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2015, p. 51]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Use sandstone core samples with higher concentrations of swellable clay minerals (smectite group).
- Use longer core samples (at least 12 feet) to quantify the effect of clay mineral migration.
- Fire both Gray Berea and Gray Bandera samples to deactivate kaolinite.
