# IMPROVING WELL PRODUCTIVITY OF TIGHT GAS RESERVOIRS BY USING NON-DAMAGING DRILLING FLUID

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the use of sodium silicate as an additive in drill-in fluids to improve well productivity in tight gas reservoirs. It evaluates the impact of varying concentrations of sodium silicate on rheological properties, barite solubility, and filter cake formation/thickness to determine an optimal concentration for minimizing fluid invasion.

---

## Research Problem

Tight gas reservoirs suffer from significant production losses due to water blockage in small pores and poor filter cake formation when using conventional drilling fluids. There is a need for non-damaging 'drill-in' fluids that provide high return permeability and prevent the accumulation of solids and clay hydration at the wellbore.

## Objectives

- Formulating a proper non-damaging barite-weighted water-based drilling fluid and determining the optimum sodium silicate concentration
- Evaluating the effect of sodium silicate on the rheological properties of the formulated drill-in fluid
- Evaluating the effect of sodium silicate on the barite solubility
- Improving well productivity by obtaining the high return permeability after forming the very thin, impermeable and easily removable filter cake
- Assessing the effect of optimum concentration of sodium silicate on the sag performance of the formulated drill-in fluid
- Evaluate the effect of various sodium silicate concentrations on the density and pH of the base drilling fluid.

## Methods

The study utilized a series of laboratory experiments: 1) Density and pH measurements for various Na2SiO3 concentrations (0.05 to 2 wt%). 2) Rheology testing using Grace M3600 Rheometer and Chandler 7550 HPHT Viscometer at temperatures up to 300°F. 3) Solubility tests of barite in a solution containing DTPA, potassium formate, and enzymes. 4) Static filtration tests using a Modified Fann HPHT Filter Press to measure filter cake thickness and volume. 5) Permeability measurements (initial and return) using a Flow Rate Controller and core samples (0.25" and 2"). 6) Structural analysis of cores via Toshiba CT Scanner and PerGeos software for slice-by-slice and cross-sectional evaluation.

## Data and Materials

The study included rheological data at three temperatures (140°F, 170°F, 300°F), solubility results for barite with sodium silicate concentrations of 2-8 wt%, filtration tests on 0.25" and 2" core samples, and CT scan data comparing 6 slices of a 0.25" core and 50 slices of a 2" core.

## Key Findings

- Sodium silicate (Na2SiO3) in a gel state forms a film-like barrier that can minimize fluid invasion and prevent water blockage by forming a thin, impermeable filter cake. ([[2017-jafarov|Jafarov, 2017, p. 31]])
- The study determined that the optimum concentration of sodium silicate reduces solid invasion completely, minimizes fluid invasion, and prevents water blockage. ([[2017-jafarov|Jafarov, 2017, p. 19]])
- A thin filter cake formed by sodium silicate has easily breakable character, allowing for high return permeability. ([[2017-jafarov|Jafarov, 2017, p. 31]])
- Sodium silicate was observed to have a positive effect on the rheological properties of the formulated drill-in fluid. ([[2017-jafarov|Jafarov, 2017, p. 19]])
- The study evaluated sodium silicate's impact on barite solubility and sag performance in the drilling fluid. ([[2017-jafarov|Jafarov, 2017, p. 19]])
- Sodium silicate addition had no significant effect on density (range 107-113 pcf) or pH (constant around 10), ensuring low corrosion rates. ([[2017-jafarov|Jafarov, 2017, p. 45]])
- Plastic Viscosity (PV) increased from 26.7 cp to 35.1 cp as sodium silicate concentration increased from 0% to 2% at room temperature. ([[2017-jafarov|Jafarov, 2017, p. 69]])
- Yield Point (YP) jumped significantly from 56.8 lb/100ft² to 83.3 lb/100ft² with just 0.05 wt% sodium silicate at room temperature. ([[2017-jafarov|Jafarov, 2017, p. 69]])
- Gel strength values for both 10-second and 10-minute intervals stabilized around 34-45 lb/100ft² at higher concentrations of sodium silicate. ([[2017-jafarov|Jafarov, 2017, p. 70]])
- Deflocculation effects were observed as a decrease in PV and YP values at temperatures above 100°F for low concentration samples. ([[2017-jafarov|Jafarov, 2017, p. 73]])
- The study determined that sodium silicate acts as an inhibitive additive against fluid invasion. ([[2017-jafarov|Jafarov, 2017, p. 64]])
- At 170°F, the yield point (YP) for low concentrations of sodium silicate remained stable around 47 lb/100 ft². ([[2017-jafarov|Jafarov, 2017, p. 83]])
- At 300°F, YP values fell below 10 lb/100 ft² at high pressures, indicating that pressure significantly impacts YP alongside concentration and temperature. ([[2017-jafarov|Jafarov, 2017, p. 89]])
- A concentration of 4 wt% sodium silicate was identified as the optimum for barite solubility, yielding an average of 82%. ([[2017-jafarov|Jafarov, 2017, p. 93]])
- The concentration of 0.075 wt% sodium silicate resulted in the lowest filter cake thickness (0.7 mm) and reduced filtrate volume by 53% and filter cake thickness by 65% compared to base fluid. ([[2017-jafarov|Jafarov, 2017, p. 131]])

## Limitations

The study noted that results for the 2" core sample were slightly inconsistent with the 0.25" samples due to potential issues with bridging materials (specifically the <38 μm size), which can vary in range.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Observe the effect of sodium silicate on calcium carbonate and manganese tetraoxide-weighted water-based drilling fluids.
- Optimize enzyme concentration for starch in complex chemical systems.
- Check the effect of finer bridging materials on filtration tests with tight core samples.
