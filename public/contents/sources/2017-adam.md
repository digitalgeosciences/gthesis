# PERFORMANCE OF MIXED CO2/N2 FOAM IN ENHANCED OIL RECOVERY FOR SANDSTONE RESERVOIRS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the performance of CO2 and N2 foam mixtures using two different surfactants (FS-51 and AOS) for enhanced oil recovery in a sandstone reservoir. Experimental results show that while foam successfully reduced gas mobility and improved recovery compared to pure gas flooding, the addition of N2 to CO2 did not improve displacement efficiency.

---

## Research Problem

Carbon dioxide flooding for EOR often suffers from poor volumetric sweep efficiency due to the high mobility of CO2, leading to early gas breakthrough. At typical reservoir conditions, CO2 is in a supercritical state, which can result in weak foam formation.

## Objectives

- Examine the performance of oil displacement by a novel foam system consisting of a mixture of CO2 and N2.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of adding Nitrogen to Carbon Dioxide to improve its ability to generate strong foam at supercritical conditions.
- Compare the performance of CO2/N2 foam against pure CO2 foam in terms of displacement efficiency.
- Study the effects of CO2/N2 ratio, surfactant type (FS-51 and AOS), foam quality, and injection rate on flooding performance.
- Assess the performance of foam mixtures composed of carbon dioxide (CO2) and nitrogen (N2) using two different types of surfactants (FS-51 and AOS).
- Evaluate the impact of foam on gas mobility and its ability to contact more areas inside the core.

## Methods

The study utilized a coreflooding system consisting of a fluid-delivery unit (including multiple pumps like Quizix and ISCO), a core unit (Hassler-type holder with a 7-micron filter as a foam generator), and a production unit (back-pressure regulator). Berea sandstone cores were prepared by drying, saturating with formation brine (TDS 253.88 g/L) or seawater brine (TDS 67.71 g/L), and aging for 3 days at 50°C to achieve mixed wettability. N-decane was used as the model oil. Experimental designs included varying the surfactant type (FS-51, an amphoteric amine oxide-based fluorosurfactant; and AOS, an anionic alpha-olefin sulfonate), foam quality (80% and 90%), and N2 fraction (0%, 20%).

## Data and Materials

The study utilized sandstone core samples for all experiments. Experimental parameters included: 100% CO2, 80% CO2/20% N2 mixtures, foam qualities of 80% and 90%, and surfactant concentrations (e.g., 0.15 vol% FS-51 or 0.5% vol AOS).

## Key Findings

- The experimental work indicated that foam significantly improved oil recovery compared to pure gas injection. ([[2017-adam|Adam, 2017, p. 14]])
- CO2 foam performance was found to be superior to CO2/N2 mixture foams in terms of overall results. ([[2017-adam|Adam, 2017, p. 14]])
- The fluorosurfactant (FS-51) demonstrated the ability to generate stable foam in high-saline environments. ([[2017-adam|Adam, 2017, p. 14]])
- Waterflooding recovery factors across all eight experiments ranged between 0.1 and 0.4, with remaining oil saturations between 0.3 and 0.6. ([[2017-adam|Adam, 2017, p. 32]])
- Pressure drops during waterflooding were observed to increase as the injection rate increased (from 1 to 2 cc/min or 2 to 4 cc/min). ([[2017-adam|Adam, 2017, p. 32]])
- The study confirmed that adding Nitrogen to CO2 improves its ability to generate stable foam at supercritical conditions. ([[2017-adam|Adam, 2017, p. 19]])
- Miscible CO2 flooding achieved a recovery factor of 48.1% in the sandstone core. ([[2017-adam|Adam, 2017, p. 60]])
- Immiscible N2 flooding resulted in a recovery factor of 40%. ([[2017-adam|Adam, 2017, p. 60]])
- CO2 foam flooding with FS-51 (Exp. 3) achieved a recovery factor of 69%, significantly higher than pure CO2 flooding. ([[2017-adam|Adam, 2017, p. 63]])
- The addition of N2 to the CO2 mixture in foam (Exp. 4) resulted in a lower recovery factor of 51% compared to pure CO2 foam (69%), because N2 reduces the solubility of CO2 in oil. ([[2017-adam|Adam, 2017, p. 65]])
- CO2/N2 foam flooding with a higher foam quality of 90% (Exp. 5) yielded a recovery factor of 50%. ([[2017-adam|Adam, 2017, p. 67]])
- CO2 foam flooding using AOS as the foaming agent achieved a recovery factor of 66.7%. ([[2017-adam|Adam, 2017, p. 69]])
- CO2/N2 foam flooding with AOS (Exp. 7) reached an ultimate oil recovery of 77.3% (or 74.4% depending on the specific comparison context). ([[2017-adam|Adam, 2017, p. 71]])
- The gas breakthrough in CO2 foam flooding occurred at approximately 0.7 PV, while in CO2/N2 foam with AOS it was 0.8 PV. ([[2017-adam|Adam, 2017, p. 71]])
- Pressure drop in experiments using AOS as a foaming agent reached values as high as 1000 psi, suggesting potential fines migration or significant mobility reduction. ([[2017-adam|Adam, 2017, p. 69]])

## Limitations

The study notes that the addition of N2 to CO2 did not improve displacement efficiency because CO2 has superior solubility and interaction with oil compared to N2. Additionally, it mentions that results are most reliable when all parameters (core characteristics, injection conditions, fluid properties) remain consistent.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Change the flooding strategy from co-injection mode to alternative injection of gas and surfactant (SAG).
- Investigate phase behavior of injected gases and surfactants upon contact with oil in place to understand molecular interactions.
- Introduce visualized testing during coreflooding to trace flood fronts and contacted regions.
