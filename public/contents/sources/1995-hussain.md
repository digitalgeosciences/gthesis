# Recovery Performance and Relative Permeabilities of Composite Cores

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the recovery performance and relative permeability calculations of composite cores (multiple core plugs joined together) in carbonate reservoirs. It compares experimental data with numerical simulations to evaluate the impact of capillary pressure end effects and different averaging methods (arithmetic, geometric, and harmonic) on determining accurate relative permeabilities.

---

## Research Problem

The determination of relative permeabilities in laboratory settings is often complicated by boundary effects (capillary end effects) and the small size of core samples, which may not accurately represent reservoir behavior. Composite cores are used to mitigate these issues, but the relationship between the average properties of individual plugs and the resulting composite core's performance remains a subject of study.

## Objectives

- To study the recovery performance of core samples and compare the values with the recovery of composite core formed by the individual core samples.
- To determine and compare relative permeabilities of individual core plugs and composite core.
- To study the averaging process of relative permeabilities of composite cores.
- Evaluate the impact of capillary pressure end effects on oil recovery and pressure drop in composite cores.
- Compare different averaging methods (arithmetic, geometric, and harmonic) for calculating relative permeabilities from multi-plug systems.
- Investigate the effect of plug ordering on the performance of composite cores.

## Methods

The study utilizes core flooding experiments to obtain oil recovery and pressure differential data for individual plugs (P-3, P-4) and a composite core (Composite-2). Numerical simulations were performed using power law equations for relative permeability. The JBN method was used to calculate relative permeabilities from the experimental and simulated data. Three types of averages—arithmetic, geometric, and harmonic—were calculated to determine which best represents the average properties of the individual plugs within a composite.

## Data and Materials

The study used carbonate reservoir rock samples (limestone). Five core plugs were analyzed: P-2, P-12, P-3, P-4, and P-16. Two composite cores were formed: Composite-1 (P-2 + P-12) and Composite-2 (P-3 + P-4 + P-16). Fluids used included dead oil (kerosene/crude mix) and 0.22% NaCl brine.

## Key Findings

- The recovery of oil from composite cores was less than that obtained from the individual core plugs due to viscous fingering and bypassing caused by the heterogeneous nature of carbonate rocks. ([[1995-hussain|Hussain, 1995, p. 19]])
- The relative permeability of a composite core is best represented by the harmonic average of the relative permeabilities of the individual plugs when capillary pressure end effects are not significant. ([[1995-hussain|Hussain, 1995, p. 125]])
- Numerical simulation results showed that the oil recovery of the composite core is the average of the individual plugs' recoveries. ([[1995-hussain|Hussain, 1995, p. 19]])
- The relative permeability of the composite core is the harmonic average of the relative permeabilities of the individual plugs, and the ordering of the plugs has no effect on the calculated average. ([[1995-hussain|Hussain, 1995, p. 19]])
- Wettability indices for all five core samples (P-2, P-12, P-3, P-4, P-16) were found to be near zero or low, indicating they were of intermediate/neutral wettability. ([[1995-hussain|Hussain, 1995, p. 57]])
- The maximum breakthrough recovery for individual plug P-2 was approximately 57% of OOIP, while the minimum breakthrough recovery for composite-1 was only 17% of OOIP. ([[1995-hussain|Hussain, 1995, p. 64]])
- For core plugs P-2 and P-12, the relative permeability to oil (kr) increased linearly after breakthrough, while the relative permeability to water (krw) decreased slowly. ([[1995-hussain|Hussain, 1995, p. 67]])
- The shift in the peak of the pressure differential curves between experimental and simulated results was attributed to non-uniform displacement fronts across the cross-sectional area due to heterogeneity. ([[1995-hussain|Hussain, 1995, p. 68]])
- Oil recovery from composite cores is lower than that of individual plugs due to the heterogeneous nature of carbonate rocks causing non-uniform fronts and early breakthrough via viscous fingering. ([[1995-hussain|Hussain, 1995, p. 124]])
- The JBN method may not be adequate for calculating relative permeabilities from displacement data subjected to viscous fingering and bypassing. ([[1995-hussain|Hussain, 1995, p. 124]])
- Ordering of individual core plugs has no significant effect on the recovery performance of a composite core if capillary pressure end effects are not significant. ([[1995-hussain|Hussain, 1995, p. 124]])
- When capillary pressure end effects are included, the pressure differential increases and the recovery of the composites is higher than that of the individual plugs. ([[1995-hussain|Hussain, 1995, p. 104]])
- Including capillary pressure end effects causes the calculated relative permeabilities to decrease compared to cases where they are not included. ([[1995-hussain|Hussain, 1995, p. 104]])

## Limitations

The study notes that the centrifuge method cannot determine if wettability is fractionally or mixed wet; it only provides an average value. Additionally, the simulation assumes one-dimensional flow in a homogeneous medium, which does not perfectly replicate the non-uniform displacement of heterogeneous carbonate rocks.
