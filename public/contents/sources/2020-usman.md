# SOURCE-ROCK CHARACTERIZATION OF LATE JURASSIC (EARLY KIMMERIDGIAN) CARBONATE MUDROCKS OF THE JAFURAH SUB-BASIN, EASTERN SAUDI ARABIA

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the petroleum generation potential, kerogen type, and thermal maturity of a late Jurassic (early Kimmeridgian) carbonate mudrock source interval in the Jafurah sub-basin. Using RockEval pyrolysis and advanced organic geochemistry on over 150 samples, it identifies significant organic richness and high hydrocarbon potential in specific lithofacies.

---

## Research Problem

The Jafurah sub-basin contains significant but under-studied Late Jurassic (Early Kimmeridgian) carbonate mudrocks with potential as an unconventional petroleum source rock. The study addresses the need for detailed characterization of their organic richness, kerodgen type, and thermal maturity.

## Objectives

- Quantitatively characterize and type the studied source formation by determining TOC and geochemical attributes.
- Identify hydrocarbon components and biomarkers to determine the origin and environment of deposition.
- Evaluate the thermal maturity of the source formation through pyrolysis and biomarker correlation.
- Integrate these geochemical results with other components of the overall project.
- Evaluate the petroleum generation potential and organic richness of the source rock interval.
- Determine the kerogen type and origin of the hydrocarbons.
- Assess the thermal maturity of the source sequence.
- Identify the depositional environment of the carbonate mudrocks.
- Evaluate organic richness and generation potential of the target source rock lithofacies
- Determine kerogen type and origin of the organic matter
- Identify the depositional environment of the carbonate mudrocks
- Assess the thermal maturity of the source-rock interval
- Quantitatively characterize the source rocks by determining TOC, geochemical attributes, and hydrocarbon distribution.
- Qualitatively characterize the source rocks by identifying hydrocarbon components and biomarkers to determine kerogen type, origin, and environment of deposition.
- Evaluate thermal maturity through pyrolysis and maturity-related biomarker correlation.

## Methods

The study utilized a multi-step analytical workflow: 1) RockEval pyrolysis on 150 samples to determine TOC, S1, S2, S3, Tmax, HI, OI, and PI. 2) Bitumen extraction using a Dionex Accelerated Solvent Extractor (ASE) 200 with dichloromethane as the primary solvent. 3) Solid Phase Extraction (SPE) of bitumen into aliphatic, aromatic, and NSO fractions using activated silica and various solvents. 4) Gas Chromatography (GC) on an Agilent 6890 system to determine Carbon Preference Index (CPI) and Pristane/Phytane (Pr/Ph) ratios. 5. Gas Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry (GC-MS) on an Agilent 6890N/5973N system for detailed biomarker analysis of the separated fractions.

## Data and Materials

More than 150 samples were analyzed from five wells within the Jafurah sub-basin. The study focused on a specific Late Jurassic (Early Kimmeridgian) carbonate mudrock interval.

## Key Findings

- Significant organic richness was identified in at least two lithofacies with very good to excellent hydrocarbon generation potential. ([[2020-usman|Usman, 2020, p. 17]])
- Hydrogen index (HI) values suggested a mixed type II/III kerogen, while n-alkane isoprenoids and biomarker analyses indicated a Type II kerogen with little to no sulfur. ([[2020-usman|Usman, 2020, p. 17]])
- Organic matter in these lithofacies corresponds to a mixed planktonic-bacterial origin deposited under anoxic to suboxic marine conditions. ([[2020-usman|Usman, 2020, p. 17]])
- Thermal maturity estimates from pyrolysis Tmax and biomarker ratios suggested a medium to high maturity source rock, analogous to the mature oil and wet-gas window. ([[2020-usman|Usman, 2020, p. 17]])
- TOC values across the five wells showed significant variation, with average values ranging from 0.41 to 2.79 mg/g (Note: text implies wt. % in Figure 13 but table shows mg/g; standard RockEval units are typically weight percent). ([[2020-usman|Usman, 2020, p. 53]])
- Most lithofacies fall into the category of mixed Type II/III and Type III kerogen, with an average HI less than 200 mgHC/gTOC. ([[2020-usman|Usman, 2020, p. 54]])
- Thermal maturity (Tmax*) for samples with TOC > 2% was found in the normal oil to condensate-wet gas window, with estimated Vitrinite reflectance (VRo) between 0.9 and 1.2%. ([[2020-usman|Usman, 2020, p. 54]])
- Non-source lithofacies and those with lower TOC values fell within the immature oil and gas window. ([[2020-usman|Usman, 2020, p. 54]])
- Gas chromatograms showed a wide distribution of n-alkanes from C11 to C35, with dominant short to medium-chain alkanes (less than C20). ([[2020-usman|Usman, 2020, p. 57]])
- Carbon Preference Index (CPI) values ranged from 0.9 to 1.4, indicating mature organic matter input. ([[2020-usman|Usman, 2020, p. 57]])
- Lithofacies 4 and 7 are characterized as good to excellent source-rock lithofacies with TOC values from 3.0 to 5.5% and average HI of 190 to 225 mgHC/gTOC. ([[2020-usman|Usman, 2020, p. 60]])
- Lithofacies 3, 5, and 9 are characterized as fair to good source-rock lithofacies with TOC values from 1.5 to 3.0% and average HI of 150 to 190 mgHC/gTOC. ([[2020-usman|Usman, 2020, p. 60]])
- Lithofacies 2, 6, 8, 10, and 11 are characterized as poor to fair source-rock lithofacies with TOC values from 0.5 to 2.0% and average HI of 130 to 140 mgHC/gTOC. ([[2020-usman|Usman, 2020, p. 60]])
- Lithofacies 1 and 12 are non-source rock lithofacies with TOC values less than 1%. ([[2020-usman|Usman, 2020, p. 60]])
- The organic matter is characterized as type II kerogen with little to no sulfur. ([[2020-usman|Usman, 2020, p. 85]])

## Limitations

Tmax values were not consistent with formation depth for samples with TOC less than 2%, requiring a secondary extraction step to obtain accurate maturity data (Tmax*).

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Organic petrography should be undertaken to directly determine vitrinite reflectance values for comparison with pyrolysis- and biomarker-derived values.
- Organic stable isotope analyses can be used to further refine the origins and environments of deposition of various source-rock lithofacies.
- SARA analysis can be used to calculate amounts of asphaltene and resins in organic matter extracts.
- Oil-to-source correlation should be performed if oil samples are recovered from the formation.
- Two-phase fluid inclusions in late diagenetic minerals (e.g., dolomite) could be used to estimate reservoir conditions.
