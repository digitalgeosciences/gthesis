# AN EXPERIMENTAL STUDY OF OIL RECOVERY FROM TARMAT RESERVOIRS USING HOT WATER AND SOLVENT FLOODING

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the effectiveness of combining solvent and hot water to improve oil recovery from tarmat reservoirs. Experimental tests using Berea sandstone core samples showed that while hot water alone is less effective than cold water in non-tar zones, it significantly improves recovery when displacing tar, especially when preceded by an optimal size solvent slug.

---

## Research Problem

Tarmat reservoirs present significant production challenges due to the high viscosity of the tar layer. The study addresses how hot-water flooding and chemical additives (reformate/naphtha) can mitigate these effects by reducing viscosity and improving mobility ratios.

## Objectives

- Investigate and evaluate the combined use of solvent and hot water to improve hydrocarbon recovery from tarmat reservoirs by displacing and dispersing the tar.
- Evaluate the effect of solvent concentration on tar viscosity.
- Evaluate the effect of temperature on interfacial tension, fluid viscosity, and relative permeabilities.
- Evaluate the impact of solvent slug size, type of solvent, and injection rate on hydrocarbon recovery.
- Assess the impact of injection rates, injection modes, and different types of solvents (reformate and naphtha) on recovery performance.
- Determine the physical properties (density, viscosity, interfacial tension) of tar and various tar-solvent mixtures.

## Methods

The study utilized a core-flooding experimental setup. Berea sandstone cores (1 inch diameter, ~23% porosity, ~300 mD permeability) were used to simulate oil, tar, and water zones. A linear composite core arrangement was used to model the sequence of solvent, tar, and oil. Fluids included 1% KCl brine, filtered kerosene (oil), and a heavy crude oil batch evaporated until reaching ~10,000 cp (tar). Two solvents, reformate and naphtha, were tested. Core flooding was performed using a Beckman model 100 A pump at rates of 1 cc/min and higher. Parameters measured included injection pressure, temperature, and fluid volumes to determine recovery and mobility.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized Berea sandstone cores (2.54 cm diameter, 100 cm length) for coreflooding experiments. Samples included tar (viscosity ~10,000 cp), reformate, naphtha, and kerosene. Experimental runs included various temperatures (25°C to 100°C) and injection rates (1, 2, and 4 cc/min).

## Key Findings

- Hot water displacement shows significantly higher recovery in the presence of tarmat compared to cold water displacement in non-tar zones. ([[1995-okasha|OKASHA, 1995, p. 10]])
- The addition of a solvent slug before hot water improves hydrocarbon recovery by displacing and dispersing tar. ([[1995-okasha|OKASHA, 1995, p. 10]])
- There is an optimal solvent slug size for each type of solvent; both very large and very small slugs are less effective than the 'optimal' size. ([[1995-okasha|OKASHA, 1995, p. 10]])
- Lower injection rates (e.g., 1 cc/min) reduced viscous fingering and water channeling, leading to higher hydrocarbon recovery in solvent-assisted runs compared to hot-water only runs. ([[1995-okasha|OKASHA, 1995, p. 149]])
- Alternating injection of small solvent and hot water slugs (Mode II) leads to higher recovery than a single large solvent slug followed by hot water. ([[1995-okasha|OKASHA, 1995, p. 10]])
- The combination of solvent and hot water reduces the required injection pressure compared to cold or hot water flooding alone. ([[1995-okasha|OKASHA, 1995, p. 10]])
- Tar viscosity is significantly reduced by the addition of solvents (reformate and naphtha). ([[1995-okasha|OKASHA, 1995, p. 40]])
- Temperature has a significant effect on the interfacial tension between aqueous and oleic phases. ([[1995-okasha|OKASHA, 1995, p. 40]])
- The effective permeability to water in the tar zone is highly sensitive to the product of tar viscosity and thickness. ([[1995-okasha|OKASHA, 1995, p. 31]])
- A large solvent slug followed by hot water was found to be more effective for homogeneous oil packs than single-mode injection. ([[1995-okasha|OKASHA, 1995, p. 34]])
- Reformate was identified as the most suitable solvent for tar displacement, showing low insolubles (less than 3% of tar) in solubility tests. ([[1995-okasha|OKASHA, 1995, p. 58]])
- The density of tar and its mixtures varies linearly with temperature, allowing for accurate estimation using a linear equation (rho = a - b(T)). ([[1995-okasha|OKASHA, 1995, p. 63]])
- Tar exhibits Newtonian behavior as shear stress is directly proportional to shear rate across all tested temperatures. ([[1995-okasha|OKASHA, 1995, p. 71]])
- The dynamic viscosity of tar decreases significantly with increasing temperature, from approximately 10,332 cp at 20°C to 11 cp at 120°C. ([[1995-okasha|OKASHA, 1995, p. 86]])
- The Shu correlation predicted the dynamic viscosity of tar-solvent mixtures with an average error of less than 6% across various temperatures and concentrations. ([[1995-okasha|OKASHA, 1995, p. 93]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Investigate recovery from tarmat reservoirs using a radial flow model of consolidated rock matrix (limestone or sandstone).
- Study the effect of viscosity of natural tar and tar thickness on recovery using radial flow models.
- Analyze effluent productions to determine percentages of oil, tar, solvent, and water.
- Investigate recovery under actual reservoir conditions of temperature and pressure.
- Conduct a feasibility study of employing thermal-miscible techniques.
