# NOVEL ENVIRONMENTALLY FRIENDLY NANOMATERIALS FOR DRAG REDUCTION OF EMULSIFIED ACID SYSTEM (EAS)

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the development and character_characterization of Pickering Emulsified Acid Systems (PEAS) using various organoclays as emulsifiers. It evaluates their thermal stability, rheological properties, and drag reduction capabilities compared to traditional surfactant-based emulsified acid systems.

---

## Research Problem

Conventional acidizing fluids in carbonate reservoirs suffer from high acidity, corrosion at elevated temperatures, and rapid reaction rates with scale/particles. While Emulsified Acid Systems (EAS) address these issues by using an oil phase as a barrier, they often exhibit high viscosity and significant drag in pipes, limiting pumping rates and depth of penetration.

## Objectives

- Implement an engineered retarded acid system with low viscosity by developing effective emulsified HCl acid blends.
- Evaluate the use of carbon nanodots (CNDs) as viscosity-reducers and drag-reducing agents (DRAs) in commercial EAS formulations.
- Develop a new Pickering emulsified acid system using organoclay (OC) as an emulsifier to reduce drag.
- Utilize environmentally friendly and widely available nanomaterials as drag-reducing agents (DRA) of EAS.
- Investigate the use of carbon nanodots (CNDs) as viscosity reducers and DRA for the EAS prepared to stimulate carbonate reservoirs.
- Evaluate the compatibility, thermal stability, and rheological properties of CNDs in an EAS formulation.

## Methods

The study involved preparing PEAS in large batches (30-40 liters) with a 70:30 acid-to-diesel ratio. The emulsifier (OC) was added to diesel and mixed using a high-speed homogenizer at 10,000 RPM for 7-8 minutes. Acid concentration was adjusted to 15% and 20%. A flow loop (drag reduction unit) equipped with a gear pump, pressure transducer, and heating system was used to measure pressure drop against flow rates across various temperatures (25, 40, 50 °C). Post-flow tests included conductivity, pH measurement, drop test, rheology (using an Anton-Paar MCR-702 Rheometer), and visual stability checks.

## Data and Materials

Materials include lab-grade HCl (37%), industrial-grade HCl (26%), deionized water, commercial diesel, and various carbon nanodots (CNDs) and organoclays (OC). Experimental parameters include acid concentrations of 15%, 20%, and 25% HCl; temperatures of 25, 40, and 50°C; and OC concentrations from 250 to 1000 ppm.

## Key Findings

- Adding CNDs to the commercial EAS formulation reduces both viscosity and drag in pipes without affecting conductivity or thermal stability. ([[2023-al-dogail|AL-DOGAIL, 2023, p. 18]])
- CNDs function as both viscosity reducers and drag-reducing agents (DRAs) for the emulsified acid system. ([[2023-al-dogail|AL-DOGAIL, 2023, p. 18]])
- Pickering EAS exhibits lower drag compared to normal EAS (using surfactant as an emulsifier), even when CNDs are added to the normal EAS. ([[2023-al-dogail|AL-DOGAIL, 2023, p. 18]])
- A Pickering emulsion is characterized by being stabilized by solid particles like silica and clay, offering improved thermal stability. ([[2023-al-dogail|AL-DOGAIL, 2023, p. 24]])
- Organoclay (OC) serves as both an emulsifier for preparing Pickering emulsions and a drag-reducing agent (DRA). ([[2023-al-dogail|AL-DOGAIL, 2023, p. 23]])
- A drag reduction of up to 65% was achieved by adding only a small amount (10-15 ppm) of DRP in oil-water systems. ([[2023-al-dogail|AL-DOGAIL, 2023, p. 43]])
- The maximum value of the coefficient of friction reduction (more than 60%) is attained by adding 4 gm XC polymer NPs. ([[2023-al-dogail|AL-DOGAIL, 2023, p. 37]])
- A drag reduction of up to 63% was observed in a small pipe (1.27 cm) compared to 48% in a larger pipe (3.81 cm). ([[2023-al-dogail|AL-DOGAIL, 2023, p. 43]])
- CNDs were found to be effective viscosity reducers for the EAS, reducing viscosity at two different HCl concentrations (15% and 20%). ([[2023-al-dogail|AL-DOGAIL, 2023, p. 71]])
- The addition of CNDs to the EAS showed compatibility with field formulations, including corrosion inhibitors and other additives. ([[2023-al-dogail|AL-DOGAIL, 2023, p. 71]])
- Drag reduction was observed across the entire range of temperatures investigated in the study. ([[2023-al-dogail|AL-DOGAIL, 2023, p. 71]])

- The addition of CNDs reduced the viscosity of the EAS at various conditions (15% and 20% HCl) while having a negligible effect on pH, conductivity, and thermal stability. ([[2023-al-dogail|AL-DOGAIL, 2023, p. 86]])
- At a flow rate of 10 L/min, the drag reduction by CNDs was ~2 psi (approx. 13,790 Pa), and at 12 L/min it was ~3 psi (approx. 20,685 Pa). ([[2023-al-dogail|AL-DOGAIL, 2023, p. 98]])
- The addition of CNDs reduced the drag even at high temperatures (40°C and 50°C), although the magnitude of reduction decreased as temperature increased. ([[2023-al-dogail|AL-DOGAIL, 2023, p. 98]])

## Limitations

The thesis notes that while some studies address drag reduction in general emulsions, these results cannot be directly translated to the drag reduction of an emulsified acid system (EAS) due to the specific chemical interactions and stability requirements of acids.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future research should focus on characterizing nanomaterials at the molecular level and identifying functional groups to better understand their interaction with acid systems. Advanced experimental methods like particle velocimetry, laser Doppler, and velocity profiling are recommended to study the mechanism of drag reduction.
