# Spontaneous Ignition Characteristics of a Super-Light Oil

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the spontaneous ignition characteristics of super-light crude oil by analyzing its thermal behavior (TGA, DTA, DTG) under various conditions including different crude types, oxygen concentrations, and water saturation. It concludes that super-light oil does not pose a risk of spontaneous ignition due to its low asphaltene content and lower heat release during the LTO phase.

---

## Research Problem

A field in central Saudi Arabia producing super-light oil faces sand production issues. The proposed solution, using LTO to create a coke-like residue for sand consolidation, carries a risk of spontaneous ignition if the crude oil is highly reactive; thus, the study aims to determine the conditions under which this specific oil might ignite.

## Objectives

- Explore the spontaneous ignition potential and characteristics of the super-light crude oil from central Saudi Arabia.
- Correlate ignition characteristics with reservoir and process conditions.
- To evaluate the effect of different crude oil types (varying in asphaltene content) on heat release during LTO.
- To assess the impact of oxygen concentration on the oxidation rate and potential for spontaneous ignition.
- To investigate the influence of water saturation on the thermal behavior of the oil/sand mixture.

## Methods

The study utilized five different oxidation reactors (varying in size and material) to monitor sand pack temperature and oxygen concentration. Thermal analysis techniques, specifically Thermogravimetric Analysis (TGA) and Differential Thermal Analysis (DTA), were used to characterize the thermal stability and chemical reactions of the oil. Sieve analysis was performed on field-produced sand and white sand to determine grain size distribution.

## Data and Materials

32 oxidation runs were conducted; 23 were reported. Materials included three types of crude oils (Oil #1: 52° API, Oil #2: 18° API, Oil #3: 22° API) and two types of porous media (field-produced sand with clay content and white sand from the Biyadh formation).

## Key Findings

- The super-light oil (Oil #1) did not show any characteristics of spontaneous ignition after 8 to 26 hours of oxidation in various reactor configurations. ([[1999-iddris|Iddris, 1999, p. 39]])
- Thermal analysis showed that low-temperature oxidation (LTO) begins at 170 °C and peaks at 270 °C, while high-temperature oxidation (HTO) begins at 380 °C and peaks at 415 °C. ([[1999-iddris|Iddris, 1999, p. 15]])
- The lack of asphaltenes in the super-light oil was identified as the primary reason for its low heat generation during LTO, leading to a lower risk of spontaneous ignition. ([[1999-iddris|Iddris, 1999, p. 15]])
- In Reactor #2, a blend of 90% Oil #1 and 10% Oil #2 (higher asphaltene content) showed an initial temperature increase of 3 °C and a drop in oxygen concentration by 11%, but the trend reversed after three hours. ([[1999-iddris|Iddris, 1999, p. 42]])
- The presence of residual water saturation (33.8% brine) did not affect the rate of oxidation or spontaneous ignition potential in Reactor #3. ([[1999-iddris|Iddris, 1999, p. 44]])
- In the largest reactor (Reactor #5), a temperature rise of up to 14 °C and an oxygen drop of 34% were observed within 90 minutes, but these did not lead to sustained ignition. ([[1999-iddris|Iddris, 1999, p. 56]])
- All three crude oils (Oil #1, #2, and #3) exhibited the same LTO peak temperature of 262 °C. ([[1999-iddris|Iddris, 1999, p. 71]])
- Oil #2 released approximately twice as much heat (110 micro-volts) at the LTO peak compared to Oil #1 (55 micro-volts), attributed to its higher asphaltene content (4.1% vs 0.18%). ([[1999-iddris|Iddris, 1999, p. 71]])
- Oxidation in enriched air (40% oxygen) resulted in a much higher differential temperature at the LTO peak (145 micro-volts) compared to ordinary air (55 micro-volts). ([[1999-iddris|Iddris, 1999, p. 75]])
- The presence of initial water saturation did not significantly affect the rate of oxidation or the LTO and HTO onset and peak temperatures. ([[1999-iddris|Iddris, 1999, p. 75]])
- Spontaneous ignition was not achieved on the super-light crude, Oil #1, under any of the tested conditions. ([[1999-iddris|Iddris, 1999, p. 80]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study suggests that air injection into a super-light oil reservoir will likely not pose a risk of spontaneous ignition; however, it notes that the suitability of such oil as a sand consolidation medium requires further investigation.
