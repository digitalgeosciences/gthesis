# Evaluating the Effect of Nanoclay and Nanosilica on the Properties of Water-Based Drilling Fluid

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of adding nanoclay and nanosilica to a calcium carbonate water-based drilling fluid (WBM) to improve its stability, rheological properties, and filtration resistance under high-pressure and high-temperature (HPHT) conditions. The study concludes that while nanosilica successfully improved gel strength at 200°F, nanoclay did not enhance the rheology and actually decreased it.

---

## Research Problem

The primary problem is the 'sticking' of drill pipes due to cuttings accumulation during periods of no circulation, which occurs when drilling fluids lack sufficient gel strength at temperatures above 120°F. The study aims to determine if nanoclay or nanosilica can improve these rheological properties.

## Objectives

- Study the stability of the drilling fluids with nanomaterials by using zeta potential.
- Assess the rheological properties of the drilling fluids with nanoparticles by using HPHT rheometer.
- Evaluate the possibility of formation damage by measuring the removal efficiency and retained permeability of the drilling fluid by using HPHT filter press.
- Compare the rheological properties and gel strength of water-based drilling fluid with different concentrations (1, 3, 5, 7.5, 10 wt%) of nanoparticles (nanoclay and nanosilica) without bentonite at various temperatures.
- Determine the optimal concentration of additives to maintain stable gel strength and manage sticking issues in high-temperature environments.
- Evaluate the stability of barite, calcium carbonate, and nanoclay using zeta potential measurements across a range of pH values.

## Methods

The study utilized several analytical techniques: Zeta potential was measured to determine the stability of colloidal suspensions (barite, calcium carbonate, and bentonite) with varying concentrations of nanosilica and nanoclay across a pH range of 2-12. XRF was used to determine the chemical composition of additives. FTIR and NMR were used to identify functional groups and organic molecules in the materials. Rheological properties (apparent viscosity, yield point, and gel strength) were measured using an HPHT rheometer at temperatures of room temperature, 120°F, 150°F, and 200°F. Filtration tests were conducted using an HPHT filter press to evaluate fluid loss and formation damage potential. Removal efficiency was calculated based on weight differences before and after treatment with a chelating agent (GLDA).

## Data and Materials

The study utilized multiple concentrations of nanoclay (1%, 3%, 5%) and nanosilica (1%, 3%, 5%, 7.5%, 10%). Testing was conducted at four temperature points: 85°F, 120°f, 150°F, and 200°F. Data were collected for gel strength over a 30-minute period and plastic viscosity/yield point across shear rates up to approximately 800 s⁻¹.

## Key Findings

- Nanosilica added at 7.5 wt% improved the gel strength of the base drilling fluid at 200 F after 30 minutes, where the base fluid was otherwise unstable. ([[2017-alakbari|Alakbari, 2017, p. 11]])
- Adding nanoclay to the base drilling fluid did not enhance rheological properties and instead decreased them. ([[2017-alakbari|Alakbari, 2017, p. 11]])
- The isoelectric point for barite (40-75 μm) was found at pH=3, while for barite (75-95 μm) it was at pH=3.5. ([[2017-alakbari|Alakbari, 2017, p. 26]])
- The isoelectric point for nanosilica was determined to be at pH=1.47. ([[2017-alakbari|Alakbari, 2017, p. 29]])
- Nanosilica with a 1:1 concentration (0.25 wt% barite and 0.25 wt% nanosilica) was not stable at any pH value, indicating that the stability of barite is affected by the concentration of nanosilica. ([[2017-alakbari|Alakbari, 2017, p. 39]])
- The average size of the first barite sample ranged from 75 to 95 μm, while the second was smaller (mean of 35 to 45 μm). ([[2017-alakbari|Alakbari, 2017, p. 37]])
- The HPHT rheometer used for testing could operate up to 1000 psi and 500 . ([[2017-alakbari|Alakbari, 2017, p. 32]])
- The HPHT filter press was capable of operating at up to 1800 psig on the cell and 750 psig on the back pressure. ([[2017-alakbari|Alakbari, 2017, p. 34]])
- The addition of nanoclay to barite resulted in a change in the isoelectric point, with the presence of high electronegativity elements like Chlorine reducing the zeta potential. ([[2017-alakbari|Alakbari, 2017, p. 44]])
- Nanosilica was found to be stable at pH 11.5; however, adding KCl and NaCl reduced its stability across all pH values. ([[2017-alakbari|Alakbari, 2017, p. 51]])
- The isoelectric point of CaCO3 is at pH=9.5, while the addition of nanoclay shifted it to pH=10.5. ([[2017-alakbari|Alakbari, 2017, p. 47]])

- Bentonite was found stable between pH 5-12; its low electronegativity values resulted in a large negative zeta potential. ([[2017-alakbari|Alakbari, 2017, p. 53]])
- At 200°F, the base drilling fluid had zero gel strength after 30 minutes, which is considered dangerous for operations as it leads to cuttings accumulation. ([[2017-alakbari|Alakbari, 2017, p. 47]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Investigate stability using zeta potential and rheological properties for both nanosilica and nanoclay with drilling fluid additives.
- Investigate rheological properties and filtration measurements of the drilling fluid using a formula without starch.
- Investigate rheological properties and filtration measurements of the drilling fluid using different particle sizes of calcium carbonate.
- Investigate stability using zeta potential and rheological properties of different nanoparticles with drilling fluid additives.
