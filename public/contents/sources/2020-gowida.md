# ACCELERATED LOW-TEMPERATURE OXIDATION FOR SAND CONSOLIDATION AND PRODUCTION CONTROL

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the use of low-temperature oxidation (LTO) of hydrocarbon materials to consolidate loose sand formations and improve their compressive strength while maintaining permeability. It specifically explores using thermochemicals to generate in-situ heat to accelerate the LTO process, which is typically slow at lower reservoir temperatures.

---

## Research Problem

Sand production in poorly consolidated formations requires effective sand control methods. Traditional LTO processes can be time-consuming; this research explores using thermochemical reactions to provide rapid in-situ heat to accelerate the conversion of asphaltene into a stable coke-like residue for faster and more efficient consolidation.

## Objectives

- Investigate the optimum oxidation temperature required for effective and stable sand consolidation process.
- Investigate the optimum oxidation time needed for producing sustainable consolidated sand packs.
- Investigate the ability of the thermochemicals to provide the required heat needed to accelerate the LTO reaction, which eventually results in a consolidated sand-pack.
- Evaluate the quality of the consolidated sand-packs in terms of its compressive strength and the retained permeability.
- Investigate the stability and sustainability of the consolidated sand matrix against aromatic solvents, acid, and formation fluids.
- Optimize the heating requirements for the oxidation process (LTO) to achieve effective sand consolidation.

## Methods

The study was divided into three phases: 1. Oxidation Temperature Optimization: Testing sand packs at 90, 135, and 180 °C for 10 hours to determine the best temperature for LTO. 2. Oxidation Time Optimization: Testing samples at 180 °C for 3, 6, and 10 hours to find the minimum time for stable consolidation. 3. Thermochemical Application: Using a specialized insulated cell to inject thermochemicals (ammonium chloride and sodium nitrite) to generate in-situ heat, followed by air injection to facilitate LTO. Evaluation included permeability measurements using Darcy's law, scratch tests via a WOMBAT machine, and stability tests against toluene, mud acid, and crude oil.

## Data and Materials

Laboratory-prepared sand packs (coarse, medium, and fine) with a mean grain size of 0.274 mm; two types of consolidating fluids (Fluid 1: 40% asphaltene in toluene; Fluid 2: 40% asphaltene in xylene); thermochemical reagents (ammonium chloride and sodium nitrite); and various testing fluids (3% KCl brine, mud acid, crude oil).

## Key Findings

- Optimal oxidation conditions were found to be 180 °C and 6 hours of air flow, resulting in a compressive strength exceeding 1800 psi and a retained permeability of 2.8 Darcy. ([[2020-gowida|Gowida, 2020, p. 17]])
- By injecting thermochemicals, the sand-pack temperature was successfully raised from 80 to 188 °C, yielding a consolidated sand pack with 1490 psi compressive strength and 3.3 Darcy of retained permeability. ([[2020-gowida|Gowida, 2020, p. 17]])
- The thermochemical reaction between NH4Cl and NaNO2 is highly exothermic (ΔH = -369 kJ/mol at 25 °C) and can be used to generate in-situ heat for LTO. ([[2020-gowida|Gowida, 2020, p. 33]])
- LTO of hydrocarbon material results in a coke-like residue that binds sand grains, providing high compressive strength and high permeability retention. ([[2020-gowida|Gowida, 2020, p. 22]])
- The reaction rate for the thermochemical process is third-order with respect to concentration and follows the Arrhenius equation, being highly dependent on temperature and acid catalysis. ([[2020-gowida|Gowida, 2020, p. 35]])
- Oxidation temperature significantly impacts compressive strength; Sample 3 at 180 °C reached a strength of 2034 psi, while Samples 1 and 2 at lower temperatures failed toluene stability tests. ([[2020-gowida|Gowida, 2020, p. 60]])
- A minimum oxidation time of 6 hours at 180 °C was determined to be sufficient for converting asphaltene into coke-residue and ensuring stable sand pack integrity. ([[2020-gowida|Gowida, 2020, p. 67]])
- The use of xylene instead of toluene in the consolidating fluid preparation had no significant effect on strength, permeability, or stability. ([[2020-gowida|Gowida, 2020, p. 102]])
- In-situ heating via thermochemicals (TCs) successfully reached temperatures of approximately 188 °C to accelerate LTO. ([[2020-gowida|Gowida, 2020, p. 75]])
- Sample 7, treated with TCs, showed a permeability of 3.277 D and a compressive strength of 1490 psi, demonstrating the viability of the method. ([[2020-gowida|Gowida, 2020, p. 78]])
- The inclusion of thermochemicals resulted in lower residual saturation of consolidating fluid (23.41% vs 29.93%) compared to air-bath heating, potentially due to more efficient displacement by TCs reaction products. ([[2020-gowida|Gowida, 2020, p. 77]])
- FTIR analysis confirmed that Sample 5 (3 hours oxidation) had insufficient conversion of asphaltene to coke, showing a distinct shift in the toluene's chemical fingerprint. ([[2020-gowida|Gowida, 2020, p. 68]])

## Limitations

The study used laboratory-prepared sand packs rather than actual reservoir cores; some differences in results between TCs and air-bath heating may be due to lower air flow rates or different residence times for the oxidation process.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Integrate metallic additives like ferric nitrate with TCs to further accelerate consolidation.
- Investigate sand packs with varying grain size distributions.
- Utilize actual sandstone cores from the field to validate laboratory findings.
