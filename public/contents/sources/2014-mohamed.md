# DEVELOPMENT OF EMULSIFIED GELS FOR WATER CONTROL IN OIL AND GAS WELLS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the development of emulsified polyacrylamide (PAM) and polyethylenimine (PEI) gels for water control in oil and gas wells under high temperature and high salinity conditions. It establishes a screening method based on the hydrophilic lipophilic balance (HLB) to select surfactants and evaluates the impact of droplet size, concentration, and temperature on gel stability.

---

## Research Problem

Produced water in oil and gas wells causes issues such as loss of productivity, corrosion, scale, and environmental concerns. While polymer gels are effective for water shut-off, they require careful formulation to ensure the gel remains stable during transport but breaks quickly enough at the target zone to allow only the water phase to gel.

## Objectives

- Develop an emulsified PAM/PEI system for high temperature and high salinity applications.
- Establish a scientific approach based on the HLB of surfactants to screen candidates for water-in-oil (W/O) emulsions.
- Evaluate organoclay as a potential substitute for traditional surfactants in these systems.
- Investigate the relationship between emulsified system droplet size and thermal stability.
- Study gel strength and gelation time using dynamic rheology.
- Investigate the possibility of utilizing Organoclay (OC) materials in forming stable water-in-oil emulsions for high temperature and high salinity applications.

## Methods

The study utilized several experimental stages: 1) Surfactant screening based on HLB values to identify suitable candidates for W/O emulsions. 2) Conductivity and dilution tests were used to determine the internal/external phases of the mixtures. 3) Thermal stability was assessed by monitoring phase separation in high-temperature test tubes (up to 120°C). 4) Dynamic rheology was employed to measure gel strength and gelation time for PAM/PEI systems. 5) Samples were prepared using a high performance dispersing instrument (Ultra-Turrax T 50 Basic) at specific RPMs and durations.

## Data and Materials

Two polymer solutions: Polyacrylamide (PAM, 20 wt% active, MW 250-500 kg/mol) and Polyethylenimine (PEI, 30 wt% active, MW 70 kg/mol). Surfactants: Organoclay (up to 1000 ppm) and Ethoxylated Amides-2. Water phases: Formation brine (TDS 221,673 mg/L), Sea water (TDS 57,642 mg/L), and Field Mixing Water (TDS 1,122 mg/L). Diesel was used as the hydrocarbon phase.

## Key Findings

- Emulsification was found to slow down cross-linking rate and reduce elastic modulus by approximately 54%. ([[2014-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2014, p. 14]])
- The gel strength of the emulsified PAM/PEI system increased by about 32% when temperature rose from 120°C to 150°C. ([[2014-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2014, p. 14]])
- A screening method based on HLB successfully identified surfactants capable of forming stable W/O emulsions in high salinity (up to 221,673 ppm) and temperature (120°C). ([[2014-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2014, p. 23]])
- Glycolic Acid Ethoxylate-2 Ether was identified as a suitable candidate for W/O emulsions based on its HLB value of 7.14. ([[2014-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2014, p. 30]])
- Amine Acetates-3 (HLB 6.8) produced flowable emulsions at low concentrations (0.5% and 0.2%) suitable for specific applications requiring fast release of the oil phase. ([[2014-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2014, p. 45]])
- Emulsions with smaller droplet sizes were found to be more stable than those with larger droplets. ([[2014-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2014, p. 21]])
- The presence of salts was established to decrease the elastic modulus (gel strength). ([[2014-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2014, p. 14]])
- Droplet size decreased from 467.67 to 61.65 µm (an 87% reduction) when mixing speed increased from 500 to 1500 rpm. ([[2014-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2014, p. 92]])
- Emulsion stability increased with higher mixing intensity; at 2000 rpm, no separation occurred compared to 18% diesel separation at 500 rpm. ([[2014-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2014, p. 92]])
- The inclusion of a cross-linker (PEI) and the use of Organoclay as an emulsifier provided stable emulsions for high temperature (120°C) and high salinity (221,673 ppm). ([[2014-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2014]])

- Emulsification slows down the cross-linking process; for example, a stable emulsified system showed an elastic modulus of 715 Pa compared to 749 Pa in non-emulsified systems (a ~5% difference). ([[2014-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2014, p. 108]])
- If the emulsified system breaks into its components within one hour, the elastic modulus decreases significantly from 746 Pa to 343.6 Pa (~54% decrease). ([[2014-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2014, p. 123]])
- No significant difference in elastic modulus was observed when switching between Ethomeen T/12 and ANSC (CRV) as emulsifiers. ([[2014-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2014, p. 124]])
- The elastic modulus of the emulsified PAM/PEI system increased by approximately 32% (from 703 Pa to 1035 Pa) when temperature was raised from 120°C to 150°C. ([[2014-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2014, p. 131]])
