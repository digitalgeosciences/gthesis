# LITHOLOGICAL CHARACTERISTICS OF GLACIAL DEPOSITIONAL ENVIRONMENT AND ITS CONTROLS ON RESERVOIR PROPERTIES

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the lithological characteristics and reservoir properties of Late Ordovician glacial deposits in the Rub' al-Khali Basin. Through petrographic analysis, XRD, and SEM-EDS on 35 thin sections from three wells (A, B, and C), it identifies seven distinct lithofacies and concludes that while the overall reservoir quality is low due to heavy cementation and poor sorting, specific intervals show improved porosity through feldspar dissolution.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the complexity of glacial depositional environments in the Late Ordovician period within the Rub' al-Khali Basin, specifically focusing on how lithofacies, mineralogy, and diagenetic processes (like silica/carbonate cementation) impact reservoir quality in these glaciogenic successions.

## Objectives

- Characterize the lithofacies and depositional environments.
- Evaluate glacial deposits heterogeneity and properties.
- Construct a predictive depositional model for the study area.
- Identify and characterize glacial lithofacies in the study area.
- Perform petrographic analysis to determine mineralogy, texture, and diagenetic history of the sandstone samples.
- Evaluate the impact of mineralogical composition and cementation on reservoir properties (porosity and permeability).

## Methods

The study utilized core analysis of six subsurface cores from three wells (Well-A, Well-B, Well-C). Analytical techniques included thin-section petrography (300 point-count modal analysis), X-ray diffraction (XRD) for bulk mineralogy, and Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) with energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) to characterize minerals, textures, and cementation. Core descriptions were based on lithology, grain size, sorting, sedimentary structures, and mineral content.

## Data and Materials

6 subsurface cores from 3 wells; 35 samples selected for thin section analysis; core data spanning approximately 70-80 feet per well; XRD analysis of 2g powder samples; SEM analysis of prepared mounts.

## Key Findings

- The study identified seven distinct lithofacies: fissile shale, massive sandstone, parallel laminated sandstone, low angle laminated sandstone, high angle laminated sandstone, soft sediment deformed sandstone, and very poorly pebbly to granule sandstone. ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 18]])
- The dominant lithology is sandstone (fine to medium sized) with moderately to well sorted detrital grains, primarily classified as sub-Arkoses, Arenite, and Arkosic-wacke. ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 18]])
- Reservoir quality in these systems is complex; visual porosity is enhanced by the dissolution of detrital feldspar grains and by structural features like fracturing. ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 18]])
- Porosity reduction (tight deposits) is attributed to variable grain size, high grain packing, and heavy cementation from silica, calcite, dolomite, anhydrite, and clay. ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 20]])
- Well-B is dominated by Massive Sandstone (75.89%) and includes a significant portion of Low Angle Laminated Sandstone (12.24%). ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 59]])
- Well-C contains high proportions of Massive Sandstone (44.15%) and Low Angle Laminated Sandstone (15.58%). ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 69]])
- Fissile Shale (FS) is identified as a potential marine sediment, possibly reworked from the Qasim marine shale. ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 76]])
- Cobble-Pebble Grained Sandstone (CP) is interpreted as being formed by the erosion of glacier high-pressure melt-water flows. ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 76]])
- Seven distinct lithofacies were identified: Fissile shale (FS), Massive sandstone (MS), Parallel laminated sandstone (PLS), Low angle interbedded sandstone (LA), High angle laminated sandstone (HAPS), Soft sediments deformation (SSD), and Cobble-pebble grained (CP). ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 125]])
- The dominant lithology is Subarkosic sandstone, with some instances of Arenite and Arkosic-wacke. ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 123]])
- Clay minerals are primarily illite (average 1.20-8.10% in Well A) and chlorite (average 0.50-4.10% in Well A), with kaolinite being rare. ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 97]])
- Carbonate cementation (calcite and dolomite) and silica cementation are the primary factors, along with compaction, that degrade reservoir quality. ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 123]])
- Reservoir quality is generally low due to high grain packing and heavy cementations, but some intervals show enhanced porosity (fair) due to dissolution of detrital feldspar grains. ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 126]])
- Well-A samples are predominantly Subarkosic (Q80.81 F17.29 L1.88), while Sample 27 is an Arkosic-wacke. ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 84]])
- Well-B samples show a range of mineralogy, with one sample (59) classified as Sandstone Arenite (Q93.26 F4.16 L2.56). ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 87]])

## Limitations

The absence of fossils in the core samples makes it challenging to assign specific depositional environments directly, requiring reliance on sedimentological structures and geometry.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The author suggests additional geological, petrophysical, and geochemical analyses from different localities (both subsurface and adjacent outcrop sections) to better understand the Ordovician glaciation period.
