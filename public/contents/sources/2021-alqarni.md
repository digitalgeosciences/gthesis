# LITHOLOGICAL CHARACTERISTICS OF GLACIAL DEPOSITIONAL ENVIRONMENT AND ITS CONTROLS ON RESERVOIR PROPERTIES

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the lithological characteristics and diagenetic impacts on reservoir properties of glacial depositional environments in an Ordovician section. Using thin-section petrography, XRD, and SEM-EDS, it characterizes the mineralogy, cementation, and clay content to evaluate how these factors influence porosity and permeability.

---

## Research Problem

Glacial deposits in the Late Ordovician of the Arabian Plate are complex and heterogeneous; understanding their lithofacies and the diagenetic/sedimentary processes that control reservoir properties is essential for better characterization and production planning.

## Objectives

- Characterize the lithofacies and depositional environments.
- Evaluate glacial deposits heterogeneity and properties.
- Construct a predictive depositional model for the study area.
- Investigate examples of glacial lithofacies deposits to understand their geological setting.
- Characterize the sedimentary texture, mineralogy, and diagenetic history of the Sarah formation.
- Evaluate the impact of glacial depositional environments on reservoir properties (porosity and permeability).
- Develop a conceptual depositional model for the study area.
- Identify and characterize lithofacies within the glacial deposits.
- Determine the impact of lithology, grain size, sorting, and cementation on reservoir quality.
- Establish a depositional model for the glacial paleovalley in the study area.
- Investigate examples of glacial lithofacies deposits to understand their role in hydrocarbon and groundwater reservoirs.
- Characterize the mineralogy and texture of glacial sediments using petrography, XRD, and SEM-EDS.
- Evaluate the impact of diagenetic processes (compaction, cementation, and clay formation) on reservoir properties.
- Identify and characterize the lithofacies of the studied paleovalley glacial deposits.
- Perform petrographical analysis to determine mineralogy and diagenetic processes.
- Evaluate the impact of mineralogy and texture on reservoir properties (porosity and permeability).
- Determine the depositional environment based on sedimentary structures and facies geometry.

## Methods

The methodology involves core description of six subsurface cores from three wells, followed by laboratory analysis. Petrographic characterization includes thin section preparation (including grinding, lapping, mounting, trimming, and sectioning) and optical microscopy. Mineralogy is quantified using bulk rock and clay X-ray diffraction (XRD). Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) is used to examine textures and mineral distribution. Sedimentological analysis involves identifying facies associations and depositional environments based on physical properties, sedimentary structures, mud content, and diagenetic features.

## Data and Materials

231 ft. (70.26m) of core data from 6 cores across 3 wells; multiple thin section samples prepared for mineralogical and textural analysis; XRD powder samples from each core; SEM-EDS analysis on selected rock samples.

## Key Findings

- The study identified seven lithofacies: fissile shale, massive sandstone, parallel laminated sandstone, low angle laminated sandstone, high angle laminated sandstone, soft sediment deformed sandstone, and very poorly pebbly to granule sandstone. ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 17]])
- The dominant lithology is sandstone (fine to medium size) with moderately to well sorted detrital grains, classified predominantly as sub-Arkoses, with some sections as Arenite or Arkosic-wacke. ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 18]])
- Note: The study identifies that visual porosity is significantly enhanced by the dissolution of detrital feldspar grains and in some intervals by structural features like glacial rock deformation and fracturing. ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021]])
- page_num_placeholder_for_finding_3_above_is_not_explicitly_linked_to_one_page_but_found_in_abstract_and_intro_sections_17-18 ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021]])
- The Sarah formation is characterized by a variety of lithofacies including Low Angle Laminated Sandstone (LA), Massive Sandstone (MS), Fissile Shale (FS), Parallel Laminated Sandstone (PLS), Soft Sediment Deformation Sandstone (SSD), Cobble-Pebble Grained Sandstone (CP), and High Angle Laminated Sandstone (HAPS). ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 45]])
- Low Angle Laminated Sandstone (LA) forms 36.66% of the logged interval in Well-A, characterized as light gray, fine grained, and silica-rich. ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 45]])
- Massive Sandstone (MS) forms 33.33% of the logged interval in Well-A, featuring a range from very fine to pebbly grains with heavy silica cementation. ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 48]])
- Fissile Shale (FS) forms 10% of the logged interval and is rich with massive pyrite framboids. ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 47]])
- The Sarah formation thickness varies significantly due to paleotopography, ranging from approximately 30 m to over 300 m in large palaeovalleys. ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 31]])
- Glacial deposits in the Arabian Plate are identified as a complex sequence of glacial and periglacial deposits including tillites and proglacial sandstone. ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 31]])
- Seven distinct lithofacies were identified: Massive sandstone (MS), Parallel laminated sandstone (PLS), High angle laminated sandstone (HAPS), Low angle interbedded sandstone (LA), Soft sediments deformation (SSD), Fissile shale (FS), and Cobble-pebble grained (CP). ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 79]])
- Well-A samples were predominantly classified as Subarkosic sandstone (Q80.81 F17.29 L1.88) with grain-supported textures. ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 84]])
- Well-B samples were primarily Subarkosic (Q81.28 F7.865 L1.03), except for one sample classified as Sandstone Arenite (Q93.26 F4.16 L2.56). ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 87]])
- Reservoir quality in the study area is generally low, with some intervals reaching fair quality due to dissolution of detrital feldspar grains and structural enhancements like fracturing. ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 82]])
- The primary factors controlling poor reservoir quality are identified as sorting, moderate-to-high grain packing, and cementation (carbonate and silica). ([[2021-alqarni|Alqarni, 2021, p. 82]])

## Limitations

The absence of fossils in the core samples made it challenging to assign specific depositional environments without relying on structural and geometric data.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The author suggests that the heterogeneity of glacial successions can be better understood through comprehensive sedimentology studies across multiple paleovalleys and by building geostatistical models to map three-dimensional relationships.
