# REACTION KINETICS OF NOVEL EMULSIFIED ACID SYSTEM WITH LIMESTONE

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the reaction kinetics and diffusion coefficients of a novel waste oil emulsified acid system compared to conventional diesel emulsified acid in limestone. Using rotating disk experiments (RDE) and core-flood tests under HPHT conditions, it evaluates dissolution rates, wormhole formation, and the impact of oil saturation on acid transport.

---

## Research Problem

The lack of data on the reaction kinetics of novel waste oil emulsified acid systems at high pressure and temperature (HPHT), as well as the inaccuracy of using low-pressure/temperature (LPLT) data for deep reservoir designs.

## Objectives

- Evaluate the reactivity of the new emulsified acid (Waste oil emulsified acid) system with limestone.
- Determine the reaction rate and diffusion coefficient (De) of conventional diesel emulsified acid at HPHT conditions (135°C, 3000 psi).
- Investigate the effect of oil saturation on the emulsified acid and limestone reaction kinetics.
- Evaluate the performance of a novel waste oil emulsified acid system compared to conventional systems using core-flood experiments.
- Determine the optimal injection rates for different acid systems to achieve wormhole breakthrough.

## Methods

The study utilized two primary experimental setups: Rotating Disk Apparatus (RDA) and HPHT Core-flood. RDA was used to determine dissolution rates and diffusion coefficients by measuring calcium concentration changes in samples taken at 2-minute intervals over a period of 20 minutes across various rotational speeds (200–1500 rpm). ICP-OES was used for chemical analysis of the acid. Core-flooding involved Indiana limestone cores subjected to 3000 psi and 135°C, where pressure drop profiles were monitored to identify wormhole propagation. CT-scanning and Pregeos software were used to visualize and quantify wormhole volumes. Rheological properties (K and n) of the emulsified acids were determined using power-law models.

## Data and Materials

The study used limestone samples from local outcrops near Riyadh (98% calcite, <6% porosity) and Indiana limestone cores (2-4 mD permeability, 15% porosity). Two acid systems were tested: a novel waste oil emulsified acid and a conventional diesel emulsified acid. Coreflood experiments involved dual core systems with varying injection rates. RDA experiments utilized disks of 1.5" diameter and 0.75" thickness.

## Key Findings

- The new waste emulsified acid system showed better potential as a stimulation fluid compared to both diesel and plain HCl, achieving the lowest pore volume of acid to break through (PVBT). ([[2017-ahmed|AHMED, 2017, p. 14]])
- Diffusion coefficient (De) values at HPHT are significantly lower than those obtained at LPLT; using LPLT data for deep reservoirs can lead to significant inaccuracies in acidizing and hydraulic fracturing designs. ([[2017-ahmed|AHMED, 2017, p. 14]])
- Two distinct De values were observed for oil-saturated limestone reaction kinetics due to the specific effect of crude oil saturation. ([[2017-ahmed|AHMED, 2017, p. 14]])
- The reaction of HCl with calcite is much faster than with dolomite, and the addition of surfactants or polymers significantly reduces the reaction rate by shifting it from mass-transfer limited to surface-reaction limited. ([[2017-ahmed|AHMED, 2017, p. 26]])
- At 3000 psi (where CO2 is in solution), there is a 50% reduction in the diffusion coefficient compared to 1000 psi due to the buffering effect of hydrogen ions. ([[2017-ahmed|AHMED, 2017, p. 28]])
- The inclusion of 1.5 vol% of cationic acrylamide copolymer or 2 vol% corrosion inhibitor significantly reduces the dissolution rate of calcite. ([[2017-ahmed|AHMED, 2017, p. 27]])
- Emulsified acid's higher viscosity allows for better distribution and coverage in heterogeneous formations compared to plain HCl. ([[2017-ahmed|AHMED, 2017, p. 22]])
- The reaction of emulsified acid with limestone is slower than that of plain HCl, which facilitates deeper penetration into the formation. ([[2017-ahmed|AHMED, 2017, p. 35]])
- The waste oil emulsified acid was found to be stable for more than 5 hours at 135°C. ([[2017-ahmed|AHMED, 2017, p. 78]])
- The optimal injection rate for the novel waste oil emulsified acid was determined to be 5.0 cc/min, while the conventional diesel emulsified acid reached its optimum at 2 cc/min. ([[2017-ahmed|AHMED, 2017, p. 80]])
- At an injection rate of 0.5 cc/min, waste oil emulsified acid did not achieve breakthrough due to high viscosity and rapid reaction at the face, resulting in a larger wormhole area but no deep penetration. ([[2017-ahmed|AHMED, 2017, p. 77]])
- The core contains more than 98% calcite, 1% dolomite, and less than 1% trace minerals (quartz). ([[2017-ahmed|AHMED, 2017, p. 73]])
- For dry limestone, the reaction was mass transfer limited at lower RPM and surface reaction limited at rpm above 1000. ([[2017-ahmed|AHMED, 2017, p. 100]])
- The reaction of diesel emulsified acid with oil-saturated calcite remained mass transfer limited even at higher RPM (1000, 1500). ([[2017-ahmed|AHMED, 2017, p. 100]])
- Two distinct diffusion coefficients were observed for oil-saturated limestone: De1 = 3.16228E-11 cm^2/s at lower rpm and De2 = 4.64758E-10 cm^2/s at higher rpm. ([[2017-ahmed|AHMED, 2017, p. 103]])

## Limitations

The study notes that some rheological parameters were derived from correlations rather than direct measurement at 135°C, and a lack of data on oil saturation effects in literature led to the need for specific experimental investigation.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Measure the rheology of the new emulsified acid directly at 135°C instead of using correlation.
- Add corrosion inhibitors and iron control agents to prevent equipment damage.
- Perform more RDA experiments specifically for dolomitic reservoirs.
- Conduct a cost/benefit analysis for waste oil emulsified acids as a stimulation fluid.
