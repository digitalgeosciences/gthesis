# Sedimentology and geochemistry of Qurayyah Sabkha, Eastern Saudi Arabia

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the sedimentology and hydrogeochemistry of the Qurayyah Sabkha, a salt flat in Eastern Saudi Arabia. It characterizes the mineralogical composition of surface sediments (gypsum, anhydrite, halite, quartz) and analyzes the seasonal variations in brine chemistry and stable isotopes to determine the influence of groundwater and evaporation.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the complex interplay between local topography, eolian sand supply, and hydrogeochemical processes in determining the mineralogy and salinity of the Qurayyah Sabkha, specifically investigating whether the brine is purely evaporative seawater or influenced by Neogene groundwater.

## Objectives

- Study the geomorphological, sedimentological, and geochemical characteristics of surface sediments and brines at Qurayyah Sabkha.
- Identify the physical, chemical, and hydrological factors controlling the deposition of sabkha minerals.
- Determine the distribution and association of different mineral assemblages within the environment.
- Investigate the origin and seasonal variations in the physical and chemical characteristics of the brine.
- Characterize the sedimentary facies and mineralogical composition of the surface sediments at Qurayyah Sabkha.
- Analyze the seasonal variations in major ion concentrations and saturation indices of the brine samples.

## Methods

The study involved field investigations to map sedimentary facies and collect surface sediment samples. Mineralogical composition was determined via X-ray diffraction (XRD). Hydrochemical analysis was performed on brine samples collected from the sabkha and Gulf seawater to monitor seasonal variations in major ions (Na, Cl, Ca, Mg, SO4) and calculate saturation indices for minerals like halite, gypsum, anhydrite, and dolomite. Stable isotope analysis of oxygen, hydrogen, and sulfur, along with radiocarbon dating, was used to distinguish between marine and groundwater components.

## Data and Materials

26 surface sediment samples from various locations; 24 additional samples at different depths from five locations; 31 XRD samples; 6 SEM-EDS samples; 2 water sampling stations; 1 thin section of lithified aragonite; brine and seawater for stable isotope analysis.

## Key Findings

- The sabkha is approximately 5 km in diameter, characterized by a thick crust of halite (10-40 cm) at the margins of water ponds. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 14]])
- Sediments are dominated by four minerals: gypsum, anhydrite, halite, and quartz sand. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 12]])
- Brine salinity increases with water level rise after rainfall and decreases in summer as fresh groundwater replaces evaporative loss. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 12]])
- Stable isotope data of the brine show enrichment of all isotopes except deuterium, indicating strong evaporation of Neogene groundwater. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 12]])
- Anhydrite is found as fine crystal laths (10-20 $\mu$m) in the brown mud layer below the evaporite crust. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 57]])
- Gypsum forms from 30-100% of surface sediments and occurs as crystal mush, needles, or flat blades. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 70]])
- Aragonite is restricted to the southeastern margin of the sabkha in a lithified layer. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 70]])
- The brown mud layer (gypsum, anhydrite, halite, and quartz) is a prominent feature of the sedimentary facies. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 58]])
- Anhydrite forms approximately 70% of the layer where it is associated with gypsum mush zones; this is likely a replacement of earlier gypsum. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 71]])
- Quartz in the northern part of the sabkha is derived from the Zabnat Zulum eolian dune, while quartz at the southeastern side is likely from old beach deposits. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 72]])
- Calcite constitutes 5-10% of the brown evaporitic mud layer and is primarily of diagenetic origin. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 72]])
- Aragonite is found only at the southeastern side as gastropod shells or as cement in the aragonitic layer. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 73]])
- The brine shows a 60% increase in Na+ and Cl- concentration from summer to winter, directly related to increased water levels from winter rain dissolving the halite crust. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 75]])
- The Mg:Ca molar ratio has a general inverse relationship to SO4 concentration; an increase in Ca through evaporation is used by the system to form calcium sulfate, while Mg continues to increase. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 80]])
- The brine is primarily supersaturated with respect to halite, gypsum, and anhydrite during fall, winter, and early summer; it becomes undersaturated in late summer. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 81]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

Further geochemical and isotope analyses of the sabkha brines and Neogene aquifer groundwaters are essential to reach firm conclusions regarding the degree of evaporative enrichment and true nature of the evaporative pumping mechanism.
