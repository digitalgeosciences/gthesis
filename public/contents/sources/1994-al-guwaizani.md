# Sedimentology and geochemistry of Qurayyah Sabkha, Eastern Saudi Arabia

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the sedimentology and geochemistry of the Qurayyah Sabkha, a salt flat on the western coast of the Arabian Gulf. It characterizes the geomorphology, mineralogy (halite, gypsum, anhydrite), and hydrogeology of the site to understand the interplay between groundwater, evaporation, and evaporite precipitation.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the characterization of a specific coastal sabkha environment (Qurayyah Sabkha) where complex interactions between sea-level fluctuations, groundwater movement, and intense evaporation lead to distinct mineralogical zones and geomorphological features.

## Objectives

- To study the geomorphological, sedimentological, and geochemical characteristics of surface sediments and brines at the Qurayyah Sabkha.
- To provide a detailed description of the physical, chemical, and hydrological factors that control the deposition of sabkha minerals and the distribution/association of mineral assemblages.
- To investigate the origin and seasonal variations in the physical and chemical characteristics of the brine.
- Characterize the geomorphology and sedimentary structures of the Qurayyah Sabkha.
- Analyze the mineralogy and geochemistry of the sabkha sediments and associated brines.
- Investigate the hydrogeology and water table dynamics of the sab_kha system.
- Characterize the mineralogy and texture of sediments across different zones of the Qurayyah Sabkha.
- Map the sedimentary facies of the surficial sabkha sediments.
- Analyze the seasonal variation in major ions and stable isotopes in brine samples to determine the source of salinity (seawater vs. groundwater).
- Describe the geomorphology of the Qurayyah Sabkha
- Analyze the mineralogy and geochemistry of the sabkha sediments
- Determine the impact of water level fluctuations on the depositional environment

## Methods

The study utilized two monitoring stations to track water level changes and collect brine samples for hydrochemical analysis. Brine chemistry was analyzed for major ions (via ICP) and trace metals, while stable isotopes were measured at the University of Ottawa. Sediment mineralogy was determined using binocular microscopy, X-ray powder diffraction (XRD), and SEM-EDS on 6 soil samples to determine composition and texture. A specialized program, SOLMIN88, was used to calculate saturation indices for minerals like gypsum, halite, and anhydrite based on brine chemistry.

## Data and Materials

26 surface sediment samples were collected from various locations; 24 additional samples were taken at different depths (up to 1m) along a transect. Brine samples were collected over a period of approximately one year (October 1992 to October 1993). One thin section was prepared for petrographic analysis.

## Key Findings

- The sabkha sediments are dominated by four primary minerals: gypsum, anhydrite, halite, and quartz sand. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 12]])
- Brine salinity increases with the rise of water level following rainfall and decreases in summer as fresh groundwater replaces evaporative loss. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 12]])
- Stable isotope data of the sabkha brine show enrichment of all stable isotopes except for deuterium, indicating strong evaporation of likely Neogene groundwater. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 12]])
- The presence of a 'C' data component suggests old groundwater in the brine is continuously rejuvenated by aquifer waters. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 12]])
- Halite forms layers ranging from 1 to more than 8 cm in thickness, separated by less than 2 mm thick silt and clay-size laminae. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 16]])
- The sedimentary facies is characterized by a central halite crust at the margin of water ponds surrounded by gypsum mush facies downwind from the center. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 12]])
- Evaporite sedimentation is primarily found on the downwind side of the sabkha due to prevailing northwestern offshore winds supplying eolian sand. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 12]])
- The Qurayyah Sabkha is a nearly circular to elliptical depression approximately 5 km in diameter, located 1 km west of the coastline. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 47]])
- Halite crusts at the margins of brine ponds range from 10-40 cm in thickness and are prone to collapse as water levels drop. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 48]])
- The sabkha features a distinct polygonal patterned surface where polygons range from 1 to 3 m in diameter with an irregular five-sided shape. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 53]])
- page_number_reference_note_for_finding_2_is_48_and_finding_3_is_53 ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994]])
- The sediments are dominated by four major minerals: gypsum (30-100% of surface sediments), anhydrite (~70% of the brown mud layer), halite (forming crusts up to 30 cm thick), and quartz. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 70]])
- Anhydrite in the brown mud layer is likely a dehydration product of gypsum, as it forms about 70% of that specific layer. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 71]])
- Quartz in the northern part of the sabkha is derived from the Zabnat Zulum eolian dune, while quartz in the southeast is likely from old beach deposits. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 71]])
- Halite crusts are found at the lowest parts of the sabkha; they are replaced by gypsum-halite-anhydrite zones as elevation increases. ([[1994-al-guwaizani|Al-Guwaizani, 1994, p. 73]])

## Limitations

The study notes that while a subsurface seawater input cannot be entirely ruled out, the lack of detailed and precise hydrological data makes it difficult to confirm the exact extent of marine influence versus groundwater dominance.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Further geochemical and isotope analyses of the sabkha brines and Neogene aquifer groundwaters are essential to reach firm conclusions regarding the degree of evaporative enrichment and true nature of the evaporative pumping mechanism.
