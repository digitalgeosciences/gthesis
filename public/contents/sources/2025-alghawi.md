# UTILIZING SAUDI BASALT FOR CARBON MINERALIZATION: IMPLICATIONS OF REDUCING CO2 EMISSION

**Summary**: The thesis evaluates the efficiency of carbon mineralization using basalt rocks from Harrat Lunayyir and Harrat Rahat in western Saudi Arabia. It investigates how factors like pressure, temperature, pH, salinity, and mineral content affect CO2 uptake and carbonate precipitation when mixed with seawater.

---

## Research Problem

High global CO2 emissions pose a risk to the environment; while some sequestration methods exist, surface carbon mineralization using basalt is an emerging technology that needs evaluation in the Saudi context (specifically regarding local rock types and environmental conditions like salinity).

## Objectives

- To characterize the basaltic rocks collected from the study area by examining their mineralogy and other relevant characteristics that influence their potential for basalt-CO2 reactions.
- To use chemical additives to improve the reaction between basalt and CO2, resulting in increased CO2 consumption.
- To identify the optimum conditions that promote CO2-basalt reaction and surface carbon mineralization including the pressure, pH, temperature, duration, salinity, and ionic content.
- To calculate the amount of CO2 consumed relative to the amount of basaltic rocks used.
- Establish a baseline for the utilization of basaltic powder in mineralizing CO2.
- Evaluate the impact of pressure on carbonate minerals' dissolution and CO2 solubility.

## Methods

Basalt samples were collected from Harrat Lunayyir and Harrat Rahat and characterized using XRF (M4-Tornado) for elemental composition and XRD (Empyrean) for mineralogy. Seawater was collected from the Umluj coast and analyzed for pH, electrical conductivity, TIC, and ionic content (Ca, Mg, Fe) via Ion Chromatography and ICP-MS. Experimental trials were conducted in a pressure vessels (Proserv accumulator) where powdered basalt was mixed with seawater/DI water under varying conditions of temperature, pressure, and duration. CO2 was injected into the system, and the mixture was shaken daily to ensure homogeneity.

## Data and Materials

Experimental data from 29 different test conditions; samples included Saudi basalt (high and low magnesium varieties), seawater, deionized water, and citric acid as a pH modifier. Measurements included CO2 uptake in g/l, TIC (mg/l), and mineral percentages.

## Key Findings

- Ionic concentration in the basalt had the highest impact on water-basalt-CO2 interaction, resulting in approximately 5.43 g/l of CO2 uptake and precipitated about 10.62 g of carbonates. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 14]])
- Decreasing the pH and increasing salinity of the rock-seawater solution made the calcium and iron minerals more soluble, resulting in greater CO2 uptake. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 14]])
- Increasing both the injection pressure and mixing time led to a higher solubility of CO2; for example, 2.84 g/l of CO2 uptake was achieved after mixing for seven days compared to only 2.14 g/l after one day. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 14]])
- Increasing the temperature of the solution from 50°C to 75°C lowered the CO2 solubility, reducing the CO2 uptake from 2.70 g/l to 2.55 g/l. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 14]])
- Maximum of 5.63 g/l of ambient CO2 was captured and 13.34 g of carbonates were precipitated after seven days at a pressure of 1000 psi, a pH of 3, and using magnesium-rich basalt dissolved in seawater. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 16]])
- Optimal conditions for carbon mineralization were determined to be higher pressures, lower pH of water, and utilizing magnesium-rich basalt at 25°C. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 16]])
- The addition of basaltic powder increased TIC by 333.2 mg/l compared to the reference experiment without basalt at 500 psi. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 66]])
- Higher pressure (1000 psi) significantly increased TIC levels in both seawater and deionized water, with a rise of approximately 400 mg/l compared to 200 psi. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 68]])
- Increasing temperature from 25°C to 75°C resulted in a decrease in TIC (from 769 to 691 mg/l) but increased the dissolution of iron and calcium. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 71]])
- Lowering pH to 3 significantly enhanced mineral dissolution, with iron increasing from 24.2 ppm (at pH 7.7) to 189.9 ppm. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 73]])
- Seawater provided the highest amount of precipitation (4.62 g at 500 psi) compared to groundwater (1.8 g) and deionized water (0.84 g). ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 88]])
- Reused basalt produced more carbonate precipitate (5.64 g) than fresh basalt (4.62 g), suggesting a change in mineral availability over time. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 88]])
- High magnesium content in basalt led to the highest TIC levels and significantly higher dissolution of calcium and iron compared to low magnesium samples. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 83]])
- The optimal condition for carbonate precipitation was identified as pH 3, 1000 psi, and high-magnesium basalt, yielding 13.34 g of carbonates. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 89]])
- Calcite (CaCO3) was the predominant mineral formed in all experiments, with percentages reaching up to 99.9% under certain conditions. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 92]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future research should evaluate the use of waste-water rich in calcium and magnesium (e.g., produced water, NF/RO reject streams). It is also recommended to study the impact of mixing intensity using an electronic high-shear mixer and to investigate the potential generation of hydrogen during experiments.
