# UTILIZING SAUDI BASALT FOR CARBON MINERALIZATION: IMPLICATIONS OF REDUCING CO2 EMISSION

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the potential of using Saudi basalt for carbon mineralization to mitigate CO2 emissions. It evaluates how various factors, including pH levels, pressure, temperature, and mineral content (specifically magnesium), affect the dissolution of minerals from basalt and the subsequent precipitation of carbonates like calcite.

---

## Research Problem

The rapid increase in global CO2 emissions poses a significant environmental risk, requiring effective carbon capture and storage (CCS) technologies that do not rely heavily on scarce water resources. The study explores the potential of using local Saudi basalt for surface carbon mineralization as an alternative to large-scale tree planting.

## Objectives

- Assess the efficiency of CO2 mineralization (uptake) using Saudi basalt rocks.
- Characterize the physicochemical properties of basalt and seawater samples.
- Investigate the effects of pressure, temperature, pH, salinity, duration, and ionic content on surface carbon mineralization processes.
- To characterize the basaltic rocks collected from the study area by examining their mineralogy and other relevant characteristics that influence their potential for basalt-CO2 reactions.
- To use chemical additives to improve the reaction between basalt and CO2, resulting in increased CO2 consumption.
- To identify the optimum conditions that promote CO2-basalt reaction and surface carbon mineralization including the pressure, pH, temperature, duration, salinity, and ionic content.
- To calculate the amount of CO2 consumed relative to the amount of basaltic rocks used.
- Evaluate the applicability of basaltic rocks in the western region of Saudi Arabia for the surface mineralization process.
- Examine the composition and properties of these rocks to assess their potential for interacting with and storing CO2.
- Determine how chemical additives (like citric acid) can improve the reaction between basalt and CO2.
- Evaluate the impact of using basaltic powder on the dissolution of calcium, magnesium, and iron.
- Assess the influence of pressure (1000, 500, 200 psi) on CO2 solubility and mineral dissolution.
- Determine the effect of temperature (25, 50, 75 °C) on the sequestration process.
- Investigate the impact of lowering pH levels (to 3 and 4.5) using citric acid on mineral dissolution.
- Compare the effectiveness of different water types (seawater, groundwater, deionized water) in the mineralization process.
- Evaluate the effect of pH levels on mineral dissolution from basaltic powder.
- Assess the influence of water salinity/type on carbon mineralization rates.
- Determine the impact of using fresh versus reused basalt on mineral availability.
- Investigate the role of mixing time and basalt dosage on carbonate precipitation.
- Identify optimal conditions (pressure, pH, temperature) for maximizing carbon sequestration.
- Evaluate the impact of different conditions (pressure, temperature, pH level, salinity, duration, and mineral concentration) on water-basalt-CO2 interaction.
- Determine the effect of using seawater versus deionized water on CO2 solubility and mineral dissolution.
- Assess the role of citric acid in lowering pH to enhance carbonate formation.
- Identify the optimal conditions for maximizing carbon mineralization using Saudi basalt.
- Evaluate the potential of Saudi basalt for carbon mineralization.
- Assess the impact of mineralogy and chemistry on CO2 storage capacity.
- Investigate the role of basaltic minerals in sequestering CO2 to reduce atmospheric emissions.

## Methods

The study involved collecting 20 samples of basalt from Harrat Lunayyir and Harrat Rahat, along with seawater from Umluj and groundwater from Madinah. Basalt was prepared by crushing into powder using a RETSCH MM 400 Mixer Mill. Chemical analysis included XRF (M4-Tornado) for elemental composition, XRD (Malvern Pan Analytical Empyrean) for mineral identification, ICP-MS for iron measurement, and Ion Chromatography for calcium and magnesium levels. Experimental trials were conducted in a Proserv accumulator under varying conditions: pressure (200, 500, 1000 psi), temperature (25, 50, 75 °C), pH (3 to 7.72), and salinity (seawater vs. groundwater/DI water).

## Data and Materials

Experimental data from 18 different test conditions involving various pressures (200, 500, 1000 psi), temperatures (25, 50, 75 °C), pH levels (adjusted with citric acid to 3 and 4.5), and two types of basalt (low magnesium and high magnesium). Samples were analyzed for carbonate precipitation weight, CO2 uptake (g/l), and mineralogical composition.

## Key Findings

- Ionic concentration in basalt had the highest impact on water-basalt-CO2 interaction, resulting in approximately 5.43 g/l of CO2 uptake and ~10.62 g of carbonates precipitated. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 13]])
- Decreasing pH and increasing salinity of the rock-seawater solution made calcium and iron minerals more soluble, leading to higher CO2 uptake. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 14]])
- Increasing injection pressure and mixing time led to higher solubility of CO2; for example, 2.84 g/l of CO2 uptake was achieved after seven days compared to 2.14 g/l after one day. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 14]])
- Increasing temperature from 50°C to 75°C lowered CO2 solubility, reducing uptake from 2.70 g/l to 2.55 g/l. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 14]])
- Maximum carbon capture of 5.63 g/l of ambient CO2 and 13.34 g of carbonates was achieved at 1000 psi, pH 3, using magnesium-rich basalt in seawater at 25°C. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 14]])
- XRD analysis showed a relationship between pressure and carbonate precipitation (calcite). ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 79]])
- XRF analysis was used to determine the mass percentage of target minerals in basalt samples. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 47]])
- Approximately 76% of greenhouse gases are attributed to CO2. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 18]])
- Saudi Arabia's per capita energy consumption in 2010 was approximately 6168 kg of oil equivalent, the highest in the world. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 19]])
- Basalt is highly reactive with CO2-rich fluids due to its high content of divalent cations (Ca2+, Mg2+, Fe2+). ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025]])
- page_number_placeholder_for_finding_3_is_not_provided_in_text_but_it_is_on_page_22 ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025]])
- Surface mineralization can be accelerated by grinding basaltic rock to increase surface area or by adding heat, acid, or other reagents. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 24]])
- Only 4% of CO2 was dissolved in water when injected into basalt without a caprock (Carbfix project). ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 24]])
- Higher pressure (up to 31 MPa) accelerates the mineralization of carbon dioxide, leading to faster precipitation of carbonate minerals like dolomite, calcite, and aragonite. ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 33]])
- Ideal pressure for mineralization occurs at partial carbon dioxide pressure between 5 to 20 MPa (approx. 725 to 2900 psi). ([[2025-alghawi|Alghawi, 2025, p. 34]])

## Limitations

Subsurface storage faces risks such as leakage due to lack of caprock in basalt, slow reaction rates, and high monitoring costs. Additionally, low permeability in some basalt formations limits injection capacity.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future research should evaluate the use of wastewater rich in calcium and magnesium (e.g., produced water, NF reject, or RO reject) for more sustainable carbon mineralization. The study also suggests investigating the impact of mixing intensity using an electronic high-shear mixer and scaling up to continuous stirred tank reactors or fluidized bed reactors.
