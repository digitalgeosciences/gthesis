# EVALUATION OF DRILLING DESIGNS FOR SMART MRC WELLS APPLIED IN SAUDI ARAMCO

**Summary**: The thesis evaluates three drilling designs—Big Cased Hole (BCH), Slim Open Hole (SOH), and a proposed Slim Cased Hole (SCH)—for Smart Multi-Rate Completion (MRC) wells in Saudi Aramco fields. It concludes that while SOH is faster and cheaper, the SCH design provides superior technical reliability regarding cement integrity, junction stability, and lateral equalization by utilizing an anhydrite cap rock as a buffer.

---

## Research Problem

The high cost and complexity of installing smart completion equipment in multi-lateral wells (MRC) require a balance between robust casing for protection/isolation and the need for reduced drilling time and costs. The study addresses the trade-offs between 'Big Cased Hole' designs (high integrity, high cost) and 'Slim Open Hole' designs (lower cost, lower assurance).

## Objectives

- To evaluate thoroughly and compare the two existing designs Saudi Aramco is currently applying in the mother-Field and evaluate their pros and cons on the following features: Total end of well cost, Amount of time required to drill and complete both types, Quality, integrity and assurance during the drilling phase of a well.
- Propose a new improved drilling design for Smart MRC Wells that will alleviate the drawbacks of the existing designs.
- Evaluate the technical challenges of Slim Open Hole (SOH) designs including well control, differential sticking, and equipment placement.
- Compare the Big Cased Hole (BCH) design against SOH in terms of cost, time, and reliability.
- Propose a new 'Slim Cased Hole' (SCH) design that utilizes an anhydrite cap rock to provide casing protection while maintaining a slim profile.
- Quantify the trade-offs between drilling speed (ROP), material costs, and technical risks across three distinct well architectures.

## Methods

The study employs a comparative analysis of three well designs: Big Cased Hole (BCH), Slim Open Hole (SOH), and Proposed Slim Cased Hole (SCH). It evaluates these designs based on drilling parameters (ROP, time), material costs (casing weight/length), and technical risks (well control, sticking, cement integrity). The analysis incorporates historical data from Saudi Aramco fields (e.g., Field-B) to model ROP in anhydrite sections and calculate cost differences between casing types.

## Data and Materials

Data includes drilling reports (e.g., Table 3.2), well logs, and technical specifications for various casing sizes (9-5/8", 13-3/8", 7" liner) across multiple fields (Field A, B, C). The study references specific wells experiencing loss of circulation (Table 3.1) and compares drilling times against 'Ultimate Drilling Curve' benchmarks.

## Key Findings

- The Big Cased Hole design requires larger casing sizes (e.g., 9-5/8" instead of 13-3/8") to accommodate smart completion equipment, leading to slower drilling rates and higher costs due to the need for larger hole sizes in upper sections. ([[2011-al-amri|Al-Amri, 2011, p. 34]])
- The Slim Open Hole design reduces cost and time but compromises well integrity and assurance of safe deployment compared to cased-hole designs. ([[2011-al-amri|Al-Amri, 2011, p. 29]])
- Loss of circulation is a significant challenge in fractured reservoirs (Field B and C), impacting the quality of the 7" liner cement job and potentially compromising inter-lateral separation. ([[2011-al-amri|Al-Amri, 2011, p. 38]])
- A 7" liner is considered vital for smart completion to provide sufficient space for flow control valves and ensure a minimum inter-lateral separation of 300 meters of cement/steel. ([[2011-al-amri|Al-Amri, 2011, p. 34]])
- The use of Mica LCM (Lost Circulation Material) pills is recommended to mitigate fluid loss in the motherbore without damaging the reservoir, as it will eventually be cased and cemented. ([[2011-al-amri|Al-Amri, 2011, p. 40]])
- The Slim Open Hole (SOH) design is the fastest to drill, with an estimated ROP of up to 60.4 ft/hr in certain sections, but it poses high risks for well control and differential sticking. ([[2011-al-amri|Al-Amri, 2011, p. 75]])
- The proposed Slim Cased Hole (SCH) design requires approximately 8.3 days more than the SOH design due to drilling through a 2000-foot anhydrite section at an estimated ROP of 10 ft/hr. ([[2011-al-amri|Al-Amri, 2011, p. 58]])
- The SCH design eliminates differential sticking risks because the completion is set against a smooth steel wall in the casing rather than an irregular open hole surface. ([[2011-al-amri|Al-Amri, 2011, p. 66]])
- SOH designs lack any means of ensuring cement integrity between laterals, whereas the SCH design utilizes the high-compressive-strength anhydrite to ensure a successful cement job. ([[2011-al-amri|Al-Amri, 2011, p. 64]])
- The SCH design allows for full equalization of each lateral by utilizing a 100-foot measured depth section in the cap rock, which is not possible in SOH or BCH designs. ([[2011-al-amri|Al-Amri, 2011, p. 72]])
- The cost difference between SCH and SOH is primarily driven by rig time (approx. 8 days) and advanced geosteering tools, but it remains lower than the cost of the Big Cased Hole design. ([[2011-al-amri|Al-Amri, 2011, p. 77]])

## Limitations

The study notes that the advantages of the proposed SCH design are only fully realized if the reservoir is capped by an impermeable and non-porous cap-rock (anhydrite) and only the top sections of the reservoir are targeted.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Propose a new improved drilling design for Smart MRC Wells that will alleviate the drawbacks of the existing designs (specifically balancing cost, time, and integrity).
