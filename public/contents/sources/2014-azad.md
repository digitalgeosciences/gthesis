# Evaluation of Novel Viscoelastic System as a Potential Chemical EOR method in Ca.

**Summary**: The thesis evaluates a novel Viscoelastic System (VES) as a chemical Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR) method specifically for heavy oil in carbonate reservoirs. It investigates the dual functionality of VES—providing both interfacial tension reduction and mobility control through the formation of worm-like micelles (WLM)—to overcome limitations of traditional methods like steam flooding or polymer flooding.

---

## Research Problem

The degradation of Viscoelastic Systems (VES) under high-temperature and high-salinity conditions in carbonate reservoirs limits their use as long-term EOR fluids. Specifically, the oxidation of unsaturated carbon-carbon double bonds in the hydrophobic tails leads to a loss of wormlike micelle (WLM) structure, resulting in a significant drop in both interfacial tension (IFT) reduction and shear viscosity.

## Objectives

- Evaluate three different VES formulations for oil recovery potential in 57,000 ppm saline water.
- Determine the optimal VES formulation based on viscosity/IFT ratio.
- Measure the effect of concentration and temperature on shear viscosity and IFT of the optimum VES.
- Analyze the ability of VES to combat formation water salinity in poorly preflushed and high permeability regions through rheological studies.
- Test the thermal stability of various formulations (VES, S/VES, VES/P) and optimize via a reducing agent (VES/R).
- Simulate the performance of a hybrid VES/hot water system in heavy oil reservoirs to address issues of thin reservoir thickness and high oil viscosity.

## Methods

The research employs a multi-stage approach: 1) Screening for optimal VES based on visual observation, IFT reduction, and viscosity generation. 2) Rheological characterization including measurement of G' and G'' to assess wormlike micelle (WLM) growth and shear-thinning behavior. 3) IFT characterization to evaluate the impact of concentration, temperature, aging, and surfactant/polymer addition. 4) Thermal stability testing using thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) for short-term and long-term stability. 5) Core flooding experiments on carbonate cores to measure permeability, water saturation, and residual oil reduction. 6) Reservoir simulation to model the performance of VES in thin heavy oil reservoirs compared to steam and polymer flooding.

## Data and Materials

The study utilizes various chemical systems including VES (viscoelastic surfactant), S/VES (surfactant/viscoelastic surfactant), and VES/P (viscoelastic surfactant/polymer). Core flooding was performed on carbonate cores with permeability between 1824 and 4992 mD. Experimental conditions included high salinity (up to 210,000 ppm) and high temperatures (up to 80°C).

## Key Findings

- The optimal VES system outperformed S/VES and VES/P systems in terms of viscosity/IFT ratio. ([[2014-azad|Azad, 2014, p. 24]])
- Core flooding with 1.5 PV of the selected VES systems reduced residual and remaining oil saturation to 0.23. ([[2014-azad|Azad, 2014, p. 24]])
- The tested VES formed wormlike micelles (WLM) in high salinity, moderate temperature conditions, which is beneficial for mobility control in carbonate reservoirs. ([[2014-azad|Azad, 2014, p. 24]])
- Thermal stability of the primary VES was improved by combining it with a reducing agent (VES/R). ([[2014-azad|Azad, 2014, p. 24]])
- Simulation results showed that thermally stable VES combined with hot water could provide higher production in thin viscous heavy oil reservoirs compared to conventional methods. ([[2014-azad|Azad, 2014, p. 26]])
- The viscosity/IFT ratio is used as a proxy for the capillary number (Nc) to evaluate the potential of the system to reduce residual oil saturation. ([[2014-azad|Azad, 2014, p. 38]])
- Viscoelastic surfactants (VES) are shown to be more stable than conventional polymers in high temperature and high salinity environments, with only a maximum of 10% viscosity loss at 90°C. ([[2014-azad|Azad, 2014, p. 75]])
- The formation of wormlike micelles (WLM) is the primary mechanism for providing viscosity in VES systems, which can grow up to several micrometers under favorable conditions like high salinity and temperature. ([[2014-azad|Azad, 2014, p. 72]])
- Note: The text mentions that a specific surfactant, Amide Tallow-(3-dimethyl amino) propyl, N-oxide, was screened as the best among three tested for carbonate reservoir conditions. ([[2014-azad|Azad, 2014]])
- page_number_placeholder_for_finding_above_is_not_explicitly_given_but_it_is_in_section_2.7 ([[2014-azad|Azad, 2014]])
- Core flooding results showed that a VES/P system provided 10.2% recovery over water flooding, while the pure VES system provided 1.90% in a study using high-concentration polymer. ([[2014-azad|Azad, 2014, p. 76]])
- A core flood study with 0.3% VES injected after polymer (900 ppm) showed an incremental recovery of 15% over polymer flooding, while 0.3% VES injected after water flooding gave 12% over water flooding. ([[2014-azad|Azad, 2014, p. 73]])
- The study identifies a 'dual mobility control' concept where VES can provide both IFT reduction and viscosity to address the limitations of single-method approaches in carbonate reservoirs. ([[2014-azad|Azad, 2014, p. 57]])
- The study identifies three candidate viscoelastic surfactants: Tallow Alkyl Amine ethoxylate (Ethomin T-15), Erucamidopropyl Hydroxypropylsultaine (Armovis), and Tallow trimethyl ammonium chloride (Arquad T-50). ([[2014-azad|Azad, 2014, p. 101]])
- The core flooding experiment was conducted on a low permeability carbonate core of 113 milli Darcy at 80°C and 210,000 ppm salinity. ([[2014-azad|Azad, 2014, p. 86]])

## Limitations

Short-scale core flooding experiments may not fully capture the effects of large-scale heterogeneity or extensive fracture networks, potentially leading to an overestimation of recovery rates. Additionally, lab studies cannot fully replicate the complex flow paths in real carbonate reservoirs.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Study the viscosity drop of VES with various crude oils to formulate optimal VES/P systems.
- Use sacrificial agents or specific chelating agent concentrations to mitigate salt-induced adsorption.
- Conduct core flooding on longer cores to identify and address surfactant-polymer interference (SPI) issues.
- Perform core flooding in fractured cores to evaluate the diverting capabilities of VES compared to smart water and chelating agents.
- Conduct contact angle measurements to determine rock-fluid interaction improvements.
- Investigate hybrid systems such as VES/P/hot water or VES/CO2 to enhance thermal stability and mobility control.
- Utilize stochastic optimization tools (CMA-ES, PSO) for determining optimal well placement and operational parameters.
