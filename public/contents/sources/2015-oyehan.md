# ISOLATION AND CHARACTERIZATON OF MICROBIAL COMMUNITIES FOUND IN OIL-POLLUTED SITES FROM 1991 GULF WAR

**Summary**: The thesis investigated the isolation and characterization of hydrocarbon-degrading bacteria from sediment samples in oil-polluted sites along the Arabian Gulf coast resulting from the 1991 Gulf War. Using enrichment cultures, microscopy, 16S rRNA sequencing, and GC-MS analysis, the study identified 15 bacterial strains (mostly Pseudomonas) with varying capabilities to degrade phenanthrene, pyrene, and diesel components.

---

## Research Problem

The Arabian Gulf suffers from significant hydrocarbon pollution, particularly from the 1991 Gulf War, where PAHs remain persistent due to their hydrophobicity and low volatility. There is a need to identify and characterize local microbial strains capable of degrading these pollutants for effective bioremediation.

## Objectives

- Isolate and characterize PAH-degrading bacteria from polluted sites from 1991 Gulf War.
- Test the degradation abilities of these isolates on phenanthrene, pyrene, and diesel components.
- Isolate microbial communities from oil-polluted sites in the Arabian Gulf.
- Characterize isolated strains using morphological, biochemical, and molecular techniques (16S rRNA).
- Evaluate the degradation capability of selected isolates on phenanthrene, pyrene, and diesel components.
- Isolate and characterize microbial communities from oil-polluted sites.
- Assess the physical and chemical properties of sediment samples from different locations.
- Identify hydrocarbon-degrading bacteria using molecular analysis (16S rRNA sequencing).
- Evaluate the degradation efficiency of isolated strains on specific hydrocarbons like diesel, phenanthrene, and pyrene.
- Identify bacterial strains using 16S rRNA sequencing.
- Evaluate the degradation capabilities of isolated strains on various hydrocarbon substrates including phenanthrene, pyrene, and diesel.
- Isolate and characterize microbial communities from oil-polluted sites along the Arabian Gulf coast.
- Evaluate the degradation efficiency of isolated strains on diesel and specific PAHs like phenanthrene and pyrene.
- Compare growth assessment methods (OD vs. CFU) for monitoring bacterial growth in hydrocarbon-containing cultures.
- Isolate microbial communities from oil-polluted sites.
- Characterize these isolates for their ability to degrade hydrocarbons.
- Identify specific bacterial strains capable of degrading PAHs.

## Methods

Sediment samples were collected from three sites in Jubail. Physical/chemical characterization included conductivity and pH measurements, and nutrient analysis via IC (using a packed-bed column). Petroleum hydrocarbons were extracted using SiteLAB UVF-3100. Microbial isolation involved enrichment cultures with specific PAHs or diesel as sole carbon sources. Characterization of isolates included morphological observation under microscopy and molecular identification via 16S rRNA gene amplification by PCR, gel electrophoresis, and phylogenetic analysis in MEGA software. Degradation assays were performed for diesel and PAHs (phenanthrene, pyrene). Analytical chemistry used GC-MS with SPME to quantify hydrocarbon concentrations.

## Data and Materials

15 isolated bacterial strains (e.g., BC1-BC10, LA, LB, LC, DSA, DSB). Samples were taken from oil-polluted sites in the Arabian Gulf. Degradation tests used 100 ml of BH mineral broth with concentrations such as 100 mg/L for phenanthrene and pyrene, or 0.5% diesel.

## Key Findings

- All 15 isolated strains were Gram-negative rod bacteria belonging to phylum Proteobacteria, with the genus Pseudomonas being dominant. ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 17]])
- 87% of the isolates were similar to P. aeruginosa; others were related to Cupriavidus taiwanensis, P. citronellolis, and Ochrobactrum intermedium. ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 18]])
- Isolates BC5 (P. aeruginosa) and LB (P. citronellolis) were excellent phenanthrene degraders, reducing 100 ppm by approximately 95% within 15 days. ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 18]])
- Isolate DSA (P. aeruginosa) was efficient in the degradation of diesel components but could not degrade aromatic components of diesel (C15-C21). ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 18]])
- The study found that more than a single strain is needed for effective degradation of diesel due to varying capabilities across different carbon chain lengths. ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 18]])
- Bacterial growth assessments in PAH-enriched media should be based on counting colony-forming units (cfu) rather than optical density (OD). ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 18]])
- All isolates were Gram-negative, rod-shaped bacteria belonging to the family Pseudomonadaceae. ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 19]])
- 78% of the isolates were similar to Pseudomonas aeruginosa. ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 19]])
- Other closely related strains included Cupriavidus novrifaciens, Sphingomonas, and Ochrobactrum. ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 19]])
- Isolates BC5 and BL were found to have excellent degradation of phenanthrene, reducing it by approximately 55% within 15 days. ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 19]])
- The order of increasing ease for degrading diesel components was: Aromatic > (C21-C24) > (C17-C18) > (C14-C16) > (C11-C13). ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 20]])
- The study indicates that more than one strain is required for effective degradation of diesel fuel components. ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 20]])
- Approximately 8 million barrels (252 million US gallons) of crude oil were released into the sea during the 1991 Gulf War. ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 21]])
- Polluted sites in the Gulf showed hydrocarbon concentrations as high as 18,000 µg/l in sediment from certain locations. ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 28]])
- Three sampling locations showed varying pollution levels: Location A (Nakheel beach) had 9.09 mg/Kg PAH, while Locations B and C were more polluted with 12.27 mg/Kg and 13.42 mg/Kg respectively. ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 57]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

The bacterial isolates should be tested on model polluted soils and scaled up for use in remediation bioreactors. Optimization of biodegradation conditions and the study of metabolites/pathways are recommended to facilitate future cloning of specific genes.
