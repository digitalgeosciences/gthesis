# ISOLATION AND CHARACTERIZATON OF MICROBIAL COMMUNITIES FOUND IN OIL-POLLUTED SITES FROM 1991 GULF WAR

**Summary**: The thesis investigated the isolation and characterization of PAH-degrading bacterial strains from oil-polluted sites in the Arabian Gulf resulting from the 1991 Gulf War. Using a combination of morphological, molecular (16S rRNA), and biochemical analyses, the study identified several promising candidates for bioremediation, including Pseudomonas species.

---

## Research Problem

The persistence of polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) and other heavy hydrocarbons in the Arabian Gulf due to the 1991 Gulf War poses a significant environmental risk. There is a need to identify and characterize indigenous microbial strains capable of degrading these pollutants for potential use in bioremediation.

## Objectives

- Isolate bacterial strains from oil-polluted sites in the Arabian Gulf.
- Characterize the isolated strains morphologically, molecularly, and genetically.
- Assess the ability of selected strains to degrade phenanthrene, pyrene, and diesel components.
- Isolate hydrocarbon-degrading bacterial strains from oil-polluted sediment samples at different locations along the Jubail coast.
- Identify the isolates using 16S rRNA gene sequencing.
- Evaluate the degradation efficiency of selected isolates on specific hydrocarbons (phenanthrene, pyrene, and diesel).

## Methods

Samples were collected from three locations (A, B, C) along the Jubail coastline. Physical and chemical characterization included pH and electrical conductivity measurements. Isolation was performed using enrichment culture methods in broth media. Characterization of isolates involved microscopy for morphology, 16S rRNA sequencing for phylogenetic analysis (using MEGA software), and biochemical assays. Degradation studies were conducted in liquid cultures with phenanthrene, pyrene, and diesel. Analytical chemistry utilized Solid-Phase Micro-Extraction (SPME) followed by Gas Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry (GC-MS) to quantify hydrocarbon degradation.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized sediment samples from three locations (A, B, and C) along the Jubail coast. A total of 15 morphologically distinct hydrocarbon-degrading strains were isolated and characterized. GC-MS was used to analyze the degradation of diesel components.

## Key Findings

- All isolates were Gram-negative rod bacteria belonging to phylum Proteobacteria, with the genus Pseudomonas being dominant. ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 17]])
- 87% of the isolates were similar to P. aeruginosa, while others were related to Cupriavidus taiwanensis, P. citronellolis, and Ochrobactrum intermedium. ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 18]])
- Isolates BC5 (P. aeruginosa) and LB (P. citronellolis) were excellent phenanthrene degraders, reducing 100 ppm by approximately 95% within fifteen days. ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 18]])
- Isolate BC1 (O. intermedium) and DSA (P. aeruginosa) were also efficient in degradation of pyrene and diesel components, respectively. ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 18]])
- The isolate DSA could not degrade aromatic components of diesel effectively; results showed a preference for longer chain compounds: Aromatic < (C8–C11) < (C12 – C14) < (C15 – C17) < (C18 – C21). ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 18]])
- Sediment samples showed extractable petroleum aromatic hydrocarbons between 9.09 and 13.42 mg/Kg. ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 37]])
- Location A (Nakheel beach) had a higher pH of 9.8 compared to other sites, which were more stagnant. ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 37]])
- Nitrogen was present in small amounts as nitrates (31.6 to 60.8 ppm), while phosphorus was detected at very low concentrations. ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 37]])
- Fifteen morphologically distinct hydrocarbon-degrading strains were isolated, with 10 derived from single PAH enrichments (phenanthrene, anthracene, pyrene) and 5 from mixed or diesel enrichments. ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 61]])
- All 15 isolates were identified as rod-shaped, Gram-negative bacteria. ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 67]])
- Molecular analysis revealed that the majority (87%) of the isolates belonged to the class Gammaproteobacteria, with a significant number belonging to the genus Pseudomonas. ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015, p. 90]])
- Isolate BC1 was identified as Ochrobactrum intermedium and showed high degradation efficiency for phenanthrene (>94%) and pyrene (~62%). ([[2015-oyehan|Oyehan, 2015]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study suggests that more than a single strain is needed for effective degradation of diesel components and highlights the need to base bacterial growth assessments on colony-forming units rather than just turbidity.
