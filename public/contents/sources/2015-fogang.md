# TOWARD UNDERSTANDING THE BREAKING MECHANISM OF VISCOELASTIC SURFACTANT IN WELL STIMULATION

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the breaking mechanisms of a sulfobetaine-based viscoelastic surfactant (VES) system in the presence of various oils (n-decane, crude oil, and EVOO) and PGA at different temperatures. It concludes that temperature and specific oil molecular structures significantly influence micelle morphology and viscosity reduction, impacting well stimulation strategies.

---

## Research Problem

The mechanisms of viscosity reduction for long-tail viscoelastic surfactants (VES) at high temperatures are not well understood, which is critical for designing effective internal breakers in well stimulation to prevent formation damage.

## Objectives

- Understand the breaking mechanism of a new class of surfactant by internal breakers after the formation of the viscoelastic gel.
- Compare the effects of four organic compounds (n-decane, crude oil, extra virgin olive oil, and polyglycolic acid) on the VES system at 30°C and 60°C.
- Investigate the effect of different oil types (n-decane, crude oil, EVOO) on the zero-shear viscosity of a sulfobetaine surfactant system.
- Evaluate the impact of temperature (30°C and 60°C) on the breaking capacity of these oils.
- Determine the effect of PGA on the micelle structure and viscosity of the VES system.
- Identify the transition regimes (HVR, TR, LVR) based on oil concentration and temperature.

## Methods

The study utilized a 3.96 wt % concentration of erucamidopropyl hydroxypropyl sulfobetaine surfactant in an aqueous solution with 6.2 wt % CaCl2. Rheology was measured using the Discovery Hybrid Rheometer (DHR-3) to determine zero-shear viscosity, storage modulus, and loss modulus at 30°C and 60°C. Cryo-TEM was performed on samples diluted in ethyl acetate or prepared as a negative stain with phosphotungstic acid to visualize micelle morphology.

## Data and Materials

The study used an erucamidopropyl hydroxypropyl sulfobetaine surfactant system (40-45 wt % concentration). Samples were tested with four organic compounds: n-decane, crude oil from the Uthmaniya field, extra virgin olive oil (EVOO), and polyglycolic acid (PGA). Measurements were taken at 30°C and 60°C.

## Key Findings

- The surfactant solution exhibited a transition from Newtonian to shear-thinning behavior at both temperatures, indicating the presence of cylindrical micelle networks. ([[2015-fogang|FOGANG, 2015, p. 45]])
- A plateau in the shear stress vs. shear rate graph at 60°C provided evidence of a cylindrical micelle network. ([[2015-fogang|FOGANG, 2015, p. 45]])
- The zero-shear viscosity was higher at 30°C than at 60°C (186.1 Pa.s vs. 138.1 Pa.s), a difference of approximately 26%. ([[2015-fogang|FOGANG, 2015, p. 45]])
- The crossover frequency for the surfactant solution was below 0.1 rad/s at both temperatures, indicating Maxwellian behavior rather than gel-like behavior. ([[2015-fogang|FOGANG, 2015, p. 46]])
- Only n-decane drastically affected the zero-shear viscosity of the surfactant solution at 30°C. ([[2015-fogang|FOGANG, 2015, p. 14]])
- The zero-shear viscosity reduced by three to five orders of magnitude at 60°C with increasing concentrations of oils (n-decane, crude oil, and EVOO). ([[2015-fogang|FOGANG, 2015, p. 15]])
- PGA did not induce considerable changes in viscosity at either 30°C or 60°C. ([[2015-fogang|FOGANG, 2015, p. 15]])
- Cryo-TEM confirmed the presence of cylindrical micelles in the pure surfactant solution at 30°C even after dilution in ethyl acetate. ([[2015-fogang|FOGANG, 2015, p. 45]])
- The surfactant solution exhibited viscoelastic behavior at both 30°C and 60°C due to the formation of cylindrical micelles, which were more entangled at 30°C than at 60°C. ([[2015-fogang|FOGANG, 2015, p. 80]])
- At 30°C, only n-decane significantly reduced zero-shear viscosity (by approximately five orders of magnitude for 2 wt %), while crude oil and EVOO showed minimal impact due to a barrier to solubilization. ([[2015-fogang|FOGANG, 2015, p. 57]])
- At 60°C, both crude oil and EVOO showed significantly improved breaking capacity compared to 30°C, with reductions in viscosity of approximately three to five orders of magnitude at higher concentrations. ([[2015-fogang|FOGANG, 2015, p. 57]])
- Three distinct regimes were identified: High Viscosity Region (HVR), Transition Region (TR), and Low Viscosity Region (LVR), based on the balance between micellization and oil solubilization. ([[2015-fogang|FOGANG, 2015, p. 58]])
- The transition from long cylindrical micelles to shorter ones was observed in the TR, where the concentration of oil was sufficient to prevent dense micelle networks from forming. ([[2015-fogang|FOGANG, 2015, p. 70]])
- Cryo-TEM confirmed the presence of short cylindrical micelles at 30°C with 0.9 wt % n-decane and microemulsions at 60°C with 3 wt % n-decane. ([[2015-fogang|FOGANG, 2015, p. 71]])
- PGA had a negligible effect on the micelle structure or viscosity of the sulfobetaine system because the surfactant remained zwitterionic despite changes in pH. ([[2015-fogang|FOGANG, 2015, p. 75]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Crude oil should not be relied on as the only breaking fluid in well stimulation.
- Breaker concentrations must be chosen based on reservoir temperature and the required duration of the stimulation job.
- The choice of breaker should consider molecular structure, cost, and environmental impact.
- PGA is not recommended as a breaker for this specific sulfobetaine surfactant system.
