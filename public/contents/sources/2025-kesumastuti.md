# SUBDUCTING SLABS BENEATH SULAWESI, INDONESIA: NEW EVIDENCE FROM TELESEISMIC P-WAVE TOMOGRAPHY

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the complex tectonic setting of Sulawesi, Indonesia, specifically focusing on subducting slabs beneath the region using teleseismic P-wave tomography. It utilizes advanced seismic processing techniques like the multi-stage Fast Marching Method (FMM) to map 3D velocity structures and identify features such as the Sula slab.

---

## Research Problem

The complex tectonic setting of Sulawesi (a K-shaped configuration) involves multiple interacting plates, but previous seismic models had limited spatial resolution or were restricted by sparse local seismicity. There is a need for high-resolution 3D imaging to understand the geometry and depth extent of subducting slabs in the northern region.

## Objectives

- Develop a relatively high-resolution 3-D velocity model beneath Sulawesi to visualize significant tectonic structures (e.g., subducting slabs and Palu-Koro fault).
- Investigate the geometry and depth extent of the subducting slabs beneath Sulawesi, particularly in the northern region, and assess how these slabs interact with surrounding tectonic structures.
- Explore other key features beneath Sulawesi and their relationship to the complex tectonic setting of Sulawesi.
- Compare the new tomographic results with previous seismic studies and geophysical data to provide a more comprehensive interpretation of the tectonic setting of Sulawesi.
- Map the 3D velocity structure of the Earth's interior beneath Sulawesi using teleseismic P-wave tomography.
- Identify and characterize subducting slabs, specifically the Sula slab, within the Indonesian region.
- Evaluate different seismic tomography parameters such as grid-based vs. ray-based methods and various regularization techniques.
- Extract relative travel-time residuals from teleseismic P-wave data.
- Construct a 3D velocity model using Fast Marching Tomography (FMTOMO).
- Evaluate the robustness of tomographic solutions through synthetic tests.
- Identify and characterize subducting slab geometries beneath Sulawesi.
- Develop a new P-wave tomographic model of the Sulawesi region using teleseismic data.
- Evaluate the resolution and reliability of the tomographic model through synthetic tests (checkerboard and spike tests).
- Identify and characterize high-velocity anomalies representing subducting slabs in the northern part of Sulawesi.
- Investigate the interaction between the Celebes, Sangihe, and north-dipping (Sula) slabs.
- Create a 3D tomographic model of the lithosphere and underlying upper mantle beneath Sulawesi using P-wave teleseismic arrival-time residuals.
- Identify and characterize subducting slabs (Sula and Celebes) in the North Sulawesi region.
- Investigate low-velocity anomalies beneath the Gulf of Tomini to determine their link to volcanic activity.
- Evaluate the lithosphere-asthenosphere coupling along the Palu-Koro Fault Zone.
- To provide new evidence of subducting slabs beneath Sulawesi using teleseismic P-wave tomography
- To characterize the lithospheric structure and upper mantle deformation in the region

## Methods

The study utilizes an adaptive stacking technique to extract travel-time residuals from 39,935 data points across 985 teleseismic events. The inversion is performed using the FMTOMO package, which employs a multi-stage Fast Marching Method (FMM) for forward modeling and a subspace inversion scheme for 3D velocity construction. Model parameters are represented via cubic B-splines on a regular grid. Robustness is assessed using L-curve analysis to determine optimal damping ($\epsilon$) and smoothing ($\eta$) factors, with values of 250 identified as optimal.

## Data and Materials

Data from approximately 89 seismic stations of the Agency for Meteorology, Climatology, and Geophysics network (BMKG). The dataset includes 985 teleseismic events used in the study. Comparison data include SASSY21 and SASSIER22 models.

## Key Findings

- The 3-D tomographic images consistently show a high-velocity perturbation in northern Sulawesi, which is interpreted as multiple subducting slabs. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 14]])
- Tomographic models reveal evidence for mantle upwelling due to an extensional tectonic mechanism in the Gulf of Tomini, providing a magma source for volcanic activity. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 14]])
- The results reveal a low-velocity zone beneath the east side of the Palu Koro Fault, appearing as a large lithospheric structure not limited to the crust. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 16]])
- Previous models (SASSY21 and SASSIER22) identified high-velocity anomalies beneath North Sulawesi suggesting slabs extending to approximately 400 km depth. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 19]])
- Sulawesi is characterized by a complex tectonic setting influenced by the convergence of the Eurasian, Indian-Australian, and Philippine Sea plates. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 21]])
- The West Sulawesi Plutono-Volcanic Arc consists of the South Arm (continental crust of Australian origin) and the North Arm (an intra-oceanic island arc). ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 22]])
- The Sula slab is a remnant of the upper portion of an Indian Ocean plate that detached at a depth of approximately 200 km during the Early Miocene. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 24]])
- The Palu-Koro fault has a slip rate of 42 mm/yr and exhibits transtensional movement, forming a pull-apart basin. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 25]])
- The Matano fault is a left-lateral fault with a slip rate of 7 mm/yr. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 25]])

- The study identified a robust balance for inversion parameters at a damping factor of 250 and a smoothing factor of 250, determined via L-curve analysis. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 39]])
- The teleseismic dataset included 985 events with 39,935 residual times, primarily consisting of direct P-phases, along with PKiKP and four PP phases. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 34]])

- The SASSY21 model reveals a near-vertical dipping slab beneath the North Sulawesi Trench at the northern arm of Sulawesi, descending to approximately 400 km. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 43]])
- The SASSIER22 model shows a strong positive velocity anomaly beneath the North Sulawesi Trench down to 200 km depth, aligning with maximum seismic activity. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 44]])

## Limitations

The number of seismic stations in the northeastern part of the study area limits data coverage, which restricts the ability to accurately map the geometry of the Sangihe slab in that specific region. Additionally, smearing occurs at the edges of models and beyond the horizontal bounds of the seismic array.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Further investigations are required to reach a more definitive conclusion regarding whether the north-dipping slab is a continuation of the Sangihe slab or an independent Sula slab.
