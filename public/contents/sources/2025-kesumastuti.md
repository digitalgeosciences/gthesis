# SUBDUCTING SLABS BENEATH SULAWESI, INDONESIA: NEW EVIDENCE FROM TELESEISMIC P-WAVE TOMOGRAPHY

**Summary**: The thesis utilizes teleseismic P-wave travel-time residuals to construct a 3D tomographic model of the lithosphere and upper mantle beneath Sulawesi, Indonesia. The study identifies multiple subducting slabs (Sangihe, Celebes, and Sula) and significant low-velocity anomalies potentially linked to deep mantle melting and lithosphere-asthenosphere coupling.

---

## Research Problem

The complex tectonic interaction of the Indian-Australian, Sunda, and Philippine plates in Sulawesi creates a complicated subduction environment. Precise mapping of these subducting slabs and their interactions is necessary to understand regional tectonics and the drivers of local volcanic activity.

## Objectives

- Develop a relatively high-resolution 3-D velocity model beneath Sulawesi to visualize significant tectonic structures (e.g., subducting slabs and Palu-Koro fault).
- Investigate the geometry and depth extent of the subducting slabs beneath Sulawesi, particularly in the northern region, and assess how these slabs interact with surrounding tectonic structures.
- Explore other key features beneath Sulawesi and their relationship to the complex tectonic setting of Sulawesi.
- Compare the new tomographic results with previous seismic studies and geophysical data in order to provide a more comprehensive interpretation of the tectonic setting of Sulawesi.
- Develop a 3D tomographic model of the lithosphere and upper mantle beneath Sulawesi using teleseismic P-wave data.
- Identify and characterize the geometry of subducting slabs (Sangihe, Celebes, and Sula) in the region.

## Methods

The study employs teleseismic P-wave tomography using 89 broadband stations from the BMKG network (2020–2023). Data preprocessing includes extracting relative travel-time residuals, resampling to 20 Hz, applying a bandpass filter (0.05–4.0 Hz), and calculating SNR (threshold > 1). Waveforms are aligned using the ak135 reference model. The inversion is performed using the FMTOMO software, which utilizes the multi-stage Fast Marching Method (FMM) to calculate travel times on a grid. The study also includes standard tomographic analysis steps: model parameterization (grid-based), forward calculation (multi-stage FMM), and iterative non-linear inversion with regularization (damping and smoothing).

## Data and Materials

The dataset consisted of 39,935 residual times from 985 events yielding direct P-phases, PKiKP phases, and four PP phases. The model grid covered a latitudinal range of 4.5°N to 7.5°S, longitudinal range of 117.5°E to 127.5°E, and depth from 1.7 km to 500 km.

## Key Findings

- The 3-D tomographic images consistently show a high-velocity perturbation in northern Sulawesi, which is interpreted as multiple subducting slabs. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 14]])
- Tomographic models reveal evidence for mantle upwelling due to an extensional tectonic mechanism in the Gulf of Tomini, providing a magma source for regional volcanic activity. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 14]])
- The results reveal a low-velocity zone beneath the east side of the Palu Koro Fault, interpreted as a large lithospheric structure not limited to the crust. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 14]])
- SASSY21 model shows a near-vertical dipping slab beneath the North Sulawesi Trench (NST) at the northern arm of Sulawesi, extending to approximately 400 km depth. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 43]])
- The SASSIER22 model provides a more detailed view of the slab beneath the NST, showing a strong positive velocity anomaly down to 200 km and a more diffuse, aseismic positive anomaly extending to the 410 km discontinuity. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 44]])
- A low-velocity anomaly near the Palu Koro Fault zone was detected at depths ranging from 5 to 25 km, interpreted as sedimentary layers with high rock porosity. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 47]])
- The tomographic model identified three distinct high-velocity zones (H1, H2, H3) in the northern part of Sulawesi, corresponding to the Celebes, Sula, and Sangihe slabs respectively. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 78]])
- A large zone of significant low-velocity anomalies (L1) was identified between longitudes 121°E and 123°E at depths of 300 km to 450 km, potentially indicating deep mantle melting. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 78]])
- A low-velocity zone (L2) was identified beneath the east side of the Palu-Koro Fault, extending to 100 km depth, suggesting lithosphere-asthenosphere coupling. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 93]])
- The Sangihe slab is observed to subduct westward beneath the Celebes slab, with its depth increasing from east to west. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 83]])
- A significant difference in the geometry of the north-dipping (Sula) slab was found compared to previous models, specifically showing no high-velocity anomaly west of 122.5°E. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 84]])
- The resolution tests confirmed that the tomographic model is capable of resolving structures down to a minimum scale of approximately 35 km. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 71]])
- A large volume of mantle material melting in the upper mantle was suggested as a driver for volcanic activity in the Gulf of Tomini. ([[2025-kesumastuti|Kesumastuti, 2025, p. 90]])

## Limitations

Previous global models (e.g., UUP07) had limited spatial resolution (100-200 km); local studies were often restricted to areas of active seismicity or sparse networks; teleseismic tomography faces challenges due to the irregular and unpredictable nature of earthquakes leading to potential smearing effects.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future work should include joint tomographic inversions, geochemical analysis, and numerical simulations to further refine the understanding of features beneath Sulawesi.
