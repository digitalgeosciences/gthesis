# INTEGRATED RESERVOIR CHARACTERIZATION OF THE VALANGINIAN CARBONATES IN CENTRAL SAUDI ARABIA

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the Valanginian carbonates in the Jinadriyah area of central Saudi Arabia to understand how depositional and diagenetic processes impact petrophysical properties. It utilizes microfacies analysis, XRF mapping, SEM imaging, and stable isotope geochemistry to characterize the reservoir quality.

---

## Research Problem

There is limited knowledge on how the interplay between depositional and diagenetic processes impacts the petrophysical properties of Valanginian carbonates in Saudi Arabia despite their global and regional economic importance.

## Objectives

- Understanding the spatial-temporal distribution of lithofacies and depositional environment in the early Cretaceous carbonates.
- To unravel the role of depositional and diagenetic processes in the reservoir quality.
- To perform 2D-3D reservoir modeling for the Early Cretaceous carbonates.
- Investigate the excellent exposures of Valanginian carbonates in central Saudi Arabia
- Identify microfacies and determine depositional models
- Analyze mineralogy and elemental composition using XRD and XRF
- Determine stable carbon and oxygen isotopes to assess geochemical conditions and paleotemperatures
- Quantify petrophysical parameters (porosity and permeability) across different facies
- Develop 3D reservoir models using Petrel software
- Investigate the excellent exposures of the Valanginian carbonates in central Saudi Arabia
- Analyze the impact of depositional settings on reservoir quality
- Evaluate the impact of diagenetic processes (micritization, cementation, dolomitization, dissolution, compaction, and fracturing) on reservoir quality
- Determine the paragenetic sequence of the study area
- Investigate the origin of diagenesis using elemental analysis and stable isotopes
- Evaluate the impact of depositional and diagenetic processes on reservoir properties
- Provide an integrated characterization of the carbonate system

## Methods

The study involved field sampling of 139 samples from seven sedimentary sections. Laboratory work included thin-section petrography for microfacies identification, SEM for mineral crystallization analysis, XRD and XRF for mineral/elemental composition, and stable isotope analysis (Kiel IV carbonate device and Thermo Finnigan MAT-253 plus mass spectrometer). Petrophysical measurements were conducted using a Core Test System INC AP-608 Porosimeter and an NER Autoscan helium permeameter. 3D modeling was performed in Petrel using pseudo-wells, stratigraphic correlation, and geostatistical distribution of porosity and permeability.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized core samples from the Jinadriyah area, including measurements of porosity and permeability for various microfacies (e.g., bioclastic floatstone, calcareous mudstone, peloidal bioclastic wackestone, sandy peloidal grainstone).

## Key Findings

- Five microfacies were identified, representing a carbonate ramp depositional setting, ranging from the inner ramp to outer ramp. ([[2024-allam|Allam, 2024, p. 11]])
- The δ18O and δ13C isotopic compositions showed differences of 2.94 and 0.64‰, with mean values of -3.58‰ and 0.45‰ respectively. ([[2024-allam|Allam, 2024, p. 11]])
- The δ18O studied samples shifted towards more negative values compared to early Cretaceous rocks, indicating early-stage burial diagenesis. ([[2024-allam|Allam, 2024, p. 11]])
- Elemental analysis of trace elements showed low concentrations of iron and manganese, indicating the area was not exposed to meteoric diagenesis. ([[2024-allam|Allam, 2024, p. 11]])
- The sandy peloidal grainstone facies has the highest reservoir quality properties with mean porosity of 13.3% and permeability of 1.01 mD. ([[2024-allam|Allam, 2024, p. 11]])
- The lowest reservoir quality facies is the calcareous mudstone, with mean porosity of 2.2% and permeability of 0.11 mD. ([[2024-allam|Allam, 2024, p. 12]])
- Calcite is the dominant mineral, with a mean content of 96.1% (ranging from 78.6% to 99.8%). ([[2024-allam|Allam, 2024, p. 40]])
- Quartz content in samples ranges from 0.2% to 17.4% with a mean of 2.6%. ([[2024-allam|Allam, 2024, p. 40]])
- Dolomite is the least common mineral, ranging from 0% to 7.4% (mean 1.3%). ([[2024-allam|Allam, 2024, p. 40]])
- Oxygen isotopes (δ18O) range from -5.41 to -2.47‰ (mean -3.58‰), yielding paleotemperatures between 23.7 and 37.49 °C. ([[2024-allam|Allam, 2024, p. 37]])
- Carbon isotopes (δ13C) show a narrow range from 0.13 to 0.76‰ (mean 0.45‰). ([[2024-allam|Allam, 2024, p. 37]])
- Moldic porosity is the prevailing pore type, followed by interparticle porosity. ([[2024-allam|Allam, 2024, p. 38]])
- Calcareous mudstone (MF-1) acts as a permeability barrier with low porosity (1.5-4.2%) and permeability (0.08-0.16 mD). ([[2024-allam|Allam, 2024, p. 38]])
- Sandy peloidal grainstone (MF-2) shows higher porosity (11.5-15.2%) and permeability (1.61-3.87 mD). ([[2024-allam|Allam, 2024, p. 38]])
- Peloidal bioclastic wackestone (MF-3) has porosity of 5.5-8.7% and permeability of 0.08-0.32 mD. ([[2024-allam|Allam, 2024, p. 38]])
