# WETTABILITY EVALUATION OF ROCKS USING NMR

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the use of Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) to evaluate and monitor rock wettability in both carbonate and sandstone systems. It compares NMR-based T2 and T1T2 measurements against traditional methods, demonstrating that NMR can effectively distinguish between water-wet and oil-wet states by analyzing surface relaxation effects.

---

## Research Problem

Traditional methods for evaluating rock wettability (e.g., Amott-Harvey and USBM) are time-consuming, laboratory-based, and difficult to implement in the field. There is a need for faster, potentially in-situ compatible techniques like NMR to characterize wettability in complex pore systems such as carbonates.

## Objectives

- Evaluate carbonate and sandstone samples with different wettability conditions using T2 and T1/T2 NMR measurements.
- Assess the impact of chemical treatment (HTS) on rock wettability.
- Investigate the effect of aging with crude oil containing varying asphaltene content on rock wettability.
- Determine if NMR can serve as a viable tool for monitoring wettability changes over time.
- Assess the impact of asphaltene content on T1/T2 ratio interpretation for wettability evaluation.
- Compare chemical treatment methods (HTS) for altering wettability in carbonates.

## Methods

The study employed two main approaches. In the first, Silurian dolomite (SD) and Edward Brown (EB) carbonate samples were treated with Hexadecyl-trimethoxy-silane (HTS) to alter their wettability from water-wet to oil-wet; T2, T1T2 NMR, and ultrasonic velocity measurements were conducted before and after treatment. In the second approach, Indiana limestone and Berea sandstone samples were used. The limestone was aged at 500 psi and 90°C with crude oils of different asphaltene contents (Oil 1: 5.67 wt%; Oil 2: 6.67 wt%). T2 and T1T2 NMR measurements were performed on these samples at various saturations (Sw=1, Swi, Sor) to monitor wettability changes during aging and subsequent drainage cycles.

## Data and Materials

The study used two carbonate types (Silurian dolomite and Edward Brown) for chemical treatment experiments. For the aging study, it utilized Indiana limestone (samples 1H, 2H) and Berea sandstone (samples 1S, 2S). Two crude oils were analyzed: Oil 1 (26.12 API, 5.67 wt% asphaltene) and Oil 2 (25.76 API, 6.67 wt% asphaltene). NMR measurements were conducted using a Magritek 2 MHz Rock Core Analyzer and an Oxford Instruments' Geospec2-75.

## Key Findings

- T1/T2 ratio of the non-wetting phase deviates from unity due to surface relaxation effects; in water-wet systems, the wetting phase (water) shows a shifted T1/T2 ratio while the non-wetting phase (oil) stays near 1. ([[2019-al-garadi|AL-GARADI, 2019, p. 25]])
- In the first approach, the treatment of SD with HTS successfully reduced permeability from 31.5 mD to 6.7 mD and shifted T2 peaks indicating an oil-wet state. ([[2019-al-garadi|AL-GARADI, 2019, p. 48]])
- The treatment of EB carbonate failed because the chemical was flushed out during subsequent brine flooding, resulting in a permeability that remained nearly constant (31.8 mD). ([[2019-al-garadi|AL-GARADI, 2019, p. 56]])
- T2 measurements for sample 1H showed a shift from 0.0864 s to 0.0599 s after one week of aging, indicating a move toward oil-wet conditions. ([[2019-al-garadi|AL-GARADI, 2019, p. 60]])
- Sample 2H (with higher asphaltene) showed a more significant shift in T2 peak to 0.0416 s after aging compared to sample 1H, confirming that asphaltene content is a key driver of wettability alteration. ([[2019-al-garadi|AL-GARADI, 2019, p. 62]])
- Berea sandstone samples (1S, 2S) remained strongly water-wet regardless of the oil used, with T2 values at Swi and Sor remaining identical to those at Sw=1. ([[2019-al-garadi|AL-GARADI, 2019, p. 64]])
- T2 measurements for bulk brine were 2.32 s (SD) and 2.78 s (general), while paraffin oil was 0.024 s (SD) and 0.0864 s (bulk). ([[2019-al-garadi|AL-GARADI, 2019, p. 45]])
- The T1/T2 ratio of water in the untreated SD sample shifted to 1.26, confirming a water-wet state. ([[2019-al-garadi|AL-GARADI, 2019, p. 49]])
- T1/T2 ratio of bulk brine is located on the unity line (1.0), while T1/T2 ratios for oils deviate from unity due to intrinsic properties like asphaltene. ([[2019-al-garadi|AL-GARADI, 2019, p. 68]])
- Sample 1H and 2H carbonate samples showed a shift in T1/T2 ratio above the unity line when saturated with brine, but this shift is difficult to distinguish from asphaltene effects without signal separation. ([[2019-al-garadi|AL-GARADI, 2019, p. 69]])
- The addition of 1 wt% asphaltene in sample 2H resulted in a higher T1/T2 value (2.01) compared to sample 1H (1.79) after 7 days of aging. ([[2019-al-garadi|AL-GARADI, 2019, p. 71]])
- Sandstone samples (1S and 2S) showed consistent T1/T2 values at different saturations, indicating strong water-wet properties. ([[2019-al-garadi|AL-GARADI, 2019, p. 74]])
- Chemical treatment with HTS failed to alter wettability because of incorrect concentrations and lack of aging time for chemical adsorption. ([[2019-al-garadi|AL-GARADI, 2019, p. 75]])
- T2 measurements successfully predicted that carbonate samples were strongly water-wet before aging and mixed-wet after aging. ([[2019-al-garadi|AL-GARADI, 2019, p. 76]])

## Limitations

The first approach's chemical treatment (HTS) failed for the EB sample because the concentration was too low or aging time was insufficient to ensure adsorption before subsequent flooding. T2 measurements alone can be difficult to interpret in carbonate rocks due to pore coupling effects and overlapping signals without T2-D separation.

## Recommendations and Future Work

To improve chemical treatment results, longer aging times at high temperatures are required to allow chemicals to adsorb onto the rock surface. NMR is recommended as a faster, more cost-effective tool for monitoring wettability changes in field applications compared to traditional methods like Amott or USBM.
