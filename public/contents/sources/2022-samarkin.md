# IMPROVING WEAK CARBONATE ROCKS’ HARDNESS THROUGH CHEMICAL ADDITIVES: APPLICATION TO HYDRAULIC FRACTURING

**Summary**: The thesis investigates methods to improve the mechanical properties and hardness of carbonate rocks using chemical additives (ZnSO4, NaF, and DAP) to mitigate proppant embedment and acid-related weakening in hydraulic fracturing. It concludes that all three chemicals improved rock hardness at ambient temperatures, with DAP showing the most promise for consolidation.

---

## Research Problem

The decline of fracture conductivity in hydraulic fracturing dueed to proppant embedment or asperities failure is particularly severe in soft carbonate formations (chalk, limestone, dolomite). Chemical additives are explored as a means to enhance the rock's hardness and durability against these stresses.

## Objectives

- Evaluate the ability of ZnSO4, NaF, and DAP chemicals to improve the hardness of carbonate rocks at ambient temperature.
- Assess the effect of chemical treatment on mechanical and petrophysical properties (permeability, porosity).
- Investigate changes in rock texture and mineralogy using SEM, XRD, and XPS.
- Analyze reaction kinetics between chemicals and rocks of different lithologies using IC and ICP-OES.
- Evaluate the effect of elevated temperatures on consolidation efficiency for high-temperature reservoir conditions.
- Evaluate the strengthening capabilities of several chemicals selected based on literature review.
- Determine the chemical with the best consolidating effect under ambient temperature conditions.
- Assess the consolidation of the most effective chemical at higher temperature conditions.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of zinc sulfate, sodium fluoride, and diammonium phosphate as carbonate rock consolidants.
- Assess the impact of chemical treatments on rock mineralogy, elemental content, and grain morphology.
- Quantify changes in petrophysical properties (permeability and porosity) following treatment.
- Analyze reaction kinetics to understand how different lithologies interact with chemical solutions.
- Evaluate the effect of temperature (ambient vs. elevated) on the effectiveness of the chemical additives.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of different chemical additives (ZnSO4, NaF, and DAP) on the hardness of carbonate rocks.
- Assess the impact of these treatments on the permeability and porosity of the samples.
- Investigate the effect of temperature on the consolidation process during DAP treatment.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of chemical additives (ZnSO4, NaF, and DAP) in strengthening carbonate rocks.
- Assess the impact of high-temperature conditions on consolidation efficiency.
- Investigate the relationship between improved rock hardness and changes in permeability.

## Methods

The study utilized three carbonate rock types: chalk, limestone, and dolomite. Mineralogy was analyzed via XRD; elemental content and morphology were assessed using SEM-EDS and XPS. Mechanical properties were measured using Brinell hardness tests, scratch tests (WOMBAT device), and impulse hammering (AutoscanTM). Permeability was measured using both steady-state gas injection (AutoscanTM) and unsteady-state gas permeameters (GP-101AE). Reaction kinetics were monitored via ICP-OES and IC. Treatments included zinc sulfate (ZnSO4), sodium fluoride (NaF), and diammonium phosphate (DAP) at ambient and elevated temperatures (50°C, 80°C).

## Data and Materials

The study utilizes carbonate rock samples (including limestone, marble, and dolostone) treated with various concentrations of chemical additives (e.g., 0.1M to 4.4M DAP; specific molar ratios for TEOS). The research references a variety of mineral transformation reactions resulting in minerals like smithsonite, fluorite, and hydrocerussite.

## Key Findings

- NaF treatment resulted in the formation of fluorite (CaF2) and improved indentation hardness of limestone and chalk by 3.8% and 6%, respectively. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 12]])
- ZnSO4 treatment showed better results, improving the hardness of chalk and limestone samples (impulse hammering) by 21% and 17%. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 13]])
- DAP treatment was the most promising for rock consolidation, increasing the indentation hardness of chalk by 30% and limestone by up to 13%. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 13]])
- None of the treatments could improve the hardness of the dolomite sample due to its weak reactivity. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 13]])
- All chemical treatments resulted in a decrease in the permeability of the rock samples. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 13]])
- Elevated temperatures were found to positively affect consolidation efficiency for DAP, allowing for up to 100% hardness improvement. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 13]])
- TEOS treatment can lead to up to 18% improvement in Young's modulus in limestone and up to 20% increase in drilling resistance. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 24]])
- DAP treatment at 3M concentration resulted in up to 461% increase in dynamic Young's modulus and 85% increase in ultrasonic velocity (UPV) in marble samples. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 26]])
- DAP treatment at 0.1M concentration increased the elastic modulus of limestone by 15%, while 4.4M concentration resulted in a 90% increase. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 26]])
- Calcium hydroxide nanoparticles can improve ultrasonic velocity propagation by up to 24% in dolostone and result in 3-38% increase in Young's modulus in limestone. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 30]])
- Calcium alkoxides (e.g., Ca(TEGMEE)2, Ca(TEGMME)2, Ca(NpO)2) outperformed other substances with a significant enhancement of sound velocity propagation by up to 28%. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 28]])
- Zinc sulfate (ZnSO4) treatment can result in up to a 35% increase in Young's modulus for Indiana limestone samples. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 31]])
- Zinc sulfate treatment of limestone and chalk resulted in a hardness increase of 17% and 21%, respectively. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 56]])
- The reaction of ZnSO4 with carbonate rocks produced smithsonite (ZnCO3) and gypsum (CaSO4·2H2O). ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 54]])
- Zinc ions in the solution decreased by 2000 ppm for limestone and 3500 ppm for chalk during ZnSO4 treatment. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 53]])

## Limitations

The study noted that scratch tests are not suitable for comparing hardness before and after treatment on the same spot due to surface heterogeneity. Dolomite samples showed inconsistent results due to their inherent heterogeneity and presence of vugs, leading to unreliable data for those specific rocks.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future work should test all consolidating agents at high-temperature conditions to determine feasibility in field operations. Environmental impacts of chemicals like fluoride and zinc must be considered before large-scale application.
