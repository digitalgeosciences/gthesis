# IMPROVING WEAK CARBONATE ROCKS’ HARDNESS THROUGH CHEMICAL ADDITIVES: APPLICATION TO HYDRAULIC FRACTURING

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the use of chemical additives (ZnSO4, NaF, and DAP) to improve the hardness of weak carbonate rocks (chalk, limestone, and dolomite) for hydraulic fracturing applications. It concludes that while all chemicals improved rock hardness—with DAP showing the most significant gains especially at high temperatures—they also caused substantial reductions in permeability.

---

## Research Problem

The decline in fracture conductivity in hydraulic fracturing due of proppant embedment or asperities failure is particularly severe in soft carbonate formations. Chemical additives are proposed to harden the fracture surface and mitigate these issues.

## Objectives

- Test the carbonate rock strengthening capabilities of several chemicals (ZnSO4, NaF, and DAP) at ambient temperature conditions.
- Identify the chemical with the best consolidating effect for further testing.
- Assess the consolidation of the selected chemical under elevated temperature conditions to evaluate feasibility in harsh reservoir environments.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of ZnSO4, NaF, and DAP as chemical additives for improving the mechanical properties of carbonate rocks.
- Assess the impact of these chemicals on rock permeability and porosity.
- Determine the effect of temperature on the consolidation process during treatment.

## Methods

The study utilized three types of carbonate rocks: chalk, limestone, and dolomite. Characterization included XRD for mineralogy, SEM-EDS/XPS for elemental content and morphology, and petrophysical measurements (permeability via steady-state gas injection and unsteady-state gas permeameter). Hardness was evaluated using Brinell hardness testing, scratch tests (WOMBAT device), and impulse hammering (AutoscanTM). Reaction kinetics were analyzed using ICP-OES and IC. Chemical treatments involved immersion in ZnSO4, NaF, and DAP solutions at ambient and elevated temperatures (50°C, 80°C).

## Data and Materials

Samples included chalk (Austin), limestone (Indiana), and dolomite (Guelph & Silurian). Samples were cut into 3-inch long plugs with diameters of 1.5 or 2.5 inches. Permeability values ranged from 7 to 7956 mD across the three lithologies.

## Key Findings

- Sodium fluoride (NaF) treatment resulted in the formation of fluorite (CaF2), improving the indentation hardness of limestone and chalk by 3.8% and 6%, respectively. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 12]])
- Zinc sulfate (ZnSO4) treatment improved the hardness of chalk and limestone samples by 21% and 17% using impulse hammering. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 13]])
- Diammonium phosphate (DAP) was identified as the most promising chemical for rock consolidation, increasing the indentation hardness of chalk by 30% and limestone by 13%. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 13]])
- None of the tested treatments could improve the hardness of dolomite samples due to its low reactivity. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 13]])
- All chemical treatments resulted in a decrease in the permeability of the rock samples. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 13]])
- Elevated temperatures (up to 80°C) were found to positively affect consolidation efficiency for DAP, allowing for up to 100% hardness improvement. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 13]])
- ZnSO4 treatment resulted in a 17% hardness increase for limestone and 21% for chalk, with XRD identifying the formation of smithsonite (ZnCO3) and gypsum (CaSO4·2H2O). ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 56]])
- NaF treatment at 0.9M concentration resulted in a 94% reaction efficiency, confirmed by XPS detection of fluorine and its corresponding mineral, fluorite (CaF2), on XRD. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 64]])
- DAP treatment led to the most significant hardness improvements, reaching up to 30% for chalk and 13% for limestone at ambient conditions. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 67]])
- High-temperature DAP treatments (80°C) resulted in extreme hardness increases of up to 99% for chalk samples. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 74]])
- Permeability reduction was significant across all treatments, with values reaching over 90% in several cases, primarily attributed to the formation of minerals that block pore throats. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 78]])
- Scratch tests confirmed a 15% increase in hardness for chalk and an increase from 42.7 MPa to 44.7 MPa for limestone under ZnSO4 treatment. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 57]])
- The study found that the reduction in permeability and the increase in rock hardness are interconnected; rocks with more pronounced permeability decreases showed higher hardness gains. ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 81]])
- DAP treatment at high temperatures resulted in a 63% increase in Young's modulus for chalk samples (B2C4) and up to 60.5% for limestone samples (B3L2). ([[2022-samarkin|Samarkin, 2022, p. 74]])

## Limitations

The study noted that dolomite samples showed inconsistent results due to their inherent heterogeneity and the presence of vugs, which can cause inaccuracies in both hardness and permeability measurements. Additionally, scratch tests were deemed unsuitable for comparing the same spot before and after treatment due to surface removal.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future studies should focus on optimizing chemical concentrations to minimize the adverse effects on permeability while maintaining high hardening results. Testing of other chemicals at high temperatures and conducting actual fracturing experiments are also recommended.
