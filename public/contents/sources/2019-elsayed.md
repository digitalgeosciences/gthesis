# EVALUATION OF ROCK TORTUOSITY USING PULSED FIELD GRADIENT NMR

**Summary**: The thesis evaluates and compares four types of rock tortuosity (geometric, hydraulic, electrical, and diffusion) in sandstone and carbonate samples using micro-CT imaging, electrical resistivity measurements, and Pulsed Field Gradient (PFG) NMR. The study concludes that while different definitions of tortuosity are used for various engineering purposes, they show distinct behaviors in carbonates versus sandstones due to the presence of conductive minerals like clay.

---

## Research Problem

The lack of a unified understanding of how different types of tortuosity (geometric, hydraulic, electrical, and diffusion) correlate with each other and how they are affected by rock type (carbonate vs. sandstone) in reservoir characterization.

## Objectives

- Correlating different type of tortuosity in sedimentary rocks using different techniques.
- Studying the effect of permeability combined with rock type in tortuosity.
- Evaluating diffusion tortuosity for different fluids using PFG NMR technique.
- Evaluate different tortuosity types using micro-CT, electrical resistivity, and PFG NMR techniques.
- Compare the relationship between permeability and various tortuosity definitions for both carbonate and sandstone samples.
- Determine the impact of fluid type (methane, water, dodecane) on diffusion-based tortuosity measurements.

## Methods

The study utilized 18 core samples (3 sandstones: Kentucky, Bandera, Berea; 3 carbonates: Indiana (1), Pink Desert, Indiana (2)). Micro-CT imaging was performed using a Zeiss VersaXRM-500 to create 3D pore network models for calculating geometric and hydraulic tortuosity. Electrical properties were measured using an Electrical Properties System (EPS) to determine electrical tortuosity. PFG NMR measurements were conducted using a Maran DRX-HF spectrometer with three different fluids (methane, water with 0.2 g/L HCl, and dodecane) to determine diffusion coefficients and diffusion tortuosity.

## Data and Materials

18 core samples (6 sandstone, 6 carbonate, 6 additional samples for specific tests), including 3 types of sandstone (Kentucky, Bandera, Berea) and 3 types of carbonate (Indiana (1), Pink Desert, Indiana (2)).

## Key Findings

- Tortuosity is inversely proportional to permeability; higher permeability indicates less restricted flow paths (lower tortuosity). ([[2019-elsayed|Elsayed, 2019, p. 16]])
- Micro CT was used to build 3D pore network models, showing that T2 relaxation distribution is comparable to the results from pore size and distribution analysis. ([[2019-elsayed|Elsayed, 2019, p. 16]])
- Carbonate samples exhibit a more heterogeneous pore structure compared to sandstone samples. ([[2019-elsayed|Elsayed, 2019, p. 16]])
- While permeability values were comparable between carbonate and sandstone, their tortuosity behaviors differed when plotting different definitions against each other due to differences in pore size and distribution. ([[2019-elsayed|Elsayed, 2019, p. 18]])
- Diffusion-based tortuosity (tau_d) is measured using PFG NMR by analyzing the attenuation of the NMR signal as a function of gradient strength. ([[2019-elsayed|Elsayed, 2019, p. 36]])
- The 13-interval Bipolar Gradients pulse sequence (BG-PFGSTE) is used to mitigate effects from internal magnetic field gradients caused by high mineral susceptibility differences in carbonate rocks. ([[2019-elsayed|Elsayed, 2019, p. 41]])
- Hydraulic tortuosity (tau_h) can be estimated using the Carman-Kozeny equation, which relates permeability, porosity, and surface area to pore volume ratio. ([[2019-elsayed|Elsayed, 2019, p. 50]])
- Electrical tortuosity (tau_e) is related to the formation factor (F), where higher values of cementation factor correspond to higher values of tortuosity. ([[2019-elsayed|Elsayed, 2019, p. 56]])
- Sandstone tortuosity values were found to be lower than carbonate values, ranging from 1.94 to 2.06 for sandstones versus 2.64 to 3.07 for carbonates. ([[2019-elsayed|Elsayed, 2019, p. 95]])
- A strong correlation (R² = 0.925) was found between permeability and electrical tortuosity in carbonate samples. ([[2019-elsayed|Elsayed, 2019, p. 97]])
- Sandstone also showed a strong correlation (R² = 0.788) between permeability and electrical tortuosity. ([[2019-elsayed|Elsayed, 2019, p. 98]])
- Micro-CT analysis revealed that connected porosity was significantly lower in tight samples (Indiana (1) and Kentucky) compared to higher permeability samples. ([[2019-elsayed|Elsayed, 2019, p. 99]])
- The average pore diameter for sandstone samples was generally larger than for carbonate samples. ([[2019-elsayed|Elsayed, 2019, p. 99]])
- T2 relaxation peak values confirmed petrophysical properties: Indiana (2) showed a bimodal system with a peak of 501.2 ms, while Pink Desert had a peak of 141 ms. ([[2019-elsayed|Elsayed, 2019, p. 103]])
- The free self-diffusion coefficient for bulk methane was determined to be 2.46 x 10^-7 m^2/s at 1450 psi. ([[2019-elsayed|Elsayed, 2019, p. 108]])

## Limitations

The PFG-NMR method may overestimate average pore size in shaly or low permeability rocks due to T1-weighting and the loss of signals from smaller pores with shorter T1s; however, this is less significant for sandstone.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Measure reservoir rock tortuosity and compare it with values used in Enhanced Gas Recovery (EGR) simulators for CO2 injection operations.
- Use NMR to address the problem of low resistivity high pay zones by measuring tortuosity as a way to compare electrical and diffusive tortuosity.
