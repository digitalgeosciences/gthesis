# Use of vibrator performance data to improve near-surface velocity determination

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the use of vibrator performance control data (stiffness and viscosity) to improve near-surface P-wave velocity estimation in land seismic surveys. By integrating these densely sampled parameters with sparse uphole survey data using geostatistical methods like cokriging, the study aims to produce more accurate static corrections for seismic processing.

---

## Research Problem

Reliable estimation of near-surface P-wave velocity is a key challenge in land seismic exploration because uphole survey data are often sparse and spatially limited. There is a need to find a way to utilize more densely sampled data, such as vibrator performance control parameters, to improve these models.

## Objectives

- Determine a relationship between densely sampled vibrator performance control data (stiffness, viscosity) and uphole P-wave velocity.
- Integrate vibrator performance data with uphole survey data using geostatistical techniques.
- Construct 3D near-surface velocity models from both standalone uphole data and integrated data.
- Evaluate the impact of these models on seismic statics and imaging results.
- Determine a relationship between densely sampled vibrator performance control data (e.g., ground stiffness, viscosity) and uphole P-wave velocity.
- Investigate the correlation between various vibrator performance metrics and near-surface earth properties.
- Evaluate if geostatistical methods can be used to link these two datasets for improved velocity determination.
- Determine a relationship between densely sampled vibrator performance control data and uphole P-wave velocity.
- Evaluate geostatistical methods, specifically cokriging, for integrating sparse primary measurements with dense secondary information.
- Improve near-surface velocity determination using integrated datasets.
- Evaluate the reliability of various vibrator parameters (phase, force, distortion, stiffness, viscosity) for velocity estimation.
- Integrate vibrator performance data with uphole data to improve near-surface velocity determination.
- Establish an analytical technique to estimate P-wave velocity from vibrator measured ground parameters.
- Integrate uphole and vibrator performance data using geostatistical modeling (kriging and cokriging) to improve velocity maps.
- Apply the resulting velocity models to calculate static corrections and compare them against conventional methods.
- Improve near-surface velocity determination using integrated data sets.
- Evaluate different static correction models (Frozen, 2-Layer, 3D Kriged, Integrated) for seismic processing.

## Methods

The study utilizes a combination of uphole survey data and vibrator performance control data. Vibrator data includes parameters like force, phase, and distortion to calculate ground stiffness. These values are processed through several steps: raw data collection, visual editing (removing outliers), and smoothing (applying three-sigma limits). The resulting ground stiffness data is then used in a multivariate regression model to predict P-wave velocity. This integrated velocity model is compared against models derived solely from uphole data. Finally, the impact of these different velocity models on seismic sections is evaluated by comparing static corrections.

## Data and Materials

The study utilizes vibrator performance control data (including average absolute phase error, maximum phase error, average distortion, maximum distortion, average fundamental ground force, maximum fundamental ground force, ground viscosity, and ground stiffness) from the Sercel VE 432 system. These are compared against P-wave velocity ($V_p$) measurements obtained from uphole surveys.

## Key Findings

- Vibrator performance control data (stiffness, viscosity, phase, force, distortion) showed significant correlation with uphole P-wave velocities down to 50 meters. ([[2002-al-ali|Al-Ali, 2002, p. 19]])
- A theoretical model successfully related ground parameters (viscosity and stiffness) to seismic wave velocity in an elastic half-space. ([[2002-al-ali|Al-Ali, 2002, p. 23]])
- The integrated velocity model, combining uphole and vibrator data, resulted in better seismic imaging than models using only uphole data. ([[2002-al-ali|Al-Ali, 2002, p. 19]])
- Integration of dense vibrator data with sparse uphole data resolved statics more effectively than standard geostatistical or conventional techniques. ([[2002-al-ali|Al-Ali, 2002, p. 19]])
- Ground stiffness ($K_g$) and viscosity ($\eta_g$) can be used to eliminate the dependence on density ($\rho$) and Poisson's ratio ($\nu$) when calculating P-wave velocity, as both parameters are linked to $V_p$ and $V_s$. ([[2002-al-ali|Al-Ali, 2002, p. 41]])
- The average of the $V_p/V_s$ ratio for near-surface materials is approximately 2.3, which serves as a reasonable approximation for both cohesive and non-cohesive soils. ([[2002-al-ali|Al-Ali, 2002, p. 42]])
- Rayleigh waves are responsible for the majority of displacements at the vibrator baseplate (approximately 67.3% of total energy) and typically carry information about properties within a half-wavelength of the surface. ([[2002-al-ali|Al-Ali, 2002, p. 43]])
- The Sercel VE 432 system provides eight specific performance control values, including ground viscosity and stiffness, which are derived from the Sallas (1984) model. ([[2002-al-ali|Al-Ali, 2002, p. 33]])
- Uphole surveys are inherently uncertain due to sparse sampling, making them a primary source for near-surface geology but requiring additional constraints. ([[2002-al-ali|Al-Ali, 2002, p. 46]])
- Refraction and shallow reflection surveys provide direct arrivals used to estimate the velocity of near-surface layers. ([[2002-al-ali|Al-Ali, 2002, p. 47]])
- Ground penetrating radar (GPR) and resistivity are identified as useful but costly tools for interpolating between upholes. ([[2002-al-ali|Al-Ali, 2002, p. 47]])
- The Hadrukh Formation is a continental formation of the Lower Miocene to early Burdigalian, with thickness varying from 20 to 120 meters. ([[2002-al-ali|Al-Ali, 2002, p. 65]])
- The Dam Formation contains shallow marine and warm water fossils and was deposited during a major Neogene transgression. ([[2002-al-ali|Al-Ali, 2002, p. 67]])
- The Hofuf Formation is considered the closing unit of the Arabian Tertiary deposits, with ages ranging from late Miocene to Pliocene. ([[2002-al-ali|Al-Ali, 2002, p. 69]])
- Ground stiffness showed a high correlation with uphole velocities (R=0.64) up to 40m, while ground viscosity showed a negative linear correlation (R=-0.58). ([[2002-al-ali|Al-Ali, 2002, p. 95]])

## Limitations

The study notes that measurements derived from vibrator data are only influenced by a relatively thin section of the earth' surface, leading to a drop in correlation at depth. Additionally, some uphole data were discarded due to non-linear travel times or potential errors caused by drilling-induced fractures.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Include stiffness and viscosity data in standard survey files (SPS files) for processing centers; implement geostatistical cokriging of vibrator parameters into statics calculations; use vibrator data to validate suspicious uphole measurements.
