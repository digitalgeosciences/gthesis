# Effect of drilling fluid particle sizes on formation damage application to horiz.

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of calcium carbonate particle size distribution on formation damage in Berea core samples using a water-based mud (WBM) system. It utilizes ultrasonic velocity profiling and leak-off tests to quantify invasion depth and return permeability across different flooding times.

---

## Research Problem

Formation damage caused by drilling fluids is particularly severe in horizontal wells due to their large surface area and the potential for poor filter cake development. There is a need to determine the optimal particle size distribution of bridging additives to minimize invasion depth and maximize return permeability.

## Objectives

- To investigate the effect of particle size & flooding time on damaged zone permeability using a leak-off experimental setup, and an ultrasonic technique for the measurement of mud invasion depth.
- To use the experimental results to attempt evaluation of the skin distribution along a horizontal well under specific conditions.
- Evaluate the impact of different calcium carbonate particle sizes on invasion depth.
- Determine the effect of flooding time on the advancement of drilling fluid filtrates and particles.
- Assess the return permeability of core samples after being exposed to various mud formulations.
- Quantify the relationship between bridging additive size and the formation of a protective filter cake.

## Methods

The study utilized Berea core samples as representative reservoir rock. A Leak-Off experiment was conducted using three types of polymeric water-based mud (WBM-1, WBM-2, and WBM-3) with different calcium carbonate (CaCO3) particle sizes (8, 12, and 21 microns). An ultrasonic technique was used as a non-destructive method to measure invasion depth. The impact of flooding time (4, 12, and 30 hours) was analyzed to determine the rate of filtration and skin development. Linear interpolation was used to model the skin profile along a horizontal wellbore based on drilling rates and exposure times.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized Berea core samples with an average porosity of 21.4%. Four types of polymeric water-based muds (WBM-1, WBM-2, WBM-3, WBM-4) were prepared using different sizes of calcium carbonate (e.g., 8.4 micron, 21 micron, and 124 micron). Experimental conditions included varying flooding times (4, 12, and 30 hours).

## Key Findings

- An increase in the median particle size of the bridging additive resulted in a decrease in invasion depth and an increase in return permeability. ([[2001-mohammed|Mohammed, 2001, p. 17]])
- As flooding time increases, permeability impairment and invasion depth increased, leading to an increase in the skin factor. ([[2001-mohammed|Mohammed, 2001, p. 17]])
- The largest invasion depth was observed in the first four hours of flooding due to the absence of a well-established filter cake to protect against fine particles. ([[2001-mohammed|Mohammed, 2001, p. 17]])
- The average dry sample velocity was measured at 2131 m/s. ([[2001-mohammed|Mohammed, 2001, p. 72]])
- Invasion depth decreased as the median particle size of bridging additives increased; for example, increasing from 8 μm to 41 μm reduced invasion depth by 1.35 cm. ([[2001-mohammed|Mohammed, 2001, p. 86]])
- Increasing CaCO3 bridging additive from 8 μm to 12 μm resulted in a reduction of invasion depth by over 40% (specifically, a 68% decrease was noted when moving from 8 to 12 μm for certain conditions). ([[2001-mohammed|Mohammed, 2001, p. 87]])
- Invasion depth increased with flooding time; for WBM-1 (8 μm), the rate was 1.263 cm/hr in the first 4 hours and 0.31 cm/hr thereafter. ([[2001-mohammed|Mohammed, 2001, p. 88]])
- Return permeability increased with larger particle sizes; for a 4-hour flood, increasing size from 8 to 12 μm improved return permeability by 15.5%. ([[2001-mohammed|Mohammed, 2001, p. 90]])
- The rate of invasion depth increment decreased as the particle size increased because larger particles were able to bridge pore entries more effectively. ([[2001-mohammed|Mohammed, 2001, p. 89]])
- More than 50% of filtrates were collected at the effluent in the first hour of flooding, indicating a lack of an established filter cake initially. ([[2001-mohammed|Mohammed, 2001, p. 89]])
- The inclusion of biopolymers (e.g., Xanthan Gum) alone or with small particles caused significant formation damage compared to calcium carbonate alone. ([[2001-mohammed|Mohammed, 2001, p. 105]])
- Invasion depth was found to vary as a function of the CaCO3 particulate size and contact time with the formation. ([[2001-mohammed|Mohammed, 2001, p. 107]])
- Increasing the median particle size of the bridging additive from 8 to 12 microns resulted in more than a 40% decrease in invasion depth. ([[2001-mohammed|Mohammed, 2001, p. 107]])
- The rate of invasion by drilling fluid filtrates and particles was observed to decrease as flooding time increased, due to the development of a filter cake. ([[2001-mohammed|Mohammed, 2001, p. 108]])
- Skin factor decreased significantly (by approximately 50%) when particle size was increased from 8 to 12 microns for a 4-hour flooding period. ([[2001-mohammed|Mohammed, 2001, p. 111]])

## Limitations

The range of particle sizes studied may not be sufficient enough to generalize the claim of lower invasion depth or higher return permeability with larger particle size without affecting rheology.

## Recommendations and Future Work

It is important to consider not only the invasion of larger particles but also the invasion of particulates down to colloidal sizes (like polymers) when sizing particles in drilling fluid systems.
