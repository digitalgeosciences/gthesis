# POTENTIAL SOURCES AND DISTRIBUTION OF NITRATE IN THE GROUNDWATER OF THE WASIA AQUIFER, AL-KHARJ AREA, CENTRAL SAUDI ARABIA

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the sources and distribution of nitrate in the Wasia aquifer within the Al-Kharj area, Central Saudi Arabia. It utilizes hydrochemical classification (Piper diagrams), Water Quality Index (WQI) calculations, stable isotope analysis ($\delta^{15}N$ and $\delta^{18}O$), and tritium dating to identify contamination sources such as synthetic fertilizers, atmospheric deposition, and animal waste.

---

## Research Problem

The presence of high nitrate concentrations in the Wasia aquifer groundwater in Al-Kharj area is known, but the specific sources (anthropogenic vs. natural) have not been clearly identified, especially in areas far from human activity.

## Objectives

- To investigate nitrate sources in the study area by analyzing the isotopes (15N-NO3 and 18O-NO3).
- To assess nitrate concentrations and distribution in the study area.
- To estimate recent recharge date, based Tritium 3H.
- Assess variations of major ion and trace element concentrations in the groundwater of the Wasia aquifer.
- Evaluate water quality for domestic and agricultural uses.
- Determine the hydrochemical characteristics of the groundwater in the Al-Kharj area.
- Identify the potential sources of nitrate contamination using dual isotope analysis (δ15N and δ18O).
- Estimate the recharge dates of the groundwater using tritium (3H) isotopes.
- Assess the hydrochemical classification of groundwater in the Wasia aquifer using Piper diagrams.
- Evaluate the drinking and irrigation water quality based on WHO standards and various indices (WQI, SAR, Na%, etc.).
- Determine the age and recharge history of the groundwater using tritium analysis.
- Identify potential sources of nitrate contamination using stable isotope analysis ($\delta^{15}N$ and $\delta^{18}O$).
- Map the spatial distribution of hydrochemical parameters and isotopes in the study area.
- Identify and characterize the source signatures of 15N–NO3 and 18O–NO3 in groundwater samples.
- Determine the distribution of nitrate across different sections of the study area.
- Evaluate the impact of various sources (fertilizers, atmospheric deposition, manure/wastewater) on the nitrogen budget.
- Assess the suitability of Wasia aquifer water for drinking and agricultural use.

## Methods

Hydrochemical classification was performed using Piper diagrams to categorize water types. Drinking water quality was assessed via a multi-step Water Quality Index (WQI) calculation involving 10 parameters. Irrigation suitability was evaluated using indices including EC, SAR, Na%, CR, KR, MR, and PI. Statistical analyses included Pearson's correlation matrix, dendrogram-based cluster analysis, and Principal Component Analysis (PCA). Spatial distribution of chemical constituents was mapped using Surfer 14 software. Tritium concentrations were measured via Liquid Scintillation Counting (LSC) to determine recharge history. Stable isotopes ($\delta^{15}N$ and $\delta^{18}O$) of nitrate were analyzed to differentiate between synthetic fertilizers, manure, and atmospheric deposition.

## Data and Materials

34 groundwater samples from the Wasia aquifer in the Al-Kharj area; 10 hydrochemical parameters for WQI (TDS, pH, Na+, Ca2+, Mg2+, K+, Cl-, NO3-, HCO3-, and SO42-); stable isotope data ($\delta^{15}N$, $\delta^{18}O$) for nitrate; tritium concentrations in units of TU.

## Key Findings

- Isotopic signatures of δ15N-NO3 and δ18O-NO3 revealed that atmospheric deposition and fertilizers are the potential sources of nitrate in the north and eastern sections. ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 12]])
- Manure and wastewater were identified as the main nitrate contributors in the western section of the study area. ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 13]])
- Only two samples showed tritium rates above the detection limit (0.8 TU), suggesting a mixture of recent and connate waters rather than just an absence of recharge. ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 13]])
- Wasia groundwater quality generally ranges from poor to unsuitable for drinking purposes according to Water Quality Index (WQI). ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 13]])
- All groundwater samples were considered appropriate for irrigation based on Sodium Adsorption Ratio (SAR), Magnesium Ratio (MR), and Kelly's Ratio (KR). ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 13]])
- The studied groundwater is basically corrosive (based on Corrosivity Ratio and Total Hardness) and requires attention for transport pipe selection. ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 13]])
- X-ray fluorescence of a Wasia outcrop in the Al-Kharj area provided elemental data concentrations in %. ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 11]])
- The average TDS value of the groundwater is 3032 mg/l (range: 1100-7712 mg/l), with 100% of samples exceeding the permissible limits for drinking water. ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 26]])
- The average total hardness is 1165.2 mg/l, indicating that all sampled groundwater exceeds the allowable drinking standard limits. ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 26]])
- Nitrate (NO3-) concentrations reached up to 395.2 mg/l in some wells, with an average of 68.2 mg/l. ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 26]])
- Boron (B), Manganese (Mn), and Iron (Fe) were the only heavy metals that exceeded permissible limits for drinking water. ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 26]])
- The average pH of the groundwater is 7.6, falling within acceptable limits. ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 26]])
- Groundwater levels in the study area showed a drop of 0 to 15 m compared to 2010 data, likely due to over-extraction. ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 27]])
- Groundwater samples were classified into three hydrochemical groups: Group 1 (Ca-SO4-Cl, high salinity 1584-7712 mg/l), Group 2 (Ca-SO4, 1240-6256 mg/l), and Group 3 (Ca-Na-SO4-Cl, 1100-2400 mg/l). ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 43]])
- Only one sample was found suitable for drinking water; the remaining 97.1% were classified as poor to unsuitable based on WQI. ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 45]])

## Limitations

The tritium (3H) detection limit of 0.8 TU using the enriched liquid scintillation counting (LSC) technique was insufficient to provide high precision, necessitating more advanced methods like helium (3He) ingrowth.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Wasia aquifer groundwater should be treated properly before use for human drinking.
- Frequent monitoring of groundwater quality in Al-Kharj area is required.
- Investigate nearby waste-water treatment plants for potential leaks.
- Monitor or close private wells that may serve as pathways for contamination.
