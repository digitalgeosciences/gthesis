# CHARACTERIZATION OF THE CARBONATE MUD ROCKS OF THE MIDDLE JURASSIC TUWAIQ MOUNTAIN FORMATION, JAFURAH SUB-BASIN, SAUDI ARABIA; IMPLICATION FOR UNCONVENTIONAL RESERVOIR QUALITY PREDICTION

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the unconventional resource potential of the Middle Jurassic Tuwaiq Mountain (TQMN) carbonate mudrocks in the Jafurah Sub-Basin. Using a combination of core analysis, pyrolysis data, and 2D/1D basin modeling, it evaluates thermal maturity, hydrocarbon generation, and gas retention to predict reservoir quality.

---

## Research Problem

The need to characterize carbonate mudrocks (Tuwaiq Mountain, Hanifa, and Jubaila Formations) in the Jafurah Sub-Basin as potential unconventional reservoir targets, specifically addressing gaps in understanding their source rock quality and maturity.

## Objectives

- Construct a stratigraphic framework to understand the spatial distribution of Tuwaiq Mountain lithofacies and rock characteristics using multi-scale data (outcrop, core, SEM, thin sections).
- Conduct a detailed petrophysical evaluation for Tuwaiq Mountain lithofacies using well logs and core data to identify reservoir and non-reservoir facies.
- Define the distribution of Tuwaiq Mountain source rocks by quantitative characterization of organic matter type, richness, and maturity variation, including a calibrated model for TOC and maturity prediction.
- Conduct basin modeling work to constrain the burial history and model the thermal maturation history at and within the control wells.
- Integrate results from Geoscience and Engineering data to identify optimal stimulation and reservoir drainage strategies.
- Characterize the organic richness and thermal maturity of the Tuwaiq Mountain, Hanifa, and Jubaila Formations.

## Methods

The study utilized a multi-step approach: 1) Core analysis and well-log evaluation for initial characterization. 2) Rock-eval pyrolysis (SRA and LECO analyses) to determine TOC, HI, and Tmax. 3) Extraction and isolation of kerogen from TQMN samples using the US Geological Survey method to obtain high-quality maturity data. 4) PetroMod® software for 1D and 2D basin modeling to simulate burial history, thermal evolution, and hydrocarbon generation. 5) Sensitivity analysis on oil saturation thresholds (varying from 5% to 100%) to accurately model gas retention in the source rock.

## Data and Materials

Data included core samples from five exploration wells (A, B, C, D, E) in the Jafurah Sub-Basin. Pyrolysis data were collected from approximately 700 core samples across three formations (Tuwaiq Mountain, Hanifa, and Jubaila). The study also utilized regional geological cross-sections and temperature gradient maps.

## Key Findings

- Six distinct lithofacies were identified in the Tuwaiq Mountain Formation based on rock composition, bioturbation, and organic richness. ([[2017-mohammed|Mohammed, 2017, p. 24]])
- The formation was divided into three tiers; Tier 1 (bottom) possesses the best shale gas characteristics with TOC up to 10%, low clay content (3-5%), and high porosity. ([[2017-mohammed|Mohammed, 2017, p. 16]])
- XRD analysis showed calcite as the dominant component (59-89% by volume, average 74%) and total clay content was very low (2.8-7.6%, average 5%). ([[2017-mohammed|Mohammed, 2017, p. 52]])
- SEM analysis of reservoir facies revealed a moderately to well-connected organic pore system with predominant pores less than 1µm (84% distribution). ([[2017-mohammed|Mohammed, 2017, p. 53]])
- The Tuwaiq Mountain Formation source rocks have an average TOC content of about 4.9%, and locally as high as 13.17%. ([[2017-mohammed|Mohammed, 2017, p. 58]])
- Rock-Eval pyrolysis showed a total pyrolytic yield (SI + S2) as high as 30.84 mg HC/g rock, indicating excellent source rock potential. ([[2017-mohammed|Mohammed, 2017, p. 58]])
- Mud gas isotope logging (MGIL) enabled clear distinction between Middle and Upper Jurassic source rocks based on their isotopic signature. ([[2017-mohammed|Mohammed, 2017, p. 17]])
- Basin modeling results suggest that TQMN source rocks started generating oil at about 100 Ma and reached peak oil generation at approximately 70 Ma. ([[2017-mohammed|Mohammed, 2017, p. 25]])
- Fracture half-length investigation using simulation models suggests ranges between 400 ft and 800 ft for field development planning. ([[2017-mohammed|Mohammed, 2017, p. 27]])
- The Tuwaiq Mountain Formation shows excellent source rock potential with average TOC values of 4.9% (max 13.17%) and hydrogen indices (HI) of 48 to 194 mg HC/g TOC. ([[2017-mohammed|Mohammed, 2017, p. 62]])
- The Tuwaiq Mountain Formation maturity ranges from late oil to early dry gas, with average Tmax values of 475°C and VRe values between 1.0% (Well-A) and 1.55% (Well-E). ([[2017-mohammed|Mohammed, 2017, p. 64]])
- The Jubaila Formation shows moderate source rock quality with average TOC of 2.44% and Tmax values reaching up to 469°C in the east. ([[2017-mohammed|Mohammed, 2017, p. 69]])
- Mud Gas Isotope Logging (MGIL) successfully distinguished between Tuwaiq Mountain and Jubaila source rocks, as their deuterium values are well separated despite overlapping carbon isotopes. ([[2017-mohammed|Mohammed, 2017, p. 76]])
- A correlation coefficient of 0.93 was found between Tmax-derived VRe and Bitumen-derived VRe for the Tuwaiq Mountain Formation, validating both maturity estimation methods. ([[2017-mohammed|Mohammed, 2017, p. 67]])
- The Hanifa Formation is predominantly a non-source facies in the study area but can be used to identify gas mixing from adjacent source rocks. ([[2017-mohammed|Mohammed, 2017, p. 104]])

## Limitations

Data on subsurface depth and temperature are not provided for specific wells due to company confidentiality. The maturity data used for calibration was primarily limited to the TQMN formation, which may be a limitation if the basin had undergone significant inversion.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future work should include more regional wells to further refine the mud gas isotope relationships and evaluate the effectiveness of hydraulic fracturing treatments using MGIL data.
