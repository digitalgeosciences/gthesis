# Pleistocene-holocene sedimentation along King fahd Causeway between Saudi Arabia and Bahrain

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the Pleistocene-Holocene sedimentary sequence along the King Fahd Causeway between Saudi Arabia and Bahrain. It characterizes two main mineralogical units: a lower dolomite interval showing upward-shallowing cycles and an upper non-dolomitic interval consisting of quartz sands and calcitic/aragonitic carbonates.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the lack of detailed research on Pleistocene-Holocene sediments on the Saudi Arabian side of the Arabian Gulf, specifically focusing on the transition between marine and subaerial environments in the Gulf of Salwa area.

## Objectives

- To determine the compositional and textural characteristics of the Pleistocene-Holocene sediments deposited between Saudi Arabia and Bahrain.
- To understand the nature and distribution of these sediments.
- To reveal the diagenetic processes in the carbonate sequences of these sediments.
- To integrate and use the obtained data in interpretation of the depositional and diagenetic history of these sediments, which will in turn contribute to an understanding of Pleistocene-Holocene sea level changes in this part of the Arabian Gulf.
- Identify and describe the lithofacies within the dolomite and non-dolomite intervals.
- Determine the diagenetic history, specifically the processes of dolomitization and calcitization.

## Methods

The study utilized 10 rock cores (up to 44m long) from the King Fahd Causeway project. Petrographic analysis was performed on 100 thin sections prepared from core samples. Mineralogical composition was determined using X-ray Powder Diffraction (XRD) with a Phillips diffractometer and SEM for detailed textural/compositional data. Acid wash (dilute HCl) was used to determine insoluble residue. Sieve analysis was conducted to characterize grain size distribution of oolitic and quartz sands.

## Data and Materials

10 rock cores up to 44 meters long; 100 rock samples for petrographic analysis; approximately 50 samples for XRD; 16 samples for SEM-EDS; several samples for acid wash tests.

## Key Findings

- The core sequence is divided into two mineralogical units: a lower dolomite interval (up to 40m thick) and an upper non-dolomite interval (3-9m thick). ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 19]])
- Non-dolomitic lithofacies include aragonitic oolitic sand/grainstone, aragonitic skeletal grainstone, quartz sand, calcitic skeletal grainstone, and quartz sandstone. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 44]])
- Aragonitic oolitic sands contain ooids with a diameter of 750 to 880 μm and are moderately to well sorted (Φ = -0.74 to -0.57). ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 46]])
- Aragonitic oolitic grains were found to contain approximately 60% aragonite, 20% calcite, and 20% quartz. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 48]])
- Quartz sand lithofacies contains approximately 75% quartz and 15% feldspar with a minor amount of aragonite. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 52]])
- Calcitic skeletal grainstone is characterized by a composition of about 80% calcite and 20% quartz, showing evidence of heavy leaching. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 55]])
- Dolomitic oolitic grainstones are identified based on size, shape, coating, and sorting due to extensive diagenetic alteration (dolomitization and neomorphism). ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 59]])
- The study identifies three distinct sedimentary cycles in the dolomite interval, including a transition from subtidal to restricted lagoonal environments. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 19]])
- Dolomitic green mudstone contains 4-15% clay minerals, primarily palygorskite and illite; palygorskite is confirmed as authigenic due to its delicate fibrous morphology. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 68]])
- Calcification of dolomite was caused by the seepage of Ca-rich fresh water during pluvial periods, with calcite content reaching up to 50% in green mudstone. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 94]])
- Dolomitization is interpreted as an early diagenetic process (150,000-110,000 y.b.p.) likely driven by high-Mg brines from coastal sabkhas. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 120]])
- A distinct quartz sand sheet (~7m thick) represents a period of aeolian dune migration during a major Pleistocene regression (110,000-90,000 y.b.p.) when sea level was ~70m below present. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 120]])
- The aragonitic skeletal and oolitic sand represents the most recent (Holocene) marine deposits following the Flandrian transgression (~6,000 y.b.p.). ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 121]])
- Dolomitic lithofacies represent upward-shallowing cycles from subtidal environments to restricted lagoonal environments. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 108]])
