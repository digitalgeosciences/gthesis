# Pleistocene-holocene sedimentation along King fahd Causeway between Saudi Arabia and Bahrain.

**Summary**: The thesis investigates Pleistocene-Holocene sedimentary units along the King Fahd Causeway between Saudi Arabia and Bahrain. It identifies two primary mineralogical units: a lower dolomite interval (up to 40m thick) and an upper non-dolomitic interval (3-9m thick), characterizing their depositional environments and diagenetic history.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the lack of detailed research on Pleistocene-Holocene sediments in the Saudi Arabian shallow shelf compared to other regions of the Arabian Gulf, specifically focusing on the interpretation of abrupt vertical lithological changes in rock cores.

## Objectives

- To determine the compositional and textural characteristics of the Pleistocene-Holocene sediments deposited between Saudi Arabia and Bahrain.
- To understand the nature and distribution of these sediments.
- To reveal the diagenetic processes in the carbonate sequences of these sediments.
- To integrate and use the obtained data in interpretation of the depositional and diagenetic history of these sediments, which will in turn contribute to an understanding of Pleistocene-Holocene sea level changes in this part of the Arabian Gulf.
- Identify the mineral content of rock samples using XRD analysis.
- Determine the composition and texture of various lithofacies through petrography and SEM.
- Analyze the diagenetic changes (cementation, micritization, dissolution, calcitization) in both non-dolomitic and dolomitic rocks.
- Identify and characterize the mineralogical units within the core samples along the King Fahd Causeway.
- Determine the diagenetic processes affecting dolomitic rocks, including micritization, neomorphism, and calcitization.
- Evaluate the models of dolomitization (seepage reflux vs. mixing) based on petrographic evidence.
- Reconstruct the sea-level history of the Gulf of Salwa during the Pleistocene and Holocene periods.
- Determine the depositional environments for the identified lithofacies.
- Analyze the diagenetic history of the carbonate and clastic sediments.
- Establish a timeline for sea-level changes and environmental shifts in the Gulf of Salwa.
- Analyze 10 rock cores along the King Fahd causeway to determine lithology and mineralogy.
- Identify distinct mineralogical units within the core samples.
- Evaluate the sedimentary characteristics of the Gulf of Salwa area.

## Methods

The study utilized 10 rock cores from the King Fahd Causeway. Petrographic analysis involved preparing thin sections of 100 samples, some stained with Alizarin Red "S". XRD was used to identify mineral content (aragonite, calcite, dolomite) and clay minerals in green mudstone. SEM-EDS provided compositional data on specific features like pyrite crystals. Sieve analysis was performed on oolitic sands and quartz sands to determine grain size distribution.

## Data and Materials

10 rock cores, each up to 44 meters long, sampled along a 10 km stretch of the King Fahd Causeway. The core sections include a lower dolomite interval (up to 40m thick) and an upper non-dolomite interval (3-9m thick).

## Key Findings

- The cores show two distinct mineralogical units: a lower dolomite interval up to 40 meters thick and an upper non-dolomite interval of 3 to 9 meters thickness. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 19]])
- The non-dolomitic interval consists of interbedded quartz sandstone, calcitic skeletal grainstone, quartz sand, and aragonitic skeletal and oolitic sand and grainstone. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 19]])
- The dolomitic lithofacies are thought to have been deposited in mid-Pleistocene and before the first major lowering of sea level. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 19]])
- A distinct sedimentary cycle in the dolomite includes upward shallowing sequences from skeletal packstone to green mudstone with breccia horizons. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 19]])
- The non-dolomitic layers show a history of sea level changes matching the Fairbridge (1961) profile, including a major late Pleistocene regression and a Fluvial Transgression around 6,000 y.b.p. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 19]])
- The study identified two main mineralogical units: a lower dolomite interval (up to 40m thick) and an upper non-dolomite interval (3-9m thick). ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 34]])
- Oolitic sand contains approximately 60% aragonite, 20% calcite, and 20% quartz. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 48]])
- Aragonitic skeletal grainstone contains about 60% aragonite, 20% calcite, and 10-20% quartz. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 48]])

- The lower dolomitic interval is approximately 40 meters thick and consists of two main types: green mudstone (containing palygorskite) and white skeletal wackestone/mudstone. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 79]])
- Dolomitic microspar in green mudstone is almost always associated with fibrous clay minerals, specifically palygorskite and/or illite. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 81]])
- Oolitic grainstones show evidence of dissolution-precipitation where aragonitic shells were dissolved leaving molds, while blocky calcite spar (20-40 um) precipitated around grains. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 81]])
- Pelletal grainstone/packstone shows extensive neomorphism to microspar (5-10 um), with some areas showing complete recrystallization leaving 'ghosts' of the original texture. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 83]])
- Dolomitization is interpreted as an early diagenetic process because original textural features are well preserved and there is a sharp stratigraphical boundary between dolomitic and non-dolomitic facies. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 87]])
- The seepage reflux model for dolomitization is supported by the presence of gypsum (up to 10% in nearshore boreholes) as a byproduct of high-Mg brine migration from coastal sabkhas. ([[1988-darwish|Darwish, 1988, p. 90]])
