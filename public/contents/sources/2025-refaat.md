# Spatial variations in the pore system of the burrow fillings and their impact on the reservoir quality – A case study from Hanifa Formation

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of burrow fillings on reservoir quality in the Hanifa Formation, specifically focusing on how depositional textures and diagenetic processes like dolomitization and compaction affect porosity and pore systems. Using a combination of thin-section petrography, micro-CT scanning, and digital rock physics modeling, it concludes that mud-rich packstones with high dolomite content maintain better reservoir quality than mud-poor packstones under compaction.

---

## Research Problem

The influence of pore system variations within burrow fillings on storage capacity and fluid flow in carbonate reservoirs remains understudied, particularly regarding how different burrowing styles (e.g., pipe framework vs. bio-retexturing) and subsequent diagenetic modifications affect the final reservoir quality.

## Objectives

- Determine how the pore system and porosity vary vertically within the burrow fillings and how they are affected by sedimentological and diagenetic processes.
- Evaluate how the porosity and permeability within the burrow fillings of packstone fabric evolved over time.
- Determine if spot permeability measurements accurately reflect the bulk permeability of bioturbated reservoirs.
- Assess the impact of rock texture heterogeneity within burrow fillings on porosity and pore systems.
- Investigate the influence of burrowing style and depositional textures on grain size distribution and shape.
- Evaluate the role of diagenetic modifications (dolomitization, compaction, dissolution) in altering original sedimentary characteristics.

## Methods

The study utilized a multi-scale approach: 1) Field observation and sampling at Jabal Al-Abakkayn to identify five burrowed intervals. 2) Petrographic analysis of thin sections (stained with blue-dyed epoxy) using an AxioScan system and ZEISS ZEN 3.8 software to characterize textures and diagenesis. 3) Medical CT scanning (Toshiba Alexion TSX-032A) for large core segments, processed via OsiriX and converted to SEG-Y for Petrel analysis. 4) High-resolution micro-CT scanning (Versa XRM-5002D) of smaller burrow filling samples at 9 μm resolution. 5) Digital rock modeling in Avizo using Non-Local Means filtering, thresholding, and Top-Hat tools for segmentation. 6) Pore Network Modeling (PNM) to quantify connectivity and calculate pore statistics (volume, area, diameter). 7) Spot permeability measurements using the AutoScan system.

## Data and Materials

The study analyzed samples from five bioturbated stratigraphic units in the upper Hanifa Formation. Data includes thin-section photomicrographs, micro-CT scan segments for pore/matrix/grain analysis, and statistical data on grain size (mean, standard deviation, aspect ratio) and pore dimensions (volume, area, diameter).

## Key Findings

- Bio-retextured intervals (2 and 3) exhibited smaller grain sizes (355–402 μm) and lower porosity (1.64–2.71%) compared to pipe-framework intervals. ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 34]])
- Pipe-framework intervals (1, 4, and 5) had larger grains (514–766 μm) and higher porosity (4–7.75%). ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 34]])
- Dolomitization was identified as a key factor preserving porosity in mud-rich packstones by resisting compaction. ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 34]])
- Compaction caused significant porosity reduction in mud-poor packstones with lower dolomite content. ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 34]])
- Open-fabric, less-compacted grainstones achieved up to 20% porosity and >5000 mD permeability. ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 20]])
- Even with >10% mud content, connected pore networks in open-fabric burrow fillings maintained permeability (>1000 mD). ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 20]])
- The pore system predominantly consists of isolated pores with vertical variations in volume, surface area, and equivalent diameter. ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 20]])
- Burrow fillings in pipe framework intervals (1, 4, 5) showed higher porosity values (7.15%, 4.00%, and 7.75%) compared to bio-retexturing intervals (2, 3), which had lower values of 1.64% and 2.71%. ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 60]])
- Grain size in burrow fillings showed no continuous vertical trend across the five intervals but was grouped by style: pipe framework intervals exhibited larger mean grain sizes (e.g., 766 ± 752 µm in interval 1) compared to bio-retexturing intervals. ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 53]])
- Aspect ratio of grains, used as a proxy for shape, showed no significant variation across the five intervals (mean values between 0.62 and 0.65). ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 56]])
- Dolomitization occurred preferentially in the muddy matrix of burrow fillings; mud-rich packstones (e.g., interval 5) resisted compaction better than mud-poor packstones (e.g., intervals 1 and 4). ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 72]])
- The largest mean pore volume was found in interval 4 (0.0062 ± 0.0118 mm³), followed by interval 1 (0.0059 ± 0.0036 mm³). ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 63]])
- The pore system is predominantly composed of isolated pores across all studied intervals. ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 62]])
- A clear correlation was found between porosity, compaction, and mud matrix percentage: higher mud content led to lower compaction and higher porosity. ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 61]])

## Limitations

Previous studies often relied on visual estimation of porosity from thin sections (ignoring heterogeneity) or spot permeability measurements which fail to capture the complexity of interconnected vs. isolated pore systems in bioturbated rocks.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The findings suggest that integrating petrographic analysis, CT scanning, and digital rock physics modeling is essential for predicting fluid flow behavior in complex bioturbated strata where direct measurement is difficult.
