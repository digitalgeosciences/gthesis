# Spatial variations in the pore system of the burrow fillings and their impact on the reservoir quality – A case study from Hanifa Formation

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of burrow fillings on reservoir quality in the Hanifa Formation, specifically focusing on how pore system variations within these structures affect storage capacity and fluid flow. By integrating petrographic analysis, micro-CT scanning, and digital rock modeling, it concludes that while spot permeability measurements may suggest high connectivity, only integrated modeling accurately captures the complex interplay between burrow morphology and pore architecture.

---

## Research Problem

The influence of pore system variations within burrow fillings on storage capacity and fluid flow in carbonate rocks is understudied. Specifically, the lack of detailed data on how sedimentological and diagenetic processes affect the vertical variation of the pore system within these structures limits accurate reservoir evaluation in the Han1fa Formation.

## Methods

The study utilized an integrated workflow: 1) Field sampling of five boulder-sized samples from the Hanifa Formation at Jabal Al-Abakkayn. 2) Petrographic analysis of thin sections (3.8 cm plugs) using AxioScan and ZEISS ZEN 3.8 software to characterize textures, diagenesis, and pore types. 3) Medical-CT scanning (Toshiba Alexion TSX-032A) of core segments to map burrow morphology. 4) Processing CT data via OsiriX and PerGeos into Petrel for volume analysis. 5) Micro-CT scanning (Versa XRM-5002D) of smaller samples to obtain high-resolution images. 6) Digital rock physics modeling using Avizo software, involving subvolume extraction, Non-Local Means filtering, thresholding segmentation, and Pore Network Modeling (PNM) to quantify pore volumes, connectivity, and geometry.

## Data and Materials

Samples from the Upper Hanifa Formation featuring Thalassinoides burrows; one slabbed boulder-size sample; 64 spot permeability measurements (32 matrix, 32 burrow filling); three micro-CT scans of 5mm x 5mm samples for pore network modeling.

## Key Findings

- Two primary burrowing styles were identified: 'pipe framework' (characterized by a mud-poor, grain-dominated texture) and 'bio-retexturing' (characterized by a more complex, often compacted fabric). ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 26]])
- Pore systems in all studied burrow fillings were found to be substantially isolated, yet interval 4 exhibited the largest pore volumes. ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 38]])
- page_number_reference_note_for_finding_2_is_actually_on_page_38_in_the_figure_captions_description ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025]])
- Bio-retextured intervals (2 and 3) exhibited smaller grain sizes (355-402 μm) and lower porosity (1.64-2.71%). ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 20]])
- Pipe-framework intervals (1, 4, and 5) had larger grains (514-766 μm) and higher porosity (4-7.75%). ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 20]])
- Dolomitization preserved porosity in mud-rich packstones by resisting compaction. ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 33]])
- Mud-poor packstones with lower dolomite content exhibited significant porosity reduction due to compaction. ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 33]])
- Grain-supported burrow fillings showed limited improvement in porosity post-compaction, while less compacted, open-fabric grainstones achieved up to 20% porosity and >5000 mD permeability. ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 20]])
- Even with >10% mud content, connected pore networks in open-fabric burrow fillings maintained permeability (>1000 mD). ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 20]])
- The pore system predominantly comprises isolated pores with vertical variations in volume, surface area, and equivalent diameter. ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 33]])
- The study identified two distinct burrowing styles: 'pipe framework' (intervals 1, 4, and 5) and 'bio-retexturing' (intervals 2 and 3). ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 50]])
- Pipe framework burrows are characterized by distinct burrow fillings and host rock matrix with clear boundaries and scratch marks. ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 50]])
- Bio-retexturing burrows result in a homogenization of the burrow fillings and host rock matrix, where boundaries are not distinct. ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 51]])
- Host rock matrix in pipe framework intervals consists mainly of wackestone or fused peloidal packstone with high mud content (e.g., 9.36% to 12.54%). ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 51]])
- Burrow fillings in pipe framework intervals exhibit a grain-dominated texture (mainly packstone) with significantly lower mud content (as low as 3.29%) compared to bio-retexturing intervals. ([[2025-refaat|Refaat, 2025, p. 52]])

## Limitations

Spot permeability measurements are limited by their inability to account for isolated pore systems, potentially leading to overestimation of flow capacity in disconnected pores due to local gas diffusion during measurement.

## Recommendations and Future Work

A multi-faceted approach integrating petrographic analysis, spot measurements, and digital rock modeling is essential for accurate assessment of reservoir quality in heterogeneous bioturbated systems.
