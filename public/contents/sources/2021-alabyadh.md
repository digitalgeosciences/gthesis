# MICROFACIES AND DIAGENESIS OF LATE JURASSIC - EARLY CRETACEOUS INNER SHELF - OOLITIC SHOAL DEPOSITS OF MONTE SACRO SEQUENCE (GARGANO PROMONTORY, ITALY)

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the microfacies and diagenetic development of a Late Jurassic to Early Cretaceous carbonate sequence in the Apulia Carbonate Platform (ACP). Using petrographic and geochemical analyses, it identifies 11 distinct microfacies and evaluates how various diagenetic alterations, particularly blocky calcite cement, impacted porosity.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the lack of high-resolution microscale data regarding microfacies and diagenetic alterations in the Gargano Promontory successions, specifically focusing on the transition from inner shelf to oolitic shoal settings within the Monte Sacro sequence.

## Objectives

- Identify, describe, and interpret different lithofacies along the transition between Sannicandro and Monte Spigo formations.
- Use thin section petrographic analysis to identify microfacies.
- Integrate mineralogical and geochemical data to explain the effects of diagenetic and depositional processes on various lithofacies.
- Construct a paragenetic sequence to reveal the timing of different diagenetic phases.
- Characterize the porosity modification processes in the facies.
- Identify and classify microfacies within the Costa Callese stratigraphic section.

## Methods

The study involved field work in Gargano Promontory (July-August 2018) to collect samples from three locations. Petrographic analysis was performed on 76 thin sections using an Axio Scan Slide Scanner and manual observation of minerals, cements, and porosity. XRD (using the Malvern Panalytical Empyrean) was used for mineralogical identification of carbonate and evaporite minerals. QEMSCAN was applied to 7 thin sections for mineral mapping and porosity estimation via void space area %. ImageJ software was utilized for ooid grain size distribution analysis and 2-D porosity estimation from thin sections. R software was employed for statistical analysis of ooid grain sizes.

## Data and Materials

76 thin sections (prepared in both polished and non-polished states), 76 hand specimen samples for XRD, and 7 thin sections analyzed via QEMSCAN; data collected from the Costa Callese area of the Gargano Promontory.

## Key Findings

- Eleven microfacies were identified, including mudstone, skeletal wackestone, packstone, and grainstone fabrics with varying components such as peloids, dasyclad algae, and ooids. ([[2021-alabyadh|Alabyadh, 2021, p. 13]])
- Several diagenetic alterations were recognized: microbial micritization, early marine circumferential fibrous to bladed cement, blocky calcite cement, dedolomite, and rhombohedral molds. ([[2021-alabyadh|Alabyadh, 2021, p. 13]])
- Blocky calcite cement was identified as a primary factor in reducing porosity within the carbonate rocks. ([[2021-alabyadh|Alabyadh, 2021, p. 13]])
- The Sannicandro Formation is characterized by peritidal parasequences (1-5m thick) representing lagoonal to supratidal environments, with an estimated thickness of 200-250 m. ([[2021-alabyadh|Alabyadh, 2021, p. 36]])
- The Monte Spigno Formation consists of oolitic and oncolitic grainstones, interpreted as a high-energy shallow-water environment, with an estimated thickness of 300-400 m. ([[2021-alabyadh|Alabyadh, 2021, p. 37]])
- Microfacies MF1 and MF2 are interpreted as being deposited in a supratidal setting due to the presence of mud intraclasts and evidence of high-energy events (storms or surges). ([[2021-alabyadh|Alabyadh, 2021, p. 89]])
- MF3 is characterized by a subtidal facies with microbial laminations and diverse skeletal grains. ([[2021-alabyadh|Alabyadh, 2021, p. 66]])
- MF4 and MF5 are interpreted as being deposited in a shallow, restricted lagoon due to low diversity of biocomponents and high abundance of peloids and faecal pellets (Favreina spp.). ([[2021-alabyadh|Alabyadh, 2021, p. 89]])
- MF6 and MF7 represent more open lagoonal environments with higher faunal diversity, including dasyclad algae (Campbelliella striata), benthic foraminifera, and mollusks. ([[2021-alabyadh|Alabyadh, 2021, p. 90]])
- The presence of ooid beds between different microfacies is interpreted as washover deposits. ([[2021-alabyadh|Alabyadh, 2021, p. 90]])
- Oolitic grainstone facies (MF9) are typically 30-70cm thick and contain well-rounded ooids (500µm) and larger, irregularly shaped ooids. ([[2021-alabyadh|Alabyadh, 2021, p. 100]])
- Oolitic peloidal packstone (MF10) is characterized by small peloids, moderately sorted ooids, and intraclasts (>2000 µm). ([[2021-alabyadh|Alabyadh, 2021, p. 104]])
- Ooid sizes in the Costa Callese section range from 0.06 to 2.5 mm with an average of 0.6 mm. ([[2021-alabyadh|Alabyadh, 2021, p. 113]])
- XRD and QEMSCAN analyses revealed a dominant calcite composition (>99%) in the samples, indicating minimal clastic input. ([[2021-alabyadh|Alabyadh, 2021, p. 124]])
- Diagenetic features include microbial micritization of bioclasts (e.g., dasyclad algae), isopachous fibrous/bladed cement, and dedolomitization. ([[2021-alabyadh|Alabyadh, 2021, p. 130]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Compare microfacies and diagenetic features from other stratigraphic sections in the Monte Sacro Sequence.
- Perform stable oxygen isotope analysis to determine cement precipitation temperatures and timing.
- Conduct Cathodoluminescence (CL) to establish relative times of different cement types.
