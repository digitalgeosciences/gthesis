# Near-surface Investigation of The Dammam Dome on The KFUPM Campus using The Gravity Method

**Summary**: The thesis investigates near-surface fault structures and cavity potential in the Dammam Dome on the KFUPM campus using gravity survey methods. It integrates high-accuracy topography mapping via GPS with gravity data processing, including spectral analysis and second vertical derivatives, to map subsurface density distributions.

---

## Research Problem

The identification of hazardous features like faults and cavities (which have collapse potential) in the D1ammam Dome area is critical for geotechnical safety, but traditional drilling methods are costly and limited in depth. A non-invasive gravity method approach is needed to map these structures effectively.

## Methods

The study involved a gravity survey of 500 stations over a 5 km2 area. Data were corrected for tide, drift, and terrain (using SRTM DEM). Regional-residual separation was performed using 2D Fourier transforms with Butterworth filters in Oasis Montaj software. Second vertical derivatives (SVD) were calculated to enhance near-surface features. A synthetic model was used to create an empirical relationship between fault dip and Delta SVD values. Forward modeling (Talwani algorithm) was applied to specific profiles showing high density contrast to identify karst potential.

## Data and Materials

Gravity measurements were taken over a 5 km2 area with a grid spacing of approximately 50 meters. Differential GPS data for 78 stations (implied by the standard deviation analysis) were used for positioning.

## Key Findings

- Sixty-seven dominant fault structures were interpreted in the study area. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 15]])
- The dominant structural trends are NW-SE and NE-SW, with average fault strike directions of N 39° W and N 48° E. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 15]])
- From the second vertical derivative and fault dip modeling analysis in the 4 fault lines, the dominant type of faults are thrust faults. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 15]])
- The calculated fault dips for the four analyzed lines were 21°, 15°, 28°, and 67°. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 15]])
- Terrain correction values derived from the Hammer chart method ranged from 0.009 to 0.062 mGal. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 12]])
- The study area covers approximately 5 km2 with a grid spacing of roughly 50 meters. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 21]])
- The Rus Formation thickness on the Dammam Dome outcrop is 50 m. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 25]])
- The Umm Er Radhuma (UER) formation can reach a subsurface thickness of up to 500 m. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 26]])
- The Bouguer density used for correction is 2.3 g/cm3, based on the average density of the Rus and UER formations. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 37]])
- A synthetic model was developed with fault dip angles ranging from 10 to 90 degrees to compare against SVD values. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 46]])
- The study identified a high concentration of fault structures in the near-surface region using SVD analysis. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 77]])
- Thrust faults were found to be dominant in the area, with specific dip angles calculated as 21°, 15°, and 28° for three different lines. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 73]])
- A normal fault was identified on Line-4 with a calculated dip of 67°. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 73]])
- The average orientation for the NW and NE fault directions were determined to be N 39°W and N 48°E respectively. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 71]])
- A karstified formation was identified with a density of approximately 1.56 g/cm3 for the Umm Er Radhuma (UER) formation, indicating potential cavities filled with partially saturated sand. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 76]])

## Limitations

The fault dip estimation is limited by the empirical nature of the formula, which assumes fixed parameters for formation thickness, density, and depth; variations in these local parameters can introduce errors in the calculated angles.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Use seismic refraction, resistivity, or TDEM surveys to better define near-surface layer interfaces.
- Employ inversion methods to validate and enhance forward models.
- Perform joint interpretations using a wider range of data (seismic, satellite/airborne gravity, magnetic).
- Conduct drilling to tie geophysical analysis directly to the geology.
