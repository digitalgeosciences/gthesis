# Near-surface Investigation of The Dammam Dome on The KFUPM Campus using The Gravity Method

**Summary**: The thesis investigates near-surface fault structures and cavity potential in the Dammam Dome on the KFUPM campus using gravity survey methods. It utilizes a combination of gravimetric data, second vertical derivative (SVD) analysis, and forward modeling to identify 67 faults and assess karstification risks.

---

## Research Problem

The identification of hazardous near-surface features such as fault structures and potential cavities in the Dammam Dome area, which are critical for geotechnical safety and understanding local geological evolution.

## Objectives

- Quantitatively map the near-surface structure and cavity potential using the gravity method.
- Estimate the subsurface density distribution of the study area.
- Analyze and interpret the near-surface structure and likelihood of cavities in the Dammam Dome on KFUPM campus by studying fault structures.
- Invert gravity data with rock density and structural constraints from laboratory measurements and literature to estimate fault dips.
- Identify fault structures and cavity potential.
- Analyze gravity data using second vertical derivative methods.

## Methods

The study involved a 500-station gravity survey using a Scintrex CG-5 Gravimeter and Leica GPS 1200+ for high-accuracy positioning. Data reduction included drift, tide, latitude, free-air, Bouguer, and terrain corrections to produce a Complete Bouguer Anomaly (CBA) map. Regional-residual separation was performed using 2D Fourier Transform (FFT) with Butterworth filters in Oasis Montaj software. The second vertical derivative (SVD) was applied to the residual field to enhance near-surface features. Fault dip estimation was conducted by comparing SVD values against a synthetic model of varying fault angles. Forward modeling using the Talwani algorithm was used to characterize density variations and identify karstified zones.

## Data and Materials

500 gravity measurements over an area of approximately 5 km2; high-resolution terrain data from SRTM satellite (1-arcsecond resolution); laboratory density values for Rus and Umm Er Radhuma formations; 67 identified fault structures.

## Key Findings

- Sixty-seven dominant fault structures were interpreted, with primary orientations of NW-SE (N 39° W) and NE-SW (N 48° E). ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 15]])
- The majority of identified faults are thrust faults, with specific dip angles calculated as 21°, 15°, 28°, and 67° for four primary lines. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 73]])
- A fault at Line-4 was identified as a normal fault with an estimated dip of 67°. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 73]])
- The average density for the Rus formation is approximately 2.31 g/cm³ (Lower: 2.28, Middle: 2.14). ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 47]])
- Karstified zones were identified as having significantly lower densities (e.g., Umm Er Radhuma Karstified at 1.56 g/cm³ compared to 2.56 g/cm3 for the standard formation). ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 76]])
- The terrain correction values ranged from 0.009 to 0.062 mGal. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 55]])
- The second vertical derivative method indicates that near-surface structures are dominated by a high concentration of fault structures. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 77]])
- Thrust faults are dominant in the near-surface, as evidenced by higher values in the second vertical derivative analysis. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 77]])
- The primary force in the study area is attributed to the dome structure, which results in thrust faulting. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 77]])
- Normal faults are identified as local structures affected by main tensional stress. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 77]])
- The dominant fault orientations are NW and NE, with average directions of N 48° E and N 39° W. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 77]])
- Fault dip angles in four interpreted lines were measured at 21°, 15°, 28°, and 67°. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 77]])
- Karstified formations are modeled as having cavities filled with partially saturated sand. ([[2018-hanif|Hanif, 2018, p. 77]])

## Limitations

Fault dip estimation is limited by the empirical nature of the formula, which assumes fixed parameters for formation thickness and density; variations in local geology may cause deviations from the model's predicted values.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Perform seismic refraction, resistivity, or TDEM surveying to better define the near-surface layer interface for forward modeling.
- Use inversion methods to validate and enhance the forward model.
- Conduct joint interpretation using a wider data range (seismic, satellite/airborne gravity, magnetic methods).
- Undertake drilling to tie analysis to geology and validate the model.
