# Interaction between modern dune and interdune deposits in North of Al Jafurah Sand Sea and their significance to Reservoir Heterogeneity and Architecture, Eastern Saudi Arabia.

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the geomorphology and sedimentological interaction between dune and interdune deposits in the Jafurah Sand Sea. Using a combination of satellite imagery, field trenching, and Ground Penetrating Radar (GPR), it characterizes how wind regimes and fluctuating water tables influence the complexity of these aeolian systems.

---

## Research Problem

The lack of understanding regarding the interaction between dune and interdune depositional facies leads to significant reservoir heterogeneity in aeolian systems, complicating subsurface modeling and exploration.

## Objectives

- Identify and document the spatial extent of modern interdune and dune bedforms.
- Study the relationship between aeolian sub-environments (dune, interdune, and sand sheet) from central to marginal areas.
- Construct a facies depositional model of the ae system for the study area.
- Investigate the aeolian bedform internal structure using GPR.
- Study the relationship between the aeolian system and adjoining systems (e.g., fluvial and marine systems).
- Study the effect of fluctuated water table on the development of the aeolian system.

## Methods

The study employed a three-part methodology: 1) Remote sensing analysis using high-resolution satellite imagery (Google Earth Pro and ESRI Imagery) to map dune types and identify locations for field work. 2) Field work involving direct measurements in trenches (dug or by bulldozer) and the collection of shallow core samples from both wet and dry settings. 3) Laboratory work including grain texture analysis (sieving) and petrography to determine sedimentological properties. Additionally, Ground Penetrating Radar (GPR) using a GSSI SIR-3000 system with a 100 MHz antenna was used to map internal structures of dunes; data were processed via time zero correction, background removal, migration, and topographic correction/depth conversion.

## Data and Materials

Satellite imagery from Google Earth Pro (10-50m resolution) and ESRI Imagery (2m resolution); 8 GPR profiles covering a single dune area of 100x200 meters; samples from trenches and shallow core tubes; rainfall data for the region over several decades.

## Key Findings

- Dunes in the study area are primarily Barchanoid types (Barchans, Barchanoid ridges) indicating unimodal wind regimes with a north-to-south migration path. ([[2022-al-alawi|Al-Alawi, 2022, p. 16]])
- Parabolic dunes are common and stable due to the presence of vegetation, often occurring in areas where the water table is higher. ([[2022-al-alawi|Al-Alawi, 2022, p. 16]])
- The Jafurah Sand Sea covers approximately 40,000 km2 and is influenced by a high humidity belt, leading to interactions between aeolian and fluvial systems. ([[2022-al-alawi|Al-Alawi, 2022, p. 15]])
- Interdune deposits are classified into dry, damp, wet, or evaporitic based on moisture levels; wet systems have thicker interdune flat accumulations due to water table influence. ([[2022-al-alawi|Al-Alawi, 2022, p. 38]])

- A single barchan dune was tracked over 15 years and migrated approximately 180 meters toward the South-East. ([[2022-al-alawi|Al-Alawi, 2022, p. 75]])
- GPR successfully mapped internal structures of dunes, identifying truncation, downlap, and bounding surfaces to confirm a unidirectional wind regime. ([[2022-al-alawi|Al-Alawi, 2022, p. 111]])

## Limitations

The study notes that while 2D GPR lines were effective for characterizing internal structures and migration direction, a 3D data cube would be highly recommended to fully map aeolian bounding surfaces.

## Recommendations and Future Work

A 3D data cube is recommended for future GPR analysis to better map the complex bounding surfaces of aeolian systems.
