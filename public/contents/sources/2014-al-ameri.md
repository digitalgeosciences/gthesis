# EFFECT OF CO2 SEQUESTRATION ON THE MECHANICAL PROPERTIES OF CARBONATE ROCKS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of CO2 sequestration on the mechanical properties (porosity, permeability, and ultrasonic wave velocities) of three carbonate rock types: Indiana limestone, Pink Deseret limestone, and Khuff limestone. The study utilizes a combination of petrographic analysis (thin-section, XRD, SEM), geomechanical testing (UCS, ITS), and ultrasonic velocity measurements to evaluate the effects of CO2 exposure.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the uncertainty regarding the long-term mechanical stability and petrophysical integrity of carbonate reservoirs (such as limestone and dolomite) when used for CO2 sequestration, specifically focusing on the effects of supercritical CO2 injection and varying brine chemistries.

## Objectives

- Evaluate the effect of CO2 sequestration on the mechanical properties of carbonate rocks by measuring the properties before and after soaking with CO2.
- Investigate the impact of CO2 storage on the mechanical parameters based on the sequestration time (14 days, 30 days, and 90 days).
- Select good candidate carbonate rocks for geologic sequestration of CO2.
- Investigate the influence of CO2 storage on the mechanical properties of carbonate aquifers and cap rocks.
- Evaluate the impact of different brine compositions (e.g., NaCl, CaCl2, MgCl2) on core permeability.
- Compare the petrophysical and mechanical properties of limestone and dolomite before and after CO2 exposure.

## Methods

The study employs a multi-stage experimental approach: 1. Core Preparation: Samples from Indiana limestone, Pink Desert limestone, and Khuff Limestone were cut into cylindrical plugs using a core cutter and ground to ensure parallel ends within 0.025 mm. 2. Cleaning/Drying: Samples were cleaned of salts using a Soxhlet extractor with ethanol and dried in a vacuum oven at 80°C for two days. 3. Fluid Characterization: Brine density was measured via pycnometer, and viscosity was determined using an Oswald viscometer. 4. Core Analysis: Porosity was calculated from weight/volume measurements; permeability was assessed through core flooding. 5. Petrography: Thin sections were prepared with blue-dyed epoxy for polarized light microscopy to analyze mineralogy and pore types. 6. Mineralogy: XRD (Rigaku Miniflex III) was used for quantitative mineral phase analysis. 7. Microscopy: SEM-EDS (FE-SEM Model FEI Nova Nano SEM 230) provided high-resolution imaging of textures and chemical composition of grains. 8. Geomechanics: Triaxial tests were conducted to determine Young's modulus, Poisson's ratio, and ultimate strength under varying confining pressures.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized core samples from three specific formations: Indiana limestone, Pink Desert limestone, and Khuff Limestone. Additionally, shale samples were used for cap rock analysis. Synthetic brine (TDS 213,734 mg/L) was prepared with specific concentrations of NaHCO3, Na2SO4, CaCl2·2H2O, MgCl2·6H2O, and NaCl.

## Key Findings

- Long time soaking of CO2 in brine allowed for the formation of enough carbonic acid to react with the cores, significantly impacting rock mechanical and acoustic properties. ([[2014-al-ameri|AL-AMERI, 2014, p. 25]])
- The significant impact of CO2 storage was noted on Khuff limestone. ([[2014-al-ameri|AL-AMERI, 2014, p. 25]])
- Indiana limestone was found to be a good candidate among the carbonate rocks studied for geological sequestration of CO2. ([[2014-al-ameri|AL-AMERI, 2014, p. 25]])
- P-wave velocity (Vp) decreased significantly under all conditions when core samples were subjected to CO2 flooding. ([[2014-al-ameri|AL-AMERI, 2014, p. 38]])
- S-wave velocity (Vs) was reduced in most cases, but showed a slight increase at high effective pressures in some samples. ([[2014-al-ameri|AL-AMERI, 2014, p. 38]])
- The average seismic velocities remained almost unchanged when temperature and pressure were kept constant near the CO2 critical point. ([[2014-al-ameri|AL-AMERI, 2014, p. 29]])
- Porosity and permeability of core samples were reduced in some carbonate formations (e.g., Khuff) due to precipitation of reaction products. ([[2014-al-ameri|AL-AMERI, 2014, p. 43]])
- In other cases, dissolution of the microcrystalline matrix led to porosity enhancement. ([[2014-al-ameri|AL-AMERI, 2014, p. 44]])
- CO2 injection caused an increase in permeability and enhancement in porosity due to the dissolution of fines that were originally impeding flow. ([[2014-al-ameri|AL-AMERI, 2014, p. 45]])
- In limestone cores, NaCl-brine injection maintained constant permeability, while CaCl2 and MgCl2 brine caused core damage (reduction in permeability). ([[2014-al-ameri|AL-AMERI, 2014, p. 46]])
- For dolomite cores, brine composition was identified as the primary factor affecting chemical reactions during CO2 sequestration; injection flow rate had no significant impact. ([[2014-al-ameri|AL-AMERI, 2014, p. 47]])
- In Pink Desert limestone, core permeability increased from 56.5 to 60.6 mD at 70°F but was damaged at 200°F. ([[2014-al-ameri|AL-AMERI, 2014, p. 47]])
- Supercritical CO2 injection into Indiana limestone led to a significant reduction in rock strength, Young's modulus, and Poisson's ratio, with the friction angle decreasing from 42° to 26°. ([[2014-al-ameri|AL-AMERI, 2014, p. 48]])
- In Ekofisk Formation (low carbonate content), CO2 injection had no significant effect on mechanical strength or time-dependent behavior compared to high-carbonate Tor Formation samples. ([[2014-al-ameri|AL-AMERI, 2014, p. 51]])
- Limestone samples showed a reduction in Young's modulus, unconfined compressive strength, and tensile strength post-CO2 treatment, with an increase in Poisson's ratio and permeability. ([[2014-al-ameri|AL-AMERI, 2014, p. 51]])

## Limitations

The duration of exposure for some sample groups was limited by the overall timeframe of the experimental program.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Conduct tests for longer storage time.
- Test other carbonate aquifer and reservoir rocks (Arab and Khuff formations) in Saudi Arabia to evaluate stability.
- Evaluate the effect of CO2 sequestration on mechanical properties of seal rocks like anhydrite or tight-carbonate mudstone from Saudi Arabia.
- Study the effect of CO2 sequestration on heterogeneous carbonate rocks.
- Use SEM and micro CT scan for more details about the effect of CO2 sequestration on grain contacts, pore space geometry and rock frame.
