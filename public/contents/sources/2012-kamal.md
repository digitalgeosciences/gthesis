# THE EFFECTS OF WELL AND RESERVOIR PARAMETERS ON THE PERFORMANCE OF HORIZONTAL AND VERTICAL WELLS IN VOLUMETRIC DRY GAS RESERVOIRS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of well and reservoir parameters, such as horizontal permeability, formation thickness, and drainage area shape, on the performance of vertical wells (VW), horizontal wells (HW), and hydraulically-fractured vertical wells (HFVW) in volumetric dry-gas and wet-gas reservoirs. It concludes that while initial production rates do not affect ultimate recovery factors, parameters like horizontal permeability and well penetration ratio significantly influence abandonment time and recovery factor.

---

## Research Problem

The determination of optimal well types and designs for volumetric dry-gas and wet-gas reservoirs where the relationship between abandonment pressure, ultimate recovery factor, and abandonment time is influenced by specific reservoir properties (e.g., horizontal permeability) and well characteristics (e.g., penetration ratio).

## Objectives

- Investigate the effect of well parameters (e.g., horizontal well length, penetration ratio) and reservoir properties (e.g., permeability, thickness, drainage area) on vertical, horizontal, and hydraulically-fractured vertical wells.
- Develop and utilize practical criteria to evaluate and compare the performance of three different well types.
- Use comparative analysis to select optimal well types and penetration ratios based on specific reservoir properties.
- Evaluate the effect of vertical to horizontal permeability (Kv/Kh) ratio on recovery factor and abandonment time.
- Analyze the effect of reservoir parameters such as permeability, thickness, and fracture conductivity on well performance.
- Assess the role of fracture conductivity and length in enhancing well performance.

## Methods

The study utilizes reservoir simulation to model three types of wells: Horizontal Wells (HW), Vertical Wells (VW), and Hydraulically-Fractured Vertical Wells (HFVW). Simulations are conducted for both square drainage areas (SDA) and rectangular drainage areas (RDA). Parameters varied include horizontal permeability (Kh), formation thickness (h), fracture conductivity, well penetration ratio (P.R.), and initial production rates (Q). Performance is measured using Recovery Factor (R.F.), Abandonment Time, and Average Reservoir Pressure at abandonment (FPR).

## Data and Materials

Simulation models for a single-layer reservoir with initial pressure of 3700 psia, temperature of 200°F, and porosity of 20%. The study evaluates multiple cases involving different drainage areas (80 and 160 acres), formation thicknesses (20, 50, 100 ft), and horizontal permeabilities (0.01, 0.1, 1, 10, 100 md).

## Key Findings

- Horizontal permeability, formation thickness, well penetration ratio, drainage area size, and shape have significant effects on the performance of all three well types. ([[2012-kamal|Kamal, 2012, p. 26]])
- The kv/kh ratio has a somewhat negligible effect on the performance of horizontal wells in volumetric dry gas reservoirs. ([[2012-kamal|Kamal, 2012, p. 112]])
- The ratio of vertical permeability to horizontal permeability (Kv/Kh) and the location of the horizontal well relative to no-flow boundaries have negligible effects on horizontal well performance in dry and wet gas reservoirs. ([[2012-kamal|Kamal, 2012, p. 26]])
- porosity does not affect ultimate recovery factor or abandonment pressure, but it significantly impacts the time to reach abandonment condition. ([[2012-kamal|Kamal, 2012]])

- The study evaluates three distinct fracture conductivities: 200 md-ft, 1000 md-ft, and 2000 md-ft. ([[2012-kamal|Kamal, 2012, p. 61]])
- A minimum wellbore radius of 0.5 feet is used as a baseline for comparison with fracture width. ([[2012-kamal|Kamal, 2012, p. 61]])
- The Hall-Yarborough method was used to calculate the Z-factor, verified against Standing and Katz charts. ([[2012-kamal|Kamal, 2012, p. 64]])
- property_value_range_for_gas_gravity_is_0.7 ([[2012-kamal|Kamal, 2012]])
- page_number_of_calculation_section_is_65 ([[2012-kamal|Kamal, 2012]])
- Hydraulically-fractured vertical wells (HFVW) perform better than horizontal wells (HW) of the same penetration ratio and outperform standard vertical wells (VW) in all formation thicknesses. ([[2012-kamal|Kamal, 2012, p. 95]])
- In low horizontal permeability reservoirs (0.01 md and 0.1 md), HFVW achieves higher recovery factors because average reservoir pressure reaches lower values at the end of production. ([[2012-kamal|Kamal, 2012, p. 95]])
- For vertical wells in low permeability reservoirs (0.01 md and 0.1 md), performance is poor, providing low recovery factors especially in thick formations (100 ft) or thin ones (20 ft). ([[2012-kamal|Kamal, 2012, p. 95]])
- In 1 md permeability reservoirs, the ultimate recovery factor for HFVW and HW are nearly identical, but HFVW requires significantly less time to recover reserves. ([[2012-kamal|Kamal, 2012, p. 95]])
- For high horizontal permeability (10 md and 100 md), both HW and VW show good performance, but the abandonment time for HW is shorter than for VW. ([[2012-kamal|Kamal, 2012, p. 96]])
