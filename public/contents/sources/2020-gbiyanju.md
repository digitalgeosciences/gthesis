# DEVELOPMENT OF CHEMOSTRATIGRAPHY AND CHEMO-MECHANICAL FACIES FRAMEWORK IN KHUFF, UNAYZAH and QUSAIBA FORMATIONS, KSA

**Summary**: The thesis develops a chemostratigraphy and chemo-mechanical facies framework for the Khuff, Unayzah, and Qusaiba formations in Saudi Arabia. It integrates non-destructive microXRF elemental analysis with impulse hammer measurements of reduced Young's modulus (E*) to link mineralogical composition to rock mechanical properties.

---

## Research Problem

Paleozoic tight sand and shale reservoirs (Khuff, Unayzah, Qusaiba) are poorly constrained biostratigraphically and share similar lithologies, making stratigraphic correlation, geosteering, and facies analysis difficult. Additionally, characterizing these unconventional reservoirs requires integrated procedures to address engineering challenges like wellbore stability and hydraulic fracturing.

## Objectives

- Establish a data output with known standard techniques (triaxial tests) to benchmark the newer impulse hammer method.
- Characterize geomechanical rock profiles for unconventional reservoirs using impulse hammer-derived properties.
- Develop a machine learning approach using geochemical signatures and XRF data to predict rock mechanical properties.
- Develop chemostratigraphy framework within the target formations using extensive geochemical analysis on over 330 core plugs.
- Establish chemo facies within the study samples using principal component analysis (PCA).
- Determine mechanical properties of study samples using impulse hammer and triaxial test techniques to establish relationships between Young's modulus and its reduced form (E*).

## Methods

The study utilized a combination of outcrop and subsurface samples. Elemental analysis was conducted using MicroXRF (Rhodium source, 50 kV, 600 µA) to determine mineralogy and grain size distribution. Rock strength was assessed via the Impulse Hammer method (AutoScan II system) to measure reduced Young's modulus (E*). Data processing included Principal Component Analysis (PCA) using singular value decomposition and Hierarchical Clustering on Principle Components (HCPC) based on Ward's method of agglomeration and Euclidean distance. Statistical analysis was performed in R software using packages such as FactoMineR, factoextra, and tidyverse.

## Data and Materials

The study analyzed 100 core plugs covering over 800 ft of interval from the Khuff, Unayzah, and Qusaiba formations. Data included elemental concentrations from XRF, reduced Young's modulus (E*) from impulse hammer tests, and triaxial test results for static/dynamic moduli.

## Key Findings

- Sample mineralogy and grain size variabilities significantly influence the impulse hammer reduced Young’s Modulus (E*). ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 28]])
- Strong correlations exist between E* and standard triaxial test Young’s Modulus (Static and dynamic). ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 18]])
- E* reveals more detailed geomechanical heterogeneity and anisotropy than Es and Ed. ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 18]])
- Five chemo-mechanical facies (CMF) were identified that were formation specific. ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 18]])
- The machine learning approach for predicting rock mechanical properties using XRF and impulse hammer data yielded R2 values of 0.83, 0.87, and 0.89 for training, validation, and testing. ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 18]])
- Significant correlation was found between Young’s modulus parameter (E*) and four elements: Co, Sr, Ti, and Zr. ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 35]])
- The study identified toughness/modulus and interface barriers as key factors affecting hydraulic fracture height growth. ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 33]])
- Reduced Young's modulus (E*) from impulse hammer measurements was found to be a reliable proxy for rock strength, showing sensitivity to mineralogy and grain size. ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 58]])
- Sample diameter significantly impacts E* values; 25.4 mm samples were inconsistent and often underestimated values compared to 38.1 mm samples due to the instability of the hammer tip. ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 69]])
- A significant inverse relationship was observed between clay content and E* in several samples, such as the transition from JF-NW2 (70 GPa) to JF-NW3 (83 GPa). ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 75]])
- Smaller grain sizes were associated with higher E* values; for example, the fine-grained BS-20 (E* = 28 GPa) was significantly stiffer than the medium-grained BS-300 (E* = 9 GPa). ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 71]])
- PCA identified two primary dimensions: a 'clay gradient' (Dimension 1, explaining high variance in minerals like Rb, Al, K) and a 'diagenetic alteration plane' (Dimension 2, related to Ni, Mn, Cr). ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 86]])
- Four elements—Cobalt (Co), Strontium (Sr), Titanium (Ti), and Zircon (Zr)—showed significant positive correlation with E* and were identified as proxy indicators for rock strength. ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 90]])
- Silicon (Si) showed a negative correlation with all elements except Mo, and its behavior relative to E* and porosity suggests diagenetic impact on original reservoir rock. ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 88]])
- Four distinct chemo-mechanical facies (CMF-1 through CMF-4) were identified using hierarchical clustering of PCA results, each representing unique lithological and mechanical behaviors. ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 93]])

## Limitations

Traditional triaxial measurements are limited by sample requirements, availability, and high costs, leading to smaller datasets that may not represent field-scale heterogeneities. Geostrategy in these formations is complicated by poor biostratigraphic resolution.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study recommends using impulse hammer measurements as an alternative, cost-effective, and time-efficient method for obtaining statistically representative rock mechanical properties for wireline data calibration.
