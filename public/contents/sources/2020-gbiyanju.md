# DEVELOPMENT OF CHEMOSTRATIGRAPHY AND CHEMO-MECHANICAL FACIES FRAMEWORK IN KHUFF, UNAYZAH and QUSAIBA FORMATIONS, KSA

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the integration of non-destructive impulse hammer measurements with traditional triaxial testing and geochemical data to characterize mechanical properties in Paleozoic tight sands and shale reservoirs. It establishes a correlation between rock elastic properties (REP) and elemental composition to develop a continuous geomechanical profile for better upscaling of reservoir parameters.

---

## Research Problem

Paleozoic tight sand and shale reservoirs in KSA are poorly constrained biostratigraphically and have similar lithologies, making stratigraphic correlation, geosteering, and facies analysis difficult. Additionally, characterizing these unconventional reservoirs requires integrated procedures to address engineering challenges like wellbore stability and hydraulic fracturing.

## Methods

The study utilized core plugs from Paleozoic formations. Elemental analysis was performed using a MicroXRF machine (Rhodium source, 50 kV, 600 µA) with M4 software to determine chemical composition. Rock elastic properties were measured using an AutoScan II platform with an Impulse Hammer system to calculate reduced Young's modulus (E*). Traditional triaxial tests were performed on polished core samples at 10 MPa confining pressure using the NER AutoLab 1500 to obtain dynamic and static moduli. Statistical analysis, including Pearson correlation and Principal Component Analysis (PCA), was used to relate geochemical data to mechanical properties.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized core samples and cuttings from the Khuff, Unayzah, and Qusaiba formations. Geochemical data included elements like Ca, Si, Fe, Al, K, etc., obtained via MicroXRF/XRF. Mechanical data consisted of E* values (ranging from 3.2 to 148.2 GPa) measured by an impulse hammer.

## Key Findings

- Established a correlation between E* (elastic modulus) and other parameters like grain size and chemical composition to identify rock strength predictors. ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 193]])
- Developed an empirical relationship between impulse hammer-derived properties and triaxial test results, allowing for non-destructive evaluation of rock mechanics. ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 99]])
- Identified a 'chemo-mechanical' facies classification based on the integration of geochemical data and mechanical properties. ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 74]])
- Successfully used XRF spectra raw data to estimate grain size and perform rock typing. ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 210]])
- Sample mineralogy and grain size variabilities significantly influence the impulse hammer reduced Young’s Modulus (E*). ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 28]])
- Strong correlations exist between E* and standard triaxial test Young’s Modulus (Static and dynamic). ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 18]])
- E* reveals more detailed geomechanical heterogeneity and anisotropy than Es and Ed. ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 18]])
- Five chemo-mechanical facies (CMF) were identified that were formation specific. ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 18]])
- The machine learning model for predicting mechanical properties from geochemical data achieved R2 values of 0.83, 0.87, and 0.89 for training, validation, and testing respectively. ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 18]])
- E* is significantly affected by sample diameter (e.g., 25.4 mm) but shows less variation with sample length. ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 29]])
- A strong correlation was observed between the reduced Young's modulus (E*) derived from impulse hammer measurements and traditional triaxial parameters (Ed, Es) and elastic properties (Vs, Vp). ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 32]])
- The Impulse Hammer method revealed details of geomechanical heterogeneity and anisotropy not possible with the traditional triaxial method. ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 32]])
- A significant correlation was found between Young's modulus parameter (E*) and four specific elements: Co, Sr, Ti, and Zr. ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 35]])

- The impulse hammer-derived Young's modulus (E*) was found to be a reliable non-destructive proxy for the static Young's modulus (Es), even when sample dimensions varied. ([[2020-gbiyanju|Gbiyanju, 2020, p. 59]])

## Limitations

Core-based measurement output is often limited in quantity, making it difficult to generate reliable empirical correlations compared to large wireline log datasets; however, this study addresses that by using non-destructive techniques and predictive modeling.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Investigate if Mn signatures are reliable proxies for lithofacies, reservoir sweetspots, porosity, and diagenetic markers.
- Conduct further research on using impulse hammer measurements as a full replacement for triaxial tests to reduce costs and uncertainty.
