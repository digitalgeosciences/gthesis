# EVALUATING THE EFFECTS OF DENSITY, VISCOSITY AND SURFACE TENSION ON TWO-PHASE FLOW IN HORIZONTAL PIPES

**Summary**: The thesis investigates how fluid properties—specifically density, viscosity, and surface tension—influence flow regime maps and pressure drops in horizontal pipes. Through a series of experiments using air/water systems modified with Calcium Bromide (density), glycerin (viscosity), and surfactants (surface tension), the study concludes that while all three properties affect flow regimes, only density and viscosity significantly impact pressure drop.

---

## Research Problem

Most existing flow regime maps for two-phase gas/liquid flow in horizontal pipes were developed without considering the effects of fluid properties like density, viscosity, and surface tension, leading to limited applicability when different fluids or pipe geometries are involved.

## Objectives

- Evaluate the effect of liquid viscosity on the flow regime map.
- Evaluate the effect (reduction) of surface tension on the flow regime map.
- Investigate the effects of these properties on boundary transitions in horizontal pipes.
- Determine the impact of these fluid properties on pressure drop calculations.

## Methods

The study utilized a horizontal pipe flow-loop (30 ft length, 1-inch diameter) with an air/liquid system. To isolate variables, different concentrations of Calcium Bromide were used to vary density, glycerin was added to increase viscosity, and surfactants (Alpha Olefin Sulfonate) were used to reduce surface tension. A high-resolution camera captured flow patterns through a transparent section of the pipe. Pressure drop was measured using differential pressure gauges over a 7 ft segment. Data analysis involved plotting gas and liquid superficial velocities to generate flow regime maps for each experimental case.

## Data and Materials

Experimental data from five systems: air-water (base case), air-0.5% surfactant, air-30% glycerin, air-25% CaBr2-0.004% surfactant, and air-75% CaBr2-0.02% surfactant. Flow parameters included liquid flow rates of 1.5 to 96.5 L/min and gas flow rates of 0.35 to 20 scf/min.

## Key Findings

- The boundary between ripple wave and inertial wave shifted to the left when surface tension was reduced from 70.5 mN/m to 59.7 mN/m (0.02% surfactant). ([[2018-al-dogail|Al-Dogail, 2018, p. 48]])
- At a surface tension of 46.5 mN/m (0.1% surfactant), more slug-annular flow was observed at high liquid and gas superficial velocities compared to the base case. ([[2018-al-dogail|Al-Dogail, 2018, p. 51]])
- Reducing surface tension to 32.4 mN/m (0.5% surfactant) resulted in more annular flow being observed at high liquid and gas superficial velocities. ([[2018-al-dogail|Al-Dogail, 2018, p. 53]])
- Increasing viscosity to 1.27 cP (5% glycerin) caused the boundary between slug and pseudo-slug to shift down. ([[2018-al-dogail|Al-Dogail, 2018, p. 54]])
- At a viscosity of 3.10 cP (30% glycerin), more annular and slug-annular flow regimes were observed compared to cases with lower viscosity. ([[2018-al-dogail|Al-Dogail, 2018, p. 59]])
- Increasing density to 1.2 gm/cc resulted in more inertial waves and fewer ripple waves, shifting the boundary between them to the right. ([[2018-al-dogail|Al-Dogail, 2018, p. 60]])
- At a density of 1.5 gm/cc, both inertial waves and ripple waves were observed more frequently, with the boundary between them shifting to the right. ([[2018-al-dogail|Al-Dogail, 2018, p. 62]])
- Density had a significant impact on pressure drop (ranging from 0 to 4.4 psi for 1.2 gm/cc and 0 to 5.2 psi for 1.5 gm/cc), while surface tension had no measurable effect on pressure drop. ([[2018-al-dogail|Al-Dogail, 2018, p. 63]])
- The range of pressure drop due to increased liquid viscosity was 0 to 4.4 psi. ([[2018-al-dogail|Al-Dogail, 2018, p. 64]])
- The reduction in surface tension resulted in a pressure drop range of 0-3.8 psi, which is identical to the range observed for the base air-water system. ([[2018-al-dogail|Al-Dogail, 2018, p. 64]])
