# PORE-SCALE INVESTIGATION OF IFT DYNAMICS FOR CHEMICAL EOR APPLICATIONS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the dynamics of interfacial tension (IFT) and wettability in chemical enhanced oil recovery (CEOR) using viscoelastic surfactants (VES), Gemini surfactants (GM), and chelating agents. It utilizes microfluidic models, MRI imaging, and core flooding to evaluate these chemicals' performance at pore and core scales, ultimately scaling results to field-scale simulations for naturally fractured reservoirs.

---

## Research Problem

The primary challenge is that high water mobility leads to poor sweep efficiency in CEOR. While surfactants reduce IFT and polymers/VES increase viscosity, the presence of salts can degrade these properties. Chelating agents are proposed to mitigate salt-related degradation while maintaining favorable rheology and low IFT.

## Objectives

- Perform fluid evaluation for VES, Gemini surfactant, and combinations with chelating agents regarding rheology and spinning drop IFT.
- Study the effects of salinity, temperature, and concentration on the phase behavior of Crude oil/VES and Squalene/VES systems.
- Utilize microfluidic techniques to evaluate recovery from VES and its combination with chelating agents.
- Estimate dynamic IFT during flooding in real geometry using Taylor's theory.
- Develop a simulation model by calibrating results from fluid evaluation, microfluidics, micro-CT, and upscaling to field level.
- Explore the phase behavior of novel VES at high-temperature conditions with crude oil and squalene under varying salinities and concentrations.

## Methods

The study employed three main stages: material characterization, core-flooding experiments, and simulation. Material characterization included micro-CT scanning for porosity/permeability mapping, rheology measurements, IFT analysis, and adsorption studies. Core-flooding was performed on Berea sandstone samples using a 12 MHz MRI flow cell to monitor T2 distribution and 2D Fast Spin Echo (FSE) scans. Simulation was conducted using CMG-STARS for field-scale modeling of an NFR with 1.32 million grid blocks, incorporating parameters like capillary number (Nc), relative permeability curves, and various injection scenarios.

## Data and Materials

Two VES concentrations (0.5% and 1.25% vol.) were tested with varying chelating agent concentrations (2% to 8% wt.). Arabian heavy crude oil (27.5° API) was used as the oil phase. Synthetic seawater (57,612 ppm TDS) was used for brine preparation. A glass micromodel (4.406 mm x 3.302 mm x 0.011 mm) with 65% porosity and a fractured network was used for microfluidic studies.

## Key Findings

- The rheology of pure VES at 0.5%, 0.75%, and 1.25% showed zero shear viscosities of 325 cP, 1101 cP, and 1953 cP respectively. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 23]])
- The lowest IFT for GLDA was 0.0022 mN/m at 4% GLDA with 1.25% VES; for DTPA, the lowest value was 0.0068 mN/m at 8% DTPA with 1.25% VES. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 23]])
- The dynamic IFT of a Gemini surfactant (GmS) showed 97% accuracy for 1.25% VES when compared to the spinning drop method, but accuracy dropped to 65% and 51% for 0.75% and 0.5% VES respectively. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 26]])
- The lowest IFT value of 0.12 mN/m was found at 0.0005% wt. for the Gemini surfactant (GmS). ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 26]])
- Microfluidic results showed ultimate recovery of 56% for 4% GLDA with 1.25% VES, 43% for 8% DTPA with 1.25% VES, and 48% for pure VES at 1.25%. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 24]])
- VES improved oil recovery by 10% and reduced water cut by 47% in naturally fractured reservoirs (NFRs) compared to waterflooding. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 24]])
- VES reduced the IFT by two orders of magnitude (100 times) compared to waterflooding in carbonate reservoir conditions. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 189]])
- In the VES/crude oil system, the emulsion volume decreased as salinity increased from 25% to 100% SW, with a concentration of 1.2% being identified as optimal for equal solubilization. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 40]])
- For the VES/crude oil system, the emulsion volume reached its minimum at 90°C and then increased above that temperature due to potential phase inversion. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 47]])
- In the VES/squalene system, the lowest emulsion value was recorded at 50% SW salinity, 90°C temperature, and 1.25% VES concentration. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 50]])
- The zero-shear viscosity for 0.5%, 0.75%, and 1.25% VES were measured at 325 cP, 1101 cP, and 1953 cP respectively. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 74]])
- The dynamic IFT was found to be directly proportional to the shear rate at low shear rates before reaching a plateau. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 76]])
- Chelating agent GLDA with 4% concentration and 1.25% VES achieved an ultimate recovery of 56% from original oil in place (OOIP). ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 82]])
- The addition of chelating agents successfully suppressed the effect of salinity on IFT, allowing for the use of raw seawater. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 82]])
- Adding 8% DTPA to 1.25% VES reduced the IFT by two orders of magnitude from 0.15 mN/m to 0.0068 mN/m. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 132]])

## Limitations

The study notes limitations of microfluidics regarding volume and rock material, leading to the use of MRI for in-situ visualization. The core-flooding results are limited by the specific properties of Berea sandstone used as a model.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study suggests that optimal VES flooding conditions should be determined through sensitivity analysis of IFT and viscosity to maximize recovery in high-temperature, high-salinity (HTHS) environments like those found in Middle Eastern carbonate reservoirs.
