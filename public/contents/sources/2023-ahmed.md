# PORE-SCALE INVESTIGATION OF IFT DYNAMICS FOR CHEMICAL EOR APPLICATIONS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of chelating agents (DTPA and GLDA) on the rheological properties and interfacial tension (IFT) dynamics of Gemini Surfactant (VES) solutions for chemical enhanced oil recovery (EOR). Using spinning drop tensiometers and microfluidic micromodels, the study concludes that while both agents reduce IFT, GLDA provides a superior balance between IFT reduction and viscosity maintenance, leading to higher ultimate oil recovery.

---

## Research Problem

The primary challenge in chemical enhanced oil recovery (CEOR) is optimizing the interplay between mobility control and interfacial tension (IFT) reduction. Specifically, the study addresses how visco-elastic surfactants (IFT-reducing agents) and chelating agents behave under high temperature/salinity conditions and their impact on flow dynamics at the pore scale.

- Perform comprehensive fluid evaluation for armovis viscoelastic surfactant (VES), gemini surfactant (GmS), and combinations of VES with chelating agents.

- Couple pore-scale results with core-scale data to build a calibrated simulation model for field-scale application in naturally fractured reservoirs (NFRs).

## Methods

The research utilizes several experimental and computational workflows: 1) Phase behavior analysis of VES with crude oil and squalene across varying salinities, concentrations, and temperatures. 2) Rheology measurements for both crude oil and VES solutions to characterize non-Newtonian behavior. 3) Spinning drop technique to measure IFT values. 4) Microfluidic micromodels (glass models) to observe pore-scale flow dynamics and calculate IFT from microfluidics. 5) Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) using T2 distribution measurements and Fast Spin Echo (FSE) scans to monitor flooding in sandstone cores. 6) Reservoir simulation using Petrel for field-scale modeling of fractured reservoirs.

## Data and Materials

The study evaluates three main fluid systems: Crude oil/VES, Squalene/SEM (used as a proxy for crude oil in some tests), and combinations of VES with chelating agents (DTPA and GLDA). Experimental data includes IFT measurements at various temperatures (60-150°C) and salinities (up to 200,000 ppm). Microfluidic models were etched into glass plates. Core flooding was performed on sandstone cores.

## Key Findings

- Crude oil exhibits shear-thinning non-Newtonian behavior during rheology measurements. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 13]])
- The study evaluated the impact of concentration and temperature on the phase behavior of VES/crude oil systems, specifically looking at solubilization ratios. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 13]])
- IFT was measured for both crude oil and squalene systems using the spinning drop method to compare with microfluidic results. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 13]])
- Microfluidic micromodels were used to observe drop movement through pore throats and calculate IFT from microfluidics. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 13]])
- Chelating agents (DTPA, GLDA) were tested for their effects on the performance of VES flooding in both micromodels and sandstone cores. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 13]])
- MRI was used to measure T2 distribution and observe the difference between 1.25% VES and 1.25% VES with chelating agents (DTPA/GLDA) in core flooding. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 13]])
- Field-scale simulation was used to evaluate the impact of viscosity, IFT, and fracture characteristics on recovery rates. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 13]])
- The spinning drop method showed 97% accuracy for 1.25% VES in determining dynamic IFT, while accuracy was lower for 0.75% (65%) and 0.5% (51%). ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 23]])
- The lowest IFT value of 0.12 mN/m was found at 0.0005% wt. for the Gemini surfactant (GmS). ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 23]])
- Microfluidic recovery tests showed ultimate recovery of 56% for 4%GLDA with 1.25% VES, compared to 43% for 8%DTPA and 48% for pure 1.25% VES. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 24]])
- At the core scale, the best ultimate recovery was reached with 4%GLDA and 1.25% VES (53%), while pure VES at 1.25% yielded 50%. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 24]])
- VES reduced the IFT by two orders of magnitude (100 times) compared to waterflooding. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 24]])
- In field-scale simulation, VES improved oil recovery by 10% and reduced the water cut by 47% compared to waterflooding in naturally fractured reservoirs (NFRs). ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 24]])
- The highest viscosity for DTPA was 717 cP at a working shear rate of 1 s-1, while the highest for GLDA was 738 cP. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 23]])
- The spinning drop method showed a precision of 97% for 1.25% VES, while accuracy dropped to 65% and 51% for 0.75% and 0.5% VES respectively. ([[2023-ahmed|Ahmed, 2023, p. 26]])

## Limitations

The study notes that the investigation of high-temperature conditions is typically challenging due to the risk of evaporation and loss of fluid volume, which required the development of a specialized closed-cell system.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study suggests that the optimal combination of chelating agents (specifically GLDA) with high-viscosity surfactants is critical for maximizing recovery in both large and small pore systems.
