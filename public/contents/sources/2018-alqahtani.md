# The Removal of ilmenite-water based filter cake in HPHT wells

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the removal of ilmenite-based filter cakes in high-pressure, high-temperature (HPHT) wells using water-based drilling fluids. It evaluates various chelating agents and a newly developed 'Green Biodegradable Removal Formulation' (GBRF) to determine their effectiveness in dissolving ilmenite particles and restoring core permeability.

---

## Research Problem

Ilmenite is used as a weighting agent in HPHT wells due to its stability, but it forms a persistent filter cake that can damage reservoir permeability. Existing methods like HCl or standard chelating agents (EDTA, GLDA) show limited effectiveness in dissolving ilmenite under extreme conditions.

## Objectives

- Evaluate the solubility of various chelating and chemical agents on ilmenite particles at HPHT conditions.
- Develop a new fluid formula to remove ilmenite filter cake efficiently.
- Assess the impact of the newly developed formulation on limestone core samples to calculate retained permeability.

## Methods

The study involved: 1) Ilmenite Kinetic Reaction Experiments using XRD and SEM to characterize particles, followed by solubility tests with DTPA, GLDA, EDTA, and HCl at 300°F. 2) Fluid Preparation and Rheology Measurements of a water-based drilling fluid (WBM) containing ilmenite, measured via mud balance, Fan 35A Viscometer, and HPHT Rheometer. 3) HPHT Filtration Tests on Indiana limestone cores to measure filtrate volume and filter cake thickness at 250°F and 300 psi. 4) Filter Cake Removal Tests using the GBRF solution (75% GBRF, 2% corrosion inhibitor, 2% intensifier). 5) Permeability measurements before and after removal to calculate removal efficiency and retained permeability.

## Data and Materials

1 gram of ilmenite (5 μm average size), 300g of ilmenite in drilling fluid, three Indiana limestone core samples (average porosity 12%, average permeability 20 mD), various chemical agents (DTPA, GLDA, EDTA, HCl).

## Key Findings

- Chelating agents alone showed low dissolution: GLDA at low pH (4) achieved only 27% solubility of FeTiO3 particles at 300°F. ([[2018-alqahtani|AlQahtani, 2018, p. 58]])
- The addition of 1 wt.% HCl to 15 wt.% GLDA increased ilmenite dissolution rate to 43%. ([[2018-alqahtani|AlQahtani, 2018, p. 58]])
- The developed GBRF solution (75 wt.%) was able to dissolve 69% of the ilmenite at HPHT conditions after 24 hours. ([[2018-alqahtani|AlQahtani, 2018, p. 14]])
- The optimized GBRF formulation (75% GBRF, 2% corrosion inhibitor, 2% intensifier) achieved a removal efficiency of up to 96.82% on limestone cores. ([[2018-alqahtani|AlQahtani, 2018, p. 72]])
- Retained permeability for the tested core samples was approximately 100% (ranging from 99.1% to 110%). ([[2018-alqahtani|AlQahtani, 2018, p. 72]])
- The water-based drilling fluid with ilmenite showed acceptable filtration behavior with filtrate volumes under 14 cm3 at 250°F and 300 psi. ([[2018-alqahtani|AlQahtani, 2018, p. 73]])

## Limitations

Differences in ilmenite removal efficiency were noted between the initial solubility tests and the HPHT filtration tests due to the presence of other additives in the completion fluid.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future studies should evaluate the new removal technology for oil-based muds (OBM) and all-oil muds, and test on different rock types such as sandstones. A study on fluid-to-solid ratios is recommended to optimize volume usage.
