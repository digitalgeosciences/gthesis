# EVALUATION OF POLYMERIC MATERIALS AS LOSS CIRCULATION AGENTS IN SHALLOW ZONES

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the rheological properties of polyacrylamide (PAM) and its derivatives as potential lost circulation materials (LCMs) in shallow drilling zones. It specifically examines how various counterions and organic crosslinkers affect the viscosity, elastic modulus, and thermal stability of these polymers to facilitate the formation of gels for sealing fractures.

---

## Research Problem

The selection of effective lost circulation materials (LCM) for shallow zones requires agents that can transition from a viscous fluid to an elastic gel to seal fractures without damaging the formation. The study addresses how different metal ions and crosslinkers affect these properties in Polyacrylamide systems.

## Objectives

- To review the existing practices for controlling loss circulation of drilling fluids using polymers.
- Studying the rheological properties of different classes of polyacrylamide (PAM) at various concentrations.
- Investigate the effect of counterions (Ca2+, Mn2+, Mg2+ and Fe3+) on the viscosity profile of PAM.
- Evaluate the mechanisms by which polymer materials work as LCM.
- Develop a see-through cell to evaluate the flow, sealing, blocking & stability of LCMs at room temperature.
- To investigate the effect of metal ions (Ca, Mg, Fe, Mn) on the viscosity profile, thermal stability, and elastic modulus of PAM.

## Methods

The study utilizes a series of rheological tests including oscillation frequency tests, flow sweep tests to determine shear thinning behavior, and temperature ramp tests for thermal stability. The Carreau model is applied via non-linear regression to determine zero-shear rate viscosity. Samples are prepared by dissolving specific weight percentages of PAM (Flopaam 3330S) with varying amounts of counterions (Calcium Chloride, Magnesium Chloride, Ferric Nitrate) in distilled water.

## Data and Materials

The study uses Flopaam 3330S (anionic PAM), Calcium Chloride (CaCl2.2H2O), Magnesium Chloride (MgCl2.6H2O), and Ferric Nitrate (Fe2(NO3)3.9H20). Samples are prepared with total solid concentrations of 1 wt% in distilled water, with specific weight ratios ranging from 0.75 to 0.95 for PAM and 0.05 to 0.25 for counterions.

## Key Findings

- Polyacrylamide (PAM) is a suitable candidate for LCM because it can withstand high temperatures without degradation. ([[2012-omer|Omer, 2012, p. 27]])
- The addition of counterions and crosslinkers to PAM results in variations in the viscosity profile, effects on elastic modulus, and reduction in thermal stability to facilitate gelation. ([[2012-omer|Omer, 2012, p. 14]])
- A specific formulation using a crosslinker (chromium III carboxylate) resulted in a gel that was unaffected by salinity of water. ([[2012-omer|Omer, 2012, p. 23]])
- The addition of crosslinkers like polyethyleneimine to PAM results in a gelation time of 18-72 hours at 65°C, which is longer than the Chromium (III) Acetate/HPAM system. ([[2012-omer|Omer, 2012, p. 31]])
- Polyacrylamide (PAM) can be used to form a gel that remains stable until an activator is added, after which it can be reverted to a liquid state by adding salt (e.g., 33% NaCl). ([[2012-omer|Omer, 2012, p. 25]])
- All tested PAM concentrations (0.05%, 0.1%, 0.5%, and 1% wt) were found to be within the Linear Viscoelastic Region (LVR) up to approximately 15% strain. ([[2012-omer|Omer, 2012, p. 55]])
- The zero-shear rate viscosities for pure PAM concentrations of 0.05, 0.1, 0.5, and 1 wt% were measured at 2.11, 5.84, 50.88, and 170.375 Pa.s, respectively. ([[2012-omer|Omer, 2012, p. 58]])
- The addition of calcium ions (Ca2+) significantly reduced the zero-shear rate viscosity; for example, a concentration of 0.25 wt% Ca resulted in a viscosity of only 0.70 Pa.s compared to 170.375 Pa.s for pure 1 wt% PAM. ([[2012-omer|Omer, 2012, p. 68]])
- The thermal stability of pure PAM was confirmed up to 70°C, with no significant degradation in storage or loss modulus observed between 25-70°C. ([[2012-omer|Omer, 2012, p. 65]])
- Increasing calcium concentration from 0.1% to 0.15% reduced the thermal stability of PAM by 15°C (from 70°C to 55°C). ([[2012-omer|Omer, 2012, p. 74]])
- The addition of magnesium ions (Mg2+) at 0.25 wt% concentration reduced the thermal stability of PAM by 30°C (from 70°C to 40.6°C). ([[2012-omer|Omer, 2012, p. 83]])
- Ferric ion (Fe3+) concentrations up to 0.15 wt% did not significantly impact the properties of PAM, with very low concentrations showing similar behavior to pure PAM. ([[2012-omer|Omer, 2012, p. 70]])
- The addition of metal ions to PAM makes the elastic component stronger than viscous (G' > G''), allowing the gel to behave more like a solid to seal fractures. ([[2012-omer|Omer, 2012, p. 109]])
- Manganese results in very little viscosity drop compared to other ions, with only about a 10% reduction at certain concentrations. ([[2012-omer|Omer, 2012, p. 103]])
- Calcium has a significant impact on reducing the viscosity of PAM, decreasing it by approximately 47% at specific concentration ratios. ([[2012-omer|Omer, 2012, p. 103]])

## Limitations

The sample with a 0.75/0.25 weight ratio of PAM and Fe3+ could not be dissolved and formed a precipitate, making it unsuitable for rheological measurements.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Testing the efficiency of the gel in an 'Experimental set up for LCM test' at room temperature.
- Using fibrous materials to increase toughness.
- Repeating rheology tests at formation temperatures.
- Enhancing experimental setups to conduct experiments at formation temperature and pressure.
- Testing the performance of different classes of polymers and comparing them with PAM.
