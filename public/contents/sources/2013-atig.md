# Identification of Discontinuous Oil Bearing Sandstone Bodies by Direct Modeling: Risk Analysis for Well Planning

**Summary**: The thesis evaluates different modeling techniques (Indicator Kriging, Sequential Indicator Simulation, and Object-Based Modeling) to identify and predict the spatial distribution of discontinuous oil-bearing sandstone bodies within the Safaniya member of the Wasia formation. The study concludes that a hybrid approach combining IK for continuous beds and OM for discontinuous features provides the best balance between prediction accuracy and geological realism.

---

## Research Problem

The difficulty of predicting the geometry and location of laterally discontinuous sand bodies (stringers) in complex deltaic environments like the Safaniya and Khafji members, which leads to high risk during horizontal well drilling.

## Objectives

- Develop a methodology for identifying and characterizing sandstone bodies from well data, core data, and analogs.
- Produce a probabilistic analysis of sand body occurrence to mitigate risks during drilling.
- Evaluate three modeling algorithms (Indicator Kr1ing, Sequential Indicator Simulation, and Object-based Modeling) to determine the best prediction method.
- Populate depositional facies into non-cored wells using neural networks.
- Construct geocellular models to characterize sand bodies predictability using Indicator Kriging, Sequential Indicator Simulation, and Object-based modeling.
- Evaluate the risk of drilling in stringer zones by producing a probabilistic analysis of sand body occurrence.

## Methods

The study utilized 13 cored wells and 188 logged wells from a non-disclosed field in the northern offshore area. Core data was used to define depositional facies (channel, splay, mouth bar, bay, swamp, marine). A neural network (back propagation) was implemented in Petrel to predict facies in non-cored wells based on logs (RHOB, NPHI, VSH, PHIE). The resulting facies were then mapped into nine sequence stratigraphic zones. Three geostatistical modeling techniques—Indicator Kriging (IK), Sequential Indicator Simulation (SIS), and Object-based Modeling (OBM)—were tested by varying semi-variogram parameters (azimuth, major range, minor range, vertical range) to determine the optimal configuration for predicting sand body geometry.

## Data and Materials

13 cored wells used for facies definition; 188 logged wells used for neural network training and prediction; 31 'blind' wells used for model validation. Well logs included Gamma Ray (GR), Density (RHOB), Neutron (NPHI), Photoelectric Factor (PEF), Sonic (DT), Effective Porosity (PHIE), and Shale Volume (VSH).

## Key Findings

- The study concluded that the Indicator Kriging technique is the optimum way to predict rock bodies compared to other methods. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 12]])
- A total of six depositional facies were identified from core descriptions: channel, splay, mouth bar, bay, swamp, and marine. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 40]])
- The neural network successfully predicted 'bay' and 'marine' facies with higher confidence than others, but required grouping of 'fluvial' categories (channel, splay, mouthbar) to improve accuracy. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 43]])
- The best prediction for the azimuth parameter was found at 60 degrees, confirming a northeast delta orientation. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 59]])
- The optimal major range for the model was determined to be 6,500 m (or 6,000m in some tests), providing a prediction success of 74.7%. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 60]])
- A minor range of 2,500 m was identified as the point where predictability became stable, suggesting an average sand body width of approximately 2,500 m. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 60]])
- The vertical range of 25 ft provided the best prediction for the model's vertical profile. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 61]])
- Indicator Kriging (IK) achieved a prediction success of 74.7% in the final model. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 83]])
- Sequential Indicator Simulation (SIS) yielded an average prediction success of 67.7%, with results varying between 67.1% and 68.8%. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 69]])
- Object-based modeling (OM) achieved a peak prediction success of 65.3% for the best configuration. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 75]])
- The hybrid model, combining IK and OM, yielded the highest overall geological realism with a prediction success of 70.3%. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 83]])
- In Object Modeling, channel amplitude and thickness were the primary parameters influencing predictability; specifically, reducing average channel thickness from 50 to 2.5 feet increased predictability by 8.9%. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 74]])
- IK results showed no significant variation in prediction success across different semi-variogram parameters (e.g., azimuth, range), while OM was highly sensitive to thickness and amplitude. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 84]])
- Neural networks failed to differentiate between depositional facies with similar electrical log characteristics, necessitating a simplification of facies into three categories: fluvial channel, marine, and bay. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 83]])

## Limitations

The study was limited by a lack of high-resolution data in some areas, making it difficult to image thin sand bodies (less than 30 feet) using standard seismic techniques. Additionally, the model's scope was restricted to the northern part of the field due to computational constraints.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Revise the current layering scheme in the Safaniya member based on recent sequence stratigraphic studies.
- Incorporate faults into models to account for potential discontinuities and impacts on fluid flow.
- Use manual interpretation for facies that are not distinguishable by neural networks due to similar log responses.
- Utilize different semi-variogram models for each zone/facies rather than a single global model.
- Increase cell thickness from 2 feet to 5 feet to improve modeling speed.
