# Identification of Discontinuous Oil Bearing Sandstone Bodies by Direct Modeling: Risk Analysis for Well Planning

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the identification and modeling of discontinuous oil-bearing sandstone bodies (stringers) within the Safaniya member of the Wasia formation. It evaluates several geostatistical methods, including Indicator Kriging, Sequential Indicator Simulation, and Object-based Modeling, to determine the most effective way to predict sand body distribution for risk reduction in well planning.

---

## Research Problem

The presence of laterally discontinuous, prolific oil-bearing sandstone bodies (stringers) intercalated with non-reservoir rocks in the Safaniya member complicates well planning. Traditional modeling may fail to accurately capture these complex geometries, necessitating advanced stochastic and deterministic modeling workflows to predict their spatial distribution.

## Objectives

- Analyze clastic facies using log and core data.
- Define the depositional environment of the study area.
- Develop techniques to transform lithological studies from logs into categorical facies based on a depositional model.
- Construct geocellular models using Indicator Kriging, Sequential Indicator Simulation, and Object-based modeling to characterize sand bodies.
- Evaluate the accuracy of these models by comparing them with actual data from newly drilled wells.
- Develop a methodology for identifying and characterizing sandstone bodies from well data, core data, and analogs using geostatistical modeling.
- Produce a probabilistic analysis of the occurrence of sand bodies to mitigate risk while drilling.
- Predict facies in non-cored wells using neural network techniques.
- Evaluate different modeling approaches (IK, SIS, OBM) for characterizing reservoir heterogeneity.
- Develop a conceptual model of the Safaniya member based on sequence stratigraphy.
- Evaluate the performance of Indicator Kriging (IK) in predicting facies distributions.
- Evaluate the performance of Sequential Indicator Simulation (SIS) for capturing thin sedimentary beds and complex geometries.
- Perform risk analysis by comparing prediction success rates between IK and SIS models.
- Evaluate different geostatistical methods (IK, SIS, OM) to model discontinuous sand bodies.
- Assess the impact of specific parameters like thickness and amplitude on model predictability.
- Develop a hybrid modeling approach combining Object Modeling with Indicator Kriging as a background.
- Provide risk analysis for well planning in stringer zones.
- Identify and predict the spatial distribution of clastic discontinuous rock bodies.
- Evaluate modeling workflows for identifying stringer bodies.
- Perform risk analysis for well planning in areas with discontinuous sandstone bodies.

## Methods

The study utilized core data to identify depositional facies, which were then simplified into three categories (marine, fluvial channel, and bay) because neural networks could not distinguish between them based on log signatures alone. These facies were populated into 188 non-cored wells using a Neural Network approach followed by manual quality checks. Geocellular models were constructed in Petrel software using four distinct methods: Indicator Kriging (IK), Sequential Indicator Simulation (SIS), Object-based Modeling (OM), and a Hybrid model combining OM with IK as the background. A trial-and-error approach was used to optimize parameters like thickness, amplitude, and variance for each method.

## Data and Materials

13 cored wells used for initial facies characterization; 188 logged wells used for neural network training and population of the model; 31 additional logs used for validation. Log types include Gamma Ray (GR), Density (RHOB), Neutron (NPHI), Photoelectric Factor (PEF), Sonic (DT), Effective Porosity (PHIE), and Shale Volume (VSH).

## Key Findings

- The study identified six depositional facies from core descriptions to be used for modeling. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 27]])
- Indicator Kriging (IK) was determined as the optimal technique for predicting rock bodies compared to SIS and Object-based models. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 12]])
- SIS models showed slightly lower prediction percentages than Indicator Kriging models. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 8]])
- The best semi-variogram azimuth for the IK model was found to be 60 degrees (between 50 and 80). ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 10]])
- The optimal semi-variogram major range for the IK model was determined to be 6,000 m. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 10]])
- Semi-variogram minor range values showed a trend of predictability enhancement up to 2,500 m; beyond this, no further improvement was observed. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 10]])
- The study identified that the Safaniya Member includes Lower Safaniya Stringers, Safaniya Main Sands, and Upper Safaniya Stringers. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 10]])
- The Safaniya member is characterized by a transition from fluvial-dominated systems in the southwest to marine-influenced systems in the northeast. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 40]])
- Core analysis identified six depositional facies: channel, splay, mouth bar, bay, swamp, and marine. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 40]])
- Swamp facies were found to have very low correlation values in neural network training due to their localized nature and small thickness (less than 1% of volume). ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 43]])
- Grouping 'channel', 'splay', and 'mouthbar' into a single 'fluvial' category improved the correlation coefficients for neural network prediction. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 43]])
- Bay facies were predicted with high confidence by all logs except RHOB, while marine facies were best predicted using RHOB and NPHI. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 43]])
- The study identified that the core lithology of the Safaniya member is primarily clastic, becoming more carbonate-rich toward the northeast. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 40]])
- The prediction success for the Indicator Kriging (IK) model reached 74.7% with an optimal azimuth of 60 degrees and a major range of 6,000 m. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 61]])
- The average prediction success for the eleven Sequential Indicator Simulation (SIS) realizations was 67.7%, which was lower than the IK model but better at representing thin sedimentary beds. ([[2013-atig|atig, 2013, p. 68]])

## Limitations

The study noted that the IK algorithm tends to smooth details and can result in 'blocky' facies or artificially connected features. Additionally, some results were limited by computational resources (RAM), restricting the model area to the northern part of the field.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Revisit the current layering scheme in Safaniya member based on recent sequence stratigraphic studies.
- Incorporate faults into the model to account for discontinuities affecting fluid flow.
- Use manual interpretation for non-cored wells when neural networks cannot differentiate facies based on log signatures.
- Perform a manual quality check and edit of output data after automated prediction runs.
- Use different semi-variogram models for each zone and each facies rather than one global model.
- Increase cell thickness to 5 feet in future models to improve construction speed.
