# Optimization of Foamed Acid Formulation for Acid Fracturing

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the optimization of foamed acid formulations for acid fracturing in carbonate reservoirs, specifically focusing on how surfactants (Duomeen TTM and Armovis), chelating agents (GLDA), corrosion inhibitors, and water chemistry affect foam stability and viscosity. The study concludes that while certain surfactants provide high-viscosity foams, additives like corrosion inhibitors can negatively impact stability, and foamed acid significantly improves fracture conductivity compared to conventional treatments.

---

## Research Problem

Conventional acid treatments in carbonate reservoirs often suffer from severe corrosion, fluid loss, and rapid reaction rates, leading to short etching lengths and poor fracture conductivity. There is a need for stable, high-viscosity foam systems that can withstand harsh conditions (high temperature/salinity) while incorporating necessary additives like corrosion inhibitors.

## Objectives

- Understand the effect of chemical additives, water chemistry, pH, gas type, foam quality, and pressure on foam viscosity under harsh reservoir conditions.
- Study the effect of foamed acid on fracture conductivity using a core flooding system.
- Investigate the rheology of foamed acid using different amine-based surfactants at 150 °C and 1,000 psi.
- Evaluate foam properties (stability, foamability, structure, bubble count, and size) under both ambient and HPHT conditions.
- Assess the impact of chelating agents (GLDA) and corrosion inhibitors on foam viscosity and stability.
- Analyze the influence of gas type (CO2 vs. N2) and pressure on foam behavior under HPHT conditions.

## Methods

The study utilized an HPHT foam analyzer (Kruss, Germany) to measure bubble structure, count, radius, foam height, and volume at 100°C and 1,000 psi. An HPHT foam rheometer was used to evaluate foam viscosity across shear rates of 100-1,000 s^-1. Surface tension was measured using an optical tensiometer (or drop-shape analyzer). Various chemical formulations were tested including different chelating agents (GLDA, DTPA, EDTA) at various pH levels, and surfactants (SURF, Armovis) in diverse water types (PW, SW, FW) with varying salt concentrations (0.1 to 1M of NaCl, CaCl2, MgCl2, Na2SO4, NaHCO3).

## Data and Materials

The study utilized two surfactants: Duomeen TTM and Armovis VES (Erucamidopropyl Hydroxypropylsultain). Three types of water were used: produced water (24,000 ppm), seawater (57,000 ppm), and formation water (213,734 ppm). Chelating agents included GLDA, DTPA, and EDTA. Samples were prepared with 1 wt% surfactant and 1 wt% chelating agent or HCl.

## Key Findings

- Duomeen TTM and Armovis were identified as superior foamers at low pH (3.5). ([[2024-al-drwish|AL-DRWISH, 2024, p. 50]])
- N2-foam is more stable than CO2-foam because the higher permeability of CO2 through the film leads to faster bubble coalescence. ([[2024-al-drwish|AL-DRWISH, 2024, p. 54]])
- The addition of corrosion inhibitors (CI) reduced foam stability for both N2 and CO2 systems, with TTM and MS showing reductions of 60% and 54% respectively. ([[2024-al-drwish|AL-DRWISH, 2024, p. 68]])
- GLDA addition to Armovis increased the stability of foam by four times compared to no GLDA addition. ([[2024-al-drwish|AL-DRWISH, 2024, p. 50]])
- The inclusion of GLDA in an Armovis system resulted in a stability that was six times higher with CO2 and more than 20 times higher with N2 compared to TTM stability. ([[2024-al-drwish|AL-DRWISH, 2024, p. 50]])
- Foam viscosity is significantly influenced by gas type; N2 produced higher initial foam height because its lower density requires less pressure/velocity to maintain structure compared to CO2. ([[2024-al-drwish|AL-DRWISH, 2024, p. 59]])
- Corrosion inhibitors (CI) had no significant effect on foamability but did impact stability by increasing liquid drainage and reducing half-life. ([[2024-al-drwish|AL-DRWISH, 2024, p. 60]])
- Foam volume in Armovis systems remained constant after initial drop, suggesting the presence of surfactant micelles in the film provided higher resistance to rupture. ([[2024-al-drwish|AL-DRWISH, 2024, p. 64]])
- Duomeen TTM produced the highest foam viscosity at 150°C and 1,000 psi due to its ability to form a 3D network of wormlike micelles (WLMs). ([[2024-al-drwish|AL-DRWISH, 2024, p. 186]])
- The apparent foam viscosity decreased by 36.9% when temperature was raised from 100 °C to 150 °C at a shear rate of 500 s^-1. ([[2024-al-drwish|AL-DRWISH, 2024, p. 97]])
- Duomeen TTM showed high stability under harsh conditions, with its half-life decreasing by only 9.7% compared to ambient conditions. ([[2024-al-drwish|AL-DRWISH, 2024, p. 111]])
- Armogel O produced the highest initial foam volume at ambient conditions but showed lower stability than Duomeen TTM under HPHT. ([[2024-al-drwish|AL-DRWISH, 2024, p. 109]])
- The addition of GLDA to Armovis (referred to as Armogel O in some sections) increased its stability from 4.53 minutes to 13.28 minutes. ([[2024-al-drwish|AL-DRWISH, 2024, p. 71]])
- Surface tension (ST) for produced water was reduced from 68.66 mN/m to 30 mN/m upon adding Duomeen TTM. ([[2024-al-drwish|AL-DRWISH, 2024, p. 79]])
- The inclusion of corrosion inhibitors (CI) significantly reduced foam viscosity and stability, reducing the half-life of the Armovis system by 79.4%. ([[2024-al-drwish|AL-DRWISH, 2024, p. 115]])

## Limitations

Most existing literature on foam stability was conducted at low temperatures and pressures, which do not accurately represent high-temperature, high-pressure (HPHT) reservoir conditions. Additionally, there is a lack of research specifically regarding the impact of corrosion inhibitors and chelating agents on foam stability.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Further studies should be conducted to address the impact of different chelating agents, salinities, and surfactant concentrations on the foamability and stability of Armovis VES. Future experiments will also investigate the effect of crude oil on foams' stability using microfluidic models.
