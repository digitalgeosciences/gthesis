# DEGRADATION OF METHYL TERTIARY BUTYL ETHER USING UV/TITANIUM DIOXIDE AND CARBON NANOTUBES

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the removal of MTBE and its degradation by-products (acetone, TBA, and TBF) from water using a combination of TiO2 photocatalysis and adsorption by multi-walled carbon nanotubes (MWCNTs). The study evaluates different materials (TiO2, MWCNT, and 5% TiO2-MWCNT) under varying dosages and UV light intensities to determine the most efficient treatment conditions.

---

## Research Problem

MTBE is a persistent and potentially hazardous fuel additive in groundwater. Existing removal methods are often inefficient or costly, necessitating the development of environmentally friendly and cost-effective advanced oxidation processes (AOPs).

## Objectives

- Evaluate the degradation of MTBE using UV/TiO2 photocatalysis.
- Assess the impact of MWCNT on the photocatalytic performance of TiO2.
- Investigate the effect of different catalyst dosages (1 mg, 10 mg, and 20 mg) on MTBE removal.
- Evaluate the influence of varying UV light intensities (0.98 mW/cm2, 1.12 mW/cm2, and 2.17 mW/cm2) on degradation rates.
- Monitor the formation of by-products such as acetone, tertiary butyl alcohol (TBA), and tertiary butyl formate (TBF).
- To demonstrate the removal of MTBE as well as its degradation by-products from MTBE-spiked water using TiO2, MWCNTs, and TiO2 deposited on MWCNTs.
- To examine MTBE removal efficiency under varying conditions of TiO2 and MWCNT dosage and UV light intensity.
- To identify the most efficient treatment condition in removing MTBE and its degradation by-products (acetone, TBA and TBF) from contaminated water.
- Develop a water treatment technique that efficiently removes MT1BE and its degradation by-products from water.
- Combine the mechanisms of TiO2-photocatalysis and adsorption by multi-walled carbon nanotubes (MWCNTs).
- Synthesize and characterize 5% TiO2-MWCNT composites.
- Evaluate the photoactivity of 5% TiO2-MWCNT, anatase TiO2 powder, and MWCNTs under various UV-A intensities.
- To evaluate the degradation of MTBE using UV/Titanium Dioxide.
- To investigate the role of carbon nanotubes in enhancing the photocatalytic process.

## Methods

The study utilized a modified sol-gel synthesis method to create 5% TiO2-MWCNT composites. Characterization of materials was performed using FE-SEM (Tescan Lyra 3), XRD (Rigaku Ultima IV MPD), and EDX (Oxford INCA Penta FETx3). MTBE-spiked water samples were prepared at ~1 ppm concentration buffered to pH 7. Treatment was conducted in a Luzchem LZC-4X photoreactor using UV-A lamps (350 nm) for two hours. Analysis of MTBE and by-products (acetone, TBA, TBF) was performed via GC-MS (Thermo Scientific Trace GC ultra) with an Rtx-502.2 column.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized three types of materials: 5% TiO2-MWCNT composite, anatase TiO2 powder, and MWCNT. Three UV-A intensities (0.98 mW/cm2, 1.12 mW/cm2, and 2.17 mW/cm2) were tested across three dosage levels (1 mg, 10 mg, and 20 mg). Calibration curves for MTBE and acetone were established using area counts to determine concentrations in ppb.

## Key Findings

- MTBE removal was primarily attributed to the photocatalytic effect of TiO2, with removal rates reaching approximately 95%. ([[2013-muhyedeen|Muhyedeen, 2013, p. 10]])
- MWCNTs combined with UV light showed limited removal capabilities for MTBE, with rates up to 68.21%. ([[2013-muhyedeen|Muhyedeen, 2013, p. 10]])
- The TiO2-MWCNT composite exhibited the lowest removal rates compared to pure TiO2 or MWCNT. ([[2013-muhyedeen|Muhyedeen, 2013, p. 10]])
- Analysis of by-products included acetone, tertiary butyl alcohol (TBA), and tertiary butyl formate (TBF). ([[2013-muhyedeen|Muhyedeen, 2013, p. 10]])
- The optimal pH for MTBE concentration was determined to be approximately 7; all subsequent experiments were conducted at this level. ([[2013-muhyedeen|Muhyedeen, 2013, p. 45]])
- FE-SEM of anatase TiO2 powder showed particles agglomerated in spherical shapes with an average grain diameter of approximately 200 nm. ([[2013-muhyedeen|Muhyedeen, 2013, p. 33]])
- EDX analysis of TiO2 powder confirmed it was composed of titanium (Ti) at 46.29 wt % and oxygen (O) at 53.71 wt %. ([[2013-muhyedeen|Muhyedeen, 2013, p. 34]])
- XRD patterns for anatase TiO2 showed peaks at 25.46, 37.94, 48.18, 55.207, and 70.44 degrees. ([[2013-muhyedeen|Muhyedeen, 2013, p. 35]])
- MWCNT samples were observed to be winding, entangled, and approximately 50 nm in diameter. ([[2013-muhyedeen|Muhyedeen, 2013, p. 37]])
- EDX of MWCNT showed carbon (C) at 57.64 wt % and oxygen (O) at 53.71 wt %. ([[2013-muhyedeen|Muhyedeen, 2013, p. 38]])
- XRD for MWCNT showed peaks at 25.62, 43.78, and 77.15 degrees corresponding to hexagonal graphite. ([[2013-muhyedeen|Muhyedeen, 2013, p. 39]])
- The 5% TiO2-MWCNT composite showed a surface structure where TiO2 particles were distributed on MWCNTs with an average diameter of approximately 0.1 µm. ([[2013-muhyedeen|Muhyedeen, 2013, p. 40]])
- XRD for the 5% TiO2-MWCNT composite confirmed the presence of both graphite structures and TiO2 peaks at 37.55 and 54.53 degrees. ([[2013-muhyedeen|Muhyedeen, 2013, p. 43]])
- In dark experiments with a dosage of 10 mg, MTBE residual was 65% for 5% TiO2-MWCNT, 78% for TiO2, and 56% for MWCNT. ([[2013-muhyedeen|Muhyedeen, 2013, p. 48]])
- At a dosage of 20 mg in dark conditions, 5% TiO2-MWCNT yielded 63% residual, while TiO2 and MWCNT yielded 68% and 76% respectively. ([[2013-muhyedeen|Muhyedeen, 2013, p. 48]])

## Limitations

The study noted that the lower removal rates observed at a UV-A intensity of 2.17 mW/cm2 may be due to the intensity not being optimal for degradation; however, further investigation is required to understand this.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Synthesize TiO2-MWCNT composites with higher TiO2 loadings and investigate their photodegradation capabilities.
- Perform a detailed analysis of by-products to ensure no harmful contaminants are generated.
- Test the effectiveness of removing other contaminants, such as benzene.
- Find techniques to overcome the problem of settling of the materials.
