# SELECTION AND OPTIMIZATION OF DEMULSIFIER BASED ON PHYSIO-CHEMICAL CHARACTERISTICS OF EMULSION

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the root causes of demulsifier ineffectiveness by evaluating two types of chemical demulsifiers (Type 1 and Type 2) on oil samples mixed with formation water and seawater. It concludes that Type 1, containing non-ionic surfactants, performs more consistently across varying crude properties and brine conditions compared to Type 2.

---

## Research Problem

The selection of an incompatible chemical demulsifier can lead to poor emulsion breakage or even re-emulsification, impacting production, transportation, and refining. The study aims to identify the root causes of this ineffectiveness by analyzing the physiochemical properties of oil, water, and the demulsifiers themselves.

## Objectives

- To determine the optimum chemical demulsifier type to break emulsions effectively through achieving a deeper understanding of the underlying physical chemistry of oil, emulsion, brine, and demulsifier used (different functional groups).
- To evaluate the performance of different commercially available demulsifiers in terms of water separation.
- To study the relationship between asphaltene, sulfur, density, viscosity, resin, type of demulsifier, and emulsion separation index.

## Methods

The study utilized 12 dead oil samples (verified for water content via Karl-Fischer titration) mixed with formation water and seawater at a 80:20 water-to-oil ratio. Samples were blended for 1.5 minutes at 300 RPM. Analysis included SARA, viscosity, density, and sulfur content for oils; pH and TDS for water; and calculation of the Emulsion Separation Index (ESI) based on volumes of separated water measured at 5, 10, 15, and 20 minutes. Two demulsifiers were tested: Type 1 (primarily non-ionic surfactants) and Type 2 (mixture of non-ionic and anionic surfactants).

## Data and Materials

12 dead oil samples from various fields; 1 formation water sample; 1 seawater sample; 2 types of chemical demulsifiers (Type 1 and Type 2); measurements taken at 4 time intervals (5, 10, 15, 20 minutes).

## Key Findings

- Oil density and viscosity both increase proportionally with increasing sulfur content, which is correlated to higher asphaltene and resin concentrations. ([[2021-altowilib|Altowilib, 2021, p. 32]])
- Formation water exhibited higher salinity, polarity, and water hardness compared to seawater, while seawater had higher sulfate ions and pH. ([[2021-altowilib|Altowilib, 2021, p. 49]])
- Type 1 demulsifier showed more stable performance in both formation water and seawater across varying oil samples compared to Type 2. ([[2021-altowilib|Altowilib, 2021, p. 55]])
- Type 1 demulsifier's efficiency remained stable despite increases in asphaltene, resin, density, or viscosity. ([[2021-altowilib|Altowilib, 2021, p. 56]])
- Type 2 demulsifier performance was very poor in seawater-based emulsions as asphaltene or resin content increased (except for Sample 10). ([[2021-altowilib|Altowilib, 2021, p. 53]])
