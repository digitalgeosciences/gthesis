# Improving Sandstone Matrix Stimulation for Oil Wells by Gas Preconditioning

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of gas preconditioning (using CO2 and N2) on the effectiveness of mud acid stimulation in sandstone reservoirs. It concludes that gas preconditioning prevents the formation of harmful emulsions/sludges from oil-acid interactions, leading to a more consistent and significant increase in permeability compared to standard acidizing.

---

## Research Problem

The research addresses the problem of inefficient acidizing in sandstone reservoirs where oil displacement is a challenge. Specifically, it investigates how preconditioning with different gases (N2 vs. CO2) affects the restoration of permeability after damage and the overall effectiveness of acid treatment for stimulation.

## Objectives

- Determine the effect of acid volume on permeability improvement without gas preconditioning to identify the optimum acid volume.
- Compare the effectiveness of different types of gas (CO2 and N2) used for preconditioning.
- Investigate the effect of gas preconditioning prior to acid injection on sandstone matrix stimulation.
- Compare the performance of N2 and CO2 as preconditioning gases.
- Determine the optimal volume of preconditioning gas required for effective stimulation.

## Methods

The study utilized 40 Berea sandstone core plugs. A multi-stage experimental procedure was employed: (1) establishing baseline water and oil permeability; (2) inducing formation damage using a mud (Bentonite/water mixture); (3) measuring post-damage permeability; (4) performing acid stimulation with 'mud acid' (12% HCl, 3% HF); and (5) repeating the process with gas preconditioning (N2 or CO2) prior to acid injection. Core conditions were maintained at 180°F and 3000 psi.

## Data and Materials

Berea core plugs; three experimental phases; gas preconditioning volumes ranging from 50-200 PV; acid volumes ranging from 100-320 PV; reservoir conditions of 3,000 psi and 180°F.

## Key Findings

- Without gas preconditioning, permeability improvement peaked at 235 pore volumes of acid with a recovery of 144%, but dropped to 104% when increased to 320 pore volumes due to oil-acid reaction precipitates. ([[1999-al-muhareb|Al-Muhareb, 1999, p. 43]])
- With N2 preconditioning, permeability improvements were significantly higher and more consistent with acid volume: 122%, 143%, 249%, and 323% for 100, 150, 235, and 320 pore volumes respectively. ([[1999-al-muhareb|Al-Muhareb, 1999, p. 44]])
- CO2 preconditioning yielded the highest average permeability improvements: 200%, 295%, 330%, and 381% for acid volumes of 100, 150, 235, and 320. ([[1999-al-muhareb|Al-Muhareb, 1999, p. 45]])
- CO2 was found to be more effective than N2 because its miscibility with crude oil is achieved at significantly lower pressures (lower MMP). ([[1999-al-muhareb|Al-Muhareb, 1999, p. 47]])
- The primary benefit of gas preconditioning is the elimination of interaction between crude oil and acid byproducts, preventing the formation of emulsions or sludges. ([[1999-al-muhareb|Al-Muhareb, 1999, p. 46]])
- Without gas preconditioning (Phase-1), permeability restoration peaked at 235 pore volumes of acid and then decreased. ([[1999-al-muhareb|Al-Muhareb, 1999, p. 80]])
- Preconditioning with N2 or CO2 improves the relative permeability to aqueous acidizing fluids and increases their effectiveness in stimulating the oil zone. ([[1999-al-muhareb|Al-Muhareb, 1999, p. 80]])
- With gas preconditioning, the response from acid treatment is roughly proportional to the acid volumes (100-320 pore volumes tested). ([[1999-al-muhareb|Al-Muhareb, 1999, p. 80]])
- 75 pore volumes of preconditioning gas were found to be the minimum volume required for effective stimulation. ([[1999-al-muhareb|Al-Muhareb, 1999, p. 80]])
- CO2 gas is more efficient than N2 gas because its miscibility pressure is significantly lower than that of nitrogen. ([[1999-al-muhareb|Al-Muhareb, 1999, p. 81]])

## Limitations

The study used core plugs which may not fully represent the scale of a full reservoir; larger core plugs are recommended for better representation of the stimulation process.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Use larger core plugs to better represent the stimulation process.
- Perform experiments at 5,000 psi to compare N2 and CO2 results under higher pressure.
- Test other preconditioning gases such as natural gas.
- Test both light and heavy types of crude oils.
