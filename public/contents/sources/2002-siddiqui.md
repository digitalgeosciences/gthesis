# Reservoir quality of the Unayzah formation in Waqr, Tinat, and Haradh fields

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the reservoir quality of the Unayzah Formation in the Waqr, Tinat, and Haradh fields by analyzing petrographic characteristics, diagenetic processes, and grain size distributions. It concludes that reservoir quality is directly correlated with depositional environments, where high-energy beach environments produced better porosity than low-energy sheltered shelf environments.

---

## Research Problem

The challenge of accurately predicting reservoir properties like porosity in the Unayzah Formation (a Permo-Carboniferous siliciclastic sequence) from well logs, specifically addressing how different classification methodologies (genetic vs. nongenetic) affect results.

## Objectives

- Study the diagenetic and depositional controls on reservoir quality.
- To determine the depositional environment using grain size analysis and factor analysis.
- Apply neural networks to predict porosity and classify lithofacies using well log data.
- To characterize the detrital and authigenic components of the Unayzah sandstone.
- To identify and quantify the primary and secondary porosity-forming diagenetic processes.
- To correlate reservoir quality with specific depositional environments.

## Methods

The study utilized thin-section petrography to identify mineral components (quartz, feldspar, lithics) and cement types (calcite, clay, quartz). SEM imaging was used to observe grain coatings and pore-filling cements. Grain size analysis was performed via mechanical sieving of core samples from three wells (WAQR-A, TINT-A, HRDH-A), followed by calculation of statistical parameters (mean, sorting, skewness, kurtosis) and plotting on phi scales. Factor analysis (Q-mode) was applied to the grain size data to identify distinct sediment populations. Well log analysis was used to interpret electrofacies and depositional environments.

## Data and Materials

Core samples from three wells: WAQR-A, TINT-A, and HRDH-A; 24 core samples were selected for grain size analysis and factor analysis; thin sections of Unayzah sandstone; SEM images of various mineral phases.

## Key Findings

- The Unayzah Formation consists of sandstones (mostly moderately well-sorted quartz arenite), shale, and siltstone deposited in a shallow or marginal marine environment. ([[2002-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2002, p. 23]])
- In the Haradh field, Unit 1 (quartz wacke) has porosity ranging from 10-15% (average 12%) with good to moderate reservoir quality; Unit 2 (argillaceous wacke) has porosity of 2-6% (average 5%) with poor reservoir quality. ([[2002-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2002, p. 43]])
- In the Tinat field, Group 1 (quartz arenite) has porosity ranging from 12-20% (average 15%), while Group 2 (quartz wacke) has porosity of 2-5% (average 3%). ([[2002-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2002, p. 50]])
- In the Waqr field, first group (quartz wacke) has porosity ranging from 5-15% (average 10%), and second group (sandy mudstone) has porosity of 1-3% (average 2%). ([[2002-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2002, p. 56]])
- Calcite is a major cement in the Unayzah sandstone, representing 10 to 65% of total cement; its dissolution is a primary driver for secondary porosity. ([[2002-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2002, p. 47]])
- Clay minerals (illite, chlorite, smectite) as cement can significantly reduce formation permeability even at low concentrations (2-3 weight%). ([[2002-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2002, p. 47]])
- Quartz overgrowths are a common and permanent form of cement that reduces porosity in cleaner sandstones. ([[2002-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2002, p. 49]])
- Genetically derived porosity predictions using neural networks were found to be in better agreement with the neutron porosity than non-genetic methods. ([[2002-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2002, p. 23]])
- The detrital component of the Unayzah sandstone is primarily monocrystalline quartz, ranging from 50-95% of total detrital components. ([[2002-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2002, p. 57]])
- Feldspars (K-feldspar and plagioclase) average approximately 2% of the detrital component; most show evidence of diagenetic alteration and dissolution. ([[2002-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2002, p. 57]])
- Quartz is the most common cement, representing 50-70% of total cement in some samples; calcite accounts for 5-40%. ([[2002-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2002, p. 59]])
- In Haradh field, the Unayzah sandstone consists of 70% detrital grains, 20% cement, and 10% pore spaces; in Tinat field, it is 85% grains, 5% cement, and 10% pores. ([[2002-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2002, p. 63]])
- Point contacts (indicating initial compaction) average 25%, while long contacts (moderate compaction) are the most common at an average of 75%. ([[2002-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2002, p. 64]])
- concavo-convex and sutured contacts, indicating high pressure solution, range from 0-15% and average 1%. ([[2002-siddiqui|Siddiqui, 2002]])

## Limitations

The nongenetic approach was noted as being less accurate for porosity prediction because it lacks the lithofacies differentiation provided by the genetic approach.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Use studied wells as a model to increase understanding of reservoir quality by adding more data from Haradh, Tinat, and Waar fields.
- Apply the neural network approach to study deeper reservoir intervals of the Unayzah Formation.
- Utilize FMI and geochemical logs to better understand the depositional history.
