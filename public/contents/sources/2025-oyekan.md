# ESTIMATION OF WATER SATURATION IN SAND DUNES USING SOURCE-RECEIVER OFFSET

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the estimation of water saturation in sand dunes using source-receiver offset and seismic velocity models. It develops a mathematical framework combining Hertz-Mindlin and Gassmann models to relate P-wave velocity to depth, porosity, and water saturation, then applies this to field data from a barchan dune near Dammam.

---

## Research Problem

Determining partial water saturation in unconsolidated sediments like sand dunes is challenging because while increasing water content typically decreases P-wave velocity, reaching full saturation causes a significant jump in velocity. Existing methods using travel times are prone to errors from poor signal quality; therefore, using source-receiver offset as an alternative metric for inversion is proposed.

## Objectives

- Develop a function that relates seismic P-wave velocity to water saturation Vp(Sw) through the Digby’s equation, Hertz-Mindlin equation, and Gassmann’s fluid substitution models to calculate the offset (X) of the DDW.
- Estimate the water saturation at the maximum depths of rays corresponding to the offsets at which the DDW is received in the field.
- Compare the results to water saturation values measured in the lab in sand samples obtained from the study area at the depths where the water saturation was inverted.
- Perform sensitivity analysis to provide insights on how water saturation derived from my inversion is affected by changes in the mineralogical composition of the dunes, dune’s porosity, and the sphericity of the sand grains.
- Investigate the use of source-receiver offset and maximum depth of direct P-waves to estimate water saturation.
- Select and apply appropriate rock physics models (Hertz-Mindlin and Gassmann) to characterize the sand dune's physical properties.
- Develop a mathematical function relating P-wave velocity to depth, water saturation, porosity, and mineral properties.
- Establish an inversion method to determine water saturation (Sw) from observed source--receiver offsets of diving direct waves.
- Perform sensitivity analysis on key parameters like mineralogy, porosity, and grain sphericity.
- Develop an inversion procedure for determining the degree of saturation in a barchan dune.
- Integrate existing rock physics models, granular contact and fluid replacement models, and adapt them to sand dune environments.
- Establish a relationship between P-wave velocity, water saturation, and maximum depth of the direct diving wave.
- Evaluate the impact of porosity and sphericity factor variations on the inversion of water saturation estimates.

## Methods

The study developed an inversion procedure using source-receiver offset data. It integrated rock physics models (e.g., Gassmann, Hertz-Mindlin) with granular contact and fluid replacement models to create a function relating P-wave velocity, water saturation, and the maximum depth of the direct diving wave. This was used to construct an objective function based on measured source-receiver offsets compared against values from numerical integration of offset-depth profiles. The method was tested using data from a barchan dune in the Eastern Province of Saudi Arabia, where lab measurements were performed on sand samples collected at various depths.

## Data and Materials

Data includes: 10 sand samples collected from a barchan dune in the Al Jafurah desert; 73 shot gathers from conventional seismic experiments (8-kg sledgehammer); and 68 total offsets from high-resolution seismic surveys. Laboratory analysis of mineralogy, porosity, and water content was performed on these samples.

## Key Findings

- Inverted water saturation values fell within the range of (0.0882 - 0.1514) with a mean of 0.1035. ([[2025-oyekan|Oyekan, 2025, p. 12]])
- Comparison with laboratory values of saturation collectively indicate a dry sand dune. ([[2025-oyekan|Oyekan, 2025, p. 12]])
- The study determined that the use of source-receiver offset rather than time is less prone to errors from inaccurate picking due to low data quality. ([[2025-oyekan|Oyekan, 2025, p. 17]])
- Sensitivity analysis showed that changes in mineralogy, porosity, and sphericity factors significantly affect the water saturation estimate derived from the inversion procedure. ([[2025-oyekan|Oyekan, 2025, p. 12]])
- Seismic refraction and reflection methods are effective for mapping water tables in aquifers but less successful for determining saturation in partially saturated unconsolided sediments due to low velocity variation below 100% saturation. ([[2025-oyekan|Oyekan, 2025, p. 28]])
- The Hertz-Mindlin model is used to calculate dry bulk modulus (Kdry) and shear modulus (mu_dry) for a random packing of spheres, incorporating a sphericity factor (f) to account for grain deviation from perfect spheres. ([[2025-oyekan|Oyekan, 2025, p. 33]])
- The Gassmann equation is used to predict the bulk modulus of saturated rock based on fluid substitution, and it is valid only at sufficiently low frequencies where pore pressures equilibrate. ([[2025-oyekan|Oyekan, 2025, p. 38]])
- The shear modulus (mu) is assumed to be independent of fluid content in the Gassmann model (mu_sat = mu_dry). ([[2025-oyekan|Oyekan, 2025, p. 39]])
- The P-wave velocity in a fluid-saturated rock is governed by the bulk modulus (kSat) and density (rhoSat), where kSat depends on dry rock properties (kDry), mineral matrix, and pore fluid properties via Gassmann's model. ([[2025-oyekan|Oyekan, 2025, p. 41]])
- The P-wave velocity of saturated sand increases by approximately 2.5 times compared to sand at 92% saturation as it becomes stiffer and more resistant to deformation. ([[2025-oyekan|Oyekan, 2025, p. 47]])
- Sand density increases by about 20% as saturation increases from 0% to 99%, while the bulk modulus only increases by approximately 4%. ([[2025-oyekan|Oyekan, 2025, p. 45]])
- The mineral composition of the study dune was determined to be 83% quartz, 4% feldspars, 3% calcite, 1% gypsum, and minor amounts of other minerals. ([[2025-oyekan|Oyekan, 2025, p. 54]])
- Measured water saturation values in the field samples ranged from 0.0139 to 0.1120 (mean 0.0513). ([[2025-oyekan|Oyekan, 2025, p. 63]])
- Inverted water saturation values using the proposed method fell within a range of 0.0882 to 0.1514 (mean 0.1035). ([[2025-oyekan|Oyekan, 2025, p. 63]])
- Both measured and inverted water content values were found to be within the expected global ranges for desert dunes. ([[2025-oyekan|Oyekan, 2025, p. 63]])

## Limitations

The accuracy of the inversion is dependent on several poorly constrained parameters, including mineralogical composition, porosity, and grain sphericity (f), which can vary significantly in real-world dune environments due to varying sorting and grain sizes.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Test other rock physics models to establish the VP(z, Sw) formula.
- Include other field measurements such as the arrival times of the DDW in the objective function.
- Include anisotropic effects of dipping stratifications within dunes.
- Include viscoelastic effects within dunes.
- Test the method on other parts of the same dune (e.g., windward, slip face), other dune types (e.g., sheets, star, parabolic), and other conditions (e.g., vegetated, higher saturation).
