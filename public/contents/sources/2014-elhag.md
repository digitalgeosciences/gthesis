# DEVELOPMENT OF STABLE BENTONITE FOR DRILLING FLUID FORMULATIONS USING LOCAL SOURCES

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the characterization and modification of local calcium bentonite from the Khulays area in Saudi Arabia to produce a sodium-activated version suitable for drilling fluids. The study concludes that treated local bentonite meets API standards for rheology and filtration, making it a viable alternative to imported materials.

---

## Research Problem

The need for high-quality, stable bentonite that meets American Petroleum Institute (API) specifications for drilling fluids, specifically focusing on utilizing locally available sources to improve supply chain reliability and cost-effectiveness in the petroleum industry.

## Objectives

- Characterize the mineralogical composition of raw bentonite using XRD and XRF.
- Evaluate the physical properties of processed bentonite including particle size distribution and purity.
- Assess the rheological properties of drilling muds formulated with local bentonite.
- Determine the thermal stability of the drilling fluids under high-pressure and high-temperature (HP/HT) conditions.
- Define the compositional variation of the local Saudi bentonite from standard drilling grade one.
- Enhance its properties using soda ash treatment.

## Methods

The study involved characterizing raw local bentonite via XRD, XRF, and SEM to determine mineralogy and purity. Purification was performed through a designed process including sifting and chemical treatment. Modification of Ca-bentonite into Na-bentonite was achieved using a thermo-chemical upgrading process with soda ash (Na2CO3). Evaluation included rheological testing (viscosity, yield point, gel strength) using a Fan viscometer, filtration tests in an API filter press, particle size distribution analysis via sieve shaker and laser diffraction, and aging tests in a rolling oven to simulate high temperature conditions. HPHT rheology was assessed using a Chandler HPHT model 5550.

## Data and Materials

Samples were collected from 8 different locations in the Khulays deposit. Two main samples (bentonite-1 and bentonite-2) were used for comparison against a standard drilling grade bentonite. Analysis included XRD patterns, XRF weight percentages, SEM images, Zeta potential measurements at various pH levels, and viscometry tests using a Fann 35 viscometer.

## Key Findings

- XRD analysis identified key minerals such as quartz (SiO2), plagioclase (An), and various clay minerals in the bentonite samples. ([[2014-elhag|ELHAG, 2014, p. 1]])
- XRF spectra confirmed the presence of major elements including Si, Al, Fe, Ca, K, Mg, and Na in the mineral samples. ([[2014-elhag|ELHAG, 2014, p. 1]])
- Particle size distribution analysis showed distinct peaks for different sample types (e.g., one peaking at 10 μm and another at 50-60 μm). ([[2014-elhag|ELHAG, 2014, p. 1]])
- The study successfully identified a process flow from mining to sedimentation to produce stable bentonite. ([[2014-elhag|ELHAG, 2014, p. 1]])
- Rheological properties including viscosity and yield stress were measured for the formulated drilling muds. ([[2014-elhag|ELHAG, 2014, p. 1]])
- Local Khulays bentonite was identified as calcium bentonite (Ca-bentonite) with a high smectite content (90% montmorillonite). ([[2014-elhag|ELHAG, 2014, p. 22]])
- The local bentonite's properties, after thermo-chemical upgrading, outperformed the commercial standard bentonite in terms of rheological properties. ([[2014-elhag|ELHAG, 2014, p. 18]])
- Treatment with only 2-4% soda ash (Na2CO3) was sufficient to significantly improve the quality and suitability for drilling fluids. ([[2014-elhag|ELHAG, 2014, p. 22]])
- The modified local bentonite successfully met the target of standard API specifications for rheology and filtration. ([[2014-elhag|ELHAG, 2014, p. 18]])
- XRD analysis confirmed that raw bentonite is mainly montmorillonite with small amounts of kaolinite and quartz. ([[2014-elhag|ELHAG, 2014, p. 34]])
- The addition of soda ash increased the Na/Ca ratio, which is essential for enhancing swelling characteristics. ([[2014-elhag|ELHAG, 2014, p. 22]])
- Sieve analysis showed that at least 96% wt. should pass the 200 mesh (75 µm) to meet drilling grade standards. ([[2014-elhag|ELHAG, 2014, p. 49]])
- Raw Khulays bentonite contains significant impurities including quartz (up to 59% in bent-1), feldspar, and iron oxide (up to 30% in raw samples). ([[2014-elhag|ELHAG, 2014, p. 89]])
- Dry sieving only recovered 15% of the sample as usable bentonite (<75 μm) because particles are sticky. ([[2014-elhag|ELHAG, 2014, p. 70]])
- Wet sieving provided a higher recovery rate (>85%) compared to dry sieving. ([[2014-elhag|ELHAG, 2014, p. 72]])

## Limitations

Standard soda ash treatment alone was insufficient to reach the required plastic viscosity for drilling fluid applications; only the combination of soda ash and hot stirring achieved significant improvements.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Use purified and treated local Khulays bentonite as a cost-effective drilling fluid additive for upper sections of oil wells.
- Conduct further investigations with polymers and other additives to improve the filtration properties of the local bentonite.
