# CULTURING, ISOLATION AND GENETIC IDENTIFICATION OF ENDOLITHIC CYANOBACTERIA FOUND IN THE ARABIAN GULF

**Summary**: The thesis investigated the culturing, isolation, and morphometric identification of euendolithic cyanobacteria from calcareous sediments in the Arabian Gulf and Costa Rica. While initial goals for genetic identification were not met due to cultivation failures, the study successfully identified six species from the Arabian Gulf and five from Costa Rica using SEM and LM.

---

## Research Problem

The difficulty in maintaining viable cultures of endolithic cyanobacteria (micro-borers) in laboratory settings, which hinders genetic and phylogenic analysis, and the need for morphometric identification to distinguish between various euendolith species in extreme environments like the Arabian Gulf.

## Objectives

- Identify and isolate endolithic cyanobacteria species from samples collected along the Arabian Gulf (Tarut Island and Mussalamia).
- Culture isolated endolithic cyanobacteria under laboratory conditions.
- Test if isolated strains have the capabilities to bore into calcium by providing carbonate substrates during culturing.
- Genetically identify the isolated endolithic cyanobacteria using molecular techniques including DNA isolation, PCR, and DGG.
- Establish a phylogeny tree for the isolated endolithic cyanobacteria of the Arabian Gulf.
- Use morphometric analysis to describe resident endolithic cyanobacteria from preserved calcareous material from Costa Rica.

## Methods

Samples were collected from Tarut, Mussalamia, and Abu Ali (Arabian Gulf) and Playa Conchal (Costa Rica). Particle size analysis was performed using a standard sieving technique (ASTM C136-01). Morphometric identification involved light microscopy (Leica CME) and scanning electron microscopy (Jsm-5900lr SEM). Samples were prepared for SEM via resin embedding (Spur Low viscosity resin) and carbonate dissolution with 3% HCl. Culturing was performed on Solid and Liquid SN media (Waterbury 1989 formulation). Genetic analysis included DNA isolation, PCR, and Denaturing Gradient Gel Electrophoresis (DGGE).

## Data and Materials

Samples included calcareous sediments from three locations along the Arabian Gulf (Tarut, Mussalamia, and Abu Ali) and sediment samples from Playa Concha, Costa Rica. The study involved measurements of apical cells, vegetative cells, and baeocyte mother cells for multiple species.

## Key Findings

- Carbonate materials from Tarut, Mussalamia, and Abu Ali are predominantly fine particles (73.63% and 63.66%) consisting of ooids, sand grains, shell fragments, and granular carbonate substances (₀₀ μm). ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 47]])
- Playa Conchal sediment consists largely of pellet size (crushed sea shells) carbonate material (94.10%). ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 47]])
- SEM revealed that materials from the Arabian Gulf are heavily bored, with evidence of epilithic communities of diatoms. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 50]])
- Hyella inconstans: Apical cells average 5.8 ± 1.2 μm wide and 11.8 ± 2.5 μm long; vegetative cells are rectangular/square. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 54]])
- Hyella salutans: Apical cells average 11.1 ± 1.9 μm wide and 16.3 ± 3.2 μm long; vegetative cells are cylindrical/isodiametric. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 59]])
- Hyella arbuscula: Apical cells average 4.3 ± 0.7 μm wide and 16.7 μm long; vegetative cells are uniseriate. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 65]])
- Average boring rates of Arabian Gulf endoliths were found to be between 166 and 510 μm³ d⁻±. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 34]])
- Six species of euendolithic cyanobacteria (Hyella reptans, H. inconstans, H. arbuscula, H. salutans, H. immanis, and H. stella) were identified from Arabian Gulf samples using SEM and LM. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 126]])
- Five new species of euendolithic cyanobacteria were found in the Costa Rican samples; three were successfully compared to Arabian Gulf counterparts based on morphometric data. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 126]])
- Hyella stella (Al-Thukair and Golubic 1991) was identified with apical cells measuring 10.0±1.8 µm wide and 11.4±2.3 µm long. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 70]])
- Hyella sp 2 (current study) showed apical cells of 6.50 ± 1.3 µm width and 10.14 ± 2.3 µm length, with branching primarily by cell slippage. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 99]])
- Hyella sp 3 (current study) showed apical cells of 8.73 ± 1.6 µm width and 9.16 ± 2.2 µm length, with a predominantly dichotomous branching pattern. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 108]])
- The re-boring test on calcite grains (2mm by 2mm) confirmed that isolated baeocytes retained their ability to bore into calcareous substrates. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 125]])
- Apical cells are the most important determinants in the development of these organisms as they are the sites of primary apical growth and cell differentiation. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 116]])

## Limitations

The study notes that the morphology of laboratory cultures may not always resemble the native form of cyanobacteria, and they are often difficult to establish in vitro as well as being slow growers and weak competitors.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Establish endolithic cyanobacteria in culture to compare the behavior of newly identified Costa Rican euendoliths with their natural populations. Future studies should focus on optimal growth factors regulating growth and morphometric identification from Costa Rica.
