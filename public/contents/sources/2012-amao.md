# CULTURING, ISOLATION AND GENETIC IDENTIFICATION OF ENDOLITHIC CYANOBACTERIA FOUND IN THE ARABIAN GULF

**Summary**: The thesis investigated the culturing, isolation, and morphometric identification of euendolithic cyanobacteria from calcareous sediments in the Arabian Gulf and Costa Rica. While laboratory cultivation was unsuccessful, morphometric analysis using SEM and light microscopy identified several species, including three that share similarities with known Arabian Gulf endoliths.

---

## Research Problem

The identification and characterization of endolithic cyanobacteria within marine calcareous sediments are critical for understanding biogeochemical cycles and adaptation to extreme environments, yet many species remain unidentified due to the limitations of culture-independent methods.

## Objectives

- Identify, isolate, and culture euendolithic cyanobacteria from calcareous sediments.
- Study the morphometric diversity of these organisms using micromanipulation, resin embedding, light microscopy, and SEM.
- Compare Arabian Gulf assemblages with known samples (e.g., Costa Rica) to identify new species.
- Identify and isolate endolithic cyanobacteria species from samples collected along the Arabian Gulf (Tarut Island and Mussalamia).
- Culture isolated endolithic cyanobacteria under laboratory conditions.
- Test if isolated strains have the capabilities to bore into calcium by providing carbonate substrates (calcite) during cultivation.
- Genetically identify the isolated end13.endolithic cyanobacteria using molecular techniques including DNA isolation, PCR, and DGG.
- Establish a phylogeny tree for the isolated endolithic cyanobacteria of the Arabian Gulf.
- Use morphometric analysis to describe resident endolithic cyanobacteria from preserved calcareous material from Costa Rica.
- Compare Costa Rican species (if any) to known endolithic cyanobacteria from the Arabian Gulf.
- Ascertain if the endolithic cyanobacteria can bore into other materials.
- Attempt to culture endolithic cyanobacteria from calcareous materials collected from Costa Rica.
- Identify and isolate endolithic cyanobacteria from calcareous sediment samples in the Arabian Gulf.
- Compare the diversity of these microorganisms with those found at Playa Conchal, Costa Rica.
- Cultivate isolated strains to determine if they retain their ability to bore into carbonate substrates.
- Characterize the morphology and population structure of various species using light microscopy and SEM.
- Culturing and genetically identifying euendolithic cyanobacteria from the Arabian Gulf
- Morphometric identification of euendolithic cyanobacteria from calcareous sediments collected from the Pacific coast of Costa Rica (Playa Concha)
- Identify endolithic cyanobacteria from calcareous sediments along the Arabian Gulf
- Isolate and culture these microorganisms
- Perform genetic identification using 16S rRNA sequencing

## Methods

Samples were collected from three sites in the Arabian Gulf (Tarut, Mussalamia, Abu Ali) and one site in Costa Rica. Particle size analysis was performed via sieve analysis (ASTM C136-01). Microscopic identification of species included Hyella inconstans, H. immanis, H. salutan, H. reptans, H. arbuscula, and H. stella. Culturing involved the preparation of Solid and Liquid SN media (based on Waterbury 1989) for isolation and growth. SEM analysis was conducted after sample dehydration in ethanol and embedding in resin (Spur Low viscosity).

## Data and Materials

Samples included calcareous sediments from three locations in the Arabian Gulf (Tarut, Mussalamia, and Abu Ali) and samples from the Pacific coast of Costa Rica (Playa Concha). Morphometric data were collected for multiple cell types across several identified species.

## Key Findings

- Three new species of endolithic cyanobacteria were identified in the Costa Rican samples based on morphometric measurements. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 15]])
- The identified species were classified under the genus Hyella and compared with known Arabian Gulf assemblages. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 15]])
- Euendoliths are photosynthetic microorganisms that play a considerable role in biogeochemical cycles and support various grazing animals. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 15]])
- The study identified euendolithic cyanobacteria from calcareous sediments at Tarut, Mussalamia, and Abu Ali. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 15]])
- Ten species of endolithic cyanobacteria were identified in the Arabian Gulf, including members of Hyella, Solentia, and Cyanosaccus. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 31]])
- The mean boring rates for seven colonies of Arabian Gulf endoliths range between 166 and 510 µm³ d⁻¹ at various growth stages. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 33]])
- A specific boring rate of 10 µm³ d⁻¹ was measured in calcite under light intensity of 20–25 μE m⁻² s⁻¹ at a 16:8 h LD cycle. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 33]])
- Carbon isotope (14C) measurements indicate that atmospheric CO2 is used by endolithic cyanobacteria rather than dissolved inorganic carbon from carbonate minerals. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 33]])
- Morphometric measurement of apical, vegetative, and reproductive cells are frequently used to differentiate species of endolithic characteristics. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 19]])
- Multi-element analyses showed enrichment of phosphorus, barium, lead, and zinc in the endolithic zone over host rock, while magnesium, calcium, iron, and copper were depleted (Page 34). ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 34]])
- Carbonate materials from Tarut, Mussalamia, and Abu Ali are predominantly fine particles (73.63% and 63.66%) consisting of ooids, sand grains, shell fragments, and granular carbonate substances. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 46]])
- Playa Conchal sediment consists largely of pellet-sized crushed sea shells (94.10%). ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 46]])
- SEM revealed that materials from the Arabian Gulf are heavily bored, with evidence of epilithic communities of diatoms. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 49]])
- Identified species in the Arabian Gulf include H. immanis, H. salutan, H. reptans, H. arbuscula, and H. stella. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 53]])
- Hyella inconstans showed average apical cell dimensions of 5.8 ± 1.2 µm wide and 11.8 ± 2.5 µm long. ([[2012-amao|Amao, 2012, p. 54]])

## Limitations

The primary challenge is the difficulty in removing organisms intact from hard carbonate substrates and the fact that laboratory cultures may not always reflect the morphology of native forms.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Establish endolithic cyanobacteria in culture to compare behavior of newly identified Costa Rican euendoliths with natural populations; identify optimal growth factors regulating growth; perform morphometric identification of Costa Rican samples.
