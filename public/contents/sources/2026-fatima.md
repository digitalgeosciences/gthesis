# Managed Aquifer Recharge for Sustainable Groundwater Management in Eastern Saudi Arabia: A Lab-Scale Study

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the potential of treated wastewater (TWW) for managed aquifer recharge (MAR) in a coastal aquifer in the Qatif region, Saudi Arabia. Using lab-scale column experiments and Electrical Resistivity Tomography (ERT), it evaluates clogging behavior across different aquifer materials and TWW types, concluding that Material D is most suitable for injection.

---

## Research Problem

Groundwater depletion and seawater intrusion in the arid coastal regions of eastern Saudi Arabia necessitate sustainable recharge methods like MAR using available treated wastewater (TWW) surpluses, which are currently hindered by potential clogging and unknown water quality impacts.

## Objectives

- Evaluating the impact of treated wastewater (TWW) on aquifer hydraulic properties using laboratory column experiments.
- Determining optimal TWW recharge rates for a sustainable managed aquifer recharge (MAR) in the Saudi Arabian sandy aquifer, ensuring the preservation of aquifer integrity.
- Making recommendations for the optimal TWW quality for MAR to prevent adverse impacts on freshwater resources and ensure long-term aquifer sustainability.
- Assess the potential of TWW for sustainable managed aquifer recharge in the Qatif region.
- Evaluate the impact of different aquifer materials and TWW types on clogging behavior.
- Investigate the effect of varying pumping rates on hydraulic conductivity and clogging.

## Methods

The study employs a multi-phase approach: 1. Characterization of three aquifer materials (U, L, D) from different depths (1m, 2m, 3m) using KSAT for hydraulic conductivity, sieve analysis for grain size distribution, and SEM-EDS, XRD, XRF, and QEMSCAN for mineralogy/morphology. 2. Characterization of two TWW types (TWW1 tertiary, TWW2 secondary) via ion chromatography and ICP-MS. 3. Flow-through column experiments with three pumping rates (1.5, 2.3, 3.0 mL/min) to evaluate clogging and hydraulic conductivity decline over 100 hours. 4. A sandbox model experiment using the best-case scenario identified in columns, monitored via Electrical Resistivity Tomography (ERT) for 2D imaging of recharge dynamics.

## Data and Materials

12 column experiments using Materials L and D; 3 types of aquifer materials (U, L, D); 2 types of treated wastewater (TWW1, TWW2); 1 sandbox experiment with a 30 cm deep sand bed; 33 ERT electrodes.

## Key Findings

- Calcite precipitation and redissolution were identified as the major contributors to clogging in the MAR systems. ([[2026-fatima|Fatima, 2026, p. 17]])
- No major change in water chemistry was observed during the recharge process, highlighting the need for advanced treatment before recharge. ([[2026-fatima|Fatima, 2026, p. 17]])
- ERT demonstrated effectiveness in monitoring MAR operations and is sensitive to salinity and porosity changes. ([[2026-fatima|Fatima, 2026, p. 17]])
- Physical clogging by suspended solids can cause a loss of up to 80-90% of initial hydraulic conductivity at the entry point of columns. ([[2026-fatima|Fatima, 2026, p. 37]])
- Silt-sized particles showed a higher clogging potential than sand-sized particles in experimental settings. ([[2026-fatima|Fatima, 2026, p. 43]])
- The study identified that TWW1 (tertiary) and TWW2 (secondary) were available as distinct sources for recharge evaluation. ([[2026-fatima|Fatima, 2026, p. 52]])
- Material U was found to be very densely packed with a low hydraulic conductivity (Ksat) of 9-14 cm/d, making it unsuitable for column experiments. ([[2026-fatima|Fatima, 2026, p. 48]])
- Material L had a Ksat of 275-388 cm/d, while Material D had a Ksat of 328-684 cm/d; both were classified as silty to fine sand. ([[2026-fatima|Fatima, 2026, p. 49]])
- TWW2 (secondary treated) caused more rapid deterioration of hydraulic performance compared to TWW1 (tertiary treated). ([[2026-fatima|Fatima, 2026, p. 56]])
- Higher pumping rates were strongly correlated with a reduction in both outflow and aquifer hydraulic conductivity. ([[2026-fatima|Fatima, 2026, p. 170]])
- Material D was found to be more suitable for managed aquifer recharge via injection than materials at shallower depths (1m and 2m). ([[2026-fatima|Fatima, 2026, p. 170]])
- No significant change in the chemical composition of TWW (ions, heavy metals, trace elements) was observed during column experiments. ([[2026-fatima|Fatima, 2026, p. 118]])
- The concentration of bromide (Br-) was higher in TWW2 (2-6 mg/L) than in TWW1 (0-2.5 mg/L). ([[2026-fatima|Fatima, 2026, p. 120]])

## Limitations

The study notes that the short run time and length of columns may have limited the observation of chemical changes in recharge water. Additionally, some heavy metals (As, Cr) were found to exceed safety limits in certain samples despite no significant attenuation.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Pre-treat recharge water to meet drinking water standards before aquifer injection; study emerging contaminants like microplastics, PCPs, and PFAS; evaluate long-term impacts of TWW on aquifer properties.
