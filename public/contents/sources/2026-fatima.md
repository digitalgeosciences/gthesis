# Managed Aquifer Recharge for Sustainable Groundwater Management in Eastern Saudi Arabia: A Lab-Scale Study

**Summary**: The thesis evaluates the suitability of treated wastewater (TWW) for managed aquifer recharge (MAR) in a sandy coastal aquifer in eastern Saudi Arabia. It utilizes lab-scale column experiments with real aquifer materials and different TWW qualities to assess hydraulic performance, clogging mechanisms, and potential for sustainable groundwater management.

---

## Research Problem

Groundwater resources in the Eastern Province of Saudi Arabia are threatened by overdraft and seawater intrusion due to low natural recharge rates. There is a surplus of treated wastewater (TWW) that could be used for managed aquifer recharge (MAR), but the impacts of TWW on aquifer hydraulic properties, clogging mechanisms, and water quality remain insufficiently understood in this specific regional context.

## Methods

The study employs an integrated methodology: 1. Characterization of real aquifer materials (U, L, D) from different depths in Al-Qatif based on saturated hydraulic conductivity, particle size distribution, chemical composition, and morphology. 2. Collection of two types of TWW (TWW1 - Tertiary; TWW2 - Secondary) from local treatment plants. 3. Flow-through column experiments at three different pumping rates to determine the most suitable material, water type, and rate. 4. Construction of a sandbox model monitored using Electrical Resistivity Tomography (ERT) to simulate large-scale implementation scenarios.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized three aquifer materials (U: clayey sand; L: silty to fine sand; D: silty to fine sand), two types of treated wastewater (TWW1 and TWW2), and a series of 12 column experiments. The sandbox experiment included an ERT system with 33 electrodes, a recharge well, and a sand compartment.

## Key Findings

- Material U had a median grain size of 0.48 mm, while Material L was 0.67 mm and Material D was 0.91 mm. ([[2026-fatima|Fatima, 2026, p. 51]])
- Hydraulic conductivity (Ksat) for material L was measured at 3.2 m/day and for material D at 4.8 m/day. ([[2026-fatima|Fatima, 2026, p. 47]])
- XRD-based mineral quantification identified quartz as a major component in materials U and L. ([[2026-fatima|Fatima, 2026, p. 81]])
- QEMSCAN analysis of material L showed significant presence of calcite and other minerals, while D was dominated by quartz. ([[2026-fatima|Fatima, 2026, p. 83]])
- XRF-based elemental quantification for materials L and D revealed high concentrations of Si and Ca. ([[2026-fatima|Fatima, 2026, p. 89]])
- TWW2 showed higher levels of chloride (1,450 mg/L) compared to TWW1 (1,360 mg/L). ([[2026-fatima|Fatima, 2026, p. 105]])
- Nitrate concentrations in the effluent were observed for both materials L and D across different pumping rates. ([[2026-fatima|Fatima, 2026, p. 99]])
- There is a treated wastewater (TWW) surplus of about 312 Mm³ per year in eastern Saudi Arabia, which can be a potential source for artificial groundwater recharge. ([[2026-fatima|Fatima, 2026, p. 17]])
- Calcite precipitation and redissolution were identified as the major contributors to clogging in MAR systems. ([[2026-fatima|Fatima, 2026, p. 17]])
- No major change in water chemistry was observed during the experiments, highlighting the need for advanced treatment before recharge. ([[2026-fatima|Fatima, 2026]])

## Limitations

The study notes that because samples from the outlet and inlet ends of the column were not analyzed separately, drastic differences in chemical composition due to microbial activity are not readily apparent from bulk analyses.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Pre-treat recharge water to meet drinking water standards before use; expand research to include emerging contaminants like microplastics, PCPs, and PFAS; extend ERT experiments to 3D to better capture plume geometry.
