# Investigation of Horizontal Well Performance Using Hele-Shaw Model

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the performance of horizontal wells under three different drive mechanisms (bottom-water, gas-cap, and simultaneous bottom-water and gas-cap) using a 2D Hele-Shaw model. It concludes that simultaneous bottom-water and gas-cap drives offer the best scenario for high oil recovery at higher production rates as long as both interfaces remain stable.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the challenge of optimizing horizontal well performance in reservoirs with multiple drive mechanisms (gas and water), specifically focusing on how production rates and well locations affect oil recovery and the stability of fluid interfaces (coning).

## Objectives

- To better understand the horizontal well performance under different drive mechanisms (bottom-water, gas-cap, and simultaneous bottom-water and gas-cap).
- To determine the best production strategy by comparing oil recovery, breakthrough times, water-cuts, pressure drops, and interface movements for various producing conditions.
- Evaluate the impact of different production rates on oil recovery and water-cut for Bottom-Water Drive (BWD).
- Evaluate the impact of different production rates on oil recovery and gas breakthrough for Gas-Cap Drive (GCD).
- Determine the optimal well location for maximum oil recovery in a SWGD scenario.
- Analyze the effects of production rates on interface stability and cone formation.

## Methods

The study utilizes a Hele-Shaw model to simulate multiphase flow. Experimental setups include a 34 cm tall cell with distinct zones for gas, oil, and water. Fluids used are glycerol-water solutions (simulating water) and oil, with specific density and viscosity ratios chosen to represent Saudi Arabian reservoir conditions. Data collection involves monitoring pressure via transducers, measuring fluid volumes in calibrated test tubes, and capturing interface movements using a video camera and still photography. The study compares three scenarios: BWD, GCD, and SWGD, across various production rates (1, 2, 4, and 7.6 cc/min) and different well locations.

## Data and Materials

A 2D Hele-Shaw model with 9 production wells (1/8" diameter), two gas injection ports, and one water injection port. Three types of pressure transducers (Validyne DP303 and Omega DP12). Two Cole-Palmer flow-controllers for rates up to 10 cc/min.

## Key Findings

- For bottom-water drive, more oil is recovered for lower production rates and for wells located farthest from the water/oil interface. ([[1998-khan|Khan, 1998, p. 20]])
- For gas-cap drive, oil recovery is higher for higher production rates as long as no significant coning of gas is produced; wells should be located farthest from the gas/oil interface. ([[1998-khan|Khan, 1998, p. 20]])
- In simultaneous bottom-water and gas-cap drive, oil recovery is still higher for higher production rates if no prominent gas or water cone develops, but the well location is closer to the water/oil interface compared to the gas/oil interface. ([[1998-khan|Khan, 1998, p. 20]])
- Simultaneous bottom-water and gas-cap drive gives the best possible scenario for oil recovery from horizontal wells at a higher production rate in a shorter time as long as the two interfaces remain stable. ([[1998-khan|Khan, 1998, p. 20]])
- The pressure of the gas at the inlet should be approximately 2.5 psig to maintain equilibrium inside the model before the start of production. ([[1998-khan|Khan, 1998, p. 46]])
- In Bottom-Water Drive (BWD), lower production rates result in higher oil recovery at breakthrough; specifically, 87.2% was achieved at 1 cc/min compared to 69.8% at 4.7 cc/min. ([[1998-khan|Khan, 1998, p. 65]])
- For BWD, the well location furthest from the initial oil/water contact (Well #1) yielded the best performance, with a higher recovery and delayed water breakthrough. ([[1998-khan|Khan, 1998, p. 60]])
- In Gas-Cap Drive (GCD), higher production rates resulted in better oil recovery; for example, 87.8% was achieved at 5.3 cc/min compared to 83.5% at 2.6 cc/min. ([[1998-khan|Khan, 1998, p. 79]])
- For GCD, the well located at the bottom of the oil column (Well #8) was found to be the most suitable location as it delays gas breakthrough and allows for a larger sweep area. ([[1998-khan|Khan, 1998, p. 77]])
- In Simultaneous Bottom-Water and Gas-Cap Drive (SWGD), well locations in the middle region of the model (Wells #6, #7, and #8) are preferred to avoid early breakthrough from either gas or water. ([[1998-khan|Khan, 1998, p. 86]])
- For SWGD at a production rate of 1 cc/min, Well #7 showed higher recovery than Well #6 because the lower rate slowed down the approaching interfaces, allowing for more time to recover oil before breakthrough. ([[1998-khan|Khan, 1998, p. 103]])
- At high production rates in BWD, the water front approaches the well rapidly and forms a cone due to higher pressure drops, leading to earlier breakthrough and lower recovery. ([[1998-khan|Khan, 1998, p. 65]])
- In SWGD, for well #6 (the furthest from the water/oil interface), the maximum recovery at breakthrough was 81.7% at a production rate of 7.6 cc/min compared to 63.7% at 1 cc/min. ([[1998-khan|Khan, 1998, p. 100]])
- For SWGD, well #8 (closest to the water/oil interface) had the lowest breakthrough time because of its proximity to the water front. ([[1998-khan|Khan, 1998, p. 108]])
- In SWGD, higher production rates lead to a sharper increase in water-cut immediately following breakthrough compared to lower production rates. ([[1998-khan|Khan, 1998, p. 110]])

## Limitations

The study notes that some results for high production rates were limited by equipment restrictions and low initial gas pressure, preventing the determination of a definitive critical rate for certain scenarios.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Oil recovery can be improved in cases with a gas-cap by using gas re-injection to maintain gas-cap pressure. Further investigation into this option is needed.
