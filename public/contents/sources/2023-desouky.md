# Sustaining Hydraulic Conductivity in Soft/Acid Damaged Carbonate Fractures: Chemical Treatment for Enhanced Performance

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of DAP (diammonium phosphate) treatment on the stiffness and hydraulic conductivity of acid-damaged carbonate rocks (Indiana limestone and Austin chalk). It concludes that DAP treatment effectively restores and enhances rock stiffness, thereby maintaining higher fracture conductivity under stress compared to untreated, acid-etched samples.

---

## Research Problem

Acid etching in carbonate reservoirs creates high initial fracture conductivity but significantly reduces the mechanical stiffness of the rock, leading to rapid closure of fractures under production stresses. This study addresses how to mitigate this damage using a chemical treatment (DAP) to restore stiffness and sustain long-term conductivity.

## Objectives

- Understand the hardening effect by measuring the hardness at different conditions.
- Design an appropriate experimental procedure to quantify the hardening treatment effect.
- Study if the rock surface hardening can withstand high stresses after using hardening chemicals to sustain long-term fracture conductivity.
- Examine the impact of chemical treatment on the ability of the formation to deliver fluid to the fracture and determine whether this effect is statistically significant and practically significant.
- Evaluate the impact of DAP treatment on the elastic stiffness of Indiana limestone and Austin chalk samples.
- Assess the effect of acid etching on surface roughness and rock stiffness.

## Methods

The study utilized two types of carbonate rocks: Indiana limestone and Austin chalk. Surface roughness was measured using a profilometer (confocal microscopy). Rock stiffness (E*) was evaluated non-destructively using the AutoScan system (impulse hammering). Acid etching was performed using 10% HCl for chalk and 15% HCl for limestone in an ACM-3000 system. DAP treatment involved immersing samples in a 1M concentration of diammonium phosphate at 75°C and 1,000 psi for 72 hours. Hydraulic conductivity was measured using the API ACM-3000 system with nitrogen gas at various flow rates (200-350 cc/min) and stress levels (up to 3,200 psi). Microscale analysis was performed via SEM and EDS.

## Data and Materials

The study used a set of Indiana limestone samples (LS1-LS8) and Austin chalk samples (Ch1-Ch8). Samples were prepared as pairs joined with aluminum tape and sealed with silicon resin to simulate fractures. Results included 30 stiffness measurements per sample, multiple flow rate readings for conductivity, and SEM/EDS elemental analysis.

## Key Findings

- 1M DAP solution significantly enhances the stiffness of soft and acid-damaged samples by forming hydroxyapatite (HAP). ([[2023-desouky|Desouky, 2023, p. 16]])
- Hydraulic conductivity in both DAP-stiffened intact and DAP-stiffened acidized fractures showed at least a twofold increase in short-term and long-term performance in Indiana limestone. ([[2023-desouky|Desouky, 2023, p. 16]])
- In Austin Chalk, the treatment resulted in up to an eightfold enhancement of hydraulic conductivity. ([[2023-desouky|Desouky, 2023, p. 16]])
- Temperature was identified as the most critical parameter influencing the DAP reaction with calcite, making it a practical solution for reservoir conditions. ([[2023-desouky|Desouky, 2023, p. 16]])
- Elastic stiffness increased by up to 181% after 72 hours at 1,000 psi and 75°C. ([[2023-desouky|Desouky, 2023, p. 23]])
- The pressure effect on stiffness was minor compared to the temperature effect (p < 0.001). ([[2023-desouky|Desouky, 2023, p. 34]])
- At 72 hours, the increase in average elastic stiffness for samples treated with LNH as a catalyst showed only a small but statistically significant difference compared to those without. ([[2023-desouky|Desouky, 2023, p. 38]])
- XRF analysis showed HAP concentration was highest at the surface and decreased toward the interior, reaching a constant value beyond 5 mm. ([[2023-desouky|Desouky, 2023, p. 40]])
- XRD results confirmed the formation of hydroxyapatite (HAP) with peaks corresponding to JCPDS file no. 9-432. ([[2023-desouky|Desouky, 2023, p. 49]])
- SEM analysis revealed various HAP morphologies, including needle-like, rod-like, plate-like, and rose-like crystals. ([[2023-desouky|Desouky, 2023, p. 41]])
- DAP aging increased the elastic modulus (E*) of chalk specimens up to 330% of the original value. ([[2023-desouky|Desouky, 2023, p. 53]])
- DAP treatment significantly increased the average E* of Austin chalk samples: 117% for sample 3T (from 3.42 to 7.41 GPa) and 177% for sample 4T (from 3.42 to 9.53 GPa). ([[2023-desouky|Desouky, 2023, p. 69]])
- In even weaker chalk samples, DAP treatment resulted in higher percentage improvements: 217% for sample 5T and 331% for sample 6T. ([[2023-desouky|Desouky, 2023, p. 69]])
- Acid etching significantly increased surface roughness (Sa) of Indiana limestone from ~0.03 mm to values as high as 0.71 mm, creating features like wormholes. ([[2023-desouky|Desouky, 2023, p. 98]])
- The final hydraulic conductivity of treated samples in Experiment 1 was approximately 100 mD.ft, which is one order of magnitude higher than the untreated samples. ([[2023-desouky|Desouky, 2023, p. 73]])

## Limitations

The study notes that while results are promising, it is not recommended to randomly allocate samples to experiments to establish cause-and-effect due to potential confounding factors like initial stiffness (E*).

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study suggests that DAP is a reliable agent for consolidating weak carbonates and provides a basis for optimizing hydraulic fracturing operations by adjusting temperature, pressure, and duration to maximize HAP formation.
