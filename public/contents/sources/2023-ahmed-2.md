# MINIMIZING THE SEGREGATION OF HEAVY-WEIGHTED OIL-WELL CEMENT

**Summary**: The thesis investigates methods to minimize the segregation of heavy-weighted oil-well cement by evaluating various weighting materials (barite, hematite, ilmenite, Micromax) and new additives (laponite, perlite, vermiculite). It evaluates these materials based on their impact on slurry rheology, gas migration, fluid loss, and petrophysical properties under high-pressure and high-temperature (HPHT) conditions.

---

## Research Problem

The settling of dense weighting agents (hematite, barite, ilmenite) in heavy-weight cement slurries leads to significant density variations (segregation), which can compromise the integrity of wellbores. The study seeks to identify optimal concentrations of perlite and vermiculice to stabilize these systems.

## Objectives

- Examine the influence of four different weighting materials (barite, hematite, ilmenite, and Micromax) on cement segregation and properties under HPHT conditions.
- Utilize nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) as a novel technique to evaluate the segregation problem in oil-well cement alongside CT scan and traditional API methods.
- Minimize the segregation issue in each weighting material using three new materials (laponite, perlite, and vermiculite) and study their effects on cement properties.
- Evaluate the physical and chemical properties of various weighting materials (barite, hematite, ilmenite, Micromax) and new additives (laponite, perlite, vermiculite).
- Evaluate the impact of different weighting materials (barite, hematite, ilmenite, Micromax) on cement slurry properties including rheology, fluid loss, and gas migration.
- Determine the effect of different additives on the petrophysical properties (porosity, permeability) and mechanical properties (compressive strength, tensile strength) of the set cement.

## Methods

The study involved characterizing materials via XRF, SEM, and particle size analysis. Slurries were prepared following API procedures with varying concentrations of weighting agents to reach a target density of 18 lb/gal. Rheological properties (yield point, plastic viscosity) were measured using a viscometer. Fluid loss was tested using an HPHT filter press. Gas migration was evaluated in a specialized apparatus under high pressure and temperature. After curing in an HPHT chamber for 24 hours at 300°F and 3000 psi, the cement samples' mechanical properties were measured using a crushing machine, petrophysical properties (porosity/permeability) were measured via helium porosimetry and gas permeametry, and elastic properties were derived from ultrasonic velocities. Segregation was analyzed through direct density measurement of three sections, CT scanning, and NMR.

## Data and Materials

The study involves multiple cement formulations: hematite-weighted (with perlite concentrations up to 3%), barite-weighted (with vermiculite concentrations up to 1.5%), and ilmenite-weighted (with vermiculite concentrations up to 4%). Samples were tested as cylindrical columns for density, rheology, and mechanical properties.

## Key Findings

- Traditional density measurements showed critical segregation in barite (24.6%), ilmenite (17.5%), and hematite (20.3%) samples, while Micromax had only 1.4% variation. ([[2023-ahmed|AHMED, 2023, p. 254]])
- Micromax as a weighting material yielded a homogeneous cement sample with only a 1.4% density variation (DV). ([[2023-ahmed|AHMED, 2023, p. 28]])
- Perlite reduced cement segregation to very low-density variations of 1.5%, 0.1%, and 0.9% in the barite, ilmenite, and hematite systems, respectively. ([[2023-ahmed|AHMED, 2023, p. 28]])
- NMR results showed no deviation window between top, middle, and bottom sections for Micromax, laponite, perlite, and vermiculite-based cements. ([[2023-ahmed|AHMED, 2023, p. 28]])
- NMR revealed a large deviation window in the porosity distribution of barite, ilmenite, and hematite-based cement samples. ([[2023-ahmed|AHMED, 2023, p. 30]])
- Laponite was found to improve the uniformity of the cement sheath, reduce density variation, enhance gel strength, and lower porosity and permeability. ([[2023-ahmed|AHMED, 2023, p. 81]])
- Micromax has a very high fineness that allows it to minimize the slurry's thickening time and its spherical shape results in low plastic viscosity. ([[2023-ahmed|AHMED, 2023, p. 66]])
- Adding barite nanoparticles (up to 5% by weight) can reduce fluid loss by 50% compared to standard barite. ([[2023-ahmed|AHMED, 2023, p. 77]])

- Micromax was found to have the smallest particle size (D50 = 2.5 µm) and a spherical morphology, resulting in the lowest density variation (1.4%) and highest homogeneity among all tested materials. ([[2023-ahmed|AHMED, 2023, p. 129]])
- Barite-weighted cement exhibited the highest segregation with a density variation of 24.6% and significant differences in porosity between top (37.9%), middle (25.1%), and bottom (17.8%) sections. ([[2023-ahmed|AHMED, 2023, p. 154]])
- Ilmenite-based systems showed a lower fluid loss of 66 mL/30 min, which was 17.9% less than the hematite system. ([[2023-ahmed|AHMED, 2023, p. 135]])
- The barite-weighted cement had the highest yield point (84.3 lbf/100 ft2) and the highest gel strengths (10-sec: 11.5 lbf/100 ft2; 10-min: 39.5 lbf/100 ft2). ([[2023-ahmed|AHMED, 2023, p. 133]])
- Micromax-weighted cement exhibited the lowest Young's modulus (15.8 GPa) and highest Poisson's ratio (0.263), indicating superior flexibility compared to barite and hematite systems. ([[2023-ahmed|AHMED, 2023, p. 143]])
- Laponite at a concentration of 0.3% BWOC reduced the density variation in ilmenite-weighted cement to its minimum value of 1%. ([[2023-ahmed|AHMED, 2023, p. 162]])

## Limitations

The study noted that none of the three weighting material systems could fully prevent gas migration because the hydrostatic pressure of the cement was lower than the gas pressure after initial setting time.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Reduce weighting material particle size; use combinations of Micromax with other materials; evaluate magnetite; test combined solutions (laponite, perlite, vermiculicate); conduct SEM analysis for microstructural understanding; utilize AI and diffusion simulation models.
