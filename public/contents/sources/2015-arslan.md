# BENTHIC FORAMINIFERA IN THE ARABIAN GULF: EFFECTS OF SEASONAL DYNAMICS, ENVIRONMENTAL PARAMETERS, AND MARINE POLLUTION ON THEIR DISTRIBUTION AND BEHAVIORS.

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the distribution and behavior of benthic foraminifera in the Arabian Gulf, focusing on three main areas: their biological characteristics, the impact of seasonal dynamics, and their response to environmental pollution. The study utilizes laboratory observations, chemical analysis of sediments (TOC, heavy metals), and population metrics to assess how these organisms respond to local environmental stressors.

---

## Research Problem

The impact of marine pollution (nutrient enrichment, high organic carbon, and heavy metal contamination) and seasonal dynamics on benthic foraminiferal communities in the Arabian Gulf is not fully understood, particularly regarding their role as bioindicators in these specific coastal environments.

## Methods

The study utilized a seasonal sampling strategy across five stations at distances of 17, 50, 125, 200, and 250 m from the shore. Foraminiferal analysis included wet-picking under a binocular microscope to determine density (FD), diversity indices (Shannon, Fisher alpha), and abundance ratios. Environmental parameters were measured using YSI multi-probe for in-situ data and PC-BODTM systems for laboratory measurements of conductivity and alkalinity. Grain size was determined via manual sieving after H2O2 treatment. TOC was analyzed using a Shimadzu TOC-Vcsh analyzer. Eutrophication indicators (SO4, PO4, NO3, NO2) were measured via Ion Chromatography (IC). Heavy metals (63 elements) were quantified using ICP-MS. Hydrocarbons were extracted using ASE 200 and analyzed via GC/FID.

## Data and Materials

The study analyzed data from multiple sampling stations across two transects. Parameters measured included water depth (cm), salinity (PSU), temperature (°C), pH, turbidity, and various sediment characteristics (Silt/Clay %, Fine/Medium/Coarse Sand %). Chemical analyses included Nitrates, Sulphates, Total Organic Carbon (TOC), Total Hydrocarbon Content (THC), and heavy metals (Cr, Ni, Cu, Zn, As, Ag, Cd, Au, Hg). Biological data included counts and percentages of specific taxa (Ammonia, Glabratellina, Miliolids, Elphidium, Peneroplidae, Brizalina) and life stages (Adults vs. Juveniles).

## Key Findings

- All genotypic groups for Ammonia were monophyletic and supported by high bootstrap values (97%-100%) in 18S rDNA phylogenetic analysis. ([[2015-arslan|Arslan, 2015, p. 60]])
- Genotypic groups for Murrayinella and Glabratellina were also found to be monophyletic with high bootstrap support (97%-100%). ([[2015-arslan|Arslan, 2015, p. 61]])
- The study identified specific behaviors such as locomotion, symbiont-bearing status, and survival responses in laboratory conditions. ([[2015-arslan|Arslan, 2015, p. 33]])
- Seasonal variations were observed in the relative abundance of six benthic foraminiferal groups in Eastern Bahrain. ([[2015-arslan|Arslan, 2015, p. 90]])
- The study identified specific locations along the Saudi coastline (Sofitel Hotel, Zabnah Lagoon) to assess pollution impacts. ([[2015-arslan|Arslan, 2015, p. 124]])
- The first component of the PCA ordination diagram can be interpreted as the depth transect (foreshore-offshore gradient). ([[2015-arslan|Arslan, 2015, p. 30]])
- The second component in the PCA ordination diagram might be related to seasonality. ([[2015-arslan|Arslan, 2015, p. 30]])
- Benthic foraminifera are among the major carbonate producers in modern Arabian Gulf waters and inhabit all marine habitats. ([[2015-arslan|Arslan, 2015, p. 18]])
- The study identifies a lack of proper cataloging of foraminifera specifically along the Saudi coastline, which is considered 'terra incognita' regarding its specific fauna. ([[2015-arslan|Arslan, 2015, p. 34]])
- Rotaliids showed faster pseudopodia extension (8 hours) and higher movement rates compared to miliolids (24+ hours). ([[2015-arslan|Arslan, 2015, p. 45]])
- The high rate of movement in rotaliids is attributed to the extension of pseudopodia through all apertures, whereas miliolids only extend them from the primary aperture. ([[2015-arslan|Arslan, 2015, p. 45]])
- Molecular analysis revealed four groups (Ammonia, Murrayinella, Glabratellina, Elphidium) with monophyletic clustering; BLAST analysis showed none were previously identified at the species level. ([[2015-arslan|Arslan, 2015, p. 46]])
- Foraminifera can survive in a wide range of temperatures (1°C to 50°C), but specific species are adapted to narrower ranges. ([[2015-arslan|Arslan, 2015, p. 37]])
- Oxygen depletion is a stress factor causing significant decrease in standing crops and diversity, potentially leading to dwarfism. ([[2015-arslan|Arslan, 2015, p. 38]])
- A pH below 7.5 can hamper the ability of living foraminifera to secrete calcite, affecting calcification rates. ([[2015-arslan|Arslan, 2015, p. 39]])

## Limitations

Only 13 out of 26 samples successfully underwent amplification for molecular analysis; no amplifications were obtained for miliolids and Peneroplis species.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study aims to provide a baseline for future environmental monitoring and impact studies as coastal habitats are threatened by infrastructure development.
