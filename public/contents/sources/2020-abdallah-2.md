# Managing depth to basement uncertainty by integrating magnetic and seismic data

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the integration of magnetic data with seismic data to reduce uncertainty in determining basement depth, particularly beneath complex structures like salt bodies. It proposes a workflow using magnetic methods (Werner and Spectral analysis) as a quality control for building seismic velocity models.

---

## Research Problem

Accurate basement imaging is difficult due to low signal-to-noise ratios, ray-path distortions, and the lack of well control in complex environments like subsalt structures. Standard velocity models often fail to accurately represent these areas, leading to uncertainties in determining sedimentary thickness and source rock maturity.

## Objectives

- Investigate the integration of magnetic data with seismic data for quality control in building seismic velocity models.
- Provide a qualitative means of improving accuracy by using magnetic data as correlating information.
- Quantitatively guide velocity model building by minimizing uncertainty associated with depth-to-basement determination.
- Generate a practical workflow for producing an accurate earth velocity model that reflects geophysical properties and geological settings.
- Integrate seismic and magnetic methods to constrain the process of building a velocity model using multiple depth-to-basement estimations from magnetic data.
- Overcome seismic imaging challenges in areas with poor illumination or lack of well control, such as subsalt structures.
- Integrate magnetic data with seismic data to constrain the vertical component of the velocity model.
- Develop a workflow to manage uncertainty in depth-to-basement estimations using 'pseudo wells' derived from magnetic methods.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of Werner and Spectral analysis for determining basement depth in sub-salt environments.
- Develop a methodology to integrate magnetic data with seismic data to constrain velocity models.
- Establish an uncertainty range for basement depth using magnetic methods (Werner and Spectral analysis).
- Validate the iterative workflow by comparing seismic results against both original models and magnetic-derived constraints.
- Evaluate the integration of magnetic and seismic data to reduce depth-to-basement uncertainty
- Compare different methods for determining basement depth from magnetic anomalies
- Assess the impact of integrating non-seismic data (gravity/magnetics) on seismic velocity model building

## Methods

The study utilizes a multi-step workflow: 1) Initial velocity model building based on compaction and TWT interpretations. 2) Determination of 'top salt' and 'base-of-salt' horizons using PSDM. 3) Calculation of depth to basement from magnetic data using Werner and Spectral analysis methods to create an uncertainty range (6300m - 6600m). 4) Iterative testing of various salt flood velocities (ranging from 2925 to 4925 m/s in 200 m/s increments) to find the best fit that produces flat gathers and falls within the magnetic uncertainty range.

## Data and Materials

The study uses a synthetic dataset: the HESS isotropic and VTI models. The seismic data includes 720 shots with a maximum offset of 7985 m and a sampling rate of 6 ms. Magnetic data was generated via forward modeling (Blakely, 1995) using a two-layer model (sediment: 0 susceptibility; basement: 0.005 SI).

## Key Findings

- The integration of magnetic data provides a qualitative means of improving velocity model accuracy by providing depth-to-basement estimates that function as correlating information. ([[2020-abdallah|Abdallah, 2020, p. 15]])
- The basement depth is expected to fall within the range obtained using the magnetic method within a 5% error range at a given depth. ([[2020-abdallah|Abdallah, 2020, p. 16]])
- Standard sedimentary velocity ranges are typically assumed between 1800-4300 m/s, while salt is often assigned a constant velocity of 4500 m/s in the Red Sea area. ([[2020-abdallah|Abdallah, 2020, p. 14]])
- The Werner method and Spectral analysis are identified as recognized methods for estimating basement depth from magnetic data. ([[2020-abdallah|Abdallah, 2020, p. 18]])
- The acoustic contrast in a basement image can be reduced to as little as 5%–15% of the actual acoustic contrast due to poor illumination and complex ray paths. ([[2020-abdallah|Abdallah, 2020, p. 24]])
- Magnetic methods are considered one of the best non-seismic techniques for defining the basement interface because the magnetic susceptibility difference between basement and sedimentary rocks is often a strong contrast. ([[2020-abdallah|Abdallah, 2020, p. 22]])
- Pre-stack depth migration (PSDM) is preferred over pre-stack time migration (PSTM) in complex environments as it accounts for ray bending, velocity contrasts, and steep dips. ([[2020-abdallah|Abdallah, 2020, p. 33]])
- Evaporite bodies are not always pure halite; variations in anhydrite or gypsum content can significantly alter seismic velocities (e.g., anhydrite at 6500 m/s vs. halite at 4500 m/s). ([[2020-abdallah|Abdallah, 2020, p. 39]])
- In 'soft-rock' environments, sedimentary velocities are primarily driven by compaction rather than lithology and can be modeled using a gradient approach (V = V0 + kZ). ([[2020-abdallah|Abdallah, 2020, p. 40]])
- The integration of magnetic data as 'pseudo wells' provides a vertical control that helps resolve the ambiguity between sedimentary structures and basement features. ([[2020-abdallah|Abdallah, 2020, p. 43]])
- Magnetic methods typically provide a depth-to-basement uncertainty range of approximately 5-10%. ([[2020-abdallah|Abdallah, 2020, p. 44]])
- The 'Sedimentary Flood' and 'Salt Flood' techniques allow for the iterative determination of correct salt velocities by monitoring gather flatness. ([[2020-abdallah|Abdallah, 2020, p. 42]])
- A depth-to-basement estimation from magnetic data is considered a valid uncertainty range to calibrate seismic images when well controls are absent. ([[2020-abdallah|Abdallah, 2020, p. 44]])
- The study identifies that the accuracy of magnetic methods for basement depth decreases as depth exceeds 6 km due to increased complexity and reduced signal clarity. ([[2020-abdallah|Abdallah, 2020, p. 45]])
- Spectral analysis yielded a depth to basement of approximately 6600 m, while the Werner method yielded 6300 m, establishing an uncertainty range of 6300-6600 m. ([[2020-abdallah|Abdallah, 2020, p. 70]])

## Limitations

Magnetic methods are less accurate at depths exceeding 6 km. The study notes that gather flatness only validates the horizontal component of velocity; vertical components require additional controls (like magnetic data) especially in sub-salt areas with poor signal-to-noise ratios.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The methodology can be applied to real 2D and 3D seismic data once validated on synthetic models. The integration of non-magnetic methods is often only qualitative, but this study proposes a quantitative approach using magnetic data for industrial application.
