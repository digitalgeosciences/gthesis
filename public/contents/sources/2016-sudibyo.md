# Mapping Environmental Sensitivity in the Western Arabian Gulf using Geographic Information System (GIS) for Oil Spill Response

**Summary**: The thesis develops an Environmental Sensitivity Index (ESI) map for the Western Arabian Gulf to assist in oil spill response planning. It integrates three key parameters—shoreline type, biological resources, and socio-economic features—into a GIS-based framework to identify high-priority protection zones.

---

## Research Problem

Oil spills pose significant risks to marine and terrestrial ecosystems (mangroves, seagrasses, coral reefs) and economic infrastructure in the Western Arabian Gulf. There is a need for an integrated mapping tool to prioritize response efforts based on environmental and socio-economic sensitivity.

## Objectives

- Identify and assess the environmentally sensitive areas, including shoreline type, biological resources and socio-economic features using GIS method.
- Define the index of environmental sensitivity from different parameters that are suitable for the Gulf, based on an Environmental Sensitivity Index (ESI) ranking method and analysis.
- Prepare thematic maps of priority sensitive areas for oil spill response planning in the Gulf.
- Map shoreline types across the study area to determine cleanup difficulty.
- Identify and map biological resources (mangroves, salt marshes, seagrasses, corals) impacted by oil spills.
- Identify and map socio-economic features requiring protection during an oil spill.

## Methods

The study utilized a multi-criteria approach to develop an ESI map. Data collection included field work, literature review, and biotope mapping from KFUPM records. Remote sensing data (Landsat 7 ETM+ and Landsat 8) were processed using ENVI 5.0 for image classification (unsupervised and supervised). GIS analysis in ArcGIS 10.2 involved: 1) Shoreline type classification based on NOAA/IPICEA standards; 2) Biological resource mapping (mangroves, seagrass, coral reefs); 3) Socio-economic feature identification via Google Maps and local data; 4) Kernel Density Analysis to quantify parameters; 5) Map Algebra and Overlay Analysis to create a cumulative sensitivity index. The study area was divided into 20 grids of approximately 25x25 km for analysis.

## Data and Materials

The study covers the Western Arabian Gulf from Khafji to Dammam, including specific sites like Safaniyah, Tanajib, Manifa, Ras al-Khair, Abu Ali Island, and Tarut Island. Data included shoreline types (quantified as lengths in km), biological resource locations, and socio-economic infrastructure points.

## Key Findings

- The study identified specific high-priority areas requiring immediate protection: Safaniyah, Khursaniyah-Jubail, Tarut Bay, and offshore islands including Karan, Kurayn, Jana, and Jurayd. ([[2016-sudibyo|Sudibyo, 2016, p. 14]])
- Shoreline types were classified into five categories (solid man-made structure, rocky-shore, sand/beach, muddy-shore, vegetated) with scores from 1 to 5. ([[2016-sudibyo|Sudibyo, 2016, p. 50]])
- Biological resources included seagrass beds (Halodule uninervis, Halophila stipulacea, H. ovalis), coral reefs, and mangroves as key sensitive habitats. ([[2016-sudibyo|Sudibyo, 2016, p. 28]])
- Socio-economic features were categorized into four types: resource extraction sites, management/protected areas, archaeological/historical locations, and high-use recreational/shoreline access areas. ([[2016-sudibyo|Sudibyo, 2016, p. 31]])
- The study area was divided into 20 grids of approximately 25x15 km to facilitate analysis at an appropriate scale for map visualization. ([[2016-sudibyo|Sudibyo, 2016, p. 40]])
- Sandy-shore was the dominant shoreline type across the study area, accounting for approximately 49% of the total length. ([[2016-sudibyo|Sudibyo, 2016, p. 58]])
- Vegetated shorelines (salt marshes and mangroves) were identified as high sensitivity areas due to cleanup difficulties; these occurred in Manifa, Khursaniyah, and Tarut Bay. ([[2016-sudibyo|Sudibyo, 2016, p. 78]])
- Socio-economic features of highest sensitivity included desalination plants, fish landing sites, and oil/gas facilities. ([[2016-sudibyo|Sudibyo, 2016, p. 80]])

## Limitations

The study area is large (Khafji to Dammam), providing only general information; specific areas like Safaniyah and Tarut Bay require more detailed analysis. The ESI map is not a full risk assessment but a tool for response planning.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Conduct more detailed studies on high-sensitivity zones: Safaniyah, Khursaniyah-Jubail, Tarut Bay, and offshore islands.
- Improve remote sensing analysis using band ratios with water correction to better identify submerged biological resources.
- Develop mobile applications integrating ESI maps with real-time oil spill modeling for field use.
- Use ecological modeling to assess the specific impact of oil on mangroves, salt-marshes, coral reefs, and seagrasses.
