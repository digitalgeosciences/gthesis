# Geology of the Early Paleogene Phosphorite Deposits of Northwestern Saudi Arabia

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the mineralogical, geochemical, and petrological characteristics of Early Paleogene phosphorite deposits in the Sirhan-Turayf basin of northwestern Saudi Arabia. It utilizes a combination of field studies, thin-section petrography, SEM-EDS, PIXE, and natural gamma-ray spectroscopy to characterize the ore and propose a practical classification for its various grades.

---

## Research Problem

While stratigraphy and micropaleontology of the Early Paleogene phosphorite succession in northwestern Saudi Arabia were known since 1965, there was a lack of detailed data regarding mineralogical composition, trace elements, and geochemical characteristics to provide a practical means for classifying ore grades.

## Methods

The study involved field sampling of 100 phosphorite samples from surface outcrops (Turayf and Thaniyat Turayf areas) and subsurface sections (wells P.1 and P.11). Petrographical analysis was conducted on 50 thin sections prepared using a Mutta Cutta Rock cutting Machine and polished to ~60 microns. Microchemical analysis and microstructure imaging were performed using SEM-EDS (JSM-5800LV system) and PIXE spectroscopy. Geochemical characterization included natural gamma-ray spectroscopy (NGS) to determine U, V, Th, K, and REE content.

## Data and Materials

Samples from multiple locations including Hazm al-Jalamid (Zone 1 & 2), Thaniyat Turayf (Zone 1, Zone 2, and Zone 5), Al-Amud (Arqah Phosphorite Member), and Umm Wu‘aal North. Data includes mineralogical counts for apatite, calcite, and dolomite; chemical concentrations of P2O5, CaO, SiO2, and trace elements like V, Cr, Sr, F, and Y.

## Key Findings

- The ore is mainly composed of microcrystalline apatite (average 46.35%), dolomite (average 36.69%), and calcite (averaging 13.14%). ([[1996-matter|Matter, 1996, p. 26]])
- Trace elements include uranium (up to 300 ppm) and vanadium (up to 3000 ppm). ([[1996-matter|Matter, 1996, p. 26]])
- A practical classification was established using cut points at 18%, 8%, and 1% for P2O5, separating phosphorites from phosphoritic and slightly phosphoritic rocks. ([[1996-matter|Matter, 1996, p. 26]])
- The mineralogical composition of the Hazm al-Jalamid deposit shows apatite as a primary component in both Zone 1 and Zone 2. ([[1996-matter|Matter, 1996, p. 17]])
- Relationship between P2O5% and SiO2% was analyzed for different zones to determine purity and mineralogical associations. ([[1996-matter|Matter, 1996, p. 19]])
- The study identified a correlation between uranium content and other elements like thorium and potassium in the phosphorite deposits. ([[1996-matter|Matter, 1996, p. 20]])
- The region contains a significant resource of high-quality phosphorite, with an estimated total quantity of 140,000 million tons at 15.9% P2O5. ([[1996-matter|Matter, 1996, p. 28]])
- Identified resources totaling 7,800 million tons at 19.7% P2O5 include a demonstrated resource of 1,200 million tons at 20.3% P2O5. ([[1996-matter|Matter, 1996, p. 28]])
- Soil geochemistry using anomalous phosphorite proved very effective in identifying locations with significant near-surface phosphorite accumulations. ([[1996-matter|Matter, 1996, p. 44]])
- Twelve microfacies types were recognized: calcareous phosphorite, siliceous phosphorite, shale, sandstone, chert, dolomite, silicified limestone, coquina, sparite, microsparite, biomicrosparite, and micaceous sparite. ([[1996-matter|Matter, 1996, p. 46]])
- The Arqah Phosphorite Member (Umm Wu'aal area) is of Middle Eocene age. ([[1996-matter|Matter, 1996, p. 46]])
- The Thaniyat Phosphorite Member (West Thaniyat) was initially dated as Middle Eocene but was later identified as having a more complex age range including Paleocene and Late Cretaceous. ([[1996-matter|Matter, 1996, p. 46]])
- The Sirhan-Turayf region represents the eastern limit of an extensive Late Cretaceous-Early Paleogene phosphorite belt extending from Morocco to Iraq along the southern edge of the Tethys Sea. ([[1996-matter|Matter, 1996, p. 46]])
- The main phosphorite mineral is apatite. ([[1996-matter|Matter, 1996, p. 43]])
- Phosphorite deposits in the region are broadly similar in nature, with phosphate content ranging from 8% to 33%. ([[1996-matter|Matter, 1996, p. 58]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study suggests that a detailed examination of petrographical, mineralogical, and chemical characteristics is necessary for economic assessment; it also highlights the need for 3D facies models to improve geological understanding.
