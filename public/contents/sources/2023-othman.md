# Optimizing Non-Freshwater Fracturing Fluid Rheology Utilizing Chelating Agents

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the compatibility and rheological performance of non-freshwater fracturing fluids, specifically focusing on the impact of various chelating agents (EDTA, DTPA, GLDA) and crosslinkers (zirconium, zirconium borate) in high-salinity environments. It evaluates these systems through scale inhibition tests, ion capture analysis, and core flooding experiments to optimize fluid design for offshore applications.

---

## Research Problem

The incompatibility between certain crosslinked fluids (specifically those using zirconium and zirconium borate) and specific chelating agents at high salinity and high temperatures leads to poor performance or sudden viscosity loss. There is a need to identify stable, effective fracturing fluid formulations that can withstand these conditions while providing adequate rheology.

## Objectives

- Formulate a cheap, effective fracturing fluid with a minimum number of additives.
- Utilize chelating agents (specifically GLDA) to capture unwanted ions and provide a thickening effect in saline water.
- Evaluate the compatibility of additives using analytical techniques like NMR and FTIR.
- Measure proppant settling velocity and evaluate fluid performance on core flooding systems.
- Optimize the rheology of seawater (SW) fracturing fluids using a chelating agent (GLDA) and CMHPG polymer.
- Evaluate the impact of different concentrations of specific ions (Ca2+, Mg2+, Na+, SO4-) on the viscosity and stability of the CMHPG polymer.

## Methods

The study utilized an Anton Paar-MCR 302 rheometer to measure the viscosity of CMHPG polymer in deionized water (DW), seawater (SW), and various salt solutions. Experiments were conducted at a constant shear rate of 511 s^-1. The research included: 1) Baseline tests comparing DW vs. SW with 0.5 wt% and 1 wt% CMHPG; 2) Concentration studies where GLDA was added in concentrations of 1, 3, 5, and 10 vol% at various pH levels (4-13.7); 3) HPHT tests at 25°C, 75°C, and 100°C under 3.45 MPa pressure; 4) Ion-specific studies where specific salts (CaCl2, MgCl2, NaCl, Na2SO4) were tested to isolate the impact of individual ions on polymer hydration.

## Data and Materials

The study used a variety of fluids including deionized water (DW), seawater (SW) with a TDS of 67.707 g/L, and specific salt solutions (CaCl2, MgCl2, NaCl, Na2SO4). Two types of GLDA were tested: low pH (4) and high pH (13.7). CMHPG polymer was used at concentrations of 0.5 wt% and 1 wt%. Over 40 unique experiments were conducted across different temperature, pressure, and concentration ranges.

## Key Findings

- Seawater contains both divalent (Ca2+, Mg2+) and monovalent (Na+, K+) ions; divalent ions were identified as the primary cause of precipitation and viscosity reduction in fracturing fluids. ([[2023-othman|Othman, 2023, p. 24]])
- Chelating agents like GLDA can act as a replacement for crosslinkers, breakers, biocide, clay stabilizers, and HCl while maintaining stability in high-temperature formations up to 300°F. ([[2023-othman|Othman, 2023, p. 31]])
- A concentration of 5.2 g/l of MgCl2 and 1.79 g/l of CaCl2 were identified as thresholds where salt content significantly impacts hydration and leads to scale formation. ([[2023-othman|Othman, 2023, p. 45]])
- The study successfully developed an optimal fracturing fluid system compatible with seawater that overcomes issues related to high salinity, scale, and component compatibility. ([[2023-othman|Othman, 2023, p. 19]])
- Zirconium crosslinkers provide irreversible crosslinking in CMHPG, resulting in minimal proppant settling at low shear rates compared to borate-based systems. ([[2023-othman|Othman, 2023, p. 48]])
- The addition of GLDA to magnesium and calcium chloride solutions increased the viscosity, confirming its role in sequestering divalent ions. ([[2023-othman|Othman, 2023, p. 84]])
- Sulfate ions (SO4-2) were found to control the rheology of seawater due to their similar response to GLDA addition compared to other ions. ([[2023-othman|Othman, 2023, p. 84]])
- The viscosity of SW-based fluids with high pH GLDA (>10) showed a significant increase after several hours of shearing, indicating a delayed hydration behavior. ([[2023-othman|Othman, 2023, p. 78]])
- page_number_reference_note_for_finding_2_is_actually_on_page_84_in_the_source_text_but_it_refers_to_the_summary_of_chapter_4 ([[2023-othman|Othman, 2023]])
- The viscosity of the CMHPG polymer in SW was slightly higher than in DW, but the water type did not significantly impact magnitude within 8 hours of shearing. ([[2023-othman|Othman, 2023, p. 78]])
- Higher GLDA concentrations (e.g., 10 vol%) and higher pH levels resulted in longer durations for the polymer to reach peak viscosity, which can be used to manage pumping power requirements. ([[2023-othman|Othman, 2023, p. 79]])
- The thickening effect of the GLDA-CMHPG system was significantly reduced at temperatures reaching 100°C. ([[2023-othman|Othman, 2023, p. 82]])
- GLDA successfully acted as a substitute for multiple additives, including crosslinkers, breakers, and scale inhibitors, while remaining biodegradable. ([[2023-othman|Othman, 2023, p. 69]])
- Sodium sulfate (Na2SO4) was identified as the primary source of viscosity reduction in seawater systems due to its ability to interfere with polymer-water hydrogen bonding. ([[2023-othman|Othman, 2023, p. 105]])
- The addition of 4% GLDA successfully restored the viscosity of calcium and magnesium solutions to levels comparable with DI water. ([[2023-othman|Othman, 2023, p. 99]])

## Limitations

The study noted that the effectiveness of certain scale inhibitors can be reduced in high-pressure, high-temperature (HPHT) environments due to molecular instability. Additionally, some studies mentioned a lack of extensive investigation into the specific impact of individual ions on polymer hydration.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Broaden the scope to include more seawater formulations with varying salinities and produced water containing organic compounds. Conduct a more in-depth study on alternative chelating agents like DTPA and EDTA, and explore the use of high-pH GLDA as a breaker for high salinity water when zirconium is used as a crosslinker.
