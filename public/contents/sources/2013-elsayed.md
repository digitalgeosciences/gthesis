# A NEW EOR METHOD FOR SANDSTONE RESERVOIRS USING HIGH PH CHELATING AGENTS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the use of high pH chelating agents (such as EDTA, HEDTA, and GLDA) as an enhanced oil recovery (EOR) method for sandstone reservoirs. It evaluates their ability to chelate multivalent cations (Ca, Mg, Fe) from brine solutions to prevent precipitation and improve oil recovery compared to standard sea water flooding.

---

## Research Problem

The challenge of using low salinity water for EOR in sandstone reservoirs is the high cost of treating seawater to remove sulfate and other minerals that cause scaling/precipitation. Chelating agents are proposed as a chemical alternative to manage these ions while maintaining high pH levels to improve oil recovery.

## Objectives

- Investigating the effect of adding chelating agents to sea water on the oil recovery
- Defining the best conditions for obtaining maximum oil recovery by testing different concentrations and pH values.
- Investigate the potential of using chelating agents as EOR fluids in sandstone reservoirs.
- Evaluate the effect of concentration, pH, and type of chelating agent on oil recovery.
- Measure the impact of chelating agents on the zeta potential of Berea sandstone to assess changes in surface charge and wettability.
- Investigate the effect of different chelating agents on oil recovery in Berea sandstone cores.

## Methods

The study utilized core flooding tests on Berea sandstone cores. Experiments were conducted using various chelating agents (Na4EDTA, NH4EDTA, HEDTA) at different concentrations and high pH levels (up to 12.2). Sea water was used as the base fluid in both secondary and tertiary stages. Zeta potential measurements were performed on crushed Berea sandstone to analyze changes in rock surface charge. Effluent ion analysis was conducted to determine the concentration of chelated cations (Ca, Mg, Fe) in the produced fluids.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized Berea sandstone cores for core flooding experiments. Experimental parameters included various chelating agent concentrations, multiple pH levels (specifically highlighting pH 12.2), and sea water as a base fluid. Analytical data included zeta potential measurements and ion concentration analysis of the effluent.

## Key Findings

- Chelating agents were able to provide additional oil recovery up to 30% from the OOIP after sea water flooding. ([[2013-elsayed|Elsayed, 2013, p. 14]])
- EDTA at pH 12.2 showed the best results for enhanced oil recovery. ([[2013-elsayed|Elsayed, 2013, p. 14]])
- High concentration EDTA was able to chelate calcium, magnesium, and iron from the rock. ([[2013-elsayed|Elsayed, 2013, p. 14]])
- Zeta potential measurements showed that chelating agents changed the rock charge to a higher negative value than deionized water. ([[2013-elsayed|Elsayed, 2013, p. 15]])
- Iron ions did not affect the zeta potential value when chelating agent was added to the solution, despite affecting it in low salinity water. ([[2013-elsayed|Elsayed, 2013, p. 15]])
- EDTA is stable at high temperatures (up to 200°C) and has a high ability to chelate multivalent cations from brine solutions. ([[2013-elsayed|Elsayed, 2013, p. 14]])
- GLDA showed higher performance than HEDTA in chelating calcium, magnesium, and iron for Berea sandstone cores at high pH values. ([[2013-elsayed|Elsayed, 2013, p. 38]])
- The permeability ratio (k_final/k_initial) for EDTA was 1.20, for HEDTA was 1.14, and for GLDA was 1.16 at pH 11. ([[2013-elsayed|Elsayed, 2013, p. 39]])
- Iron (III) solubility significantly decreases as pH increases; above pH 6, there is almost no trace amount of iron remaining in solution. ([[2013-elsayed|Elsayed, 2013, p. 41]])
- Chelating agents were found to be more effective than standard sea water flooding; for example, 5% Na4EDTA recovered an additional 7% of OOIP compared to sea water alone. ([[2013-elsayed|Elsayed, 2013, p. 64]])
- Increasing the pH of the EDTA solution from 10.7 to 12.2 significantly improved oil recovery, increasing it by approximately 30%. ([[2013-elsayed|Elsayed, 2013, p. 65]])
- Higher concentrations of NH4EDTA (10% vs 5%) resulted in higher incremental oil recovery (18% vs 12%). ([[2013-elsayed|Elsayed, 2013, p. 77]])
- The study found that EDTA is a stronger chelating agent than HEDTA, as it chelated more cations and led to greater weight loss of the rock. ([[2013-elsayed|Elsayed, 2013, p. 83]])
- Chelation of iron ions was directly correlated with oil recovery; for instance, 10% NH4EDTA successfully chelated 315.769 mg of iron from the rock. ([[2013-elsayed|Elsayed, 2013, p. 80]])
- Zeta potential measurements showed that chelating agents shifted the surface charge of Berea sandstone toward more negative values, improving wettability compared to low-salinity water. ([[2013-elsayed|Elsayed, 2013, p. 84]])
