# ACOUSTIC PROPERTIES AND SEISMIC SIGNATURE OF BIOTURBATED STRATA OF THE JURASSIC HANIFA FORMATION

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of bioturbation styles (Burrow Networks and Bioretexturing) on the acoustic properties and seismic signatures of carbonate rocks in the Hanifa Formation. Using a combination of field sampling, CT scanning, thin section analysis, and DEM modeling, it concludes that bioturbation style significantly influences the relationship between porosity and velocity.

---

## Research Problem

Bioturbation creates complex, heterogeneous pore structures in carbonate rocks that complicate the relationship between acoustic properties (like Vp) and rock properties (like porosity). This complexity makes it difficult to determine the correct REV for laboratory measurements and leads to potential inaccuracies in seismic interpretation and reservoir characterization.

## Objectives

- To establish the minimum required sample size (representative elementary volume) to study the acoustic properties of the bioturbated upper part of Ulayyah Member of Hanifa Formation based on the bioturbation style and intensity.
- To model the seismic signature of the upper part of Ulayyah Member of Hanifa Formation and study the effect of bioturbation style and intensity on the seismic signal.
- Assess the effect of sample size on laboratory acoustic velocity measurements in bioturbated carbonate rocks.
- Evaluate the impact of burrowing styles (BN vs. BR) on porosity and compressional wave velocity (Vp).
- Determine the Representative Elementary Volume (REV) for different bioturbation types to ensure accurate petrophysical characterization.
- Quantify the influence of bioturbation intensity (Burrow Percentage) on acoustic properties using CT scan data.

## Methods

The study utilized rock samples (cores and large prisms) from the Hanifa Formation. Acoustic velocity (Vp) was measured using a Proseq system with 24 kHz transducers on oven-dried samples. Porosity was calculated from bulk density and grain density (calcite). Medical x-ray CT scans (Toshiba Alexion TSX-032A) were used to identify bioturbation styles and quantify Burrow Percentage (BP) via MATLAB scripts. Thin section analysis (AxioScan system, ZEISS ZEN 3.8 software) provided petrographic evidence of mineralogy and pore structure. Differential Effective Medium (DEM) modeling was used to estimate the effective pore aspect ratio (EPAR). Seismic modeling involved creating 1-D velocity models with varying resolutions and thicknesses to generate synthetic seismograms using Ricker wavelets. Permeability was measured using both a portable system (NER TinyPerm3) and a laboratory system (NER AutoScan), followed by statistical analysis (ANOVA, regression) and graphical methods (Bland-Altman, Box & Whisker).

## Data and Materials

Samples from the Hanifa Formation in the northwestern region of Riyadh; core samples including standard size (1.5-inch) and larger diameters (3-inch or greater); permeability measurements for host rock matrix (HRM) and burrow fillings (BF).

## Key Findings

- BN samples exhibit a moderate positive correlation between Vp and core diameter (R² = 0.67), indicating that larger cores (3 inches or more) are necessary to capture the full extent of burrow structures. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 17]])
- BR samples display a weaker correlation between Vp and core diameter (R² = 0.05), suggesting that standard 1.5-inch cores are sufficient for accurate measurements. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 17]])
- BN samples exhibited higher mean Vp (approx. 4931 m/s) compared to BR samples (approx. 4630 m/s) for similar porosity ranges. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 54]])
- A DEM model showed that an EPAR value of 0.155 effectively differentiates the two bioturbation style groups on the Vp-porosity plot. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 64]])
- The average density of human body parts (1.03 to 1.16 g/cm³) was insufficient for the medical x-ray CT scanner to penetrate rock prisms (>2.2 g/cm³), necessitating the use of thinner slabs. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 20]])
- The study identified two distinct bioturbation styles: Burrow Networks (BN) and Bioretexturing (BR). ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 47]])
- The correlation between Vp and porosity was significantly stronger in BR samples (R² = 0.87 after removing outliers) than in BN samples, where other factors like matrix stiffness were more influential. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 60]])
- Vp for BN samples showed a positive correlation with core diameter (R² = 0.67), indicating that the REV for BN is larger than standard 1.5-inch cores, potentially stabilizing at ~3.39-inch. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 61]])
- Vp did not show significant influence from core diameter in BR samples (R² = 0.05), suggesting a smaller REV for the more homogeneous BR style. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024]])

- DEM modeling identified an EPAR value of 0.155 as a potential cutoff between BN and BR styles, with lower Vp in some BR samples attributed to elongated pore structures. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 63]])
- A positive correlation was found between Burrow Percentage (BP) derived from CT scans and acoustic velocity (Vp). ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 82]])
- Thin section analysis showed BN samples had higher cementation and fewer well-rounded pore spaces in the host rock matrix compared to BR samples. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 83]])
- The study confirmed that core diameters larger than 1.5 inches are necessary for accurate Vp measurements in BN samples, while 1.5-inch cores are sufficient for BR samples. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 65]])
- A comparison of permeability measurement techniques showed a general agreement between the portable TinyPerm3 and laboratory AutoScan systems. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 103]])

## Limitations

The segmentation algorithm for CT scans struggled with BR style areas where no clear burrow boundaries exist, leading to some inaccurate BP values in those sections. Additionally, small sample sizes (under 3 inches) were found to be unreliable for capturing the complexity of BN structures.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future research should investigate a wider range of geological settings and carbonate rock types, explore other bioturbation parameters like burrow size distribution, and investigate interactions between bioturbation, pore structure, and elastic properties.
