# ACOUSTIC PROPERTIES AND SEISMIC SIGNATURE OF BIOTURBATED STRATA OF THE JURASSIC HANIFA FORMATION

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of bioturbation styles (Burrow Networks and Bioretexturing) on the acoustic properties and seismic signatures of carbonate rocks in the Hanifa Formation. It specifically examines how sample size affects laboratory velocity measurements and concludes that larger core diameters are necessary to accurately capture the effects of burrow structures on compressional wave velocity.

---

## Research Problem

Bioturbation introduces significant heterogeneity into carbonate reservoirs, complicating the relationship between acoustic properties (like Vp) and rock properties (porosity). There is a lack of research on how bioturbation affects seismic signatures and whether standard core sizes are sufficient to capture these effects in large-scale burrow structures.

## Objectives

- Determine the minimum sample size (representative elementary volume) needed to study the acoustic properties of bioturbated carbonate rocks.
- Investigate the relationship between bioturbation, rock properties, and elastic properties in the Hanifa Formation.
- Model the effect of bioturbation style and intensity on the seismic signal.
- To establish the minimum required sample size (representative elementary volume) to study the acoustic properties of the bioturbated upper part of Ulayyah Member of Hanifa Formation based on the bioturbation style and intensity.
- To investigate the effect of bioturbation style and intensity on acoustic velocity of the upper part of Ulayyah Member of Hanifa Formation.
- To model the seismic signature of the upper part of Ulayyah Member of Hanifa Formation and study the effect of bioturbation style and intensity on the seismic signal.
- Assess the effect of sample size on laboratory acoustic velocity measurements in burrowed carbonates.
- Compare the impact of Burrow Networks (BN) and Bioretexturing (BR) bioturbation styles on porosity and compressional wave velocity (Vp).
- Determine the Representative Elementary Volume (REV) for different bioturbation styles to improve petrophysical characterization.
- Quantify the impact of different bioturbation styles on acoustic velocity measurements.
- Correlate burrow percentage with acoustic properties using medical CT scan data.
- Evaluate the role of pore geometry (EPAR) in the Vp-porosity relationship via DEM modeling.
- Model seismic signatures to determine how bioturbation affects high-frequency seismic surveys.
- Evaluate the accuracy and reliability of the TinyPerm3 portable system compared to the AutoScan laboratory system for measuring permeability in bioturbated rocks.
- Assess the agreement between measurement results from both systems across different lithologies (host rock matrix vs. burrow fill).
- Determine if a handheld device can serve as a viable alternative for field-based permeability assessment.
- Investigate the impact of sample size on acoustic velocity measurements in a laboratory setting.
- Evaluate the impact of bioturbation style (burrow network and bioretexturing) and intensity on acoustic properties.
- Compare the performance of the AutoScan spot permeameter with the portable TinyPerm3 permeameter for measuring permeability in bioturbated rocks.
- Evaluate the impact of bioturbation on the physical properties (porosity, permeability) of Hanifa Formation carbonates.
- Determine the acoustic properties of bioturbated vs. non-bioturbated strata.
- Identify unique seismic signatures associated with bioturbation in Jurassic carbonate reservoirs.

## Methods

The research utilized a combination of field work, laboratory measurements, and computer simulation. Field trips were conducted to the Hanifa Formation outcrop northwest of Riyadh to collect boulders from four distinct bioturbated strata. These were cut into 30x30 cm prisms and 10x30 cm slabs. Medical x-ray CT scans (Toshiba Alexion TSX-032A) were used to image samples in 3D to quantify bioturbation styles. Thin sections were prepared, stained with blue-dyed epoxy, and analyzed using an AxioScan system and ZEISS ZEN 3.8 software. Porosity was measured via helium gas porosimetry and calculated from bulk density for larger cores. Compressional wave velocity (Vp) was measured using ultrasonic pulse transducers. Forward modeling was used to simulate seismic data based on varying thickness and velocity scenarios.

## Data and Materials

Samples were collected from four bioturbated resistive strata of the Ulayyah Member in the Hanifa Formation at Jabal Al-Abakkayn outcrop. A total of 30 cores were analyzed, including 21 standard 1.5-inch core plugs and larger cores (2, 2.5, 4, and 6 inches). XRD analysis confirmed nearly 100% calcite content in the samples.

## Key Findings

- Bioturbation style significantly impacts acoustic properties: samples with Burrow Network (BN) style exhibited higher average compressional wave velocity (Vp) compared to those with Bioretexture (BR) style at similar porosity levels. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 15]])
- The DEM model successfully differentiated the two bioturbation styles using an EPAR value of 0.155 on the Vp-porosity plot. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 64]])
- A core diameter of 3 inches or more is required to obtain representative velocity measurements in samples with BN bioturbation style, as smaller cores show a positive logarithmic correlation between velocity and diameter. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 39]])
- page_number_reference_note_for_finding_2_is_64_based_on_figure_caption_description ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024]])
- The study confirmed that both TinyPerm 3 and AutoScan systems provide comparable permeability measurements for the host rock matrix (HRM) with a correlation of 1.18. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 93]])
- TinyPerm 3 showed an overestimation of permeability in burrow fillings (BF) at high values compared to AutoScan, as evidenced by an intercept of -0.28 on a log-log plot. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 97]])
- BN samples exhibit a moderate positive correlation between Vp and core diameter (R² = 0.67), suggesting that larger cores (3 inches or more) are necessary to capture the full extent of burrow structures. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 17]])
- BR samples display a weaker correlation between Vp and core diameter (R² = 0.05), indicating that standard 1.5-inch cores are sufficient for accurate measurements. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 17]])
- XRD analysis shows almost 100% calcite for the samples in the study area. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 23]])
- The upper part of the Ulayyah Member consists of four resistive strata interbedded with muddy carbonate, bounded by a lower recessive muddy carbonate marker bed and an upper cross-bedded carbonate marker bed. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 12]])
- BN samples exhibited a mean Vp of 4931 m/s (std dev 158 m/s), while BR samples showed a lower mean Vp of 4630 m/s (std dev 228 m/s). ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 54]])
- The relationship between Vp and porosity for BN samples was weak, suggesting factors like matrix stiffness or cementation are more influential than bulk porosity. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 54]])
- For BR samples, a stronger correlation between Vp and porosity (R² = 0.64) was found, which improved to R² = 0.87 when low-porosity outliers were removed. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 54]])
- Vp in BN samples showed a moderate positive correlation with core diameter (R² = 0.67), indicating that standard 1.5-inch plugs do not represent the full burrow network. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 57]])
- Vp in BR samples was not significantly influenced by core diameter (R² = 0.05), as their more homogeneous matrix allows for accurate representation in standard sizes. ([[2024-allehyani|allehyani, 2024, p. 57]])

## Limitations

The study noted that the TinyPerm3 may overestimate permeability at higher values (above 250 mD), potentially due to lower contact pressure compared to laboratory systems. Additionally, it notes that surface weathering can affect measurements on exposed outcrops.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Further investigation is needed into why the Tiny1m3 overestimates high-permeability values; this may be related to its specific mechanical operation or contact pressure. The study suggests portable devices are valuable for overcoming logistical challenges of transporting large samples and for making in-situ measurements on weathered surfaces.
