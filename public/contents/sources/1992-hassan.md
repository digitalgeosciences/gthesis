# Hydrogeochemistry of Alat and Khobar aquifers in Eastern Saudi Arabia

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the hydrogeochemistry of the Alat and Khobar aquifers in Eastern Saudi Arabia, focusing on mineral saturation indices, chemical evolution patterns, and seawater intrusion. It concludes that both aquifers are significantly impacted by seawater intrusion in the north and exhibit distinct geochemical signatures related to gypsum dissolution and cation exchange.

---

## Research Problem

Characterizing the hydrogeochemical behavior, mineral saturation states (calcite, dolomite, gypsum), and environmental impacts such as seawater intrusion and industrial suitability of the Alat and Khobar aquifers in Eastern Saudi Arabia.

## Objectives

- Determine distribution of major ions and their ratios.
- Determine distribution of water quality parameters such as total dissolved solids (TDS) and sodium adsorption ratio (SAR).
- Determine distribution of saturation indices (Sf) with respect to various aquifer minerals.
- Identify chemical evolutionary sequence of groundwaters and the factors affecting the water composition.
- Evaluate the quality of waters in relation to domestic, agricultural, and industrial uses.
- Determine the major ion distribution and calculate saturation indices for calcite, dolomite, and gypsum in both aquifers.

## Methods

The study utilized hydrochemical analysis to determine major ion concentrations. Saturation indices (S.I.) were calculated using the PCWATEQ program (which incorporates the extended form of Debye-Huckel and Davies equations) to assess mineral stability. Piper's trilinear plots and Stiff diagrams were used to identify hydrogeochemical facies. The sulfate/chloride ratio was analyzed to distinguish between gypsum dissolution and seawater intrusion. Empirical relationships between TDS and EC were established for both aquifers.

## Data and Materials

347 well samples from the Eastern Province, Saudi Arabia; 250 samples from the Khobar aquifer; 83 samples from the Alat aquifer; 14 additional samples from GDC. Data included major ions (mg/l), EC, TDS, pH, temperature, and silica.

## Key Findings

- Both aquifers are over-saturated with calcite and dolomite, and under-saturated with gypsum. ([[1992-hassan|Hassan, 1992, p. 19]])
- The Piper's trilinear plots show the presence of three different facies in the Alat aquifer (Na-Cl-Ca-SO4, Na-Ca-Cl, and Na-Cl) and four facies in the Khobar aquifer. ([[1992-hassan|Hassan, 1992, p. 19]])
- The groundwater evolutionary pattern in both aquifers generally follows Chebotarev's sequence. ([[1992-hassan|Hassan, 1992, p. 19]])
- TDS in the aquifers exceeds recommended levels for most uses, but is mostly suitable for municipal, stock farming, and some industrial use. ([[1992-hassan|Hassan, 1992, p. 19]])
- Alat aquifer thickness ranges from 10m to 50m with transmissivity of 3.1 x 10^2 to 2.3 x 10^3 m^2/s and storativity of 1.5 x 10^-3 to 2.6 x 10^{-2}. ([[1992-hassan|Hassan, 1992, p. 21]])
- Khobar aquifer thickness ranges from 20m to 70m with an average transmissivity of 2.9 x 10^3 m^2/s and storativity of 2.1 x 10^{-2}. ([[1992-hassan|Hassan, 1992, p. 22]])
- The concentration of calcium in the Alat aquifer varies from 100 mg/l to 1300 mg/l. ([[1992-hassan|Hassan, 1992, p. 45]])
- Sodium concentration in the Alat aquifer reaches a maximum value of 5800 mg/l near Al-Jubayl. ([[1992-hassan|Hassan, 1992, p. 50]])
- The chloride concentration in the Khobar aquifer reaches its highest concentration of more than 3000 mg/l at the north of Jubayl. ([[1992-hassan|Hassan, 1992, p. 64]])
- TDS in the Alat aquifer varies from 2000 mg/l to more than 9000 mg/l. ([[1992-hassan|Hassan, 1992, p. 53]])
- The correlation coefficient (r^2) for the TDS-EC relationship in the Alat aquifer is 0.93 with a fit of TDS = 0.64 EC. ([[1992-hassan|Hassan, 1992, p. 56]])
- The Alat aquifer is mostly oversaturated with respect to calcite (S.I. 0.2 to 0.4) and undersaturated with respect to gypsum. ([[1992-hassan|Hassan, 1992, p. 87]])
- The Khobar aquifer is also oversaturated with respect to calcite and dolomite, but shows a distinct lack of a general pattern in variation compared to the Alat aquifer. ([[1992-hassan|Hassan, 1992, p. 91]])
- Seawater intrusion is clearly evident in both aquifers north of Jubayl, characterized by high sulfate, magnesium, sodium, and chloride concentrations. ([[1992-hassan|Hassan, 1992, p. 116]])
- In the Alat aquifer, a cation exchange process (calcium replaced by sodium) was identified near Abqaiq due to the presence of shaly layers. ([[1992-hassan|Hassan, 1992, p. 102]])

## Limitations

Limited data for nitrate content meant that its impact on water quality was not discussed. The study notes that some negative S.I. values in the Alat aquifer might not represent real values due to insufficient data.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Water from both aquifers requires treatment before use for domestic purposes or food processing, but can be used for heavy industries and livestock farming.
