# Development of Oil-Well Cement Formulations for CO2-Rich Environment Relevant to Geologic Carbon Sequestration

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the development of oil-well cement formulations modified with various additives (nanoclay, polypropylene fiber, olive waste, laponite, and graphite) to improve resistance against carbonation in CO2-rich environments. The study concludes that these additives, particularly nanoclay, significantly enhance compressive strength, reduce permeability, and limit carbonation depth compared to base Class G cement.

---

## Research Problem

Standard Class G cement is susceptible to degradation when exposed to CO2-saturated brine in high-temperature/high-pressure environments typical of geological carbon sequestration. This degradation involves the conversion of portlandite (CH) into soluble carbonates or the formation of less stable phases, leading to increased porosity and reduced mechanical integrity.

## Objectives

- To investigate the effect of adding different materials such as nanoclay, polypropylene fiber, olive waste, and laponite on the cement strength, structure, and failure envelop after exposing the cement to CO2 under GCS conditions.
- To study the mechanisms of the reactions of the base cement materials and the cement containing specific additives with CO2-saturated brine which could affect the cement carbonation.
- To develop new cement formulations for applications of GCS which have the ability to minimize the reaction rate of the cement and CO2.
- Evaluate the effect of nanoclay (NC) on carbonation depth, permeability, and mechanical strength of Class G cement.
- Investigate the impact of synthetic polypropylene fiber (PPF) as a filler and reinforcement for carbonation resistance.
- Assess the effectiveness of olive waste as an additive to mitigate carbonate-induced degradation.

## Methods

The study involved preparing six cement formulations (Base, NC1, PPF1-3, OW1-3, Lap1-5, Gr1-3) using Saudi Class G oil well cement and various additives. Samples were characterized via XRD to evaluate chemical changes in hydration products. The samples were then placed in an HPHT reaction chamber where they were exposed to CO2-saturated 0.5 M NaCl brine at 95°C or 130°C and 10 MPa for specific durations (e.g., 10, 20, or 30 days). Post-reaction analysis included compressive strength testing (ASTM/API standards), tensile strength measurement using the Brazilian test method, permeability measurements using nitrogen injection under high pressure, and micro-CT scanning to determine carbonation depth.

## Data and Materials

6 cement formulations; samples tested for 10, 20, and 30 days of exposure; XRD analysis of powder samples; CT scan imaging of cylindrical samples; permeability tests on cylinders (1.5" diameter); compressive strength tests on cubes (2"x2"x2"); tensile strength tests on cylinders (1.5" diameter x 0.9" length).

## Key Findings

- Incorporating 1% BWOC of nanoclay (NC) reduced the carbonation depth and limited the reduction in compressive and tensile strengths to 8.56% and 9.82%, respectively, compared to 45.67% and 27.39% for base cement. ([[2020-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2020, p. 19]])
- Incorporating 0.1% BWOC of olive waste maintained a matrix permeability 33.9% lower than the base cement after 20 days of exposure to CO2-saturated brine. ([[2020-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2020, p. 74]])
- Incorporating 0.125% BWOC of polypropylene fiber (PPF) reduced carbonation depth by 31.2% compared to base cement after 20 days of exposure. ([[2020-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2020, p. 19]])
- Adding 0.1% BWOC of olive waste reduced the carbonation depth and rate by 13.6% and 18.6%, respectively, and decreased permeability by 33.9% compared to base cement after 20 days. ([[2020-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2020, p. 19]])
- Incorporating 0.3% BWOC of laponite reduced carbonation depth and rate by 27.0% and 26.8%, respectively, after 10 days of exposure. ([[2020-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2020, p. 19]])
- Adding 0.2% BWOC of graphite reduced the carbonation depth by 29.8% after 10 days and improved compressive/tensile strengths by 16.4% and 23.8%, respectively. ([[2020-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2020, p. 20]])
- XRD analysis showed that 1% NC increased the amorphous content to 71.5 wt% (from 67.0%) and decreased portlandite (CH) from 20.4 wt% to 14.7 wt%. ([[2020-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2020, p. 28]])
- The addition of NC increased the original compressive strength by 15.0% (from 60.47 to 69.53 MPa). ([[2020-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2020, p. 28]])
- The final permeability of sample NC1 after 30 days was 0.0029 mD, while the base sample (NC0) was 0.0060 mD. ([[2020-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2020, p. 31]])
- Addition of 1% BWOC of NC particles reduced the maximum carbonation depth by 42.3% (from 4423.4 μm to 2552.5 μm) and improved compressive strength from a 45.67% reduction to only an 8.56% reduction. ([[2020-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2020, p. 54]])
- The inclusion of 0.125% BWOC of PPF resulted in the lowest carbonation depth (31.2% less than base cement) and a 50.6% higher compressive strength after 20 days. ([[2020-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2020, p. 62]])
- The addition of 0.125% BWOC of PPF reduced the carbonation rate to 118 μm/Day, which is 31.0% less than the base cement (171 μm/Day). ([[2020-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2020, p. 63]])
- The addition of 0.3% BWOC of laponite reduced the carbonation depth from 3144 μm (base) to 2296 μm, representing a 27.0% reduction. ([[2020-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2020, p. 81]])
- Laponite-modified cement with 0.3% BWOC showed a 50.0% lower permeability (0.0021 mD) compared to the base sample (0.0042 mD) after 10 days of carbonation. ([[2020-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2020, p. 86]])
- Graphite addition at 0.2% BWOC reduced the carbonation depth by 29.8% compared to the control sample (from 2081 μm to 1460 μm). ([[2020-mahmoud|Mahmoud, 2020, p. 71]])
