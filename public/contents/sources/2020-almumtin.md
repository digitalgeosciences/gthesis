# Rock Physics Based Seismic Characterization of Saudi Clastic Reservoirs: Rock Physics Diagnostics of Wireline Data

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the development of a Rock Physics Model (RPM) to link elastic properties (P-wave and S-wave impedance, density) to volumetric properties (porosity, mineralogy, and hydrocarbon saturation) in Saudi clastic reservoirs. By utilizing wireline data from 8 wells and applying rock physics diagnostics (RPD), the study establishes an RPM that is validated through upscaling to ensure applicability at the seismic scale.

---

## Research Problem

The challenge of accurately interpreting volumetric rock properties, such as porosity and mineralogy, from seismic data due to the limitations of resolution and the need for a robust link between elastic and volumetric properties in clastic reservoirs (specifically the Unayzah Formation).

## Objectives

- Establish a theoretical rock physics model (RPM) that honors and explains the wireline data under examination.
- Identify an RPM suitable for both well-scale analysis and seismic-scale interpretation.
- Validate the selected RPM at the seismic scale through an upscaling process.
- Establish a Rock Physics Model (RPM) using wireline data for Saudi clastic fields.
- Evaluate the impact of spatial scale on rock physics models when moving from well-scale to seismic-scale data.
- Assess the accuracy and uncertainty of applying these models at the seismic scale.
- Determine the feasibility of future seismic interpretation based on the established rock physics diagnostics.
- Establish a Rock Physics Model (RPM) using wireline data to relate elastic and volumetric rock properties.
- Upscale well log data to seismic resolution using the Backus average running window method.
- Predict porosity and mineralogy from seismically derived volumes.
- Identify cut-offs for distinguishing clean sand, silty sand, and tight zones in both wireline and seismic resolutions.

## Methods

The study employs Rock Physics Diagnostics (RPD) to establish a Rock Physics Model (RPM). This involves cross-plotting elastic variables (e.g., Vp) against volumetric variables (e.g., total porosity) and color-coding by a third variable (e.g., clay content). Fluid substitution using the Batzle and Williams equations is used to bring data to a common fluid denominator. Several models, including Raymer's model, the stiff-sand model, and the soft-sand model, are evaluated against wireline data from 8 wells. Finally, an upscaling approach is applied to ensure the RPM remains valid at seismic resolution.

## Data and Materials

Wireline log data from 8 wells in the Unayzah formation, including measurements for porosity (2-10%), bulk density, Vp, Vs, Poisson's ratio, and P-wave impedance. Reference data includes legacy laboratory results from Han (1986).

## Key Findings

- The wireline data for the studied reservoir are best fit by the stiff-sand model and Raymer's model, both relevant to fast and stiff clastic rock. ([[2020-almumtin|Almumtin, 2020, p. 14]])
- The study established that the resulting velocity-porosity trends from wireline data are practically the same as those in a classic laboratory dataset. ([[2020-almumtin|Almumtin, 2020, p. 14]])
- The selected stiff-sand model is more rigorous and allows for more parameter adjustment compared to Raymer's model. ([[2020-almumtin|Almumtin, 2020, p. 14]])
- The study confirms that the chosen RPM remains valid at the seismic scale after applying an upscaling approach. ([[2020-almumtin|Almumtin, 2020, p. 15]])
- The stiff-sand model was successfully used to predict clay content in wells where it was not provided by the original petrophysical analysis. ([[2020-almumtin|Almumtin, 2020, p. 43]])
- The study area's rocks are characterized as very stiff, leading to a weak dependency of elastic properties on pore fluid types. ([[2020-almumtin|Almumtin, 2020, p. 43]])
- The stiff-sand model accurately matched the measured P-wave and S-wave data across multiple wells (199-3, 199-4, 199-5, 199-6, 199-17, 199-29, 199-134). ([[2020-almumtin|Almumtin, 2020, p. 48]])
- New data cross-plots showed high consistency with legacy laboratory data from Han (1986) for 50 MPa confining stress. ([[2020-almumtin|Almumtin, 2020, p. 48]])
- Porosity was found to increase as clay content decreased and quartz content increased across the studied wells. ([[2020-almumtin|Almumtin, 2020, p. 51]])
- The stiff-sand model successfully fit the wireline data for all eight wells, showing that the rock is a fast and stiff clastic reservoir. ([[2020-almumtin|Almumtin, 2020, p. 52]])
- A Poisson's ratio cut-off of 0.18 can effectively separate clean sand from silty sand in both wireline and seismic resolutions. ([[2020-almumtin|Almumtin, 2020, p. 44]])
- P-wave impedance can be used to identify porous vs. tight sands using a 13 km/s cut-off at seismic resolution. ([[2020-almumtin|Almumtin, 2020, p. 44]])
- The Backus-upscaled curves for porosity, density, P-wave velocity, S-wave velocity, Poisson's ratio, and P-wave impedance showed good agreement with original log curves except where high-frequency changes exceeded seismic resolution. ([[2020-almumtin|Almumtin, 2020, p. 41]])
- The average frequency at the reservoir level is approximately 30 Hz with a mean compressional velocity of 4.89 km/s, resulting in a resolvable thickness of approximately 67 ft. ([[2020-almumtin|Almumtin, 2020, p. 41]])

## Limitations

The thesis notes a significant difference in spatial scale between well logs (approximately one foot) and seismic data (tens or hundreds of feet), requiring careful consideration during the transition from wireline-derived models to seismic interpretation.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The established rock physics model and mesh can be used to predict porosity and mineralogy from seismically derived volumes for sweet spot identification in clastic reservoirs.
