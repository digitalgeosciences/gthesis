# Corrosivity of water-base drilling fluids in deep, hot wells.

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the corrosion behavior of water-based drilling fluids in high-pressure, high-temperature (HPHT) environments typical of deep wells. It evaluates the impact of temperature, pH, and aging on the rheological properties and corrosion rates of steel components using weight loss methods, electrochemical measurements, and SEM analysis.

---

## Research Problem

The corrosiveness of water-based drilling fluids is a major cause of drill pipe failures and damage to downhole equipment in deep, hot wells. The study aims to evaluate the impact of temperature, pressure, and mud chemistry (specifically pH) on the corrosion rates of steel components.

## Objectives

- Establish the thermal stability of the drilling fluid.
- Evaluate the corrosivity of a typical drilling fluid under conditions simulating deep, hot wells in the presence of mild steel type 1018.
- Evaluate the thermal stability of water-based muds at temperatures up to 400°F.
- Determine the impact of aging on the rheological properties (plastic viscosity and effective viscosity) of drilling fluids.
- Measure corrosion rates of steel coupons in various pH environments using weight loss methods.
- Perform electrochemical measurements (potentiodynamic polarization and AC impedance) to study corrosion kinetics.

## Methods

The research utilized a variety-prepared water-based muds (Base Mud I and Base Mud II). Rheological properties were measured using an HPHT Viscometer (FANN-70) at 3000 psi. Corrosion was studied via weight loss of steel coupons in a dynamic flow loop and a roller oven to simulate downhole conditions. Electrochemical measurements included potentiodynamic polarization tests using a potentiostat and AC impedance analysis using a Frequency Response Analyzer (FRA). Surface morphology was analyzed using a Jeol JSM 840 SEM.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized mild steel type 1018 coupons and a typical water-based drilling fluid. Experimental data were collected from tests involving varying temperatures (up to 400°F), different pH levels (ranging from 4.1 to 10.9), and various flow conditions in a dynamic loop.

## Key Findings

- Corrosion rate increases with an increase in temperature and a decrease in pH. ([[1994-shokoya|Shokoya, 1994, p. 19]])
- Corrosion rates are low at mildly alkaline to mildly acidic pH range (specifically between pH 8.0 and 10.0). ([[1994-shokoya|Shokoya, 1994, p. 19]])
- The drilling fluid generally attacks the grain boundaries of the steel. ([[1994-shokoya|Shokoya, 1994, p. 19]])
- Diffusion was found to be the rate-limiting step for the corrosion reaction; once diffusion resistance is overcome by increased flow rate or agitation, corrosiveness increases tremendously. ([[1994-shokoya|Shokoya, 1994, p. 19]])
- At pH of 10.9, corrosion rates were measured at temperatures of 200°F, 250°F, 300°F, and 400°F. ([[1994-shokoya|Shokoya, 1994, p. 13]])
- At pH of 4.1, AC impedance data were collected at static conditions and at rotation speeds of 500, 1000, and 2000 rpm. ([[1994-shokoya|Shokoya, 1994, p. 14]])
- Effective and plastic viscosities decrease with increase in temperature and aging due to the aggregation of clay platelets at high temperatures. ([[1994-shokoya|Shokoya, 1994, p. 86]])
- Corrosion rates were found to increase with temperature increase for all mud formulations tested, particularly when using the PAIR method. ([[1994-shokoya|Shokoya, 1994, p. 58]])
- The weight loss of steel coupons in a roller oven showed that corrosion rate increases as temperature increases at low pH, but decreases as pH increases even at high temperatures. ([[1994-shokoya|Shokoya, 1994, p. 55]])
- Iron oxide (ironite sponge) was found to effectively eliminate pitting tendencies of carbon steel in solutions containing sulfide ions at pH of 8 and above. ([[1994-shokoya|Shokoya, 1994, p. 54]])
- The study confirmed that measurement from the PAIR method is more accurate than weight-loss for identifying localized corrosion tendencies. ([[1994-shokoya|Shokoya, 1994, p. 58]])
- A reduction in plastic viscosity was observed as aging time increased at temperatures of 200°F, 250°F, 300°F, and 400°F. ([[1994-shokoya|Shokoya, 1994, p. 95]])
- The use of a dynamic flow loop allowed for the simulation of shear rates at the drill bit to break up gels formed by mud. ([[1994-shokoya|Shokoya, 1994, p. 67]])
- The drilling mud is thermally stable for temperatures up to 400°F. ([[1994-shokoya|Shokoya, 1994, p. 138]])
- Yield strength, plastic viscosity, and gel strength decrease with increasing temperature and aging time; the effect of aging diminishes as temperature increases. ([[1994-shokoya|Shokoya, 1994, p. 96]])

## Limitations

The study noted that some measurement methods (like weight loss) may not detect pitting corrosion, which is a major cause of pipe failure. Additionally, certain mud components like barite were excluded from testing to avoid equipment erosion and plugging.

## Recommendations and Future Work

It is recommended not to use mud in the pH range below 8.0 for high-temperature applications. Future work should include chemical analysis of corrosion products, more advanced electrochemical studies at high rotation speeds, and erosion-corrosion studies.
