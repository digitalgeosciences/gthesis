# Fate of Bromate Formation in Water Treated by ozone-based Advanced Oxidation Processes

**Summary**: The thesis investigated the efficiency of pH suppression and ammonia addition in controlling bromate formation during the removal of phenol using ozone-based Advanced Oxidation Processes (AOP). The study compared batch and continuous ozonation methods, finding that while both could degrade phenol, only specific conditions involving lower pH and ammonia were effective at minimizing bromate.

---

## Research Problem

The formation of bromate, a regulated byproduct in drinking water treated with ozone-based Advanced Oxidation Processes (AOP), poses a significant challenge when removing organic contaminants like phenol. The study specifically addresses the need to find optimal conditions for pH suppression and ammonia addition to mitigate bromate while ensuring effective phenol degradation.

## Objectives

- Investigate the extent of bromate formation under different ozone-based AOP treatment conditions.
- Evaluate the effects of pH adjustment on bromate minimization.
- Evaluate the effects of ammonia addition on bromate minimization.
- Determine optimal treatment conditions to prevent bromate formation in Saudi Arabian water.
- Evaluate ozone-based AOPs for the removal of phenol from water.
- Identify optimal conditions (pH and ammonia levels) to minimize or prevent bromate formation during ozonation.
- Determine the most effective ozone-based AOP technique for rapid phenol removal while maintaining bromate levels below regulatory limits.
- To degrade Phenol in water with the most appropriate AOP technique.
- To assess the extent of Bromate formation under different ozone-based AOP treatment conditions such as UV light intensity, ozone dosage and contact time.
- To study the efficiency of bromate minimization under different levels of pH and Ammonia.
- To identify the optimum conditions of pH and Ammonia levels to achieve the minimum concentration of bromate in treated water.
- Evaluate the degradation of phenol using different UV light sources (LP and MP) alone.
- Assess the impact of combining UV light with continuous ozonation on phenol degradation and bromate formation.
- Investigate the effect of pH adjustment on minimizing bromate formation during ozonation.
- Determine the optimal ammonia concentration to inhibit bromate formation in water containing bromide.
- Investigate the extent of bromate formation under different AOP treatment conditions (UV light, ozone dosage, and contact time).
- Study the efficiency of bromate minimization strategies (ammonia addition and pH suppression).
- Identify the optimum conditions to inhibit the formation of bromate in water.
- Quantify dissolved ozone concentration during the treatment process.
- Identify and quantify by-products such as phenol using HPLC.

## Methods

The research was conducted at the Centre for Environment and Water (CEW) at KFUPM. Experimental procedures included batch and continuous ozonation, as well as UV-only treatment. Ozone concentration was determined using the indigo method with a UV/VIS Spectrophotometer (Aquamate AQA2000E). Bromate concentrations were analyzed using Ion Chromatography (DIONEX ICS 3000) equipped with a Conductivity Detector. Phenol levels were monitored using an HPLC system (WATERS BINARY HPLC SYSTEM 1525) with a C-18 column and EMPOWER software.

## Data and Materials

The study used de-ionized water, phenol stock solution (50 ppm), potassium bromide (KBr) to create a bromide stock (5 mg/L), ammonium hydroxide for ammonia addition, and various reagents like indigo dye and sodium dithionite for ozone measurement. Experiments were conducted in a NORMAG tabular photoreactor with either low pressure (254 nm) or medium pressure (200-300 nm) UV lamps.

## Key Findings

- Continuous Ozonation at a flow rate of 1 liter per minute is the most effective ozone-based AOP technique for complete degradation of phenol in Saudi Arabian water within five minutes. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 13]])
- A pH level of 6.0 and an ammonia dosage of 1.5 ppm were found to be the optimum treatment conditions needed to prevent bromate formation in Saudi Arabian water. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 13]])
- The concentration of hydroxyl radicals (OH•) is a key driver for the destruction of organic contaminants like phenol, styrene, and benzene in AOP systems. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 17]])
- Bromate formation during ozonation is dependent on bromide content, ozone dosage, and pH levels. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 16]])
- Bromate is the only ozonation byproduct regulated in drinking water with a limit of 10 µg/L by WHO, EU, and USEPA. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 25]])
- Saudi Arabian groundwater is noted to have high bromide content (approximately 5 ppm). ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 26]])
- Bromate formation increases significantly with higher pH, specifically from 10 mg/L at pH 6.5 to 50 mg/L at pH 8.2. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 33]])
- The addition of 1.5 mg/L of ammonia can reduce bromate formation by approximately 5 mg/L in water containing 100 mg/L Br-. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 36]])
- Bromate formation follows a linear relationship with the 'Ct' factor (ozone dose multiplied by contact time). ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 34]])
- No phenol was removed due to mixing alone, and no bromate formation occurred during the 30-minute mixing period. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 57]])
- Batch ozonation (10 ppm ozone) at pH 7 achieved 86.8% removal of phenol (from 50 ppm to 6.92 ppm), while at pH 6, it achieved 77.4% removal. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 59]])
- Continuous ozonation at a rate of 1 L/min completely degraded phenol (from 50 ppm to 0 ppm) in less than 5 minutes at both pH 6 and 7. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 60]])
- Bromate formation was found to be inversely related to the ozone concentration; lower ozone concentrations resulted in lower bromate levels. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 63]])
- Bromate concentration at pH 7 with continuous ozonation (1 L/min) showed a trend of decreasing values as ozone exposure decreased, specifically showing 0.00 μg/L at the lowest levels. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 64]])
- Bromate concentration at pH 6 with continuous ozonation (1 L/min) showed a range of values from 0.97 to 6.44 μg/L depending on the ozone exposure. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 64]])

## Limitations

Lower concentrations of ammonia (0.1, 0.5, and 1.0 ppm) were found to be insufficient to prevent bromate formation even at low pH levels, as the resulting bromate levels remained above the maximum allowable limit of 10 ppb set by USEPA and EU.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Investigate the removal of different contaminants other than Phenol with continuous ozonation combined with bromate minimization strategies.
- Investigate other AOP techniques such as Ozone/hydrogen peroxide.
- Conduct a detailed kinetic investigation on the fate of added Ammonia.
- Perform proper identification and quantification of by-products formed during ozonation to ensure no new contaminants are generated.
