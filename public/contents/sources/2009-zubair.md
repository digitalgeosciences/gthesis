# Fate of Bromate Formation in Water Treated by ozone-based Advanced Oxidation Processes

**Summary**: The thesis investigated the formation of bromate, a potential carcinogen, during the ozone-based Advanced Oxidation Process (AOP) treatment of water containing bromide. The study evaluated how pH adjustment and ammonia addition could mitigate bromate formation while effectively degrading phenol in Saudi Arabian water.

---

## Research Problem

The formation of bromate during the treatment of groundwater containing bromide ions using ozone-based advanced oxidation processes (AOP) poses a significant risk in drinking water. The study specifically addresses how to mitigate this formation while effectively degrading organic contaminants like phenol in Saudi Arabian groundwater, which has high bromide content.

## Objectives

- To degrade Phenol in water with the most appropriate AOP technique.
- To assess the extent of Bromate formation under different ozone-based AOP treatment conditions such as UV light intensity, ozone dosage and contact time.
- To study the efficiency of bromate minimization under different levels of pH and Ammonia.
- To identify the optimum conditions of pH and Ammonia levels to achieve the minimum concentration of bromate in treated water.
- Evaluate the efficiency of bromate minimization strategies (ammonia addition and pH suppression).
- Identify the optimum conditions to inhibit the formation of bromate in water treated by ozone-based AOP.

## Methods

The study utilized a bench-scale experimental setup. Ozone concentration was determined using the indigo method via UV/VIS Spectrophotometry (Aquamate UV/VIS Spectrophotometer). Bromate concentrations were analyzed using Ion Chromatography (DIONEX ICS 3000) with a Conductivity Detector. Phenol and its degradation by-products were quantified using HPLC (WATERS BINARY HPLC SYSTEM 1525) with an Empower software system. Experimental procedures included batch ozonation, continuous ozonation at 1 L/min, and UV treatment using LP (15W) and MP (150W) lamps. Mitigation strategies involved varying ammonia concentrations (0.1, 0.5, 1.0, and 1.5 ppm) and adjusting pH levels from 4 to 9.

## Data and Materials

The study used simulated water samples containing 50 ppm phenol and 5 ppm bromide. Samples were analyzed at various time intervals (0, 0.17, 5, 10, 20, and 30 minutes) under different conditions: pH levels (4, 5, 6, 7, 9), ammonia concentrations (0, 0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5 ppm), and ozonation methods (batch vs. continuous).

## Key Findings

- Continuous Ozonation (1 liter per minute) is the most effective ozone-based AOP technique for complete degradation of phenol in Saudi Arabian water within five minutes. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 13]])
- A pH level of 6.0 and an ammonia dosage of 1.5 ppm were found to be the optimum treatment conditions needed to prevent bromate formation in Saudi Arabian water. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 13]])
- Bromide conversion to bromate during ozonation typically ranges between 10-50%. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 32]])
- Bromate formation increases significantly as the pH of the water increases, with a jump from 10 mg/L at pH 6.5 to 50 mg/L at pH 8.2. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 33]])
- The addition of ammonia (1.5 mg/L) can reduce bromate formation by approximately 5 mg/L in water containing 100 mg/L Br- under constant conditions. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 36]])
- A concentration of 50 μg/L of ammonia is likely sufficient to block free bromine pathways for several minutes. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 40]])
- Continuous ozonation at a rate of 1 L/min was found to be more effective than batch ozonation, achieving 100% phenol removal in less than 5 minutes across all tested pH levels. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 60]])
- Batch ozonation (10 ppm ozone) resulted in incomplete phenol degradation (86.8% at pH 7 and 77.4% at pH 6). ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 59]])
- Bromate formation was significantly reduced by lowering the pH; for example, a reduction from pH 7 to pH 4 resulted in a 35% reduction in bromate concentration. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 77]])
- The addition of 1.5 ppm ammonia successfully inhibited bromate formation at all pH levels below 7 (pH 4, 5, and 6), resulting in 0 ppm bromate detected. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 83]])
- At higher pH levels (7 and 9), an ammonia dosage of 1.5 ppm was insufficient to prevent bromate formation, only achieving reductions of 13% at pH 7 and 35% at pH 9. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 84]])
- UV treatment alone (LP 15W or MP 150W) resulted in only partial phenol degradation (18% for LP and over 50% for MP) and did not contribute to bromate formation. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 65]])
- The primary degradation by-products of phenol identified via HPLC were catechol, hydroquinone, para-benzoquinone, and ortho-benzoquinone. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 76]])
- Stripping experiments with nitrogen gas showed only a 5% reduction in phenol concentration, confirming that degradation was due to ozonation rather than volatilization. ([[2009-zubair|Zubair, 2009, p. 74]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Investigate the removal of different contaminants other than Phenol with continuous ozonation combined with bromate minimization strategies.
- Investigate other AOP techniques such as Ozone/hydrogen peroxide.
- Conduct a detailed kinetic investigation on the fate of added Ammonia.
- Perform proper identification and quantification of all by-products formed during ozonation to ensure no new contaminants are introduced.
