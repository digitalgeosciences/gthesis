# Iron Sulfide Scales Quantification Using Spectral Induced Polarization

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the use of Spectral Induced Polarization (SIP) to quantify iron sulfide scales and monitor weighting material sag in drilling fluids. It demonstrates that SIP signals, particularly real conductivity and impedance, are effective for detecting low concentrations of pyrite and monitoring the settlement of barite and calcite.

---

## Research Problem

The detection and quantification of iron sulfide (FeS) scales in limestone formations and the monitoring of weighting materials (barite/calcite) in drilling fluids are critical for maintaining production and ensuring well integrity, yet current methods lack high sensitivity or real-time capabilities.

## Objectives

- Utilize SIP to characterize pyrite concentration in limestone formations.
- Evaluate the detection limits of SIP for iron sulfide scales in a mixed mineral environment.
- Monitor drilling fluid weighting materials (barite and calcite) concentration and sag using SIP.
- Develop a method to detect weighing materials sag in short time periods under static, atmospheric conditions.
- Characterize the content of calcium carbonate in drilling fluids as an indicator of its role as a weighting material and potential scaling agent.
- Monitor the weighting material content (barite and calcium carbonate) in drilling fluid and characterize their sag in static conditions.

## Methods

The study utilized X-Ray Fluorescence (XRF) to confirm the purity of pyrite samples (>96% iron sulfide). SIP was employed as the primary detection tool, measuring four signals: phase shift, imaginary conductivity, real conductivity, and impedance. Samples included pyrite-limestone mixtures with varying concentrations (0% to 100%) and drilling fluids containing different amounts of calcite and barite. Real conductivity and impedance were used for concentration quantification, while time-lapse SIP measurements at 10 kHz were used to monitor the settlement (sag) of weighting materials in vertical columns.

## Data and Materials

7 different limestone samples with varying pyrite content (starting from 0.25% by bulk volume); 6 samples of bentonite drilling fluid with varying calcium carbonate content; 3 types of KCl buffer solutions for instrument calibration; and historical core flooding data for two cores (NF-1 and NI-1).

## Key Findings

- The SIP method successfully detected pyrite as low as 0.25% per bulk volume in a 17 cc sample chamber. ([[2023-badhafere|Badhafere, 2023, p. 15]])
- Pyrite concentration in limestone was found to be directly proportional to both the SIP phase shift and imaginary conductivity signals. ([[2023-badhafere|Badhafere, 2023, p. 15]])
- Calcium carbonate concentration in bentonite drilling fluid was successfully characterized using the real part of the SIP complex conductivity signal, showing a sharp decrease followed by a gradual increase with increasing concentration. ([[2023-badhafere|Badhafere, 2023, p. 16]])
- Barite and calcite sag were detected early (within 3 hours) via SIP, evidenced by a decrease in impedance and an increase in real conductivity signals over time. ([[2023-badhafere|Badhafere, 2023, p. 16]])
- Iron sulfide presence in limestone pores caused a reduction of approximately 68% in the initial permeability of core NF-1 (from 9 mD to 2.9 mD). ([[2023-badhafere|Badhafere, 2023, p. 51]])
- XRF analysis confirmed the pyrite sample was more than 96% pure iron sulfide (S: 70.64%, Fe: 23.83%). ([[2023-badhafere|Badhafere, 2023, p. 54]])
- Phase shift peaked at 316 Hz for all samples, indicating uniform particle size. ([[2023-badhafere|Badhafere, 2023, p. 57]])
- SIP successfully detected pyrite in limestone at low concentrations up to 0.25% in bulk volume using phase shift and imaginary conductivity. ([[2023-badhafere|Badhafere, 2023, p. 101]])

- Real conductivity and impedance can be used as qualitative measures for iron sulfide concentration but are not suitable for early detection due to potential interference from surrounding non-conductive particles. ([[2023-badhafere|Badhafere, 2023, p. 102]])
- Calcium carbonate in water-based sodium bentonite drilling fluid was successfully detected at concentrations as low as 0.1% per weight of the total fluid sample. ([[2023-badhafere|Badhafere, 2023, p. 102]])
- A sharp decrease in real conductivity was observed immediately upon adding 0.1% calcium carbonate to the system, demonstrating high sensitivity. ([[2023-badhafere|Badhafere, 2023, p. 102]])
- Barite and calcite sag were successfully detected in less than 5 hours using SIP, with real conductivity and impedance being the most sensitive parameters for tracking particle migration. ([[2023-badhafere|Badhafere, 2023, p. 103]])
- For barite drilling fluid, a 11.5 uS/cm drop in real conductivity and a 4.9 ohms increase in impedance were recorded during sag detection. ([[2023-badhafere|Badhafere, 2023, p. 103]])
- The system showed high sensitivity for real conductivity (0.5 uS/cm) and impedance (0.25%) for monitoring changes in drilling fluid components. ([[2023-badhafere|Badhafere, 2023, p. 103]])

## Limitations

The study notes that while SIP is highly sensitive, its interpretation depends on the specific mineral's electrical properties (e.g., pyrite as a semiconductor) and the concentration of these minerals in the matrix.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The results establish a basis for future development to expand the range of applications of the SIP method in both iron sulfide characterization and drilling fluid components monitoring.
