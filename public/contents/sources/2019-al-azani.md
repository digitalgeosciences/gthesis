# MITIGATION OF CO2 GRAVITY OVERRIDE BY SOLVENT BLENDING

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the mitigation of CO2 gravity override during enhanced oil recovery (EOR) by blending supercritical CO2 (scCO2) with organic solvents, specifically triethyl citrate (TEC) and ethyl benzoate (EB). Through core-flooding experiments on limestone samples under Saudi Arabian reservoir conditions, the study evaluates how these blends affect density, viscosity, and ultimate oil recovery.

---

## Research Problem

The primary challenge in CO2-EOR is gravity override, where the lower density of scCO2 relative to reservoir fluids causes it to rise to the top of the reservoir, leading to poor sweep efficiency and early breakthrough. Existing methods like foam or polymers are often unsuitable for high temperature/salinity environments.

## Objectives

- Identify and evaluate solvents capable of blending with CO2 to increase its density and viscosity.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of triethyl citrate (TEC) and ethyl benzoate (EB) as potential candidates for mitigating gravity override.
- Conduct core flooding experiments to compare pure scCO2, CO2-TEC blends, and CO2-hydrocarbon gas mixtures.
- Identify different solvents that can blend with scCO2 and produce blends with densities close to those of crude oil and water.
- To test the ability of the selected solvents to blend with CO2 and examine the effectiveness of the blends to minimize the effect of CO2 gravity override through core-flooding experiments.
- Identify different solvents that can blend with carbon dioxide to increase its density for the purpose of mitigating the effect of gravity override during scCO2 enhanced oil recovery process.

## Methods

The study involved four phases: (1) Literature survey for solvent identification; (2) Solubility experiments using a PVT 300/700 FV EDU system to determine saturation pressures and densities of CO2-solvent blends; (3) Core-flooding experiments on Indiana limestone samples under conditions of 4,000 psi confining pressure, 3,500 psi back pressure, and 100°C temperature; (4) Evaluation of the effectiveness of TEC and EB in reducing gravity override. Analytical techniques included HPLC for SARA analysis, GC for gas composition, and viscosity measurements using a Rolling-Ball viscometer.

## Data and Materials

The research utilized Indiana limestone core samples. Two types of fluids were tested: dead light crude oil from Saudi Arabia and live crude oil (crude oil blended with a mixture of methane, ethane, propanes, and butanes). Solubility experiments were conducted for the selected solvents (TEC and EB) in CO2.

## Key Findings

- The core flood using pure scCO2 on dead light crude yielded an oil recovery at breakthrough of 14.82% and a final recovery of 60.14%. ([[2019-al-azani|AL-AZANI, 2019, p. 18]])
- The core flood using a CO2-TEC blend (85 mol% CO2) on dead light crude increased the breakthrough recovery to 31.99% and final recovery to 72.67%. ([[2019-al-azani|AL-AZANI, 2019, p. 18]])
- In live oil experiments, pure scCO2 yielded a breakthrough of 15.19% and a final recovery of 49.13%. ([[2019-al-azani|AL-AZANI, 2019, p. 19]])
- In live oil experiments, the CO2-TEC blend (85 mol% CO2) yielded a breakthrough of 27.06% and a final recovery of 67.89%. ([[2019-al-azani|AL-AZANI, 2019, p. 19]])
- The results demonstrated that the addition of TEC successfully delayed gas breakthrough and increased total oil recovery in both dead and live crude systems. ([[2019-al-azani|AL-AZANI, 2019, p. 19]])
- Ethyl benzoate (EB) was excluded from core flooding tests because it showed high solubility in crude oil, requiring large amounts to achieve the desired density increase compared to TEC. ([[2019-al-azani|AL-AZANI, 2019, p. 21]])
- The average porosity of the limestone core samples was 18.91% with an average permeability of 267.99 mD. ([[2019-al-azani|AL-AZANI, 2019, p. 61]])
- The crude oil used had a density corresponding to 30.33 °API gravity and a viscosity of 2.69 cP at 100 °C. ([[2019-al-azani|AL-AZANI, 2019, p. 63]])
- Ethyl benzoate (EB) was found to be completely miscible with oil but showed high solubility in oil, leading to its exclusion from core-flooding tests to preserve blend integrity. ([[2019-al-azani|AL-AZANI, 2019, p. 78]])
- Triethyl citrate (TEC) showed only slight miscibility with oil and no solubility in distilled water or seawater. ([[2019-al-azani|AL-AZANI, 2019, p. 78]])
- The estimated Minimum Miscibility Pressure (MMP) for CO2 with dead crude oil was approximately 3,098 psia at 100 °C. ([[2019-al-azani|AL-AZANI, 2019, p. 86]])
- A core-flooding experiment using pure scCO2 to displace dead oil showed a baseline for comparison (Run 1-DO). ([[2019-al-azani|AL-AZANI, 2019, p. 89]])
- The study determined that the addition of TEC as a blending agent aims to produce a blend with density closer to crude oil to mitigate gravity override. ([[2019-al-azani|AL-AZANI, 2019, p. 57]])
- The CO2-TEC blend at 85 mol% CO2 had a viscosity of the same order as reservoir fluids, reducing the viscosity difference and potentially improving sweep efficiency. ([[2019-al-azani|AL-AZANI, 2019, p. 99]])
- At 100 °C and 90 mol% CO2, one barrel of TEC can blend with up to 14,000 SCF of CO2, while one barrel of EB can only blend with approximately 7,000 SCF. ([[2019-al-azani|AL-AZANI, 2019, p. 100]])

## Limitations

The study notes that PR-EOS overestimated saturation pressures for both blends at higher temperatures/concentrations, requiring tuning. Additionally, the short length of core samples made it difficult to accurately estimate the fraction of recovery from gas-oil miscibility versus displacement.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Conduct flooding experiments on sandstone samples and study physical/chemical interactions between TEC and rock.
- Investigate other potential solvents.
- Conduct flooding experiments under conditions promoting CO2-oil miscibility.
- Perform a simulation study to model laboratory results and forecast future work.
- Examine effectiveness of tested solvents with other gases for EOR.
- Implement advanced equations of state (CPA or SAFT) for better prediction of saturation pressures.
