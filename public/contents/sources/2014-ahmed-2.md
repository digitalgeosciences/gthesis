# A PARETO FRONTIER WELL PLACEMENT AND RATE OPTIMIZATION

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the optimization of well placement and operating rates in petroleum reservoirs using a multiobjective differential evolution algorithm to find Pareto optimal solutions. It compares this approach against weighted sum and single-objective methods across two synthetic reservoir models, concluding that coupled optimization yields superior results for balancing economic (NPV) and environmental (VIR) objectives.

---

## Research Problem

The challenge of balancing conflicting objectives—maximizing economic return (NPV) while minimizing environmental risk/impact (quantified as Voidage Imbalance Ratio, VIR)—during the simultaneous optimization of well placement and production rates in complex reservoir geometries.

## Objectives

- Examine the success of the multiobjective differential evolution algorithm with Pareto ranking.
- Use the proposed technique to find the optimum well placement.
- Use the same method to optimize the operating condition (well rates).
- Optimize well locations and operating rates simultaneously.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of a Pareto-based multiobjective differential evolution algorithm for well placement and rate optimization.
- Compare the Pareto-based approach against single-objective optimizations (NPV only, VIR only) and weighted sum techniques.

## Methods

The study utilizes a multiobjective differential evolution algorithm with Pareto ranking to optimize two objectives: NPV and VIR. The optimization is performed over three scenarios: (1) Well Placement Optimization (WPO), where only x-y coordinates are varied; (2) Well Rate Optimization (WRO), where only production/injection rates are varied; and (3) Well Placement and Rate Optimization (WPRO), where both are optimized simultaneously. Two synthetic reservoirs were used: a channel reservoir (75x75x2 gridblocks) and a distributed permeability reservoir (64x64x3 gridblocks). Economic parameters for NPV calculation include capital costs, oil prices, and operating costs. The algorithm uses a population size determined by problem dimensions, with mutation factor F=0.90 and crossover factor CR=0.90 over 300 generations.

## Data and Materials

Two synthetic reservoir models: (1) Channel Reservoir (75x75x2 gridblocks, 200ft x 200ft x 100ft per block) and (2) Distributed Permeability Reservoir (64x64x3 gridblocks). Both reservoirs contain undersaturated oil with a minimum well spacing of 10 acres. Simulation parameters include 18 producers and 12 injectors over a 12-year production period.

## Key Findings

- Multiobjective optimization (MOBJ) is necessary when objectives are conflicting, such as economic profit vs. environmental safety. ([[2014-ahmed|Ahmed, 2014, p. 18]])
- The Net Present Value (NPV) serves as the primary indicator of economic performance for field development. ([[2014-ahmed|Ahmed, 2014, p. 19]])
- The Voidage Replacement Ratio (VRR) is used as an indicator of environmental safety and reservoir pressure balance; a value close to unity (1.0) is ideal. ([[2014-ahmed|Ahmed, 2014, p. 19]])
- A variance in VRR leads to imbalances in reservoir pressure, potentially damaging the environment. ([[2014-ahmed|Ahmed, 2014, p. 19]])
- The Differential Evolution (DE) algorithm is characterized by its robustness, simplicity, and fast convergence compared to other stochastic methods. ([[2014-ahmed|Ahmed, 2014, p. 28]])
- Pareto ranking allows for the identification of a set of non-dominated solutions rather than a single global optimum, providing more options for decision-makers. ([[2014-ahmed|Ahmed, 2014, p. 18]])
- In the channel reservoir (Scenario 3), the Pareto-based approach for joint optimization (WPRO) yielded an NPV of 11.3 x 10^9 and a VIR of 0.0049, outperforming both single-objective and weighted sum methods. ([[2014-ahmed|Ahmed, 2014, p. 82]])
- In the distributed permeability reservoir (Scenario 1), the Pareto-based approach provided multiple optimal solutions; the best NPV solution yielded 4.792 x 10^9 while the best VIR solution yielded 2.473 x 10^9. ([[2014-ahmed|Ahmed, 2014, p. 91]])
- The Pareto-based approach for well rate optimization (Scenario 2) in the channel reservoir resulted in an NPV of 9.965 x 10^9 and a VIR of 0.0278. ([[2014-ahmed|Ahmed, 2014, p. 75]])
- The inclusion of environmental factors (VIR) significantly impacts the resulting NPV values compared to 'NPV-only' optimization cases. ([[2014-ahmed|Ahmed, 2014, p. 68]])
- For Example 1, Scenario 3, the Pareto-based technique provided a set of three superior solutions for joint well placement and rate optimization. ([[2014-ahmed|Ahmed, 2014, p. 81]])
- The coupled optimization of well placement and rate (WPRO) yielded better results than optimizing each component individually in terms of both NPV and VIR values. ([[2014-ahmed|Ahmed, 2014, p. 109]])
- In Scenario 2, the Pareto-based approach for multi-objective optimization provided five superior solutions forming a Pareto front where all solutions were optimal in some sense for both environmental and economic criteria. ([[2014-ahmed|Ahmed, 2014, p. 98]])
- For Example 2, Scenario 3 (coupled), the Pareto-based approach yielded an NPV of 9.249 (best) and a VIR of 0.026 (best), significantly outperforming the Base Case (NPV 2.392, VIR 0.3379). ([[2014-ahmed|Ahmed, 2014, p. 104]])
- The Pareto-based approach for Scenario 2 produced a best NPV of 8.734 and a best VIR of 0.0348. ([[2014-ahmed|Ahmed, 2014, p. 98]])

## Limitations

The text notes that traditional 'weighted sum' methods require prior knowledge to determine appropriate weights for each objective, which is often unavailable in real-world scenarios; the Pareto approach addresses this by providing a range of optimal solutions.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Incorporate variable costs and prices that vary with time; include horizontal and multilateral wells; extend research to determine optimal operating rates over the entire field life; determine the optimum number of production and injection wells.
