# A STUDY OF THE ADSORPTION/DESORPTION OF METHANE/CARBON DIOXIDE ON THE SURFACE OF DIFFERENT RESERVOIR ROCKS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the adsorption and desorption behavior of methane (CH4) and carbon dioxide (CO2) on various rock types, including carbonate, tight sandstone, and two shale samples (SH1 and SH2). The study concludes that CO2 has a higher affinity for these rocks than CH4, making it potentially useful for enhanced gas recovery (EGR) applications.

---

## Research Problem

Standard adsorption models like Langmuir often fail to accurately represent the complex behavior of gas interaction with heterogeneous rock surfaces in diverse reservoir types (carbonate, sandstone, shale), leading to potential errors in estimating initial gas in place and modeling production decline.

## Objectives

- Study methane adsorption isotherm for different reservoir rock types at different temperatures to obtain a comprehensive understanding of how the rock system behaves at different conditions.
- Study the competitive adsorption between CO2 and CH4 with different mixture of CO2 and CH4 and at different temperatures.
- Determine adsorption capacity of CH4 and CO2 on carbonate, sandstone, and shale rocks at varying temperatures (50, 100, 150°C).
- Evaluate the impact of mineralogy (e.g., illite content) on desorption behavior.
- Quantify thermodynamic parameters (Gibbs free energy, enthalpy, entropy) for adsorption processes.
- Assess the potential of CO2 injection for Enhanced Gas Recovery (EGR).

## Methods

The study utilized three rock samples: Pink Desert limestone (carbonate), Kentucky tight sandstone, and two shale gas rocks. Samples were prepared as 0.5 cm3 cubes. Characterization was performed using XRD for mineralogy and SEM-EDS for surface analysis. Adsorption/desorption experiments were conducted using a Rubotherm magnetic suspension balance to measure weight changes under varying pressure (up to 50 bar) and temperature (50, 100, and 150°C). Data fitting was performed using MATLAB and Excel to compare Langmuir, Freundlich, and BET models.

## Data and Materials

Samples included Pink Desert limestone (carbonate), Kentucky sandstone (tight sandstone), and two shale samples (SH1 and SH2) from the Qusaiba field in Saudi Arabia. Measurements were taken at 50, 100, and 150°C with pressures up to 50 bar.

## Key Findings

- CO2 adsorption on carbonate was significantly higher than CH4 at all temperatures (e.g., 190 mg/g for CO2 vs 48 mg/g for CH4 at 50°C). ([[2016-elnaeem|Elnaeem, 2016, p. 46]])
- Adsorption of methane on Pink Desert limestone decreased from 50 mg/g at 50°C to 12.4 mg/g at 150°C. ([[2016-elnaeem|Elnaeem, 2016, p. 39]])
- Adding 10% CO2 to methane increased adsorption from 23 mg/g (pure CH4) to 30 mg/g (10% CO2 mixture) at 100°C. ([[2016-elnaeem|Elnaeem, 2016, p. 39]])
- Langmuir isotherm model failed to accurately represent the adsorption behavior of the studied rocks, while Freundlich and BET models provided superior fits with higher R² values (e.g., R² = 0.99 for BET at 150°C). ([[2016-elnaeem|Elnaeem, 2016, p. 46]])
- The average pore size of the Pink Desert limestone was 50-300 microns, with a porosity of 25%. ([[2016-elnaeem|Elnaeem, 2016, p. 44]])
- XRD analysis showed Pink Desert limestone is composed of 95% calcite and 5% dolomite. ([[2016-elnaeem|Elnaeem, 2016, p. 44]])
- Hysteresis in the CO2/CH4 mixture (10%) was observed, where less gas was desorbed than adsorbed at high temperatures, potentially due to non-uniform carbonate pores or reversible interactions. ([[2016-elnaeem|Elnaeem, 2016, p. 48]])
- At 50°C, the adsorption of CO2 on Pink Desert limestone was significantly higher than CH4 (143.1 mg/g vs 27.75 mg/g). ([[2016-elnaeem|Elnaeem, 2016, p. 65]])
- The addition of 10% CO2 to methane increased the adsorption on carbonate rock from 23 mg/g to 30 mg/g at 100°C. ([[2016-elnaeem|Elnaeem, 2016, p. 54]])
- In Kentucky sandstone, the presence of illite caused a significant increase in adsorption uptake due to thermal-induced structural changes and expulsion of bound water above 120°C. ([[2016-elnaeem|Elnaeem, 2016, p. 69]])
- For SH1 shale, CO2 content increased total gas uptake by approximately 33% at 150°C compared to pure methane. ([[2016-elnaeem|Elnaeem, 2016, p. 56]])
- SH2 shale showed no CH4 adsorption at 50°C due to high TOC blocking access to the surface, but showed significant uptake of CO2 (85 mg/g) at both 50°C and 100°C. ([[2016-elnaeem|Elnaeem, 2016, p. 84]])
- The Freundlich isotherm was identified as the best fit for experimental data in most cases, while BET was more accurate than Langmuir. ([[2016-elnaeem|Elnaeem, 2016, p. 67]])
- Negative values of ΔHads confirmed the exothermic nature of physical adsorption for both CH4 and CO2. ([[2016-elnaeem|Elnaeem, 2016, p. 53]])
- CO2 adsorption on the studied rocks was approximately 4-5 times higher than that of CH4. ([[2016-elnaeem|Elnaeem, 2016, p. 98]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study suggests that low to mid-range temperature reservoirs are preferred for CO2 injection due to the high affinity of carbonate rocks toward CO2.
