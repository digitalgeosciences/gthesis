# OIL WELL CEMENT WITH CARBON NANOTUBE FOR HIGH PRESSURE HIGH TEMPERATURE APPLICATIONS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the mechanical and physical properties of oil well cement (OWC) modified with varying concentrations of carbon nanotubes (CNT) for use in high- pressure, high-temperature (HPHT) environments. The study concludes that adding 0.1% to 0.5% CNT improves compressive strength, reduces porosity and permeability, and enhances rheological properties compared to standard Class G cement.

---

## Research Problem

Standard Class G cement undergoes significant degradation in compressive strength and experiences issues like free water separation when exposed to extreme HPHT conditions (>230°F). There is a need for additives that can stabilize the slurry and maintain integrity in deep well environments.

## Objectives

- Examine the significance of Carbon nanotube incorporation in Type G Oil well cement (OWC) pastes.
- Investigate the performance of Carbon Nanotube admixture in controlling the properties of oil well cement slurries under severe conditions.
- Determine the most favorable slurry design based on results from different percentages of CNT used in experiments.
- Evaluate the effect of carbon nanotubes (CNT) on the compressive strength of oil well cement under HPHT conditions.
- Analyze the microstructural changes in cement resulting from CNT addition.
- Assess the rheological properties and free water separation of CNT-reinforced cement slurries.

## Methods

The study utilizes a variety of laboratory tests following API Specifications to evaluate cement properties. These include: 1) Thickening Time Test using an HPHT Consistometer; 2) Density measurement using a pressurized mud balance; 3) Rheology measurements (plastic viscosity, yield point, and gel strength) using an HPHT Viscometer; 4) Free Water Separation tests in graduated cylinders; 55) Compressive Strength testing via the crushing method in an HPHT curing vessel; 6) Microstructural analysis using Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) and X-ray Diffraction (XRD). The cement used is API Class G high sulfate-resistant oil well cement, with various concentrations of carbon nanotubes added to the mix.

## Data and Materials

The study uses API Class G oil well cement (specific gravity 3.14) and a variety of chemical additives including silica flour (35%), expanding agents, retarders, and fluid loss control agents. Carbon nanotubes are tested at concentrations of 0.1%, 0.25%, and 0.5% by weight of cement (BWOC). The study references a typical Saudi Arabian well with a depth of 14,000 ft, BHCT of 228°F, and BHST of 290°F.

## Key Findings

- Carbon nanotubes are characterized by a high surface area-to-volume ratio, which leads to an increasing dominance of surface forces over body forces, potentially enhancing mechanical properties like strength and heat resistance. ([[2015-khan|Khan, 2015, p. 26]])
- The study identifies that cement slurry must have a thickening time of more than 11 hours before over-retardation significantly lengthens the cement transition time. ([[2015-khan|Khan, 2015, p. 49]])
- At temperatures above 230°F, Portland cement undergoes significant phase changes resulting in a substantial decrease in compressive strength and an increase in permeability (strength retrogression). ([[2015-khan|Khan, 2015, p. 35]])
- Carbon nanotubes (CNT) are reported to have moduli of elasticity exceeding 1 TPa. ([[2015-khan|Khan, 2015, p. 51]])
- The study evaluates the impact of CNT on thickening time, free water separation, rheological properties, compressive strength, density, and porosity/permeability. ([[2015-khan|Khan, 2015, p. 31]])
- The addition of 0.5% or 1% wt MWCNTs significantly reduced the number of mesopores with diameters around 10 nm-50 nm in cement paste. ([[2015-khan|Khan, 2015, p. 54]])
- Research indicated that there is up to 70% increase in the compressive strength with the addition of 0.05 wt% of carbon nanotubes to Portland cement. ([[2015-khan|Khan, 2015, p. 55]])
- The inclusion of nano-materials like silica or CNTs leads to a more uniform microstructure with reduced pore size and volume, increasing resistance to chemical attack. ([[2015-khan|Khan, 2015, p. 56]])
- Multi-walled carbon nanotubes (MWCNTs) can reach a tensile strength of 63 GPa, which is approximately 100 times stronger than steel. ([[2015-khan|Khan, 2015, p. 69]])
- The average flexural strength reported for MWCNTs fixed as a cantilever beam was 14.2 ± 8.0 GPa. ([[2015-khan|Khan, 2015, p. 69]])
- Addition of lower concentrations of CNT (0.1% and 0.25%) acted as a retarder, increasing thickening time compared to the base mix. ([[2015-khan|Khan, 2015, p. 91]])
- The 0.5% CNT system showed accelerated hydration, achieving its thickening time in only 2 hours (1 hour faster than the base mix). ([[2015-khan|Khan, 2015, p. 91]])
- 70Bc was identified as the 'right angle set' point where slurry becomes unpumpable. ([[2015-khan|Khan, 2015]])

- The addition of CNT resulted in higher early compressive strength; specifically, the 0.1% CNT system provided high compressive strength after 48 hours. ([[2015-khan|Khan, 2015, p. 105]])

## Limitations

The results are restricted to the specific well conditions, cement type (Portland Saudi 'G'), chemical additives, CNT percentages used, and the specific slurry preparation/testing procedures described in the study.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Investigate CNT effects in shallow applications with low pressure and temperature; test different water-to-cement ratios; investigate other nanomaterials like nano-alumina, nano-clay, nano-silica, and nano-zeolite; explore combinations of different nanomaterials for HPHT cement.
