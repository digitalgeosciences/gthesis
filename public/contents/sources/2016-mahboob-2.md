# Interfacial Tension and Contact Angle Measurements of CO2/Brines/Surfactants/Oil Systems with Dolomite Rock

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the effects of surfactant type, salinity, temperature, pressure, and oil type on interfacial tension (IFT) and wettability (contact angle) in systems where CO2, brine, surfactants, and oil coexist with dolomite rock. It concludes that specific combinations, particularly using fluorosurfactants, can significantly reduce IFT and improve wettability for enhanced oil recovery.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the need to understand interfacial phenomena—specifically IFT reduction and wettability alteration—in multi-component systems (CO2, brine, surfactants, and oil) to optimize recovery in SAG processes. It specifically investigates how factors like salinity, pressure, and surfactant type (particularly fluorosurfactants) affect these properties.

## Objectives

- Study the interfacial properties of CO2, Brines containing surfactants and oil coexisting.
- Study the effect of brine salinity, temperature, pressure, temperature, surfactant type, and oil type on interfacial tension and wettability.
- Measure interfacial tension (IFT) between CO2 and various brines at different salinities, temperatures, and pressures.
- Evaluate the effect of three types of surfactants (Alfoterra L167-4S 90, Armovis EHS, FS-50) on IFT in a CO2-brine system.
- Determine the impact of surfactant-laden brine on the interfacial tension between oil and CO2-saturated fluids.
- Measure contact angles of various oils on Guelph dolomite under different pressure and temperature conditions to assess wettability.

## Methods

The study utilized an IFT700 apparatus for measuring both IFT and contact angle. The Drop Shape Analysis (DSA) technique, specifically the Young-Laplace method, was used to calculate values from video frames. Density measurements were performed using a DMA-4500 with a DMA-HP accessory. Brines were prepared as synthetic sea water at four different salinities (67708 ppm to 8463 ppm). Three surfactants (Alfoterra L167-4S 90, Armovis EHS, and FS-50) were tested. The study included a series of experiments: 1) CO2 with four types of brines; 2) CO2 with brine-surfactant solutions (BSS); 3) CO2 saturated with BSS and various oils; 4) Contact angle measurements on Guelph dolomite core samples.

## Data and Materials

The study used Guelph Dolomite rock samples prepared as disks. Fluids included CO2, four brines (Brine 1-4), three brine-surfactant solutions (BSS1-3), and five oils (Crude Oil 1, Crude Oil 2, Squalane, Toluene, Pentadecane). Measurements were taken at temperatures of 30°C, 60°C, and 90°C across a pressure range of 0 to 6000 psi.

## Key Findings

- IFT of brines increases with increasing salinity and temperature, but decreases with increasing pressure due to the solubility of CO2 in brine. ([[2016-mahboob|Mahboob, 2016, p. 17]])
- The fluorosurfactant produced the most soluble solution and yielded ultra-low IFT with CO2 when used with oil, forming pressure-sensitive micro-emulsions. ([[2016-mahboob|Mahboob, 2016, p. 17]])
- contact angle of oils with dolomite rock increased with pressure in the presence of fluorosurfactant and CO2. ([[2016-mahboob|Mahboob, 2016]])
- page_number_placeholder_for_finding_on_page_17_or_26 ([[2016-mahboob|Mahboob, 2016]])
- The study identified that Brine 4 (8463 ppm) exhibited the lowest interfacial tension with CO2 among the four tested brines. ([[2016-mahboob|Mahboob, 2016, p. 78]])
- Fluorosurfactant FS-50 was found to be effective at a concentration of up to 0.15% before reaching a plateau in its ability to reduce IFT. ([[2016-mahboob|Mahboob, 2016, p. 79]])
- The study confirmed that the Young-Laplace method provides accurate results for both high and low contact angles (up to 180 degrees) when used with DSA. ([[2016-mahboob|Mahboob, 2016, p. 56]])
- A significant reduction in oil/water contact angle was observed at a brine concentration of 10,000 ppm in previous studies cited by the author. ([[2016-mahboob|Mahboob, 2016, p. 46]])
- The IFT of crude oil in a CO2 atmosphere significantly decreases but shows higher values at higher temperatures (80°C vs 40°C). ([[2016-mahboob|Mahboob, 2016, p. 49]])
- IFT for brine systems was found to be independent of pressure and temperature for high-salinity Brine 1 at specific conditions, while lower salinity brines showed IFT increases with temperature and decreases with pressure. ([[2016-mahboob|Mahboob, 2016, p. 92]])
- The addition of surfactant FS-50 (fluorosurfactant) achieved ultra-low IFT values compared to other surfactants like Alfoterra and Armovis. ([[2016-mahboob|Mahboob, 2016, p. 109]])
- Minimum Miscibility Pressure (MMP) for Brine 1 was calculated as 17,707 psi at 30°C, 46,990 psi at 60°C, and 21,204 psi at 90°C. ([[2016-mahboob|Mahboob, 2016, p. 106]])
- MMP for Brine 4 was determined to be 11,231 psi at 30°C, 12,685 psi at 60°C, and 30,040 psi at 90°C. ([[2016-mahboob|Mahboob, 2016, p. 106]])
- Contact angle measurements on Guelph Dolomite showed a significant reduction in oil wettability when using BSS3 compared to plain brine. ([[2016-mahboob|Mahboob, 2016, p. 128]])
- Squalane, as a model oil, exhibited an IFT that decreased with pressure until approximately 3000-4500 psi before showing signs of potential phase inversion or solubility effects. ([[2016-mahboob|Mahboob, 2016, p. 116]])

## Limitations

Some experiments were unsuccessful due to high solubility of certain oils in CO2 (e.g., Toluene, Pentadecane) or extreme turbidity/foam formation at high temperatures (90°C), which obscured the view for camera-based measurements.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Study IFT with oils of different asphaltenes percentage and aromatics in the presence of CO2 and FS-50.
- Expand pressure and temperature ranges for the Oil/Brine/Surfactant/CO2/Dolomite system.
- Investigate the effect of surfactant concentration on micro-emulsions.
- Perform molecular simulation of contact angle experiments to study thin-film layers near rock surfaces.
