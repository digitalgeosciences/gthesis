# SEISMIC ATTRIBUTES AND ADVANCED COMPUTER ALGORITHM TO PREDICT FORMATION PORE PRESSURE: QALIBAH FORMATION OF NORTHWEST SAUDI ARABIA

**Summary**: The thesis investigates a novel method for predicting formation pore pressure in the Qalibah Formation of Northwest Saudi Arabia using Support Vector Machine (SVM) algorithms applied to multiple 3D seismic attributes. The study concludes that combining several seismic attributes, particularly Vp/Vs and P-Impedance, through SVM successfully isolates pore-pressure signals from lithological and fluid effects.

---

## Research Problem

Predicting pre-drill pore pressure is critical for safety and well design; however, standard velocity-based methods are often unreliable due to the influence of lithology and fluid content. There is a lack of research utilizing advanced machine learning (SVM) and seismic attributes specifically for the Qalibah Formation in Northwest Saudi Arabia.

## Objectives

- Investigate the utility of the Support Vector Machine (SVM) algorithm to learn patterns of high pore pressure regimes.
- Utilize a suite of seismic attributes and well attributes as training data for SVM.
- Predict pore pressure trends across the 3D seismic survey area.
- Focus on techniques that combine seismic attributes with advanced pattern recognition software (Support Vector Machine) to predict pore pressure.
- Identify the best combination of well and seismic attributes to reliably predict formation pore pressure.
- Evaluate the accuracy of SVM in predicting reservoir properties from computed seismic attributes.
- To evaluate various 3D seismic attributes for their ability to indicate pore pressure.
- To implement a Support Vector Machine (SVM) algorithm to establish relationships between 3D seismic attributes and actual formation pore pressure.
- To differentiate pore-pressure-affected regimes from other rock properties like lithology and hydrocarbon content.
- Utilize pattern recognition algorithms (SVM) to learn the signature of over-pressure sediments on seismic data.
- Predict pore pressure conditions away from control points using learned seismic signatures.
- Develop a method to determine appropriate mud weights for drilling safety and identifying fluid migration.

## Methods

The study utilizes eight specific 3D seismic attributes: Instantaneous Frequency, Average Frequency, Reflection Intensity, t* Attenuation, Cosine of Phase, Vp/Vs, P-Impedance, and Reflective Acoustic Impedance. These are processed to create volumes where the SVM algorithm is trained using well data (mud-weight curves) from three training wells (Well-1, Well-2, and Well-3). The SVM model uses a kernel function to map complex data into higher dimensions for better classification. A blind test well (Well-4) is used to validate the prediction by comparing the predicted mud-weight profile against actual drilling logs.

## Data and Materials

The study utilizes 3D seismic attribute volumes (Instantaneous Frequency, t*Attenuation, Cosine of Phase, Vp/Vs ratio, P-Impedance, Reflection Acoustic Impedance, and Dominant frequency) and well data from at least four wells (three training wells: Well-1, Well-2, & Well-3; one blind test well: Well-4).

## Key Findings

- The pore pressure trend in the Qalibah formation can be predicted using SVM with a combination of seismic and well attributes. ([[2013-mark|Mark, 2013, p. 12]])
- SVM successfully identified subtle variations of pressure within the thick succession of shale units of the Qalibah formation. ([[2013-mark|Mark, 2013, p. 12]])
- The study utilized seven specific seismic attributes: Instantaneous phase, t*Attenuation, Cosine of Phase, Vp/Vs ratio, P-Impedance, Reflection Acoustic Impedance, and Dominant frequency. ([[2013-mark|Mark, 2013, p. 11]])
- The Qusaiba Member is characterized by overpressure conditions that may have resulted from hydrocarbon generation within the formation and surrounding beds, forming a bounding seal against hydrocarbon migration. ([[2013-mark|Mark, 2013, p. 50]])
- The Qusaiba Member is the source rock of over 90% of the Paleozoic hydrocarbons found in the region and also serves as a seal to the underlying Ordovician reservoirs. ([[2013-mark|Mark, 2013, p. 44]])
- The Qusaiba Member contains 256 m of organic-rich, layered shale at its type section. ([[2013-mark|Mark, 2013, p. 48]])
- The Qusaiba 'Hot Shale' is shown to be in the lower 70 meters of the W-1 well, exhibiting a sharp increase in gamma radiation typical of organic-rich facies. ([[2013-mark|Mark, 2013, p. 50]])
- The Vp/Vs ratio had the highest correlation index of 0.431195 with mud-weight indicators, making it a primary driver in the SVM prediction. ([[2013-mark|Mark, 2013, p. 86]])
- P-Impedance showed a significant correlation index of 0.291844 for pore pressure prediction. ([[2013-mark|Mark, 2013, p. 86]])
- Other attributes like Reflection Intensity (0.057) and Cosine of Phase (0.041) showed much lower correlation to mud-weight. ([[2013-mark|Mark, 2013, p. 86]])
- The SVM prediction successfully identified the pressure contrast between the high-pressure Qalibah Formation and the hydrostatically pressured Sarah Formation. ([[2013-mark|Mark, 2013, p. 98]])
- Well-4, located approximately 7 km from training wells, showed a mud-weight increase to over 100 ppcf in the lower Qalibah formation. ([[2013-mark|Mark, 2013, p. 94]])
- The SVM algorithm successfully predicted the mud-weight for Well-4, showing a very close correlation to the actual mud-weight used during drilling. ([[2013-mark|Mark, 2013, p. 104]])
- SVM is superior to other algorithms like Neural Networks because it avoids over-fitting of data around control points. ([[2013-mark|Mark, 2013, p. 103]])
- The Qusaiba shale member (bottom section of the Qalibah Formation) has been shown to contain abnormally high pore pressure in wells drilled in the area. ([[2013-mark|Mark, 2013, p. 103]])

## Limitations

The author noted an inability to find a suitable empirical equation for the direct conversion of mud-weight values to pore pressure, resulting in the study predicting mud-weight rather than absolute pore pressure.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future research should focus on developing well data that continuously measure pore pressure and finding better methods to convert predicted mud-weight volumes into pore pressure volumes.
