# DEPOSITIONAL CONTROLS ON CO2 STORAGE IN CARBONATE RESERVOIRS: FROM SUBSURFACE TO SIMULATION

**Summary**: The thesis investigates how depositional facies and stratigraphic heterogeneity influence CO2 trapping mechanisms (residual, dissolution, and structural) in the Upper Kharaib carbonate reservoir. Using a combination of mercury injection capillary pressure (MICP) analysis and 3D reservoir simulations, it concludes that heterogeneous reservoirs with low-permeability baffle zones significantly increase residual and dissolution trapping while reducing structural trapping.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the challenge of predicting CO2 migration and trapping efficiency in carbonate reservoirs where depositional heterogeneity impacts pore throat size and permeability. Specifically, it evaluates how different facies (e.g., Shoal-Inner Ramp vs. Middle Ramp) and internal baffle zones affect the distribution of trapped CO2.

## Objectives

- To perform adequate geological characterizations on carbonate reservoir model of the Lower Cretaceous Upper Kharaib Member from subsurface open data COSTA Model (2022).
- To gain insights on the impact of depositional heterogeneity in determining the potential CO2 storage sites and potential trapping mechanisms in heterogeneous carbonate reservoirs.
- To propose a set of preliminary geological criteria for carbon storage in carbonates based on its facies distribution.
- To Model the impact of CO2 Injection (Field Scale) on reservoir heterogeneity, Subsurface Dynamics and Potential Trapping Mechanisms Profile in the Upper Kharaib Carbonate Reservoirs – Deep saline aquifer case.
- Evaluate the impact of depositional facies on reservoir quality (porosity and permeability) in the Upper Kharaib formation.
- Identify optimal lithofacies for CO2 storage based on petrophysical properties and trapping potential.

## Methods

The study employs a two-part methodology: 1. Reservoir Characterization: Includes stratigraphy/depositional analysis, well log data analysis (GR, density, neutron-density, sonic), core analysis, and petrophysical calculations (Windland r35 equation to determine pore throat radius). 2. Reservoir Simulation & Modeling: Three cases were modeled using CMG-GRID and Petrel software. Case 1 is a Layer Cake model; Case 2 includes internal baffle zones (0.1 mD) for sensitivity analysis; Case 3 is a 3D Geological Model incorporating facies distribution from well data. Simulations used the CMG-GEM software with PITZER models for high salinity, rock geochemical reaction modules, and relative permeability hysteresis.

## Data and Materials

The study utilizes data from the Upper Kharaib Member (Thamama Group) carbonate reservoirs. It compares three simulation cases: Case 1 (layer cake), Case 2 (layer cake with baffles), and Case 3 (3D geological model). Data includes porosity (10-30%), permeability (0.1-2000 mD), and mercury injection capillary pressure profiles.

## Key Findings

- Four distinct facies associations (FA1-FA4) were identified: FA1 (Shoal) and FA2 (Inner Ramp) have average thickness of 60 ft, porosity 20-35%, and permeability 50-1000 mD. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 16]])
- FA3 (Middle Ramp) covers approximately 60% of the UKM's total thickness with average net thickness of 100 ft, porosity 10-25%, and permeability 5-50 mD. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 16]])
- FA4 (Outer Ramp) has an average thickness of 10 ft, with the poorest reservoir quality (porosity < 5% and permeability < 1 mD). ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 16]])
- Simulation results showed increased calcite dissolution, lower reservoir pH, and higher reservoir pressure due to CO2 injection. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 16]])
- Residual trapping is generally more significant in the Middle Ramp Facies (FA3), while structural trapping is more prevalent in the Upper Ramp and Shoal Facies (FA1, FA2). ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 16]])
- The Upper Kharaib Member exhibits a thickness variation ranging from 140 to 220 feet. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 39]])
- FA1 (Inner Shoal-Shoal) exhibits the highest reservoir quality with porosity of 25-30% and permeability of 10-1000 mD, making it the most ideal target for structural trapping. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 60]])
- FA2 (Upper Ramp) shows moderate petrophysical properties (porosity 20-35%, permeability 1-100 mD) and is suitable for a combination of residual and structural trapping. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 68]])
- FA3 (Middle Ramp) has lower permeability but significant thickness (up to 150 ft), making it an attractive target for residual trapping due to its potential for mineral dissolution-enhanced permeability. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 68]])
- A negative correlation of -0.86 was found between Bulk Density (RHOB) and Porosity, confirming that higher density rocks correlate with lower porosity. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 62]])
- Case 1 simulation showed maximum gas saturation of approximately 0.88 after 200 years. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 70]])
- In Case 3, the high-resolution model showed that structural trapping was reduced by 15.4% in FA3 compared to FA1 at the end of the monitoring period. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 98]])
- Maximum calcite dissolution values were recorded as FA1=-2.57e8, FA2=-3.27e8, and FA3=-3.6e8 gmole in Case 1. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 72]])
- The maximum fraction of CO2 dissolved in brine reached 1.1% in Case 1 and up to 2.5% in Case 2. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 83]])
- A significant correlation (0.61) was observed between Bulk Density (RHOB) and Gamma Ray (GR), indicating lithological variations. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 63]])

## Limitations

The study is limited to considering only stratigraphic and facies heterogeneity; the presence of faults and fractures was not included in the analysis due. Consequently, it may not fully capture complexities introduced by structural features in real-world scenarios.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Improve data quality by obtaining additional capillary pressure data to reduce reliance on assumptions.
- Conduct a detailed seal integrity study to identify risks of CO2 leakage.
- Perform geomechanical studies for each rock type/lithofacies to ensure safety and wellbore stability.
- Determine maximum injection rates for different lithofacies to optimize strategy.
- Incorporate faults and fractures into future models to capture full structural complexities.
