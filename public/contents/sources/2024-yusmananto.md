# DEPOSITIONAL CONTROLS ON CO2 STORAGE IN CARBONATE RESERVOIRS: FROM SUBSURFACE TO SIMULATION

**Summary**: The thesis investigates how depositional facies and stratigraphic heterogeneity influence CO2 trapping mechanisms (residual, dissolution, and structural) in carbonate reservoirs, specifically the Upper Kharaib Member. It uses simulation to compare different reservoir models and concludes that heterogeneous formations provide higher volumes of residual and dissolution trapping.

---

## Research Problem

The primary challenge is understanding how inherent depositional heterogeneity and post-depositional diagenesis in carbonate reservoirs (specifically the Upper Kharaib Member) affect CO2 containment, trapping mechanisms, and subsurface dynamics during injection.

## Objectives

- Investigate the impact of depositional heterogeneity on identifying CO2 storage sites and trapping mechanisms in heterogeneous carbonate reservoirs.
- Propose preliminary geological criteria for carbon storage based on facies distribution.
- Model the effects of CO2 injection at a field scale on subsurface dynamics within the Upper Kharaib Carbonate Reservoirs, particularly in a deep saline aquifer context.
- To perform adequate geological characterizations on carbonate reservoir model of the Lower Cretaceous Upper Kharaib Member from subsurface open data COSTA Model (2022).
- To gain insights on the impact of depositional heterogeneity in determining the potential CO2 storage sites and potential trapping mechanisms in heterogeneous carbonate reservoirs.
- To propose a set of preliminary geological criteria for carbon storage in carbonates based on its facies distribution.
- To Model the impacts of CO2 Injection (Field Scale) on reservoir heterogeneity, Subsurface Dynamics and Potential Trapping Mechanisms Profile in the Upper Kharaib Carbonate Reservoirs – Deep saline aquifer case.
- Characterize the reservoir using stratigraphy, well log data, and core analysis to identify facies associations.
- Evaluate the impact of geological heterogeneity on CO2 migration patterns.
- Compare three different modeling cases (Layer Cake, Layer Cake with barriers, and 3D Geological Model) to assess how structural and lithological variations affect CO2 storage.
- Evaluate the correlation between grain texture, rock types, and petrophysical properties in the Upper Kharaib reservoir.
- Assess the impact of different depositional facies associations on CO2 trapping mechanisms using simulation.
- Quantify mineral dissolution (calcite) and pH changes resulting from CO2 injection across various lithofacies.
- Evaluate the impact of depositional facies on reservoir quality (porosity, permeability) in the Upper Kharaib reservoir.
- Identify suitable rock types for CO2 storage based on petrophysical properties and trapping mechanisms.
- Model the behavior of CO2 plumes and geochemical reactions post-injection using numerical simulation.
- Assess the effectiveness of different trapping mechanisms (structural, residual, solubility) in specific carbonate facies.
- Evaluate the impact of depositional facies on reservoir quality and CO2 storage potential.
- Assess the role of stratigraphic and facies heterogeneity in influencing CO2 distribution and trapping.
- Analyze the effect of internal baffle layers (low permeability zones) on CO2 plume migration and pressure profiles.
- Compare different simulation models (homogeneous vs. heterogeneous) to quantify trapping mechanisms.
- Evaluate the impact of reservoir heterogeneity on CO2 plume patterns and trapping mechanisms.
- Model potential migration routes of CO2 plumes in carbonate reservoirs.
- Investigate how depositional facies influence CO2 storage capacity and behavior.

## Methods

The study is divided into two main workflows: Reservoir Characterization and Reservoir Simulation. Characterization includes stratigraphy/depositional analysis, well log data analysis (GR, density, neutron, sonic), core porosity-permeability cross-plots, and the Windland r35 equation to determine pore throat radius. Simulation involves three cases: Case 1 (Layer Cake) with uniform horizontal properties; Case 2 (Layer Cake & 0.1 mD Baffle layers) to test the impact of low-permeability barriers; and Case 3 (3D Geological Model) using Petrel for spatial distribution of facies and properties. Simulation was performed using CMG-GRID and CMG-GEM, incorporating PITZER models for high salinity, rock geochemical reaction models, and relative permeability hysteresis.

## Data and Materials

Data includes well logs from the Upper Kharaib reservoir, core plug data showing correlation between porosity and permeability, and simulation results for three cases over a 200-year period. The study references specific carbonate facies associations (FA1, FA2, FA3) and their corresponding petrophysical properties.

## Key Findings

- Four distinct facies associations were identified: FA1 (Shoal) and FA2 (Inner Ramp) in the upper part of the reservoir show excellent properties (porosity 20-35%, permeability 50-1000 mD), making them suitable for structural trapping. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 16]])
- FA3 (Middle Ramp) covers approximately 60% of the UKM's total thickness with moderate to low reservoir quality (porosity 10-25%, permeability 5-50 mD), offering potential for residual/capillary trapping. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 16]])
- FA4 (Outer Ramp) is the thinnest average thickness (10 ft) with the poorest reservoir quality (porosity < 5%, permeability < 1 mD). ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 16]])
- Simulation results indicate that CO2 injection leads to increased calcite dissolution, lower reservoir pH levels, and higher reservoir pressure. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 16]])
- Residual trapping is significantly more prevalent in the Middle Ramp Facies (FA3) compared to other facies. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 16]])
- Structural trapping is more prevalent in the Upper Ramp and Shoal Facies (FA1, FA2). ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 16]])
- Case 3 (3D Geological Model) provides a more complex representation of heterogeneity compared to Case 1 (Layer Cake) and Case 2 (Layer Cake with baffles). ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 100]])
- The FA3 depositional units (middle to lower part of the reservoir) cover approximately 60% of the total thickness of the Upper Kharaib Member, with average net thickness of 100 ft and porosity ranging from 10-25% and permeability between 5-50 mD. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 19]])
- The FA4 depositional units are thinner (average 10 ft) and exhibit the lowest reservoir quality, with porosity below 5% and permeability below 1 mD. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 19]])
- Simulation results indicate an increase in calcite dissolution, a decrease in the reservoir's pH profile, and an increase in reservoir pressure following CO2 injection. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 19]])
- trapping mechanisms are generally higher in the middle slope (FA3) layers, while structural trapping is more prevalent in the upper slope and bank layers. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024]])

- The Upper Kharaib Member is divided into four Facies Associations: FA1 (Inner Shoal), FA2 (Upper Ramp), FA3 (Middle Ramp), and FA4 (Outer-ramp to basinal). ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 56]])
- FA1 shows high quality with porosity of 25-30% and permeability of 10-1000 mD. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 59]])
- FA4 exhibits the lowest quality, with a porosity range of 0.1% to 11% and permeability of 0.001 to 2 mD. ([[2024-yusmananto|Yusmananto, 2024, p. 60]])

## Limitations

The study notes that access to subsurface data in the Middle East is often limited by confidentiality concerns, necessitating the use of available open data or partially synthetic datasets for academic analysis.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Improve data quality by obtaining more comprehensive datasets like capillary pressure.
- Conduct a detailed seal integrity study to mitigate risks of CO2 leakage.
- Perform geomechanical studies for each rock type to ensure storage security and wellbore stability.
- Determine maximum injection rates for different lithofacies to optimize strategy.
