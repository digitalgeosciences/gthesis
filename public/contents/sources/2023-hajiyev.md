# Effect of Live-Crude-Oil, GOR, and HPHT on Optimal Salinity of EOR Surfactant Formulation

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of live crude oil, gas-oil ratio (GOR), and high-pressure/high-temperature (HPHT) conditions on the optimal salinity of surfactant formulations for enhanced oil recovery. It concludes that while pressure significantly affects solubilization ratios, it has a negligible effect on optimal salinity, whereas GOR inversely correlates with the range of microemulsion formation.

---

## Research Problem

Existing literature provides conflicting data regarding the impact of live-oil, GOR, and HPHT on optimal salinity in surfactant systems; specifically, many studies use dead or synthetic oils at atmospheric pressure, which may lead to inaccurate predictions for actual reservoir conditions where gas is present and pressures are high.

## Objectives

- Perform Salinity Screening experiments at high temperature and ambient pressure to determine the optimum surfactant formulation range.
- Conduct High-Pressure and High-Temperature (HPHT) Phase Behavior experiments with Live Crude Oil containing methane.
- Investigate the effect of methane on Phase Behavior by testing three Live Oil samples at GOR values of 180 scf/stb, 280 scf/stb, and 380 scf/stb.
- Explore the effect of pressure on each experiment with various GOR values across a range of 2500 psi to 4000 psi.
- Evaluate the impact of live crude oil versus dead crude oil on microemulsion phase behavior.
- Determine the effect of Gas-Oil Ratio (GOR) on the optimal salinity and solubilization ratios.

## Methods

The study utilized salinity screening experiments at atmospheric pressure to identify suitable surfactant formulations that exhibit Type III microemulsion behavior. These formulations were then subjected to HPHT Phase Behavior experiments using live oil containing methane (at GORs of 180, 280, and 380 scf/stb) in high-pressure rated sapphire cells. The investigation included a pressure range of 2500 psi to 4000 psi to evaluate the impact of gas content and pressure on solubilization ratios and optimal salinity.

## Data and Materials

The study used Light Saudi crude oil (LS), deionized water, seawater, and formation brine. Samples were tested at temperatures of 25°C, 50°C, and 90°C. Live crude samples were prepared with GORs of 180, 280, and 380 scf/stb.

## Key Findings

- High-temperature salinity screening identified surfactant formulations showing Type III microemulsion, suitable for HPHT Phase Behavior studies. ([[2023-hajiyev|Hajiyev, 2023, p. 12]])
- Pressurizing the surfactant system increases water solubilization ratio and decreases oil solubilization ratio, meaning more water is solubilized with the surfactant. ([[2023-hajiyev|Hajiyev, 2023, p. 13]])
- The presence of methane contributed to better microemulsion generation compared to dead crude oil, indicating lower interfacial tension between water and oil. ([[2023-hajiyev|Hajiyev, 2023, p. 13]])
- Optimal solubilization ratios increased and optimal salinity decreased with increasing GOR. ([[2023-hajiyev|Hajiyev, 2023, p. 13]])
- A target of an optimal solubilization ratio (SR) of 10 or more is used for microemulsion phase behavior screening to ensure low interfacial tension. ([[2023-hajiyev|Hajiyev, 2023, p. 32]])
- The study confirmed that live oil systems exhibit larger solubilization ratios and lower IFT values compared to dead oil systems. ([[2023-hajiyev|Hajiyev, 2023, p. 49]])
- Live oil provides lower IFT values than dead crude, making it easier to displace in experiments. ([[2023-hajiyev|Hajiyev, 2023, p. 50]])
- The optimal salinity (S*) is influenced by the Equivalent Alkane Carbon Number (EACN), where higher EACN leads to a shift toward water-in-oil microemulsions. ([[2023-hajiyev|Hajiyev, 2023, p. 52]])
- Pressure has a negligible effect on the value of optimal salinity (S*) but significantly impacts the solubilization ratios (SR) of oil and water. ([[2023-hajiyev|Hajiyev, 2023, p. 97]])
- Increasing GOR leads to an increase in optimum SR and a reduction in optimal salinity (S*). ([[2023-hajiyev|Hajiyev, 2023, p. 112]])

## Limitations

The study's scope for high GOR (380 scf/stb) was limited by the fact that no microemulsion was observed in the tested salinity range (up to 17283 ppm), suggesting optimal salinity for such cases lies at higher concentrations.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future work should include core flooding experiments to confirm surfactant efficiency, investigate different crude oil types, and analyze the impact of mineralogy on recovery performance.
