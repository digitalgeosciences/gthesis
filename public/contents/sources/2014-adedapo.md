# Spatial Distribution of the Levels of Trace Metals in the Topsoil of Dammam Area, Eastern Province of Saudi Arabia

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the spatial distribution and concentration of trace metals in topsoil samples across various zones (agricultural, industrial, residential, and background) in the Dammam area. Using geochemical mapping and sieve analysis, it identifies 'hot spots' of contamination and evaluates soil suitability for human health and environmental safety.

---

## Research Problem

Rapid urbanization and industrialization in the Dammam area have led to potential trace metal contamination of topsoil from sources like fossil fuel combustion and industrial waste. There is a lack of specific data regarding these concentrations and their spatial distribution in the region.

## Objectives

- To ascertain the concentration of trace metals in the topsoil of selected zones in Dammam area.
- To assess the spatial distribution of trace metals in the topsoil and identify "hot spots" areas.
- Produce geochemical maps of trace metals concentration
- Identify areas of high metal concentration ('hot spots') in the topsoil of Dammam area
- Identify areas of high metal concentration 'hot spots' in the topsoil of Dammam area
- Assess the spatial distribution of trace metals across different land-use zones

## Methods

The study involved collecting 132 representative topsoil samples from various zones (agricultural, industrial, residential, and background). Grain size distribution was determined to classify soil types using the Unified Soil Classification System. Trace metal concentrations for eleven elements (As, Ba, Cd, Cr, Cu, Hg, Ni, Pb, Ti, V, Zn) were analyzed. Spatial mapping was performed by recording coordinates with handheld GPS, importing data into AutoCAD to overlay on maps, and utilizing Surfer 8 to generate concentration maps. Finally, ArcGIS 10.0 was used to overlay these concentrations onto satellite imagery.

## Data and Materials

132 representative topsoil samples from the Dammam area; analytical data for 11 trace metals including Arsenic, Barium, Cadmium, Chromium, Copper, Mercury, Nickel, Lead, Titanium, Vanadium, and Zinc.

## Key Findings

- Mean concentration levels of trace metals were: Arsenic (As) (1.21±0.69 mg/kg), Barium (Ba) (110.62±262 mg/kg), Cadmium (Cd) (1.18±4.33 mg/kg), Chromium (Cr) (31.79±37.9 mg/kg), Copper (Cu) (6.76±12.54 mg/kg), Mercury (Hg) (0.08±0.18 mg/kg), Nickel (Ni) (8.95±5.66 mg/kg), Lead (Pb) (6.34±14.55 mg/kg), Titanium (Ti) (119.27±85.74 mg/kg), Vanadium (V) (9.98±6.08 mg/kg), and Zinc (Zn) (23.44±84.43 mg/kg). ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 12]])
- The levels of As, Ba, Hg, Pb, Ni, and V were within the international allowable limits usually found in topsoil. ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 12]])
- Cd, Cr, Cu, and Zn exceeded the allowable limits with industrial and residential areas having the highest concentrations. ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 12]])
- Elevated levels of target metals in some locations may be attributed to atmospheric deposition and related to activities capable of discharging such pollutants. ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 13]])
- Cadmium (Cd) levels in world topsoils vary between 0.07 and 1.1 mg/kg, with values above 0.5 mg/kg indicating anthropogenic sources. ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 24]])
- The average concentration of Cd in European topsoil was reported as 0.03-0.8 mg/kg with a median value of 0.14 mg/kg. ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 25]])
- Note: The specific results for the Dammam area samples are not fully detailed in the provided text excerpt, which focuses on literature review and background values. ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014]])
- Lead (Pb) is considered a multimedia pollutant; its concentration in soil can range from 10 mg/kg to 30 mg/kg. ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 31]])
- Mercury (Hg) content in soil is reported to range from 20 µg/kg to 625 µg/kg, with higher levels typically found in urban soils and the top 5 cm of the surface. ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 32]])
- Arsenic (As) is a metalloid that can be an industrial byproduct of smelting for Cu, Pb, and Zn. ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 33]])
- The study identified four distinct sampling zones: residential (R), industrial (ID), agricultural (AG), and background (BG). ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 38]])
- Agricultural Sample 1 (AG1) was classified as a poorly graded sand with silt (GP) based on a Trask sorting coefficient of 2.51, Cu=5.4, and Cc=0.8. ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 48]])
- ICP-OES was utilized to detect metals at concentrations as low as one part in 10^12 (part per trillion). ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 50]])
- Arsenic (As) was found in higher abundance in the agricultural area compared to all other sampling locations, potentially due to its use in pesticides and feed additives. ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 71]])
- Barium (Ba) levels exceeded the 2000 mg/kg limit for industrial areas, attributed to its use in various industrial activities like glassmaking and electronics. ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 74]])

## Limitations

The study did not assess soil pH or redox potential at the sampling locations, which are factors that influence the retention and mobility of metals like Chromium (Cr).

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Collaborate between KFUPM, other universities, and government agencies to establish reliable guidelines for Saudi Arabia.
- Extend assessment to trace metals in plants and air.
- Conduct seasonal studies (winter vs. summer) on metal levels.
- Investigate metal concentration with depth and their interaction with regional soil layers.
- Identify specific sources of the trace metals.
