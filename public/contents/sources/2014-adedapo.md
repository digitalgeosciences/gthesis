# Spatial Distribution of the Levels of Trace Metals in the Topsoil of Dammam Area, Eastern Province of Saudi Arabia

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the spatial distribution and concentration of trace metals (As, Ba, Cd, Cr, Cu, Hg, Ni, Pb, Ti, V, Zn) in topsoil samples across agricultural, industrial, residential, and background zones in the Dammam area. Using ICP-OES for chemical analysis and GIS/AutoCAD for mapping, the study concludes that while most metals fall within acceptable limits, specific 'hot spots' of concern exist in certain industrial and agricultural zones.

---

## Research Problem

The potential threat to human health and ecological systems posed by the accumulation of toxic trace metals in topsoil from various sources in the Dammam area, Eastern Province, Saudi Arabia.

## Objectives

- To ascertain the concentration of trace metals in the topsoil of selected zones in Dammam area.
- To assess the spatial distribution of trace metals in the topsoil and identify "hot spots" areas.
- Produce geochemical maps of trace metals concentration
- Identify areas of high metal concentration 'hot spots' in the topsoil of Dammam area
- Assess the contamination levels of trace metals across different land-use zones (agricultural, industrial, residential, and background)

## Methods

Topsoil samples were collected using an auger and georeferenced via GPS. Samples were air-dried, sieved through a nylon mesh to remove debris, and subjected to sieve analysis to determine grain size distribution. Trace metal concentrations were determined using ICP-OES following the USEPA method 3050B for acid digestion (HNO3, HCl, H2O2). Spatial mapping was performed by importing GPS coordinates into AutoCAD and then utilizing Surfer 8 to generate concentration maps, which were finally overlaid onto satellite imagery using ArcGIS 10.0.

## Data and Materials

132 representative topsoil samples collected from 4 zones (residential, industrial, agricultural, and background) in the Dammam area; chemical reagents including nitric acid (HNO3), hydrochloric acid (HCl), and hydrogen peroxide (H2O2); equipment included a Spectro Ciros Vision ICP-OES, Auger, GPS, and mechanical sieve shaker.

## Key Findings

- Mean concentration levels of trace metals: Arsenic (As) (1.21±0.69 mg/kg), Barium (Ba) (110.62±262 mg/kg), Cadmium (Cd) (1.18±4.33 mg/kg), Chromium (Cr) (31.79±37.9 mg/kg), Copper (Cu) (6.76±12.54 mg/kg), Mercury (Hg) (0.08±0.18 mg/kg), Nickel (Ni) (8.95±5.66 mg/kg), Lead (Pb) (6.34±14.55 mg/kg), Titanium (Ti) (119.27±85.74 mg/kg), Vanadium (V) (9.98±6.08 mg/kg), and Zinc (Zn) (23.44±84.43 mg/kg). ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 12]])
- Levels of As, Ba, Hg, Pb, Ni, and V were within the international allowable limits typically found in topsoil. ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 12]])
- Concentrations of Cd, Cr, Cu, and Zn exceeded the allowable limits, with industrial and residential areas exhibiting the highest concentrations. ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 12]])
- Elevated levels of trace metals in some locations were attributed to atmospheric deposition and activities capable of discharging such pollutants. ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 12]])
- Arsenic (As) was found in higher abundance in agricultural areas compared to all other sampling locations, potentially due to its use in pesticides and feed additives. ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 71]])
- Barium (Ba) showed elevated levels in industrial samples, with some exceeding the 2000 mg/kg limit for industrial areas. ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 74]])
- Cadmium (Cd) exceeded the allowable limit in two specific locations: sample 6 in residential area and sample 4 in industrial area. ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 77]])
- Chromium (Cr) showed elevated levels in several industrial samples (13, 14, 15, 17, 18, and 30) and some residential samples (5, 18, 24). ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 79]])
- Mercury (Hg) levels were found to be within the allowable limits across all sampled zones. ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 83]])
- Nickel (Ni) was within limits in background and residential areas but showed higher concentrations in agricultural and industrial samples. ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 85]])
- Lead (Pb) and Titanium (Ti) were found to be within the allowable range across all sampled zones. ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 87]])
- Vanadium (V) showed no hot spots, with all samples falling within the acceptable limit of 130 mg/kg. ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 91]])
- Zinc (Zn) exceeded the allowable limit in some industrial samples due to potential industrial release of Zn-containing materials. ([[2014-adedapo|Adedapo, 2014, p. 93]])

## Limitations

The study did not assess soil pH or redox potentials, which are critical factors for determining the mobility and retention of metals like Cr, Ni, and Hg in the soil.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Collaborate with other universities and agencies to establish official national guidelines for trace metals in Saudi Arabian soil.
- Extend assessment to include metal concentrations in plants and air.
- Conduct seasonal studies (winter vs. summer) on metal levels.
- Investigate the depth of metal penetration into the soil profile.
- Identify specific sources of trace metal contamination.
