# AN INNOVATIVE APPLICATION OF CHELATING AGENTS FOR EOR IN CARBONATE RESERVOIRS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the use of chelating agents, specifically EDTA (ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid), as a novel chemical enhanced oil recovery (EOR) method for carbonate reservoirs. It evaluates how these agents can improve oil recovery by altering rock wettability toward water-wet and reducing interfacial tension while preventing scale formation.

---

## Research Problem

Carbonate reservoirs often exhibit poor oil recovery due to being neutral to oil-wet, which hinders the efficiency of standard waterflooding. Existing chemical EOR methods like surfactant flooding are complex or expensive, and some alternatives like high-pH alkali floods can cause severe scaling issues in carbonate systems.

## Objectives

- Investigate the potential of EDTA as a new EOR chemical fluid in seawater solution through flooding process.
- To better understand the mechanism leading to incremental oil recovery by low salinity (LowSal) waterflooding.
- Optimize the additional recovery using different concentrations of EDTA.
- Study the effect of different chelating agents such as EDTA, HEDTA, and BCA solutions on the seawater injection.
- Providing the first step towards implementation of the newly proposed method on industrial scale.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of EDTA as a chelating agent to prevent calcium sulfate precipitation during seawater flooding.

## Methods

The study utilized a custom-designed coreflooding apparatus capable of handling temperatures up to 150°C and pressures up to 3000 psi. Indiana limestone core plugs (porosity 15-21%, permeability 70-100 mD) were prepared, saturated with formation brine, and aged at 90°C for two weeks in crude oil. Core flooding tests were conducted using several fluids: raw seawater (SSW), low-salinity water, and various concentrations of chelating agents (3% and 5% NH4EDTA). The effluent from the coreflooding experiments was analyzed using Ion Chromatography (IC) to quantify concentration levels of Ca2+, Mg2+, and SO4(2-) ions.

## Data and Materials

The study used Indiana limestone cores (e.g., 6-inch length, 1.5-inch diameter). Experimental parameters included injection rates of 0.25 cc/min to 0.5 cc/min and temperatures of 100°C. Various concentrations of EDTA (1%, 5%, 10%) and HEDTA were tested.

## Key Findings

- Chelating agents with high pH (>12) have the ability to reduce interfacial tension (IFT), which leads to a higher capillary number, a key parameter in EOR. ([[2013-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2013, p. 16]])
- The proposed method using chelating agents can prevent the precipitation of metal sulfates and scale formation by sequestering cations in the solution. ([[2013-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2013, p. 21]])
- Chelating agents like EDTA are effective at forming wormholes in limestone even at moderate, non-acidic pH (4-13) and low flow rates where HCl is ineffective. ([[2013-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2013, p. 33]])
- EDTA can be used as a stable chelating agent for various metal ions including Ca(II), Mg(II), Fe(III), and Cu(II). ([[2013-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2013, p. 40]])
- The study notes that the proposed EDTA method is simpler and less expensive than surfactant flooding as it does not require complex laboratory testing for field design. ([[2013-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2013, p. 21]])
- Seawater injection into core #5 resulted in an oil recovery of 67.99% after 13 PV of seawater. ([[2013-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2013, p. 69]])
- IC analysis for seawater flooding showed no ion exchange as Ca2+ and Mg2+ concentrations remained constant in the effluent. ([[2013-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2013, p. 69]])
- 3 wt% NH4EDTA solution provided only an additional 8.93% recovery over seawater, with IC analysis showing no dissolution of calcium from the rock matrix. ([[2013-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2013, p. 71]])
- 5 wt% NH4EDTA solution resulted in a significant additional recovery of 20.16% compared to seawater. ([[2013-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2013, p. 73]])
- The 5% EDTA concentration successfully dissolved precipitated sulfate and promoted rock dissolution, as evidenced by Ca2+ concentrations in the effluent being 600 ppm higher than the injected fluid. ([[2013-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2013, p. 73]])
- Diluting seawater ten times increased the pressure drop by a factor of 5 to 10 due to the reduction in calcium sulfate solubility. ([[2013-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2013, p. 53]])
- 5 wt% EDTA injected at Swi resulted in an earlier recovery increase over seawater, with a recovery factor of approximately 72% compared to 58% for continuous seawater injection. ([[2013-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2013, p. 74]])
- One pore volume of 5 wt% H3HEDTA (pH=2.5) produced enough CO2 to recover more than 80% of the residual oil. ([[2013-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2013, p. 77]])
- H2Na2EDTA at pH 4.5, in continuous injection, increased oil recovery by more than 90% from initial oil in place. ([[2013-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2013, p. 77]])
- EDTA was the best chelating agent in preventing sulfate scale and enhanced core permeability from 80 mD to 100 mD (at 5 wt%) and from 83 mD to 177 mD (at 10 wt%). ([[2013-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2013, p. 81]])

## Limitations

The study used Indiana limestone core plugs as a proxy for carbonate reservoirs; specific field-scale geological complexities were not addressed.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The industry should look for new, cost-effective and environment-friendly low pH chelating agents compatible with seawater at a wide range of concentrations for EOR and in-situ CO2 generation in carbonate reservoirs.
