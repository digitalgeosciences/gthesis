# Investigation of the production performance of horizontal wells in fractured bottom water drive reservoirs

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the production performance of horizontal wells in a bottom water drive reservoir, comparing various wellbore geometries and fracture configurations (longitudinal, single orthogonal, and multiple orthogonal). It concludes that fractures significantly improve oil recovery and delay water breakthrough compared to unfractured horizontal wells.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the challenge of managing water breakthrough and optimizing oil recovery in fractured reservoirs under bottom water-drive conditions, where gravity and capillary forces significantly influence fluid movement between matrix blocks and fractures.

## Objectives

- Investigate experimentally and theoretically the production performance of horizontal wells without and with fractures in bottom water-drive reservoirs.
- Compare the performance of horizontal wells with that of fractured and unfractured vertical wells.
- Analyze the impact of fracture parameters (penetration ratio, extension ratio) on recovery and breakthrough.
- Provide a basis for determining the best production strategy for such types of reservoirs.
- Evaluate the effect of wellbore geometry on oil recovery and water breakthrough.
- Investigate the impact of fracture orientation (longitudinal vs. orthogonal) on production performance.

## Methods

A 3D physical model was constructed using plexiglass to simulate a section of a bottom-water drive reservoir. The model included an oil zone, a water aquifer, and a perforated plate for communication between zones. A variety of well configurations were tested: vertical wells (VW), vertical wells with vertical fractures (VWF), horizontal wells (HW), horizontal wells with longitudinal fractures (HWLF), and horizontal wells with single or multiple orthogonal fractures (HWOF). Displacement was performed by injecting water into the bottom aquifer at a constant rate while producing from the wells. Pressure, flow rates, and volumes were monitored using pumps, transducers, and chart recorders.

## Data and Materials

The study utilizes a series of comparative models including: 1. Horizontal wells with zero, one, two, three, and four orthogonal fractures; 2. Vertical wells with vertical fractures (FVW); 3. Horizontal wells with single-orthogonal fractures (FHW/SOF); 4. Horizontal wells with longitudinal fractures (FHW/LF). Parameters such as fracture width (0.181 in), depth (2 in), and extension (10-16 in) are used to calculate contact areas.

## Key Findings

- Horizontal wells with greater contact area provide more production before water breakthrough and experience lower pressure drop compared to vertical wells. ([[1993-kandil|Kandil, 1993, p. 10]])
- Fractured horizontal wells performed better than non-fractured horizontal wells, which in turn were superior to vertical wells. ([[1993-kandil|Kandil, 1993, p. 10]])
- Very deep fractures extending toward the water zone improved production performance. ([[1993-kandil|Kandil, 1993, p. 10]])
- Long horizontal wells at reasonable spacings can provide performances equivalent to or better than arrays of closely spaced vertical wells. ([[1993-kandil|Kandil, 1993, p. 10]])
- The critical flow rate for a horizontal well is higher than that of a vertical well, allowing it to stay below the coning threshold more easily. ([[1993-kandil|Kandil, 1993, p. 35]])
- A variable-diameter horizontal well offers superior performance compared to constant diameter wells by reducing resistance and improving sweep efficiency. ([[1993-kandil|Kandil, 1993, p. 29]])
- Perforated casings can significantly impede flow of heated oil/steam and lead to high steam-oil ratios (SOR). ([[1993-kandil|Kandil, 1993, p. 29]])
- Sweep efficiency for horizontal wells increases with increasing well length (L_D) for a fixed well spacing. ([[1993-kandil|Kandil, 1993, p. 46]])
- Increasing the well length for a fixed well spacing results in delaying water breakthrough. ([[1993-kandil|Kandil, 1993, p. 46]])
- The study found that horizontal wells with longitudinal fractures can provide significant production benefits in low-permeability reservoirs when properly oriented relative to stress fields. ([[1993-kandil|Kandil, 1993, p. 76]])
- Transverse fractures are not recommended for high permeability formations as they may not be effective if the fracture conductivity is below a certain threshold (specifically, a value of 5). ([[1993-kandil|Kandil, 1993, p. 73]])
- The model showed that higher initial water saturations (26%-36%) were obtained in the 3D model compared to 16.5% in linear models due to the nature of two-phase flow and streamline convergence. ([[1993-kandil|Kandil, 1993, p. 102]])
- The study determined that horizontal wells are particularly beneficial for thin formations and wide spacing, where they can increase production rates without increasing wellhead pressure. ([[1993-kandil|Kandil, 1993, p. 80]])
- Horizontal wells offer advantages in CO2 flooding by delaying breakthrough due to better sweep efficiency and maintaining minimum miscibility pressure (MMP). ([[1993-kandil|Kandil, 1993, p. 81]])
- Unfractured horizontal wells (UHW) achieved 58% OOIP at water breakthrough compared to only 23% for unfractured vertical wells (UVW). ([[1993-kandil|Kandil, 1993, p. 109]])

## Limitations

The study is based on a scaled physical model using glass beads and kerosene, which may not perfectly replicate the complexities of real-world reservoir fluids and rock types.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study suggests that a production strategy using horizontal wells with relatively deep fractures (FPR of 0.3 to 0.5) is economically feasible as it can produce most oil before water breakthrough, potentially eliminating the need for costly surface facilities for handling produced water.
