# EXPERIMENTAL STUDY OF THE ALKALINE-SURFACTANT-GAS (ASG) FLOODING IN A CARBONATE RESERVOIR

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the performance of Alkaline-Surfactant-Gas (ASG) flooding in carbonate reservoir cores using different surfactants (FS-50, FS-51), alkaline additives (NaOH), and gas injection to improve oil recovery. The study concludes that surfactant FS-51 outperforms FS-50 and that adding alkali does not significantly improve recovery compared to the standard ASG process.

---

## Research Problem

Carbonate reservoirs often have low permeability and contain vugs and fractures, making traditional polymer-based EOR methods less effective or impractical. There is a need for an alternative mobility control agent like foam to improve displacement efficiency in these specific geological settings.

## Objectives

- To evaluate the effect of surfactant type on the recovery of oil during ASG process.
- To carry out the aqueous stability and phase behavior tests to determine the stability of chemicals at a given salinity (21,600 ppm) and temperature (60°C).
- To study the foamability and the stability of the foam and select the favorable surfactant for ASG process.
- To carry out coreflooding experiments and determine the effectiveness of ASG process as potential EOR technique.
- Studying the effect of the permeability, alkaline, and type of surfactants on the performance of the ASG process.
- Determine the optimal concentration for surfactants to ensure stable foam formation.

## Methods

The study involved aqueous stability tests to evaluate surfactant solubility/stability in electrolyte solutions across varying salinities. Phase behavior tests were conducted to determine oil- and water-solubility and identify micro-emulsion types (e.g., Winsor Type I). Foam stability was assessed to select optimal surfactants for the ASG process. Coreflooding experiments were performed on limestone rocks to evaluate oil recovery, pressure response, and effluent characteristics under different conditions including varying permeability, alkaline addition, and surfactant types.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized 2 types of limestone cores: high permeability (IL-005, ~202 md) and low permeability (IL-013, ~2.05 md). Four distinct experimental setups (ASG1-4) were conducted using various surfactant concentrations (0.6 wt%), alkali additions (NaOH), and different surfactants (FS-50, FS-51).

## Key Findings

- Maximum recovery of 47.7% of residual oil in place in low permeability was observed compared to 43% of residual oil in place on high permeability. ([[2013-geri|Geri, 2013, p. 17]])
- In the Arabic summary, a higher recovery rate of 74.4% of remaining oil was reported for low permeability cases, and 74% for high permeability cases (Note: discrepancy between English/Arabic abstracts). ([[2013-geri|Geri, 2013, p. 19]])
- It was observed that the phase behavior is oil-in-water micro-emulsion (Winsor Type I Behavior). ([[2013-geri|Geri, 2013, p. 16]])
- Lower interfacial tension (IFT) can be obtained at high solubility ratio for the optimum salinity concentration. ([[2013-geri|Geri, 2013, p. 16]])
- The addition of alkaline preflush protects synthetic surfactants from precipitation by removal of divalent ions and reduces surfactant adsorption. ([[2013-geri|Geri, 2013, p. 27]])
- Coreflooding pressure data showed that the pressure drop increased during slug injection but decreased as soon as the drive injection started, then followed an increasing trend until reaching steady state due to trapped surfactant. ([[2013-geri|Geri, 2013, p. 30]])
- Lower gas velocity results in more stable foam and thus better foam propagation. ([[2013-geri|Geri, 2013, p. 31]])
- The presence of surfactants significantly reduces gas permeability at higher liquid velocities compared to cases without surfactant. ([[2013-geri|Geri, 2013, p. 50]])
- Capston FS-61 was found to be insoluble in brine at low concentrations due to precipitation, leading to its exclusion from phase behavior tests. ([[2013-geri|Geri, 2013, p. 72]])
- Tergitol NP-9 and Triton X-100 showed strong, stable foam for over 1 hour at a concentration of 1.0 wt% in high-salinity brine. ([[2013-geri|Geri, 2013, p. 71]])
- Capston FS-50 and Capston FS-51 both exhibited optimal foam stability and volume at a concentration of 0.6 wt%. ([[2013-geri|Geri, 2013, p. 79]])
- The optimum salinity for high oil solubilization was determined to be 36,000 ppm. ([[2013-geri|Geri, 2013, p. 82]])
- Core IL-005 exhibited an absolute permeability of 202.38 md and a starting oil saturation (Soi) of 75.7%. ([[2013-geri|Geri, 2013, p. 91]])
- The initial oil recovery was 63% using water flooding, which increased to 75% after foam injection with Capston FS-51. ([[2013-geri|Geri, 2013, p. 98]])
- Foam injection with Capston FS-51 resulted in an additional 12% oil recovery (representing 43% of the remaining oil in place). ([[2013-geri|Geri, 2013, p. 98]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Use live oil instead of dead oil for future experiments.
- Study the adsorption and reactions of surfactants with rock surfaces.
- Test selected foam agents on sandstone rocks.
- Evaluate surfactant performance at higher salinities and temperatures.
- Conduct tests using heavy oil samples.
- Investigate the effect of aging on recovery.
