# Characterization of Fluid Drainage Mechanism at Core and Pore Scales

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the drainage mechanisms in multimodal rock systems (macropores and micropores) using NMR T2 measurements, capillary pressure, and electrical resistivity. It evaluates how surfactant treatment affects these displacement mechanisms and identifies specific patterns such as series, parallel, and series-parallel drainage based on petrophysical properties.

---

## Research Problem

The complexity of carbonate reservoirs due to multimodal pore systems makes it difficult to apply standard petrophysical equations. There is a need to understand the drainage mechanism at both core and pore scales to optimize EOR, gas injection, and storage.

## Objectives

- Investigate the drainage mechanism in multimodal rock systems using NMR, Pc, and RI.
- Evaluate the effect of surfactant on electrical resistivity index and capillary pressure curves for a surfactant enhanced gas injection and storage in water-wet reservoirs.
- Develop a workflow to probe the drainage mechanism in multimodal rocks and determine the contribution of different pore systems.
- Analyze various rock types (carbonates, sandstones, and dolomites) with differing petrophysical properties.
- Identify the drainage mechanism in multimodal pore systems and evaluate the effect of surfactant on the displacement mechanisms.
- Carry out a pore scale analysis and characterize the drainage mechanism in each pore type (macropore and micropore).

## Methods

The study utilized nine rock samples (sandstones, carbonates, and dolomites) with varying petrophysical properties. Samples were cleaned using solvent reflux (toluene/methanol), dried in a vacuum oven, and saturated with either synthetic brine or surfactant-laden brine. Capillary pressure was measured using a porous plate desaturator. NMR T2 relaxation times were measured using an NMR Geospec 2.1 rock analyzer to determine pore saturation. Electrical resistivity index curves were obtained via an electrical resistivity meter (EPS A Vinci). CT scanning was used on specific samples (e.g., D-17) to analyze internal heterogeneity and pore structure.

## Data and Materials

The study analyzed multiple rock samples including carbonates, sandstones, and dolomites with varying petrophysical properties (porosity, permeability). Experimental setups included a porous plate desaturator for gas/nitrogen displacement and surfactant solution saturation to evaluate the impact of surfactants on drainage profiles.

## Key Findings

- The drainage pattern correlates with the ratio of macro and micro pores (pore type dominance), rock permeability, and pore connectivity. ([[2020-isah|Isah, 2020, p. 15]])
- A workflow was developed to break down the capillary pressure (Pc) curve into Pc for macropores and Pc for micropores. ([[2020-isah|Isah, 2020, p. 15]])
- The study found that the drainage mechanism in multimodal rocks is influenced by the interplay between macropore and micropore systems. ([[2020-isah|Isah, 2020, p. 50]])
- NMR T2 relaxation times are used to determine petrophysical properties including effective porosity, free fluid saturation, and irreducible water saturation. ([[2020-isah|Isah, 2020, p. 49]])
- The study confirms that surfactant treatment can significantly change the water-oil or water-gas interfacial tension, leading to a reduction in capillary pressure for reservoir pore systems. ([[2020-isah|Isah, 2020, p. 17]])
- Foam formation from surfactant and gas leads to the differentiation of flow between high permeability macropores and low permeability micropores. ([[2020-isah|Isah, 2020, p. 48]])
- Three drainage mechanisms were identified: series, parallel, and combined (series-parallel), depending on the petrophysical properties of the rock. ([[2020-isah|Isah, 2020, p. 49]])
- Sample NG (sandstone) and others like B-18 and C showed a series drainage mechanism where macropores are drained before micropores. ([[2020-isah|Isah, 2020, p. 50]])
- Samples D-17 and S-26 exhibited a series-parallel pattern, transitioning from series at low pressures to parallel as pressure exceeded the micropore entry point (approx. 15 psi). ([[2020-isah|Isah, 2020, p. 50]])
- A multimodal rock system dominated by micropores with moderate permeability and connectivity showed a parallel drainage pattern. ([[2020-isah|Isah, 2020, p. 70]])
- Systems where macropores are the predominant pore type with high permeability and well-connected systems tended to drain in series. ([[2020-isah|Isah, 2020, p. 70]])
- Surfactant treatment (Triton X-100) resulted in higher produced fluid volumes from micropores compared to gas-brine displacement. ([[2020-isah|Isah, 2020, p. 69]])
- The addition of surfactant caused the drainage pattern of sample B-11 to shift from parallel to series-parallel. ([[2020-isah|Isah, 2020, p. 69]])
- CT imaging revealed that heterogeneity (e.g., vugs and fractures) in sample D-17 led to a V-shaped saturation profile at the center of the core. ([[2020-isah|Isah, 2020, p. 72]])
