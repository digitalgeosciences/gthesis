# Identifying Optimum Conditions for Stable Wormholes Created by Chelating Agents

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the optimal conditions for creating stable wormholes in Austin Chalk and Indiana Limestone core samples using various chelating agents (HEDTA, DTPA, EDTA) at different concentrations and pH levels. It evaluates these conditions based on normalized volume and time to breakthrough, comparing experimental results with predictive models.

---

## Research Problem

Traditional HCl acid stimulation of carbonate formations faces issues like high corrosion rates, rapid acid consumption, and the formation of precipitates (sludge) at high temperatures. Chelating agents are proposed as safer alternatives but require optimization regarding pH, concentration, and injection rates for effective wormhole creation.

## Objectives

- Evaluate the solubility of various chelating agents (EDTA, HEDTA, GLDA, DTPA) in deionized water and sea water at different pH levels.
- Measure density and viscosity of chelates as function of temperature and calcium ions in the solution.
- Perform matrix stimulation treatments using different chelating agent formulations at different conditions and solution concentrations.
- Identify the effectiveness of using deionized water and sea water-based solutions on creation and propagation of wormholes in carbonate rocks.
- Examine the effect of rock integrity alterations around the wormholes after stimulation on the overall integrity of the rock.
- Test the applicability of the existing wormhole propagation models on simulating the results of the current work.

## Methods

The study involves: 1) pH solubility screening using a pH meter to determine stable ranges for EDTA, DTPA, and HEDTA in deionized and sea water. 2) Physical property measurement (density and viscosity) using Fischer density hydrometers and Koehler capillary tube viscometers across varying temperatures and calcium concentrations. 3) Core flooding experiments to evaluate wormhole creation and propagation. 4) Post-stimulation evaluation of rock integrity using CT scan, NMR, and acoustic measurements. 5) Modeling of results by comparing actual pressure drops against pore volumes injected using established models (e.g., Damköhler number analysis).

## Data and Materials

The study used 4 core samples: 2 from Indiana Limestone (porosity ~7.8-13.1%, permeability 0.08-15.0 md) and 2 from Austin Chalk (porosity ~15.5-29.1%, permeability 8.4-15.5 md). Various chelating agents were tested at concentrations of 0.25M and pH levels ranging from 2.0 to 10.0.

## Key Findings

- Solubility screening showed that DTPA and HEDTA chelating agents were soluble at pH values as low as 2.0 in all chelant concentrations prepared by deionized water and sea water. ([[2015-barri|Barri, 2015, p. 22]])
- EDTA was not soluble at pH values less than 5.0 when sea water was used for dilution. ([[2015-barri|Barri, 2015, p. 22]])
- 5% DTPA and HEDTA chelating agents were found to be good iron control agents for 20% HCl acid. ([[2015-barri|Barri, 2015, p. 22]])
- Core flooding studies showed that the optimum injection rate for the chelating agents was in the range of 1.5 – 2.0 cc/m when both minimum chelant volume and minimum experiment time were used. ([[2015-barri|Barri, 2015, p. 22]])
- Chelating agents had negligible effect on relatively strong carbonate rocks such as Indiana Limestone, whereas less strong rocks like Austin Chalk could be highly affected. ([[2015-barri|Barri, 2015, p. 22]])
- EDTA is capable of stimulating calcite formations at pH 4-13 and can create wormholes at low injection rates. ([[2015-barri|Barri, 2015, p. 36]])
- DTPA and HEDTA stimulated more efficiently than HCl when injected at low rates. ([[2015-barri|Barri, 2015, p. 37]])
- GLDA was found to be the best concentration of GLDA (20% by weight) for stimulation in both parallel and single cores without using diverting agents. ([[2015-barri|Barri, 2015, p. 39]])
- The optimum Damköhler number for most fluids investigated, including chelating agents, was found to be approximately 0.29. ([[2015-barri|Barri, 2015, p. 37]])
- EDTA solubility is significantly affected by pH; it requires a pH of at least 6.5 for 20% concentration in deionized water and at least 5.4 when prepared with sea water. ([[2015-barri|Barri, 2015, p. 65]])
- HEDTA and DTPA remained soluble at pH values as low as 2 in all concentrations up to 40% by weight. ([[2015-barri|Barri, 2015, p. 64]])
- DTPA was the only chelate that remained soluble at concentration of 2.5% when mixed with 20% HCl and sea water. ([[2015-barri|Barri, 2015, p. 69]])
- HEDTA and DTPA successfully kept 3000 ppm of ferric ions in solution for pH up to 4.35 and 4.35 respectively when used as iron control agents. ([[2015-barri|Barri, 2015, p. 73]])
- GLDA only stabilized ferric ions at pH levels below 3.0; higher concentrations of ferric ions (2000 ppm) resulted in precipitation at pH 3.4. ([[2015-barri|Barri, 2015, p. 74]])
- Acetic and citric acids were effective iron control agents but only for HCl concentrations up to 20% and were limited to a maximum concentration of 10% wt. ([[2015-barri|Barri, 2015, p. 69]])

## Limitations

Measuring elastic and mechanical properties specifically on the walls of the wormholes was not possible; therefore, measurements were taken for the entire core sample as an evaluation tool.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Perform more studies on the effect of solution pH on stimulation treatments using fluids prepared with sea water.
- Investigate the impact of rock properties (porosity, permeability) and core length on stimulation results.
- Further investigate the effects of chelating agents on elastic properties across a wider range of pH levels, concentrations, injection rates, and temperatures.
