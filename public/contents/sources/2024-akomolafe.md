# SYSTEMATIC INVESTIGATION OF DRILLING FLUID IMPACT ON NMR INTERPRETATION FOR FORMATION EVALUATION

**Summary**: The thesis investigates how drilling fluid properties, specifically bulk relaxation and additives like barite, bentonite, and hematite, affect NMR T2 relaxometry in permeable and tight limestone samples. It concludes that while bulk relaxation significantly impacts the interpretation of pore size distribution in permeable rocks, its effect is minimal in tight formations where surface relaxivity dominates.

---

## Research Problem

The invasion of mud filtrate into a formation during drilling alters near-wellbore properties, and because NMR has a shallow depth of investigation, the bulk relaxation of the drilling fluid (influenced by viscosity and additives like perlite or hematite) can lead to inaccuracies in evaluating porosity, permeability, and pore size distribution.

## Objectives

- Explore how the bulk relaxation properties of drilling fluid impact NMR T2 relaxometry responses.
- Evaluate how experimental procedures during filtration experiments (e.g., using rock vs. filter paper) influence results.
- Systematically vary factors controlling drilling fluid's bulk relaxation: XC polymer concentration, perlite concentration, and hematite concentration.
- Assess how host rock properties (highly permeable limestone vs. tight limestone) influence the T2 response to mud invasion.
- Examine how the viscosity and bulk T2 relaxation time of drilling fluids affect NMR relaxation behavior and the response of original reservoir fluids during mud filtrate invasion.
- Investigate the influence of host rock properties, especially permeability and pore size distribution, on NMR measurements, focusing on the impact of invading particles on rock damage and measurement accuracy.
- Assess the impact of iron-rich particles (hematite) used as drilling fluid additives on NMR signals, specifically changes in relaxation properties.
- Validate the experimental procedure of using rock as a filter during filtration experiments against standard filter paper usage.
- Assess how different levels of viscosity influence the bulk relaxation characteristics of the drilling fluid and its correlation with NMR T2 responses.
- Examine the influence of perlite on the physical properties of drilling fluid and its subsequent effect on NMR T2 measurements.
- Investigate the impact of iron-rich additives (hematite) on surface relaxivity and overall NMR measurements.
- Validate the experimental procedure of using rock as a filter during filtration experiments against the common practice of using standard filter paper.
- Evaluate the impact of varying polymer concentrations on NMR T2 relaxation in permeable and tight rocks.
- Assess the effect of solid additives (perlite and hematite) on NMR signals and the formation of filter cakes.
- Validate the use of rock as a filtration medium compared to standard filter paper to better simulate real-world drilling conditions.
- Investigate how different drilling fluid properties, including bulk relaxation and the presence of various additives, influence NMR T2 relaxometry responses in near-wellbore conditions.
- Understand the impact of these factors on pore size distribution and relaxation mechanisms in both permeable and tight limestone samples.
- Evaluate the role of particulate additives (e.g., bentonite, barite, perlite, and hematite) in shaping NMR T2 responses.
- Compare different filtration setups (filter paper vs. rock-based) to simulate mud invasion conditions more accurately.

## Methods

The study employed three distinct approaches to vary drilling fluid bulk relaxation: 1) varying XC polymer concentration (viscosifier), 2) varying perlite content, and 3) varying hematite content. Mud-filtration experiments were conducted using both a custom rock-filter setup and a standard filter paper press under HPHT conditions (66°C, 500 psi hydrostatic). Core flooding was used to inject filtrate into cores at 0.5 cc/min with 1000 psi backpressure for 18 hours. Analytical techniques included SEM-EDS for characterizing additive morphology and composition, Laser Particle Size Analysis (LPSA) using the ANALYSETTE 22 Nano Tec plus for determining particle size distribution of perlite and hematite, and NMR relaxometry using a GeoSpec rock core analyzer to measure T2 relaxation times in three stages: baseline, post-filtration/invasion, and after filter cake removal.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized 14 distinct drilling fluid formulations (Table 3.1, 3.2, 3.3) containing varying amounts of XC polymer, perlite, and hematite. Two classes of limestone samples were used: high-permeability (cores A-H) and low-permeability (cores I-N). Particle size distributions for perlite and hematite were measured using the ANALYSETTE 22 Nano Tec plus.

## Key Findings

- Drilling fluid bulk relaxation significantly affects the NMR T2 values and their distribution, particularly in invaded zones. ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 12]])
- Additives such as XC polymer, bentonite, hematite, and barite decrease the T2 bulk values of the drilling fluid, which subsequently reduces the overall T2 values in the invaded rock, especially in macropores. ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 12]])
- The pronounced impact in macropores is due to their higher sensitivity to bulk relaxation effects compared to micropores. ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 12]])
- Rocks with larger pore sizes exhibit more substantial changes in T2 values, while tighter rocks (where surface relaxation mechanisms dominate) show minimal changes. ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 13]])
- Using rock as a filter during the filtration experiment provided a more realistic simulation of the reservoir's response to fluid invasion compared to standard filter paper. ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 13]])
- The T2 relaxation time (T2) is particularly vulnerable to the effects of mud invasion due to the shallow depth of investigation associated with NMR logging. ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 12]])
- The study identifies that the limited depth of investigation associated with NMR logging makes its T2 spectrum highly susceptible to the effects of mud invasion. ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 30]])
- Previous studies (Van der Zwaag et al., 2001) showed that water-based filtrate invasion caused a shift to shorter T2 values, while oil-based filtrate resulted in signals suggesting larger free-fluid volumes. ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 30]])
- Bageri et al. (2021) found that NMR measurements of filter cake porosity and infiltrated solids had a stronger correlation with cake thickness compared to other methods, though NMR reported lower porosity values. ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 31]])

## Limitations

The study primarily relies on controlled laboratory experiments, which may not fully replicate the complexities and variability of in-situ conditions encountered in actual drilling environments, such as temperature, pressure, and fluid dynamics.

## Recommendations and Future Work

1. Account for bulk relaxation time (T2B) of drilling fluids when interpreting NMR data in formations with larger pore sizes. 2. Prioritize rock-fluid interaction setups over filter paper setups to simulate real-world conditions. 3. Assess the impact of magnetic susceptibility from additives like hematite on T2 distributions. 4. Avoid or critically consider NMR logging in areas where drilling fluids contain iron additives. 5. Develop drilling fluids with controlled T2B values to minimize interference with NMR measurements.
