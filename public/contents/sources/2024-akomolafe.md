# SYSTEMATIC INVESTIGATION OF DRILLING FLUID IMPACT ON NMR INTERPRETATION FOR FORMATION EVALUATION

**Summary**: The thesis investigates how drilling fluid properties, specifically bulk relaxation (T2B) and particulate additives like perlite and hematite, affect NMR T2 relaxometry measurements in both permeable and tight limestone samples. It concludes that while tight formations are dominated by surface relaxivity, permeable formations are highly susceptible to bulk relaxation effects from drilling fluids, leading to potential misinterpretations of pore size distribution.

---

## Research Problem

The invasion of mud filtrate into the formation during drilling alters near-wellbore properties, and because NMR logging has a shallow depth of investigation, the T2 spectrum is highly sensitive to these changes. Specifically, the bulk relaxation of drilling fluids (which are often more viscous than original reservoir fluids) was previously overlooked in literature, potentially leading to inaccurate assessments of porosity, permeability, and fluid saturation.

## Objectives

- Examine how the viscosifier and bulk T2 relaxation time of drilling fluids affect NMR relaxation behavior and the response of the original reservoir fluids during mud filtrate invasion.
- Investigate the influence of host rock properties, especially permeability and pore size distribution, on NMR measurements, with a focus on the potential impact of invading particles on rock damage and measurement accuracy.
- Assess the impact of iron-rich particles used as drilling fluid additives on NMR signals, particularly the changes they induce in relaxation properties and enough influence on measurement outcomes.
- Validate our experimental procedure of using rock as a filter during the filtration experiment against the common practice in the literature, where a standard filter paper is used instead.
- Assess the impact of XC polymer concentration on bulk T2 shifts in permeable and tight rocks.
- Evaluate the effect of particulate additives (perlite and hematite) on NMR T_2 distribution and filter cake formation.

## Methods

The study utilized four phases: 1) varying XC polymer concentration to assess viscosity and bulk relaxation; 2) adjusting perlite concentration to evaluate particle invasion effects; 3) varying hematite concentration to assess surface relaxivity impacts from iron-rich additives; and 4) validating the 'rock-as-filter' experimental procedure against standard filter paper. Laboratory procedures included a specialized filtration loss setup (high pressure/temperature), core flooding tests for permeability measurement, SEM-EDX for mineralogical characterization of additives, Micro-CT scanning for non-destructive imaging of invasion, and Laser Particle Size Analysis (LPSA) to determine the distribution of particles in the drilling fluid. NMR relaxometry was performed using a GeoSpec rock core analyzer with a CPMG pulse sequence.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized limestone samples (both high permeability and tight), various drilling fluid formulations containing XC polymer, perlite, and hematite, and a specialized filtration setup for 30-minute tests at 66°C and 500 psi. NMR measurements were conducted on samples before and after filtration.

## Key Findings

- Drilling fluid bulk relaxation significantly affects the NMR T2 values and their distribution, particularly in invaded zones. ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 12]])
- Additives such as XC polymer, bentonite, hematite, and barite decrease the T2 bulk values of the drilling fluid, which subsequently reduces the overall T2 values in the invaded rock, especially in macropores. ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 12]])
- The pronounced impact in macropores is due to their higher sensitivity to bulk relaxation effects compared to micropores. ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 12]])
- Rocks with larger pore sizes exhibit more substantial changes in T2 values, while tighter rocks, where surface relaxation mechanisms dominate, show minimal changes. ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 12]])
- The experimental procedure of using rock as a filter provided a more realistic simulation of the reservoir's response to fluid invasion compared to standard filter paper. ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 13]])
- NMR-permeability calculations based on the Timur-Coates Free Fluid model showed variations by up to one order of magnitude due to drilling fluid invasion. ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 30]])
- The study found that T2 bulk relaxation is no longer negligible for viscous drilling fluids, as it can distort pore size interpretations when $1/T_{2B}$ becomes significant. ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 48]])
- In permeable Indiana limestone, increasing XC polymer concentration from 0.5g to 5g caused a significant shift in T2 distribution, where macropore signals were diminished and overlapped with micropore signals due to bulk relaxation (T2B) becoming comparable to T2S. ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 47]])
- In tight Buda limestone samples, the impact of drilling fluid on NMR signal was minimal because surface relaxivity remained the dominant mechanism despite mud invasion. ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 63]])
- The addition of perlite resulted in a thicker filter cake and a more pronounced shift in T2 distribution compared to fluids without particles, due to the much lower T2B of particle-laden fluids. ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 53]])
- Hematite addition significantly impacted NMR response by increasing magnetic susceptibility, leading to accelerated relaxation and a shift toward shorter T2 values (below 1 ms). ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 76]])
- The use of rock as a filter medium provided a more realistic representation of subsurface conditions than filter paper, which only showed minor changes in T2 distribution because it removed solid particles and additives. ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 59]])
- Using the mean-T2 model on samples with perlite led to an overestimation of formation damage (48.1% permeability reduction) compared to actual laboratory measurements (32.5%). ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 53]])
- Hematite presence led to a significant underestimation of porosity in NMR (14.9 p.u.) compared to the lab-measured value (18.87%). ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 76]])
- Perlite particles showed a median size (D50) of 62.39 µm and were confirmed as 65-80% silica via SEM-EDS. ([[2024-akomolafe|Akomolafe, 2024, p. 40]])

## Limitations

The study relies on controlled laboratory experiments, which may not fully replicate the complexities and variability of in-situ conditions such as temperature, pressure, and fluid dynamics in actual drilling environments.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study suggests that measuring the T2 bulk relaxation properties of drilling fluids is essential for reliable NMR-based pore size and permeability evaluations. It also recommends employing filtration systems that closely mimic field conditions (using rock as a filter) to improve the accuracy of laboratory experiments.
