# Reaction Kinetics of Fresh & Spent Acid in Dolomite Rock at High Pressures

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the reaction kinetics of fresh and spent HCl acid with dolomite rock under high pressure conditions (up to 3000 psi) using a rotating disk apparatus. It concludes that higher pressures lead to lower diffusion coefficients due to increased CO2 solubility, and that spending acid reduces dissolution rates compared to fresh acid.

---

## Research Problem

The use of fresh acid kinetics to model wormhole propagation often overestimates dissolution rates because it does not account for the 'spent' nature of acid (containing CO2 and other products) as it moves deeper into a carbonate reservoir. This study addresses how high pressure affects these kinetics in dolomite specifically.

## Objectives

- Study the reaction kinetics of HCl with Dolomite at high pressure using various spent acid concentrations (12.5, 10 and 7.5 wt. %).
- To study the reaction kinetics of dolomite rock with fresh acid (15 wt. %) at a temperature of 65°C at two different pressure ranges: 1000 psi and 3000 psi.
- To determine the diffusion coefficient of hydrogen ions from the bulk solution to the rock surface.
- Study the effect of pressure on dissolution rates and diffusion coefficients in dolomite rock.
- Evaluate the impact of different rotation speeds (RPM) on reaction regimes (mass transfer vs. surface reaction limited).
- Compare the effects of rock morphology (Guelph vs. Silurian dolomite) on acid interaction.

## Methods

The study utilized Rotating Disk Apparatus (RDA) to measure reaction kinetics. Samples included Guelph Dolomite and Silurian Dolomite. XRD was used to confirm dolomite purity (>95%). Micro XCT (Versa XRM-500) provided 3D visualization of pore structures before and after experiments. Acid concentration was determined via titration with NaOH. AAS (Thermo Scientific ICE 3000 Series) quantified Ca2+ and Mg2+ ions in samples to calculate dissolution rates and diffusion coefficients. Weight loss measurements were taken before and after the experiment.

## Data and Materials

Two types of dolomite core samples: Guelph Dolomite (porosity ~10%) and Silurian Dolomite. 15 experiments conducted across two pressure levels (1000 psi, 3000 psi) with varying rotation speeds (250 to 1250 rpm). Fresh acid used was 15 wt. % HCl.

## Key Findings

- Dissolution rates and diffusion coefficients decreased at high pressure conditions because CO2 was more soluble in aqueous solution and tended to buffer the diffusion of hydrogen ions from the bulk to the rock surface. ([[2014-khalid|Khalid, 2014, p. 15]])
- When fresh acid was spent to 12.5 wt. %, there was a slight decrease in the diffusion coefficient results because of the slow reaction rate of dolomite. ([[2014-khalid|Khalid, 2014, p. 15]])
- HCl/dolomite reaction was mass transfer limited at low disk rotational speeds and reaction limited at high disk rotational speeds. ([[2014-khalid|Khalid, 2014, p. 15]])
- Diffusion coefficient was significantly affected by rock porosity due to variation in surface reaction area, causing increased dissolution rates as observed by Micro XCT images. ([[2014-khalid|Khalid, 2014, p. 15]])
- The diffusion coefficient of a spent acid system is significantly lower than the fresh acid system (30% less) because reaction products (CO2 and counter ions) buffered the diffusion of H+ ions. ([[2014-khalid|Khalid, 2014, p. 26]])
- At high pressure, CO2 tends to stay in aqueous phase which slows down the reaction of HCl and the calcite rock samples compared to low pressure where CO2 is in gaseous phase. ([[2014-khalid|Khalid, 2014, p. 27]])
- Guelph dolomite samples were confirmed to have a purity of approximately 99% dolomite via XRD analysis. ([[2014-khalid|Khalid, 2014, p. 76]])
- The reaction between Guelph dolomite and fresh acid at 1000 psi was determined to be mass transfer limited, as the weight loss increased with increasing disk rotation speed. ([[2014-khalid|Khalid, 2014, p. 80]])
- The diffusion coefficient obtained using both weight loss and AAS results for Guelph dolomite at 1000 psi was 2.83 x 10^-6 cm^2/sec. ([[2014-khalid|Khalid, 2014, p. 83]])
- At high rotation speeds (e.g., 1250 rpm), acid penetrated deeper into the dolomite, creating larger holes and more significant dissolution compared to lower speeds (e.g., 500 rpm). ([[2014-khalid|Khalid, 2014, p. 83]])
- The concentration of fresh acid was determined to be 4.38 M via titration. ([[2014-khalid|Khalid, 2014, p. 79]])
- At low pressure (1000 psi), CO2 remains in the gas phase, increasing mass transfer of H+ ions and leading to overestimated dissolution rates; at high pressure (3000 psi), CO2 is soluble, buffering the system and resulting in lower reaction rates. ([[2014-khalid|Khalid, 2014, p. 108]])
- The diffusion coefficient for Silurian dolomite decreased by approximately 23% when moving from 1000 psi to 3000 psi (from 7.155 x 10^-07 cm^2/sec to 5.610 x 10^--07 cm^2/sec). ([[2014-khalid|Khalid, 2014, p. 109]])
- For Guelph dolomite, the reaction was mass transfer limited until 1250 rpm; for Silurian dolomite, the transition to a surface reaction limited regime occurred at 750 rpm. ([[2014-khalid|Khalid, 2014, p. 109]])
- Guelph dolomite showed higher dissolution rates and non-uniform dissolution (including holes) compared to Silurian dolomite due to its irregular morphology and presence of natural fractures. ([[2014-khalid|Khalid, 2014, p. 112]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Use dolomite marbles to eliminate the effects of rock porosity, permeability, and mineralogy.
- Conduct experiments for spent acids at higher rotational speeds (>1250 rpm) to identify the reaction-limited regime.
- Study emulsified acids and organic acids to assess their specific impacts on diffusion coefficients.
- Integrate Micro XCT during the experiment to visualize 3D reaction phenomena in real-time.
