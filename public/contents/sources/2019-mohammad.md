# DEVELOPMENT OF A POLYMER FOR GREEN ENHANCED OIL RECOVERY

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the development and performance of a chemically modified xanthan gum (NP) for green enhanced oil recovery. The study evaluates the polymer's chemical structure via FT-IR and NMR, its rheological properties under varying temperature and salinity, and its performance in core flood experiments compared to standard xanthan gum.

---

## Research Problem

Standard chemical polymers like HPAM used in EOR pose environmental concerns and have limitations in harsh reservoir conditions; while biopolymers like xanthan gum are greener but often lack sufficient viscosity under high temperature and salinity.

## Objectives

- Synthesize a chemically modified xanthan gum by reacting it with acrylic acid.
- Examine the synthesized xanthan's chemical structure by FT-IR and NMR spectra.
- Investigate the rheological properties of xanthan and synthesized xanthan gum solutions at different temperatures to determine the success of the synthesis process.
- Explore the relationships among EOR performance and xanthan and modified xanthan gums.
- Characterize the chemical structure of the modified polymer using FT-IR and NMR spectroscopy.
- Evaluate the rheological properties (viscosity) of both standard and modified xanthan gum under varying temperatures and salt concentrations.

## Methods

The study involved chemical modification of xanthan gum with acrylic acid to create a new polymer (NP). Characterization was performed using FT-IR spectroscopy and 1H NMR spectroscopy. Rheological tests were conducted by measuring viscosity at various shear rates, temperatures (25C, 60C, 70C), and salinity levels (0%, 5%, 10%, 15% NaCl). Core flood experiments were performed on two core samples to compare the recovery of standard xanthan gum versus the modified NP. The process involved saturating cores with brine and oil, followed by flooding with either polymer type for 3 pore volumes (PV), and a final brine flush.

## Data and Materials

12-inch Berea sandstone core (divided into two 6-inch segments), 3% NaCl brine (30,000 ppm), Arabian light oil (31.14 API, 19.8 cp at 25°C), xanthan gum (Sigma Aldrich), acrylic acid, and N-[3-(dimethylaminopropyl)]-N-ethylcarbodiimide hydrochloride.

## Key Findings

- Chemical modification of xanthan with acrylic acid was confirmed by FT-IR showing a new peak at 1,645 cm⁻¹ (C=C) and NMR showing new peaks at 5.80, 6.02, and 6.41 ppm. ([[2019-mohammad|Mohammad, 2019, p. 60]])
- A 1,500 ppm xanthan solution had a viscosity of 578 mPa.S at a 0.1 s⁻¹ shear rate, while the chemically modified xanthan gum reached approximately 2,200 mPa.S under similar conditions. ([[2019-mohammad|Mohammad, 2019, p. 13]])
- In core flood experiments, standard xanthan gum provided an additional recovery of about 14% of the initial oil in place (IOIP), while modified xanthan gum showed a 19% tertiary recovery. ([[2019-mohammad|Mohammad, 2019, p. 14]])
- The difference in recovery between standard and modified xanthan gum was at least 5%, which is significant for enhanced oil production. ([[2019-mohammad|Mohammad, 2019, p. 16]])
- Physical blending of xanthan gum with PVA, gluconic acid, or acrylic acid did not improve viscosity compared to the base polymer. ([[2019-mohammad|Mohammad, 2019, p. 13]])
- Viscosity of xanthan gum was found to be inversely proportional to both temperature and shear rate. ([[2019-mohammad|Mohammad, 2019, p. 64]])
- Adding 2% NaCl (simulating reservoir conditions) had a noticeably negative impact on viscosity at temperatures up to 50°C, but the effect diminished as temperature increased toward 80°C. ([[2019-mohammad|Mohammad, 2019, p. 66]])
- The addition of PVA resulted in a minor and negligible increase in xanthan viscosity (e.g., from 0.018 mPa.S to 0.020 mPa.S at 50°C). ([[2019-mohammad|Mohammad, 2019, p. 68]])
- Blending xanthan with gluconic acid at a 1:1 ratio slightly decreased the viscosity of the gum. ([[2019-mohammad|Mohammad, 2019, p. 70]])
- Blending xanthan with acrylic acid (1:1, 1:3, 1:4) resulted in a decrease in viscosity due to dilution effects rather than chemical reaction. ([[2019-mohammad|Mohammad, 2019, p. 72]])
- The modification of xanthan with acrylic acid successfully produced a new polymer (NP), confirmed by FT-IR peaks at 1,645 cm-1 and additional NMR peaks at 5.80, 6.02, and 6.41 ppm. ([[2019-mohammad|Mohammad, 2019, p. 90]])
- The modified polymer (NP) showed a higher viscosity than standard xanthan gum at the same shear rate due to stronger bonds formed by the acrylic acid reaction. ([[2019-mohammad|Mohammad, 2019, p. 75]])
- At a 0.1 s-1 shear rate, the viscosity of NP was 2,200 mPa.S at 25C and 1,070 mPa.S at 50C, while xanthan gum was 578 mPa.S and 205 mPa.S respectively. ([[2019-mohammad|Mohammad, 2019, p. 77]])
- The reduction in viscosity for NP due to temperature increase from 25C to 50C was approximately 50%, compared to a 65% reduction for standard xanthan. ([[2019-mohammad|Mohammad, 2019, p. 77]])
- At higher temperatures (60C and 70C), the effect of temperature on the viscosity of the modified xanthan became smaller, with values becoming very close to each other. ([[2019-mohammad|Mohammad, 2019, p. 82]])

## Limitations

The rheometer used had a maximum temperature limit of 80°C; however, the study notes that some literature values for xanthan were reported at higher temperatures (e.g., 60°C and 80°C).

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Test the proposed modification for Xanthan gum on carbonate rock.
- Conduct further research to identify other modified xanthan gums with higher concentrations of acrylic acid.
- Adjust reaction temperatures based on the sensitivity of the chemical reaction to temperature.
- Investigate synthesis of Xanthan gum using acryloyl chloride and 3-ethoxy acryloyl chloride.
