# IMPROVING HYDROCARBON SATURATION ASSESSMENT IN LOW RESISTIVITY BEDS USING HIGH-RESOLUTION IMAGE LOGS AND CORE MEASUREMENTS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the challenge of Low Resistivity Pay (LRP) in both carbonate and clastic environments, specifically focusing on thin-bedded formations where conventional logs lack sufficient vertical resolution. It proposes an advanced workflow integrating high-resolution borehole image logs with core measurements to accurately identify thin beds and improve hydrocarbon saturation calculations.

---

## Research Problem

Conventional petrophysical analysis often underestimates hydrocarbon volumes in thinly laminated sand-shale formations because the vertical resolution of standard resistivity tools is insufficient to resolve thin beds, leading to low-resistivity readings that mask productive sands.

## Objectives

- Expand the application of existing resistivity imaging technology by utilizing advanced techniques to resolve thin beds for accurate resistivity measurement.
- Improve the accuracy of hydrocarbon saturation calculation in low-1resistivity formations.
- Enhance the accuracy of hydrocarbon assessment and reservoir evaluation in complex environments.
- Evaluate the impact of high-resolution electrical image logs on identifying thin bed boundaries in low-resistivity pay zones.
- Compare conventional petrophysical analysis with an advanced workflow using high-resolution data for hydrocarbon saturation and net-pay estimation.
- Develop a method to resolve thin beds and improve volumetric calculations in laminated formations.

## Methods

The study compares two workflows: 1. Standard Analysis: Uses triple-combo logs (gamma ray, density, neutron) and standard resistivity logs to calculate porosity and water saturation using the dual water equation. 2. Advanced Analysis: Incorporates high-resolution electrical image logs to identify thin bed boundaries. The image log's high-resolution resistivity data is processed via a 'square blocking' technique to assign values to specific electrical facies (5 types identified). An earth model is constructed where standard log responses are scaled and mapped onto these high-resolution facies. Finally, petrophysical properties are calculated using the high-resolution squared logs.

## Data and Materials

The study utilizes a case study involving high-resolution borehole electrical image logs, core samples from hydrocarbon-bearing sands, and standard wireline log suites including Gamma Ray (GR), Density, Neutron, Induction, and Laterolog data. Specific measurements include bulk density, neutron porosity, and various resistivity profiles.

## Key Findings

- High-resolution borehole images allowed for the identification of hydrocarbon-bearing sands in a thin laminated formation with thicknesses as small as 0.24 inches. ([[2019-alqunais|Alqunais, 2019, p. 15]])
- The integration of high-resolution data resulted in an increase of 65% in net pay calculation compared to standard log analysis. ([[2019-alqunais|Alqunais, 2019, p. 15]])
- Average porosity was found to be 8% higher when using the advanced workflow for thin bed identification. ([[2019-alqunais|Alqunais, 2019, p. 15]])
- Average water saturation (Sw) was reduced by 27% in the study's case study, leading to a more accurate assessment of bypassed pay. ([[2019-alqunais|Alqunais, 2019, p. 15]])
- Standard petrophysical evaluations often underestimate hydrocarbon volumes in thin beds by 30% or more due to the lack of vertical resolution in conventional tools. ([[2019-alqunais|Alqunais, 2019, p. 69]])
- The presence of conductive minerals like pyrite or clay with high cation exchange capacity (CEC) can significantly lower resistivity readings, leading to LRP. ([[2019-alqunais|Alqunais, 2019, p. 22]])
- Thinly laminated beds result in a true resistivity (Rt) that is an average of the sand and shale layers; if the shale layer is conductive, it dominates the measurement even if the sand has high resistivity. ([[2019-alqunais|Alqunais, 2019, p. 73]])
- The advanced workflow identified a net pay of 43% compared to the conventional analysis which only identified 26%. ([[2019-alqunais|Alqunais, 2019, p. 110]])
- Average porosity increased from 7% in standard analysis to 15% in high-resolution evaluation. ([[2019-alqunais|Alqunais, 2019, p. 110]])
- Average water saturation decreased from 56% (standard) to 41% (high-resolution). ([[2019-alqunais|Alqunais, 2019, p. 110]])
- The high-resolution image log revealed five distinct electrical facies with specific resistivity cutoffs: Facies 1 (<1.78 ohm.m), Facies 2 (1.78-6.05 ohm.m), Facies 3 (6.05-10 ohm.m), Facies 4 (10-29 ohm.m), and Facies 5 (>29 ohm.m). ([[2019-alqunais|Alqunais, 2019, p. 105]])
- The high-resolution evaluation resolved thin beds down to 0.24 inches. ([[2019-alqunais|Alqunais, 2019, p. 110]])

## Limitations

Conventional logs have limited vertical resolution, making them unable to differentiate thin-bed properties in many cases. Additionally, certain conditions like heavy mud cake or washouts can negatively impact micro-resistivity data quality.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study recommends using high-resolution analysis (micro image logs) for intervals where bed thickness is higher than 1 foot and a low-resolution statistical approach for zones with even thinner beds to improve the accuracy of hydrocarbon quantification.
