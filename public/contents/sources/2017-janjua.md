# OPTIMUM SELECTION OF VES SYSTEMS AS EOR FLUID FOR WAFRA RATAWI FIELD

**Summary**: The thesis evaluates the performance of Viscoelastic Surfactant (VES) and VES with Chelating Agent (VES-CA) systems as Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR) fluids for the Wafra Ratawi carbonate reservoir. Through a series of laboratory tests including IFT, adsorption, zeta potential, and core flooding, the study concludes that while both systems effectively reduce interfacial tension and improve mobility, the addition of chelating agents provides only marginal improvements over standalone VES.

---

## Research Problem

The primary challenge is the application of chemical EOR (cEOR) in carbonate reservoirs with high temperature and salinity (HTHS), where conventional surfactants and polymers degrade or precipitate. Specifically, the Ratawi field has exceptionally high levels of divalent cations, necessitating a robust system like VES/CA to maintain stability and provide both mobility control and IFT reduction.

## Objectives

- Determine the optimum concentration of VES for lowering interfacial tension (IFT).
- To determine the thermal stability of the VES systems in sea water.
- To study the adsorption of VES on real reservoir carbonate samples.
- To test the compatibility of the potential VES, and VES/CA systems by combining VES with chelating agent.
- To perform core flooding and evaluate the performance of the optimum VES systems in carbonate rocks.
- Evaluate the effect of chelating agents (DTPA) on the performance of VES systems.

## Methods

The study utilized a multi-step experimental approach: 1. Thermal Stability: TGA (SDT Q600 TA) for short-term stability, and long-term aging tests at 90°C and 120°C followed by FTIR (Bruker) and NMR (JEOL 500 MHz) to monitor structural changes. 2. IFT Measurement: Spinning drop tensiometer was used to measure IFT between crude oil and various VES/CA concentrations across different temperatures and times. 3. Adsorption: Static adsorption was measured using TOC analysis on crushed limestone samples from the Wafra Ratawi formation, modeled using Langmuir and BET isotherms. 4. Rheology: Viscosity and shear-thinning behavior were measured using a kinematic viscometer and rheometer. 5. Core Flooding: Core samples (1.5" diameter) from Well R-118 and R-146 were prepared, cleaned with methanol/toluene, and subjected to core flooding using equipment from VINCI Technologies to evaluate oil recovery under reservoir conditions.

## Data and Materials

The study utilizes carbonate samples from the Ratawi Oolite reservoir in the Wafra-Ratawi Field. It involves multiple formulations of Viscoelastic Surfactants (VES) and combinations with chelating agents (CA). Experimental data includes IFT measurements, thermal stability tests over time, adsorption studies on rock samples, and core flood results for two distinct fluid systems.

## Key Findings

- Viscoelastic surfactants are identified as superior to conventional surfactants/polymers in HTHS carbonate environments because they provide both mobility control (like polymers) and IFT reduction (like surfactants). ([[2017-janjua|Janjua, 2017, p. 17]])
- The inclusion of chelating agents (CA) is specifically intended to balance the effect of high divalent salts on VES, which are known for their affinity to salts. ([[2017-janjua|Janjua, 2017, p. 18]])
- Ultra-low interfacial tension (IFT) and thermal stability were observed for both VES and VES/CA systems, making them suitable for heavy oil recovery in carbonate reservoirs. ([[2017-janjua|Janjua, 2017, p. 18]])
- The study confirms that a reduction of IFT from 20-30 mN/m to 0.001-0.01 mN/m leads to the lowest residual oil saturation. ([[2017-janjua|Janjua, 2017, p. 43]])
- Viscoelastic fluids provide higher displacement efficiency and recovery factors compared to Newtonian fluids for the same capillary number due to different flow lines in pores and larger micro-forces. ([[2017-janjua|Janjua, 2017, p. 47]])
- The Ratawi field is characterized by high levels of divalent cations, making it a challenging environment for standard chemical EOR. ([[2017-janjua|Janjua, 2017, p. 19]])
- Carbonate core samples from the Wafra Ratawi reservoir exhibit porosity ranging from 15% to 27% and gas permeability between 1 and 653 mD. ([[2017-janjua|Janjua, 2017, p. 86]])
- The crude oil from the Wafra Ratawi field is characterized as heavy crude with an API gravity of 17°. ([[2017-janjua|Janjua, 2017, p. 86]])
- Spinning drop tensiometry can measure ultra-low interfacial tension in the range of 10^-4 to 10 mN/m, which is critical for evaluating EOR fluids. ([[2017-janjua|Janjua, 2017, p. 74]])
- The addition of chelating agents (DTPA) helps maintain the stability and performance of VES systems in high-salinity environments. ([[2017-janjua|Janjua, 2017, p. 90]])
- TGA analysis is used to determine the thermal degradation limits of VES and VES/CA samples, with a significant weight change identifying the upper limit. ([[2017-janjua|Janjua, 2017, p. 98]])
- Long-term aging tests (up to 30 days) at 90°C and 120°C are required to ensure EOR fluids remain stable under high-temperature conditions. ([[2017-janjua|Janjua, 2017, p. 98]])
- The study notes that for low permeability carbonate reservoirs (10-100 mD), optimized injectivity and viscosity of the fluid are critical. ([[2017-janjua|Janjua, 2017, p. 53]])
- A concentration of 0.3% VES was selected as a baseline for evaluating the effects of chelating agents and aging. ([[2017-janjua|Janjua, 2017, p. 97]])
- Porosity of core samples from Well R-146 ranged from 22.02% to 26.51%, with permeability values between 215.68 mD and 403.06 mD. ([[2017-janjua|Janjua, 2017, p. 110]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Perform molecular simulation and pore-scale studies to evaluate chemical behavior under various conditions.
- Analyze chelating agents of different pH levels to enhance recovery potential.
- Extend the study to other field crude oils beyond Wafra Ratawi.
- Conduct contact angle measurement experiments for VES systems in carbonate reservoirs.
- Perform pilot tests on-field after evaluating all screening criteria.
