# FILTER CAKE REMOVAL OF BARITE WATER-BASED MUD

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the development of a new chemical formulation to remove barite filter cakes from wellbores, which are typically resistant to standard acid treatments. The study evaluates various chelating agents (DTPA, EDTA, HEDTA) combined with catalysts and enzymes to improve dissolution rates in high-pressure, high-temperature (HPHT) conditions.

---

## Research Problem

Barite is a common weighting agent in drilling fluids that forms a dense filter cake; its low solubility in acids makes it difficult to remove using conventional methods. The study addresses the need for an environmentally friendly, high-efficiency chemical formulation to dissolve barite filter cakes and scale in deep wells.

## Objectives

- Use a high temperature solubility system to test and calibrate the optimal concentration of different chelating agents (DTPA, EDTA, HEDTA) alone.
- Study the effect of different catalysts (converters) at various concentrations on the rate of barite dissolution as a function of time and temperature.
- Investigate the compatibility between different enzymes and the formulation to determine the best enzyme for breaking polymeric coats.
- Evaluate filter cake removal efficiency using real field barite-weighted water-based mud samples in HPHT conditions.
- Verify the designed formulation by testing the solubility rate of drilling fluid solids collected after separation in the field.
- Analyze the microscopy and chemical structure of filter cake before and after treatment using SEM and XRD.

## Methods

The research involved: 1) Solubility tests where 4g of barite was mixed with 100ml of chelating agents (DTPA, EDTA, HEDTA) at 95°C for 24 hours to determine optimal concentrations. 2) Addition of catalysts (potassium carbonate, potassium chloride, potassium formate) and enzymes (7% and 10% weight) to the formulations. 3) HPHT static fluid loss tests at 300 psi and 270°F using real field samples to form filter cakes on ceramic disks. 4) Removal trials where the formulated solution was applied to these cakes for 24 hours. 5. Analytical characterization of solids before/after treatment via SEM, XRD, and XRF. 6. Kinetic studies using a Rotating Disk Apparatus (RDAs) and FTIR analysis.

## Data and Materials

Industrial barite (BaSO4), real water-based mud from a deep gas well in the Middle East, three types of enzymes (Alpha Amylase, Endoglucanase, mannans), and various chelating agents (DTPA, EDTA, HEDTA). Samples included industrial barite, solids collected from de-sanders during well flow back, and filter cake samples.

## Key Findings

- The removal formulation consisting of high pH potassium base DTPA (20% wt), enzyme, and a catalyst (potassium carbonate, formate, or chloride) dissolved more than 90% of the filter cake formed by real barite drilling fluid in a single stage within 24 hours. ([[2016-geri|GERI, 2016, p. 18]])
- The addition of converting agents/catalysts increased the barite solubility from 67% to 90%. ([[2016-geri|GERI, 2016, p. 18]])
- DTPA performed the highest solubility of barite solids at 25°C (7.6 g/L) compared to other chelating agents like EDTA (4.85 g/L). ([[2016-geri|GERI, 2016, p. 33]])
- The maximum thickness of the filter cake in the studied horizontal sections was 1.3 mm. ([[2016-geri|GERI, 2016, p. 50]])
- In the first part of the horizontal section, filter cake porosity increased from 5% to 35% and permeability from 0.01 mD to 0.25 mD as more feet were drilled. ([[2016-geri|GERI, 2016, p. 50]])
- The growth in filter cake porosity from 5% to 35% reduced the liquid-to-solid ratio in the removal process from 28 g/500 ml to 18 g/ml. ([[2016-geri|GERI, 2016, p. 50]])
- Barite (barium sulfate) is insoluble in water and acids such as HCl, formic, citric, and acetic acids. ([[2016-geri|GERI, 2016, p. 17]])
- The initial filter cake porosity was 5% and permeability was 0.01 mD at the start of drilling operations. ([[2016-geri|GERI, 2016, p. 64]])
- Filter cake porosity increased significantly to an average of 35% in the first part of the horizontal section due to non-uniformed shapes of formation cuttings. ([[2016-geri|GERI, 2016, p. 53]])
- DTPA-K5 at a concentration of 20 wt.% and pH > 11 yielded the maximum barite dissolution rate of approximately 67 wt.%. ([[2016-geri|GERI, 2016, p. 79]])
- EDTA-K4 at 20 wt.% concentration yielded a solubility of 25.6 g/L in 24 hours at pH > 11 and 200°F. ([[2016-geri|GERI, 2016, p. 67]])
- The solid-to-liquid ratio for treatment volume was reduced from 52 to 33 gm/1000 ml when accounting for a 35% filter cake porosity compared to zero porosity. ([[2016-geri|GERI, 2016, p. 60]])
- A recommended treatment ratio of 4 gm of filter cake solids per 100 ml of solvent was established for an average 2 mm thick filter cake and 9.5/8 inch hole diameter. ([[2016-geri|GERI, 2016, p. 64]])
- Barite solubility in DTPA-K5 remained stable at 20 wt.% concentration for up to 48 hours, while higher concentrations led to precipitation of solids. ([[2016-geri|GERI, 2016, p. 84]])
- The maximum dissolution rate was reached within a 24-hour period; further time did not significantly increase barite solubility. ([[2016-geri|GERI, 2016, p. 86]])

## Limitations

Previous studies did not consider wellbore constraints (volume of chelating agent vs. barite weight) or the inclusion of catalysts to enhance solubility in specific chelating agents.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study suggests that the developed dimensionless number will help re-evaluate filter cake removal and stimulation recipes by accounting for varying filter cake properties (thickness, porosity, mineralogy) across long horizontal sections.
