# Design of an Alkaline/Surfactant/Polymer Enhanced Oil Recovery Scheme for a Saudi Arabian Limestone Reservoir

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the design of an Alkaline-Surfactant-Polymer (ASP) flooding scheme for a Saudi Arabian limestone reservoir characterized by high temperature (90°C) and high salinity (57,000 ppm). The study evaluates chemical compatibility, surfactant adsorption, and polymer rheology to optimize the formulation for improved oil recovery.

---

## Research Problem

The primary challenge is designing an effective ASP flooding scheme for a limestone reservoir where high temperature and high salinity typically cause significant degradation of chemicals and precipitation issues with divalent ions.

## Objectives

- Design a suitable alkaline-surfactant-polymer (ASP) flooding process for a Saudi Arabian limestone reservoir.
- Minimize precipitation reactions between chemical slugs and rock minerals/brines.
- Reduce surfactant adsorption onto the rock matrix.
- Evaluate the impact of alkali on the viscosity and stability of polymer solutions.
- Determine the thermal stability of surfactants B1083 and Tritron X-102.
- Observe microemulsion formation and study rheology in alkaline-surfactant systems.

## Methods

The study involved screening four types of alkalis (Sodium Hydroxide, Sodium Carbonate, Sodium Bicarbonate, and Sodium Tripolyphosphate) via titration to monitor pH and precipitation. Surfactant selection focused on ethoxylated sulfonates (B1083) for thermal stability. Emulsification tests were conducted at 90°C to observe phase behavior. Polymer rheology was measured using a Brookfield Model LVT-30 viscometer across various concentrations and temperatures. Interfacial tension (IFT) was measured using a University of Texas Interfacial Tensiometer (UTSDI model 500). Static surfactant adsorption was determined by comparing initial and residual surfactant concentrations in limestone samples via Inductively Coupled Plasma (ICP) analysis. Surface area of rock samples was determined using the Brunaucr-Emmett-Teller (BET) method.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized Arab-D Crude Oil from the Uthmaniyah area, B1083 anionic surfactant, Tritron X-102 nonionic surfactant, and Flocon 4800C biopolymer. Two brine types were used: a 57,000 ppm synthetic Qurrayah sea water and a corresponding softened brine. Rock samples consisted of Arab-D limestone formation from the same reservoir as the oil.

## Key Findings

- The B1083 surfactant was selected because it remained thermally stable at the target reservoir temperature of 90°C. ([[1994-eme|Eme, 1994, p. 24]])
- The addition of alkali to the surfactant solution led to a decrease in the viscosity of the polymer (Flocon 4800C) solution. ([[1994-eme|Eme, 1994, p. 24]])
- The adsorption of surfactant in the presence of alkali was lower than that without alkali, though the percent reduction in adsorption decreased with increasing surfactant concentration. ([[1994-eme|Eme, 1994, p. 24]])
- Sodium tripolyphosphate (C) was found to precipitate at much lower concentrations at 90°C compared to other alkalis, making it less suitable for high-temperature applications. ([[1994-eme|Eme, 1994, p. 58]])
- A 1% mixture of sodium bicarbonate and sodium carbonate was chosen as the optimal alkali system because it maintained a pH of 9.3, which is above the rock's zero point of charge (ZPC) of 8.1-8.4. ([[1994-eme|Eme, 1994, p. 58]])
- The presence of divalent ions in the 57,000 ppm brine caused significant loss of alkalinity and precipitation compared to the softened brine. ([[1994-eme|Eme, 1994, p. 54]])
- B1083 surfactant showed no turbidity up to the maximum test temperature of 95°C, while Tritron X-102 became turbid at 68°C and separated into a gel at 84°C. ([[1994-eme|Eme, 1994, p. 60]])
- A water-in-oil microemulsion was formed in the surfactant system, containing approximately 90% oil by volume. ([[1994-eme|Eme, 1994, p. 61]])
- The polymer Flocon 4800C exhibited pseudoplastic behavior (shear thinning) where viscosity decreased with increasing shear rate. ([[1994-eme|Eme, 1994, p. 69]])
- The presence of alkali and surfactant was found to decrease the viscosity of the polymer solution. ([[1994-eme|Eme, 1994, p. 70]])
- Polymer degradation at 90°C was identified as thermal-oxidative, necessitating an oxygen scavenger in the chemical slug. ([[1994-eme|Eme, 1994, p. 84]])
- The crude oil-brine IFT was approximately 20 dynes/cm; reduction to below 10^{-2} dynes/cm is required for mobilization. ([[1994-eme|Eme, 1994, p. 92]])
- The Langmuir type L4 isotherm best fit the surfactant-rock system, while the General Isotherm best fit the alkali-surfactant-rock system. ([[1994-eme|Eme, 1994, p. 108]])
- Alkali reduced surfactant adsorption by over 85% for initial concentrations below 0.4%. ([[1994-eme|Eme, 1994, p. 110]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Explore other surfactants that can achieve a lower IFT than B1083 at reservoir conditions.
- Investigate the effect of dissolved oxygen levels on polymer thermal degradation.
- Investigate the effect of temperature on the reduction in surfactant adsorption by alkali.
