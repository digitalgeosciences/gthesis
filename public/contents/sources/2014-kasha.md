# Impact of Injection Water chemistry on Zeta Potential of Carbonate Rocks

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of different injection water chemistries, specifically Arabian Gulf Seawater (AGSW) and its diluted versions, on the zeta potential and surface morphology of carbonate rocks (calcite and dolomite). The study concludes that while 50% diluted AGSW does not significantly release oil compared to original AGSW, the addition of calcium ions can influence surface roughness and mineral interactions.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses how different components in injection water—specifically calcium, magnesium, and sulfate ions, as well as sodium chloride—affect the zeta potential and physical surface characteristics of carbonate reservoirs (calcite, carbonate-rich dolomite, and MEC) during water flooding.

## Objectives

- Develop a fundamental understanding about the individual and relative effects of the main PDIs (Ca2+, Mg2+, SO42-) present in Arabian Gulf Seawater (AGSW) on the surface charges of typical carbonate minerals.
- Investigate potential structural changes in rock surfaces resulting from brine/rock/oil interactions using microscopic imaging.
- Correlate measured zeta potential values with observed physical and chemical changes to understand wettibility alteration mechanisms.
- Characterize the mineralogy and morphology of calcite, dolomite, and Middle East Carbonate using SEM, EDS, and Raman spectroscopy.
- Prepare a model oil with a Total Acid Number (TAN) of 2.0 mg KOH/g to simulate reservoir conditions.
- Evaluate the effect of various ions in brine solutions on the zeta potential of carbonate rocks.

## Methods

The study utilized zeta potential measurements to evaluate surface charges of modified carbonate powders (calcite, dolomite, and MEC). Surface characterization was performed using Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) with Energy-Dispersive X-ray Spectroscopy (EDS) for elemental analysis. Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM) was used to measure surface roughness and height. Optical microscopy was employed to observe mineral textures and precipitates. Experimental designs included varying the concentration of Mg2+ and SO4^2- ions in 50% diluted Arabian Gulf Seawater (AGSW).

## Data and Materials

The study utilized three types of carbonate samples: calcite, dolomite, and a 'Middle East Carbonate' (MEC) mixture. Samples were prepared as powders and subjected to various brine concentrations including AGSW, 50% diluted AGSW, and sequentially diluted brines (2x, 10x, 20x, 100x dilution).

## Key Findings

- Calcite and MEC samples exhibited negative surface charges in deionized water and NaCl brine due to the preferential dissolution of calcium ions from their surfaces. ([[2014-kasha|Kasha, 2014, p. 20]])
- Dolomite rocks showed positive surface charges in deionized water, attributed to a high concentration of cations on the rock lattice. ([[2014-kasha|Kasha, 2014, p. 20]])
- Adsorption of negatively charged carboxylic materials (from model oil) increased the magnitude of negative surface charges on calcite and MEC while decreasing the magnitude of positive charges on dolomite. ([[2014-kasha|Kasha, 2014, p. 21]])
- Increasing SO42- concentration increased the original negative surface charges of calcite and MEC and completely altered the surface charges of dolomite from positive to negative, indicating high affinity for carbonate surfaces. ([[2014-kasha|Kasha, 2014, p. 21]])
- Increasing Ca2+ and Mg2+ concentrations increased the magnitude of positive surface charges on dolomite but altered the original negative surface charges of calcite and MEC. ([[2014-kasha|Kasha, 2014, p. 21]])

## Limitations

Due to high roughness on MEC chips, no solid conclusion could be made regarding specific surface dissolution processes for that material using microscopy alone.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Conduct similar experiments on other carbonate minerals like magnesite, anhydrite, and gypsum.
- Study different types of carboxylic acids or crude oil compounds (asphaltenes, resins).
- Use high-resolution techniques to distinguish between organic and inorganic compounds on surfaces.
- Perform detailed solubility studies to assess the risk of scale precipitation.
