# Transmission Amplitude Variation with Offset (TAVO)

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the estimation of elastic parameters from transmission amplitude variation with offset (TAVO) using simplified, linearized versions of the Aki and Richards equations. It compares these new approximations to original models to evaluate their accuracy in determining properties like $\beta/\alpha$ and $\Delta\rho/\rho$ from both TPP and TPS wave modes.

---

## Research Problem

Standard AVO analysis relies on reflected waves, which are subject to attenuation, signal degradation, and complex modeling due as they travel through deeper formations. This study addresses the need for a more robust method by utilizing transmission amplitudes from VSP data to estimate elastic parameters while minimizing these undesirable effects.

## Objectives

- Derive appropriate approximations that make TAVO analysis similar to conventional AVO analysis.
- Invert derived equations relating transmission amplitudes to incidence angle for elastic parameter contrasts.
- Develop formulations that facilitate the use of TAVO with existing AVO technologies.
- Derive simplified mathematical expressions for TPP and TPS as functions of incidence angle only.
- Convert these equations into AVO-convenient forms (linear relations between amplitude and angle).
- Evaluate the accuracy of these new approximations against original Aki and Richards (2002) equations and full Zoeppritz equations using three test models.
- Derive new approximations for the transmission PP (TPP) and PS (TPS) coefficients based on Aki and Richards (2002) equations.
- Linearize these equations to facilitate the use of linear regression analysis for estimating elastic properties.
- Evaluate the accuracy and sensitivity of these new approximations compared to original models.
- Perform error propagation analysis to quantify the impact of fitting uncertainties on derived parameters.

## Methods

The author derived simplified expressions for TPP and TPS from the Aki and Richards (2002) equations by eliminating dependence on refracted angles. A Maclaurin series expansion was used to approximate the TPS equation into a 3-term sine series (C sin θ + D sin³θ + E sin⁵θ). The study also included a methodology for converting offset data from VSP surveys into incidence angles using both analytical and numerical methods, ultimately favoring an analytical approach. Synthetic data were generated using three test models (Model 1: oil sand/shale; Model 2: gas-sand/shale; Model 3: Gulf of Mexico gas-sand) to validate the approximations.

## Data and Materials

The study utilizes three distinct published simple reservoir models (Model 1, Model 2, and Model 3) to generate synthetic data for testing the proposed TAVO approximations. The analysis focuses on parameters such as P-wave velocity, S-wave velocity, and density across interfaces.

## Key Findings

- The study found that transmission P-wave amplitude versus offset response is similar for gas sand models but differs from the oil sand model's response. ([[2014-kola|Kola, 2014, p. 14]])
- All three models showed similar trends for the transmission converted S-wave (TPS) response, characterized by a general decrease of amplitude with offset. ([[2014-kola|Kola, 2014, p. 14]])
- Inversion resulted in a fairly good estimation for two parameters: average S-wave/P-wave velocity and S-wave velocity difference/average S-wave velocity up to an intermediate angle of incidence. ([[2014-kola|Kola, 2014, p. 15]])
- The inversion failed to recover the density difference/average density parameter with reasonable accuracy due to high non-linearity of the starting TPS equation and high dependence on higher-order terms. ([[2014-kola|Kola, 2014, p. 15]])
- TAVO is proposed as a method that avoids issues like amplitude loss from absorption, losses from deeper reflections, and wavelet non-stationarity caused by long travel paths in reflected waves. ([[2014-kola|Kola, 2014, p. 3]])
- The TPP approximation (Equation 3.4) showed a fit with the Aki and Richards expression within less than 0.001% across all models. ([[2014-kola|Kola, 2014, p. 40]])
- For TPS, the one-term approximation was accurate to within 10% up to an incidence angle of 55 degrees in Model 1. ([[2014-kola|Kola, 2014, p. 40]])
- The three-term TPS approximation (Equation 3.8) showed errors less than 10% for models where no critical angle exists up to 55 degrees. ([[2014-kola|Kola, 2014, p. 40]])
- In Model 2, the 3-term TPS approximation had an error of only 4.5% at a 45-degree incidence angle. ([[2014-kola|Kola, 2014, p. 40]])
- In Model 3, the 3-term TPS approximation had an error of approximately 4.0% at a 45-degree incidence angle. ([[2014-kola|Kola, 2014, p. 40]])
- The TPP equation was easily linearized without further approximation, resulting in nearly 0% error compared to the Aki and Richards model. ([[2014-kola|Kola, 2014, p. 40]])
- Model 1: P-wave velocity increased by 18%, S-wave velocity by 37%, and density decreased by 3.8% across the interface. ([[2014-kola|Kola, 2014, p. 42]])
- Model 2: P-wave velocity decreased by 20%, S-wave velocity increased by 31%, and density decreased by 11%. ([[2014-kola|Kola, 2014, p. 42]])
- Model 3: P-wave velocity decreased by 15%, S-wave velocity increased by 38%, and density decreased by 9%. ([[2014-kola|Kola, 2014, p. 42]])
- TPP approximation is easier to linearize and does not require further approximation compared to TPS, which requires additional steps for linearization. ([[2014-kola|Kola, 2014, p. 61]])

## Limitations

The analysis based on receiver gathers assumes a homogeneous medium and may not be suitable for cases involving lateral inhomogeneity or anisotropy. The study also notes that the TAVO method is specifically applicable to VSP data as presented in this work.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- More modeling studies should be carried out to obtain representative theoretical curves for offset-angle transformation.
- Other expansion methods should be considered for approximating and linearizing Zoeppritz equations to improve accuracy at higher angles of incidence.
- A common transmission point (CTP) sorting method should be developed to account for lateral heterogeneity and anisotropy, similar to the CDP concept in reflection AVO.
- The method should be extended to multi-layer models and tested with synthetic data from FWM software and real VSP data.
