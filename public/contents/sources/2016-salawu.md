# REMOVAL OF MERCURY (II) FROM WATER USING BIOSYNTHESIZED SILVER AND IRON NANOPARTICLES

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the synthesis of silver, iron oxide, and nanoscale zerovalent iron nanoparticles using plant extracts (Basil, Myrtle, and Aloe Vera) for the detection and removal of mercury (II) from water. The study concludes that biosynthesized silver nanoparticles are highly sensitive and selective tools for detecting Hg2+ and that adsorption efficiency is significantly influenced by pH, contact time, and adsorbent dosage.

---

## Research Problem

Mercury (II) is a highly toxic, persistent heavy metal pollutant in water systems due to its widespread use in industrial, medical, and agricultural sectors. Existing removal methods like activated carbon are often expensive or less effective at reaching low concentration limits required for safety.

## Methods

The study utilized three plant species (Basil, Myrtle, and Aloe Vera) to synthesize silver (AgNP), iron oxide (Fe2O3), and nanoscale zero-valent iron (nZVI). Characterization was performed using FE-SEM/EDX for morphology and elemental composition, FT-IR for functional groups, XRD for crystallinity, TGA for thermal stability, AFM for particle size, and BET for surface area. Colorimetric detection of Hg(II) was conducted via UV-vis spectroscopy. Batch adsorption experiments were designed to test variables like pH, time, and dosage. A filter membrane was fabricated using the electrospinning technique (using a NANON apparatus) to create a polymer/nanoparticle composite. Fixed-bed adsorption studies were performed in a filtration cartridge, with data analyzed using Minitab 16 for multi-factor analysis.

## Data and Materials

Chemicals included Silver nitrate (99.8%), Sodium chloride, Calcium chloride, Barium chloride, Zinc nitrate hexahydrate, Nickel chloride, Manganese sulphate hydrate, Mercury chloride, Copper sulphate, Potassium chloride, Hydrochloric acid, Sodium hydroxide, Nitric acid, and Iron III chloride hexa-hydrate. Plant materials: 40g of Basil leaves/flowers, 40g of Myrtle leaves, and 75g of Aloe Vera. Analytical equipment included a Lyra3 TESCAN FESEM, Nicolet 6700 FT-IR, Rigaku Miniflex II XRD, Agilent 5100 AFM, Micromeritics ASAP 2020, and Specord 50 UV-vis spectrophotometer.

## Key Findings

- Biosynthesized silver nanoparticles (AgNP) showed distinct peaks in XRD analysis confirming their formation. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 75]])
- FT-IR spectra of AgNP confirmed the presence of functional groups involved in the stabilization and reduction of particles. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 73]])
- The colorimetric method using AgNPs showed a clear response to Hg(II) compared to other metal ions, allowing for selective detection. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 80]])
- Adsorption of Hg(II) by AgNP followed both Langmuir and Freundlich isotherm models. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 98]])
- Fe2O3 and nZVI also showed significant adsorption capacities for mercury removal. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 102]])
- The inclusion of nanoparticles in the electrospun membrane was confirmed by SEM and EDX mapping. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 114]])
- AgNPs showed high selectivity for detecting Hg2+ alone compared to other cations. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 20]])
- The limit of detection for Hg2+ using biosynthesized silver nanoparticles was as low as 6.25 x 10^-8 mol L-1. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 20]])
- Adsorption efficiency increased with both time and agitation speed. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 21]])
- Adsorption efficiency increased when pH of the solution increased from 4 to 7, but decreased as pH reached 8 and 9. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 21]])
- Increased adsorbent dosage significantly affected the percentage removal of Hg2+. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 21]])
- Biosynthesized nanoparticles are more environmentally benign and easier to synthesize compared to chemically synthesized nanoparticles. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 43]])
- The color change in silver nanoparticles used as a colorimetric sensor is associated with surface plasmon resonance. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 45]])
- Adsorption of mercury (II) by silver particles results in the formation of a mercury-silver amalgam, where the ratio of mercury to silver changes from 1:2 to 1.125:1 as particle size decreases. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 54]])
- Iron oxide nanoparticles exhibit super-paramagnetic properties that allow for the removal of heavy metals like chromium VI, cadmium, copper, and arsenic from wastewater. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 54]])

## Limitations

The study's adsorption modeling was limited to the two most common models (Langmuir and Freundlich). The detection limit for biogenic synthesized nanoparticles remains higher than that of chemically synthesized and modified silver nanoparticles.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Test real water samples from contaminated sites to verify practical application.
- Investigate other environmentally friendly polymer materials and insoluble membranes.
- Explore different types of biosynthesized nanoparticles for mercury removal.
- Investigate the functionalization of adsorbents with biomaterials like Moringa oleifera.
