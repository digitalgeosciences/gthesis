# REMOVAL OF MERCURY (II) FROM WATER USING BIOSYNTHESIZED SILVER AND IRON NANOPARTICLES

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the synthesis and characterization of biosynthesized silver (AgNP), iron oxide (Fe2O3), and nanoscale zero-valent iron (nZVI) nanoparticles using plant extracts from Basil, Myrtle, and Aloe Vera. These nanoparticles were evaluated as both colorimetric sensors for mercury (II) detection and as adsorbents in a fixed-bed system to remove mercury from water.

---

## Research Problem

Mercury (II) is a highly toxic, persistent heavy metal that poses significant risks to human health and the environment, requiring effective and environmentally friendly removal technologies, particularly in regions with scarce water resources like Saudi Arabia.

## Objectives

- To demonstrate the removal of mercury II from water using biosynthesis silver and iron oxide nanoparticles in batch adsorption processes
- To study the effect of solution conditions such as pH, contact time, mixing rate, mercury (II) concentration and biosynthesized nanoparticle dosage in the adsorption efficiency
- To design a filter membrane for the removal of mercury (II) from water
- To appraise the adsorption isotherms and kinetics of the experimental data under optimum treatment conditions
- Synthesize silver, iron oxide, and nanoscale zero-valent iron nanoparticles using plant extracts (Basil, Myrtle, and Aloe Vera).
- Characterize the physical and chemical properties of the synthesized nanoparticles using FE-SEM, EDX, FT-IR, XRD, TGA, and BET analysis.

## Methods

Nanoparticles were synthesized via plant extract reduction: AgNPs from Basil, Fe2O3 from Myrtle, and nZVI from Aloe Vera. Characterization included FE-SEM/EDX for morphology and elemental composition, FT-IR for functional group identification, XRD for crystallinity, TGA for thermal stability, and BET for surface area/porosity. UV-vis spectroscopy was used to monitor the reduction process and perform colorimetric sensing of Hg(II). A fixed-bed adsorption system was constructed using an electrospun membrane (polyacrylonitrile) embedded with nanoparticles. Batch experiments were conducted to optimize pH, contact time, and dosage. Minitab 16 was used for experimental design and kinetic/isotherm modeling.

## Data and Materials

Materials included various metal salts (AgNO3, FeCl3·6H2O), plant extracts (Basil, Myrtle, Aloe Vera), and a polyacrylonitrile polymer. Analytical data included UV-vis spectra of multiple concentrations of Hg(II) and other metals, XRD patterns for three nanoparticle types, and adsorption data from batch tests (varying pH 4-9, time up to 120 min, dosages up to 40 mg) and fixed-bed studies.

## Key Findings

- AgNPs showed high selectivity for detecting Hg2+ alone compared to other cations and high sensitivity at different concentrations of Hg2+. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 20]])
- The limit of detection for Hg2+ was as low as 6.25 x 10^-8 mol L-1 using biosynthesized silver nanoparticles. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 20]])
- Adsorption efficiency increased with time and agitation speed. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 21]])
- Adsorption efficiency increased when pH of the solution increased from 4 to 7 but decreased when pH reached 8 and 9. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 21]])
- The removal of mercury (II) via amalgamation is enhanced as silver particle size decreases, with a ratio change from 1:2 to 1.125:1. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 54]])
- Nanofiber filters can offer up to a 100-150 times increase in surface area compared to standard membranes, potentially lasting twice as long. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 55]])
- AgNPs showed a distinct color change from brown to dark brown during synthesis, with the peak shifting toward 430-440 nm at 240 minutes, indicating successful reduction. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 80]])
- The optimal conditions for AgNP formation were found at pH 10, using a 2mM AgNO3 solution and a 1:25 plant extract to precursor ratio. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 86]])
- FE-SEM analysis confirmed that all three types of nanoparticles (AgNP, Fe2O3, nZVI) formed nanoclusters/agglomerates with sizes ranging between 30 and 50 nm. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 88]])
- BET surface area measurements were: AgNP (14.03 m2/g), Fe2O3 (14.76 m2/g), and nZVI (15.17 m2/g). ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 92]])
- TGA analysis showed that nZVI experienced a more rapid weight loss in the first 100°C compared to Fe2O3 and AgNP, likely due to water loss. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 93]])
- FT-IR confirmed functional groups such as O-H (3400 cm-1), C=O (1637 cm-1), and Fe-O (624 cm-1) in the respective nanoparticles. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 95]])
- AgNPs demonstrated high selectivity for Hg(II); other metal ions (Ca, Cu, Mn, Ni, Na, Zn, Ba, K) showed no significant color change or absorbance shift. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 101]])
- The detection limit of mercury using AgNPs was determined to be 6.25 x 10^-8 M (62.5 nM). ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 110]])
- Adsorption equilibrium for Hg(II) was reached within 120 minutes for all tested adsorbents. ([[2016-salawu|Salawu, 2016, p. 111]])

## Limitations

The mercury (II) detection limit for these biogenic synthesized nanoparticles remains higher than the limits for chemically synthesized and modified silver nanoparticles used in colorimetric Hg (II).

## Recommendations and Future Work

Recommendations include: 1. Testing real water samples from contaminated sites; 2. Exploring other environmentally friendly polymer materials and insoluble membranes; 3. Testing other types of biosynthesized nanoparticles; 4. Functionalizing the adsorbent with biomaterials like Moringa oleifera.
