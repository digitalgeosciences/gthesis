# SAND CONSOLIDATION USING ENZYME-INDUCED CARBONATE PRECIPITATION

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the use of Enzyme-Induced Carbonate Precipitation (EICP) as an environmentally friendly method for sand consolidation in oil and gas reservoirs. It evaluates the impact of temperature, reactant concentration, and biopolymer additives on the precipitation yield, mineralogy, and resulting mechanical strength of various sand types.

---

## Research Problem

Sand production poses significant risks to infrastructure and safety in the upstream oil and gas industry; while chemical consolidation is a common mitigation strategy, many existing methods have environmental drawbacks or limited effectiveness under high-temperature reservoir conditions.

## Objectives

- To achieve environmentally friendly method of chemical sand consolidation
- To achieve optimum EICP reactant and catalyzer concentration in terms of temperature stability, efficiency, and strength
- Investigate EICP as an environmentally friendly sand consolidating agent
- Determine parameters affecting EICP performance
- Evaluate the impact of enzyme concentration on precipitation efficiency
- Assess the effect of temperature on urea hydrolysis and enzyme activity

## Methods

The study utilized ICP-OES to monitor ionic consumption (Ca and Mg) as a proxy for precipitation progress. XRD was used to determine the mineralogy of precipitates. A WOMBAT scratcher was employed to measure the Unconfined Compressive Strength (UCS) of treated sand samples. Sand samples were categorized by size (Fine, Medium, Coarse, Mix). Biopolymers (xanthan gum) were added to improve spatial control and durability. The experimental design included varying reactant concentrations (M2, M3, M4) and catalyst volumes (V1, V2, V3) across a temperature range of 25°C to 90°C.

## Data and Materials

The study included samples of fine, medium, and coarse sand. Experimental conditions included enzyme concentrations ranging from 0.5 to 3 M/L (or specific ppm values), temperatures up to 90°C, and the addition of xanthan gum in varying amounts.

## Key Findings

- EICP reaction can still progress and yield adequate amount of precipitation in temperatures up to 90 °C, despite the degradation of the catalyzer's performance. ([[2023-omarov|Omarov, 2023, p. 11]])
- High-temperature environments can favor economic feasibility by reducing the required amount of high-cost catalyst. ([[2023-omarov|Omarov, 2023, p. 11]])
- Xanthan gum addition resulted in at least 2 times stronger consolidation compared to base solutions, with strength increasing from 40 psi to 324 psi (lower concentration) and 133 to 388 psi (higher concentration). ([[2023-omarov|Omarov, 2023, p. 12]])
- High-concentration EICP solution with added polymer successfully consolidated sand samples uniformly with an average of 2500 psi. ([[2023-omarov|Omarov, 2023, p. 12]])
- M4V1 (low concentration, high catalyst ratio) showed the highest efficiency in terms of ion conversion, reaching nearly 80% for Ca ions. ([[2023-omarov|Omarov, 2023, p. 56]])
- page_number_not_specified_in_source ([[2023-omarov|Omarov, 2023]])
- Enzymes from jack urease bean improved precipitation efficiency; however, higher concentration solutions showed lower efficiency but produced more total precipitate. ([[2023-omarov|Omarov, 2023, p. 59]])
- High concentration EICP (3 M/L) yielded significantly higher strength, reaching approximately 3000 psi, which is at least 3 times stronger than its nearest followers. ([[2023-omarov|Omarov, 2023, p. 61]])
- The addition of xanthan gum improved consolidation strength by at least 2 times (e.g., from 179 psi to 503 psi or higher). ([[2023-omarov|Omarov, 2023, p. 61]])
- Consolidation was non-uniform in coarse and medium sand, with the highest strength concentrated at the bottom of the sample. ([[2023-omarov|Omarov, 2023, p. 62]])
- Fine sand samples achieved more uniform consolidation but generally lower strength compared to high-concentration medium/coarse samples. ([[2023-omarov|Omarov, 2023, p. 66]])
- Temperatures up to 90°C are favorable for high concentration EICP solutions due to improved jack urease bean performance and potential urea hydrolysis without a catalyst. ([[2023-omarov|Omarov, 2023, p. 69]])
- For high concentration solutions (240,000 ppm), Mg is less reactive than Ca2+, making it impractical to use both ions simultaneously for effective precipitation. ([[2023-omarov|Omarov, 2023, p. 69]])

## Limitations

The study noted that while xanthan gum increased strength, it did not significantly improve the uniformity of consolidation despite increasing viscosity. Additionally, some coarse sand samples cracked during curing due to insufficient binding between large particles.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Design controllable EICP reactions for long-distance delivery.
- Conduct research on surface charge studies to enhance plugging in highly porous media.
- Investigate different biopolymer types and their hydrolysis rates.
