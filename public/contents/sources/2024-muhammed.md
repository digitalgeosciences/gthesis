# Hydrogen Storage in Depleted Gas Reservoirs

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the feasibility of hydrogen storage in depleted gas reservoirs by evaluating the geochemical and petrophysical impacts of H2 and various cushion gases (CH4, N2, CO2) on sandstone samples. It concludes that while carbonate minerals like dolomite undergo dissolution, clay minerals can cause pore blockage, and specific cushion gas formulations are optimal for maintaining reservoir integrity.

---

## Research Problem

Hydrogen storage in porous media faces challenges due like its high reactivity with minerals and potential loss through chemical reactions or microbial activity; the study addresses these issues by evaluating two gas formulations (CH4 and N2 cushions) in a clay-rich sandstone reservoir under realistic conditions.

## Objectives

- Investigate surface phenomena (rock wettability and interfacial tension) at reservoir conditions for optimum cushion gas selection in a 2-phase system.
- Study the impact of losses due to hydrogen and cushion gas injection from the optimum 2-phase design.
- Examine the effect of loss caused by microbial by-product (acetic acid) in the presence of cushion gas from the optimum 2-phase design.
- Conduct a core flooding experiment to investigate the storage and withdrawal capacity of the injected hydrogen gas using the optimum 2-phase design.
- Evaluate the performance of different cushion gas formulations (CO2, N2, and CH4) for hydrogen storage.
- Investigate geochemical losses of hydrogen in various cushion gas environments.

## Methods

The study utilized several experimental stages: 1. Surface phenomena investigation: Measuring contact angle (CA) and surface tension (ST) for three cushion gas formulations (CH4, N2, CO2) across a range of pressures (500-3000 psi), temperatures (30-70°C), and salinities (2-20 wt.%). 2. Core analysis: Using thin-section petrography, XRD, XRF, SEM-EDX, and acoustic measurements to evaluate rock properties before/after treatment with H2+cushium gas in brine. 3. Geochemical & Microbial studies: Assessing mineralogical changes and hydrogen loss using FTIR, XRD, SEM-EDX, pH, and ion analysis under specific conditions (42°C, 1350 psi). 4. Core flooding: Evaluating storage/withdrawal capacity of H2 with different cushion gases at various injection rates (0.2 to 10 cc/min) using a core holder and LCR system.

## Data and Materials

Experimental data from three sets of gas mixtures (CH4, N2, and CO2) as cushion gases; 7 distinct test cases for CH4; 4 mix types for N2; and 4 mixture types for CO2. Samples included pristine quartz (>98.99% purity). Measurements were conducted across a range of pressures (500-3000 psi), temperatures (30-70 °C), and salinities (2-20 wt.%).

## Key Findings

- Formulation I (CH4 cushion) was identified as the best for maintaining high gas column at investigated pressures/temperatures, followed by N2 then CO2. ([[2024-muhammed|Muhammed, 2024, p. 34]])
- N2 cushion gas showed lower hydrogen loss (1.3% vs 3%) in microbial byproduct experiments compared to CH4. ([[2024-muhammed|Muhammed, 2024, p. 36]])
- CO2 cushion gas yielded the highest recovery percentage (34.35%) at high injection rates (1.2-10 cc/min). ([[2024-muhammed|Muhammed, 2024, p. 34]])
- At lower injection rates (0.2 to 0.6 cc/min), CH4 cushion gas formulation exhibited the highest recovery (46.33%). ([[2024-muhammed|Muhammed, 2024, p. 284]])
- The optimal scenario for H2 injection and withdrawal was determined to be a mixture of 60–70% H2 with a corresponding 30–40% CH4 fraction. ([[2024-muhammed|Muhammed, 2024, p. 205]])
- Depleted hydrocarbon reservoirs are the most cost-effective option for large-scale UHS, accounting for 74.2% of existing facilities due to known geology and infrastructure. ([[2024-muhammed|Muhammed, 2024, p. 30]])
- Hydrogen has a much higher mobility and lower viscosity compared to CH4 and CO2, leading to significant challenges in containment and potential loss via diffusion/dispersion. ([[2024-muhammed|Muhammed, 2024, p. 13]])
- N2 and CO2 are preferred as cushion gases over CH4 due to superior gas wettability and ability to mitigate hydrogen loss through dissolution. ([[2024-muhammed|Muhammed, 2024, p. 13]])
- Salt caverns offer high impermeability, with estimated gas losses through well leaks being less than 1%. ([[2024-muhammed|Muhammed, 2024, p. 37]])
- Core flooding experiments were conducted on Bandera grey sandstone to evaluate the effect of injection rate and cushion gas type on hydrogen recovery. ([[2024-muhammed|Muhammed, 2024, p. 36]])
- For CH4 cushion gas, contact angle (CA) measurements ranged from 20 to 41° regardless of reservoir pressure, temperature, or salinity. ([[2024-muhammed|Muhammed, 2024, p. 74]])
- Surface tension (ST) for CH4 systems decreased with increasing temperature and pressure but increased with higher salinity. ([[2024-muhammed|Muhammed, 2024, p. 74]])
- Test cases 3 and 4, featuring H2 (50-60%) and CH4 (30-40%) fractions, were identified as optimal for CH4 cushion gas based on CA and ST. ([[2024-muhammed|Muhammed, 2024, p. 74]])
- For CO2 cushion gas, CA ranged from 29 to 51° and was independent of pressure, temperature, or salinity; it increased with pressure but decreased with higher temperature. ([[2024-muhammed|Muhammed, 2024, p. 91]])
- Mixtures 2 and 3 (H2: 40-60%, N2: 30-50%) were identified as the optimal cushion gas for N2 systems under studied conditions. ([[2024-muhammed|Muhammed, 2024, p. 91]])

## Limitations

The study notes that while some results are promising, the use of pristine quartz as a proxy for sandstone may simplify the impact of brine droplet spreading on measurement accuracy. Additionally, it mentions that pure hydrogen storage in aquifers has not yet been tested on the ground.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Use formation water instead of simple brine; use reservoir plugs from different rock types to account for heterogeneity; investigate effects of pressure, temperature, and salinity using real oil; study H2 losses through geochemical and microbial experiments; investigate in-situ microbe presence (e.g., H2S).
