# EVALUATING THE REMOVAL EFFICIENCY OF SEWAGE SLUDGE-DERIVED ACTIVATED CARBON FOR METHYL-TERT BUTYL ETHER CONTAMINATED - WATER.

**Summary**: The thesis investigated the synthesis and characterization of activated carbon derived from sewage sludge (SDAC) to treat water contaminated with Methyl Tert-Butyl Ether (MTBE). The study utilized chemical activation with zinc chloride followed by pyrolysis, evaluating the material's performance through BET, TGA, SEM-EDX, and FTIR analyses.

---

## Research Problem

The accumulation of MTBE in groundwater and other water sources poses significant environmental risks, requiring efficient and cost-effective removal technologies using sustainable materials like sewage sludge-derived activated carbon (SDAC).

## Methods

The study involved synthesizing SDAC by chemically activating sewage sludge with zinc chloride followed by pyrolysis. Characterization techniques included BET (surface area), TGA (thermal stability), SEM-EDX (morphology/composition), and FTIR (functional groups). Batch adsorption experiments were conducted to evaluate MTBE removal under varying conditions: pH (3-9), contact time (30-220 min), dosage (0.5-4 g/L), initial concentration (0.5-10 ppm), agitation speed (50-200 rpm), and temperature (15-40 °C). Adsorption behavior was modeled using Langmuir and Freundlich isotherms, and pseudo-first and pseudo-second-order kinetic models.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized sewage sludge as the primary raw material to produce SDAC. Comparison was made against commercial activated carbon (CAC). Experimental data included measurements of MTBE concentration in aqueous solutions across various parameters: pH levels 3, 6, and 9; contact times up to 220 minutes; adsorbent dosages from 0.5 to 4 g/L; initial concentrations from 0.5 to 10 ppm; agitation speeds from 50 to 200 rpm; and temperatures from 15 to 40 °C.

## Key Findings

- The surface area values for the four adsorbents after acid washing were 451 m2/g (3K-700-30), 385 m2/g (2Z-700-90), 53 m2/g (2K-500-90), and 38 m2/g (2Z-500-30). ([[2018-adeyemi|Adeyemi, 2018, p. 12]])
- SEM-EDX analysis confirmed that minerals (Fe, Al, Mg, Mn, Ca, Na) and biological components were removed by higher pyrolytic temperatures and acid-washing. ([[2018-adeyemi|Adeyemi, 2018, p. 12]])
- FTIR results identified hydroxyl groups, carboxylates, and aliphatic -CH3 as the primary functional groups of the adsorbent. ([[2018-adeyemi|Adeyemi, 2018]])

- Adsorbent 2Z-700-90 was selected as the best performer with a yield of 70%, thermal stability of 50%, and a BET surface area of 385 m2/g. ([[2018-adeyemi|Adeyemi, 2018, p. 12]])
- The removal efficiency of MTBE by adsorbent 2Z-700-90 reached 70% under optimal conditions: pH 6, agitation speed of 150 rpm, contact time of 60 minutes, dose of 2g/L, and initial concentration of 1 ppm. ([[2018-adeyemi|Adeyemi, 2018, p. 12]])
- The adsorption behavior of the SDAC followed a pseudo-second-order kinetic model. ([[2018-adeyemi|Adeyemi, 2018, p. 12]])
- Chemical activation using ZnCl2 at 800°C for 120 min produced activated carbon with a BET surface area of 1092 m²/g. ([[2018-adeyemi|Adeyemi, 2018, p. 22]])
- The highest reported BET surface area for physically activated sludge using steam was 226 m²/g. ([[2018-adeyemi|Adeyemi, 2018, p. 20]])
- Washing char with HCl before activation increased the BET surface area from 7 to 269 m²/g. ([[2018-adeyemi|Adeyemi, 2018, p. 20]])
- Fenton reagent was found to be highly effective, degrading 99% of initial MTBE concentration (15mM) in 120 min at pH 2.8. ([[2018-adeyemi|Adeyemi, 2018, p. 31]])
- MTBE is a water-soluble compound that persists in soil and water, with mean concentrations in groundwater often below 0.2 μg/l (94.7% of samples). ([[2018-adeyemi|Adeyemi, 2018, p. 24]])
- Raw sludge analysis showed a moisture content of 84.65% for fresh samples and 15.58% for dry samples. ([[2018-adeyemi|Adeyemi, 2018, p. 35]])
- The raw sludge contained various heavy metals, with Zinc (Zn) at 0.845 ppm in dry sludge and Copper (Cu) at 0.455 ppm. ([[2018-adeyemi|Adeyemi, 2018, p. 36]])
- TOC content of the dry sludge was measured at 27,325 mg/kg. ([[2018-adeyemi|Adeyemi, 2018, p. 37]])

## Limitations

The study notes that the presence of natural organic matter can slow down adsorption kinetics and reduce capacity in natural water; also, some chemical processes like biodegradation are hindered by the presence of other compounds.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study suggests that developing a cost-effective, environmentally friendly, and sustainable use of SDAC will be highly significant for managing waste and treating MTBE contamination.
