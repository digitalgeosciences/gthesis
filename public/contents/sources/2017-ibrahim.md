# Reaction Kinetics of Carbonate Rocks with Chelating Agents/Seawater System

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the reaction kinetics and coreflooding performance of three chelating agents (GLDA, DTPA, and EDTA) diluted with both deionized water and seawater in carbonate rocks (Indiana limestone and Austin chalk). It concludes that while seawater reduces reaction rates compared to fresh water due to high salt content, these systems offer a safer alternative to HCl for deep gas well stimulation.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the limitations of using HCl in high-pressure/high-temperature (HPHT) carbonate reservoirs, specifically regarding corrosion, rapid reaction rates, and formation damage from salt precipitation when using seawater. It evaluates chelating agents as safer alternatives to determine their effectiveness in creating stable wormholes.

## Objectives

- Defining the reaction controlling process for each fluid system whether mass transfer or surface reaction limited.
- Studying the effect of seawater on the reaction rate of chelating agents with calcite.
- Comparing the results with the results available in the literature for the same chelating agents when diluted with deionized water.
- Investigating the effect of porosity type rock facies on the reaction parameters by using rocks with same porosity, lithology and designed different facies.
- Determine the reaction kinetics of carbonate rocks with various chelating agents (GLDA, EDTA, DTPA) in fresh water and seawater systems.
- Identify the rate-limiting step (mass transfer vs. surface reaction) for different acid systems using RDA.

## Methods

The study utilizes a Rotating Disk Apparatus (RDA) to measure dissolution rates of carbonate disks. A 1-inch thick disk is spun at various RPMs in a reaction vessel under 1000 psi pressure to keep CO2 in solution. Samples are taken every 2 minutes to measure calcium concentration over time. The dissolution rate (R) is calculated from the slope of the Ca concentration vs. time plot. Comparison between DI and seawater systems identifies if salt ions inhibit reactions. Coreflooding experiments were used to validate the optimal injection rates for wormhole propagation.

## Data and Materials

The study uses carbonate rock samples including Indiana limestone (bioclastic grainstone), Austin chalk, and others. Three chelating agents are tested: GLDA (20 wt%), DTPA (15 wt%), and EDTA (15 wt%). Experiments were conducted at temperatures of 150°F, 200°F, and 250°F with pressures of 1000 psi.

## Key Findings

- The reaction of GLDA with Indiana limestone was surface reaction limited at 150°F but became mass transfer limited as temperature increased to 200°F and 250°F due to the high concentration of solutes in the system. ([[2017-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2017, p. 99]])
- The reaction between 3.8 pH 20% GLDA/SW and Austin chalk is surface reaction limited at 200°F. ([[2017-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2017, p. 20]])
- An optimum injection rate of 0.43 cm3/min was calculated using a mathematical model for 20 wt.% GLDA/SW at 1000 psi and 250°F, compared to 0.5 cm3/min from coreflooding experiments. ([[2017-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2017, p. 20]])
- The presence of salts (from seawater) reduced the dissolution rate of lactic acid in Indiana limestone at high RPMs. ([[2017-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2017, p. 32]])
- Dissolution rates for EDTA and DTPA are influenced by the kinetics of chelation and change with both type of chelating agent and pH. ([[2017-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2017, p. 32]])
- The reaction rate of carbonate rocks with HCl is limited by Hydrogen ions diffusion to the rock surface, whereas sandstones are only slightly acid soluble. ([[2017-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2017, p. 23]])
- The diffusion coefficient for GLDA/SW with Indiana limestone was significantly lower than GLDA/DI because salt content in seawater creates a higher resistance to mass transfer. ([[2017-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2017, p. 100]])
- HCl prepared in seawater caused formation damage (calcium sulfate scaling) that plugged pore throats smaller than 5 μm, resulting in an ICN ratio of 0 for the interconnected systems. ([[2017-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2017, p. 80]])
- Chelating agents (EDTA and GLDA) showed high ICN ratios (e.g., 89% for EDTA), indicating they do not cause formation damage even when diluted in seawater. ([[2017-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2017, p. 82]])
- The diffusion coefficient of GLDA/SW with Austin chalk was an order of magnitude higher than that of GLDA/SW with Indiana limestone at 200°F and 1000 psi. ([[2017-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2017, p. 86]])
- SEM analysis showed that GLDA/SW produced a more uniform reaction on the rock surface compared to GLDA/DI, which only reacted with fine grains. ([[2017-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2017, p. 102]])
- The reaction of GLDA/SW with Indiana limestone was mass transfer limited at all temperatures (150-250°F), while GLDA/DI became mass transfer limited only above 150°F. ([[2017-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2017, p. 100]])
- Austin chalk, being more porous and less consolidated than Indiana limestone, showed higher reaction rates and was suitable for stimulation with lower concentrations of chelating agents. ([[2017-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2017, p. 108]])
- The optimal injection rate for GLDA/SW in a 6-inch core was determined to be 0.5 cm3/min based on the pressure drop ratio (PDR). ([[2017-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2017, p. 114]])
- DTPA/SW showed a much lower corrosion rate of 0.0034 g/cm2 in 6 hours compared to HCl's 0.205 g/cm2, well below the industry limit of 0.0244 g/cm2. ([[2017-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2017, p. 130]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

The results will help design successful HP/HT stimulation treatments using rotating disk results at the same lithology, pore geometry, pressure and temperature of the treated formations without any need for tedious coreflooding experiments.
