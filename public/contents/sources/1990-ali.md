# The Effect of High Temperature and Aging on Water-Base Drilling Fluids

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of high temperature (up to 490°F) and extended aging times (up to 30 days) on the rheological properties of water-based drilling fluids. Using a Fann Model 70 HTHP Viscometer and Baroid Roller Oven, the study concludes that while temperature reduces viscosity and yield point, aging time increases these values but shows diminishing effects at higher ages.

---

## Research Problem

Drilling fluid property changes due to elevated temperature and aging time frequently cause problems in drilling deep wells, specifically regarding rheological stability under high-temperature (490°F) and long aging conditions.

## Objectives

- Investigate the effect of both high temperature and long aging time on the rheological properties of water-base drilling fluids.
- Evaluate the performance of a specific mud formulation containing bentonite, attapulgite, sodium chloride, lime, gypsum, and VSVA in fresh water.
- To study the effect of temperature on the rheological properties (viscosity, yield point, gel strength) of water-base drilling fluids.
- To evaluate the shear stress-shear rate relationship under varying temperatures and aging times.

## Methods

The study utilized two primary pieces of equipment: (1) Baroid Roller Oven to simulate high-temperature aging by rolling the mud in an aging cell at temperatures up to 490°F for durations of 0, 1, 3, 7, 10, 15, 20, and 30 days. (2) Fann Model 70 HTHP Viscometer to measure rheological properties (effective viscosity, plastic viscosity, yield point, gel strength, and shear stress-shear rate relationship) at temperatures ranging from 77°F to 490°F under a constant pressure of 3000 psig. The mud formulation included bentonite, attapulgite, sodium chloride, lime, gypsum, and VSVA (vinylsulfonate/vinylamide).

## Data and Materials

The study used a specific drilling fluid formulation consisting of: 100g fresh water; 2g Wyoming bentonite; 3g attapulgite; 1.5g VSVA; 3g sodium chloride; 0.85g gypsum; and 0.28g calcium hydroxide (lime). Measurements were taken at 8 different aging intervals (0, 1, 3, 7, 10, 15, 20, 30 days) and 6 specific temperatures (77, 122, 212, 302, 392, and 490°F).

## Key Findings

- Effective viscosity, plastic viscosity, yield point, and gel strength all decrease gradually with the increase in temperature for all aging times. ([[1990-ali|Ali, 1990, p. 61]])
- Shear stress for a particular temperature increases with the increase in shear rate, but shear stress at a given shear rate decreases with the increase in temperature. ([[1990-ali|Ali, 1990, p. 68]])
- Viscosity, yield point, and gel strength at a given temperature increase with aging time, while aging effects are diminishing with the increase in aging time. ([[1990-ali|Ali, 1990, p. 14]])
- Shear stress at a given study shear rate increases with the increase in aging time, and aging effects are decreasing with the increase in aging time. ([[1990-ali|Ali, 1990, p. 91]])

- The power law index 'n' was found to be always less than unity, indicating pseudoplastic (shear-thinning) behavior. ([[1990-ali|Ali, 1990, p. 14]])
- The addition of VSVA (vinylsulfonate/vinylamide) provided stability for the drilling fluid at temperatures up to 490°F and in the presence of high electrolyte concentrations. ([[1990-ali|Ali, 1990, p. 32]])
- The inclusion of attapulgite helped compensate for the effects of temperature on clay minerals, providing some protection against dehydration. ([[1990-ali|Ali, 1990, p. 76]])
- Effective viscosity, plastic viscosity, yield point, and gel strengths (10 sec. and 10 min.) decrease with the increase in temperature. ([[1990-ali|Ali, 1990, p. 91]])
- Shear stress at a given shear rate decreases with the increase in temperature, and temperature effects are decreasing with the increase in temperature. ([[1990-ali|Ali, 1990, p. 91]])
- Aging effects on effective viscosity, plastic viscosity, yield point, and gel strengths are diminishing with the increase in aging time. ([[1990-ali|Ali, 1990, p. 91]])
- The values of exponent of the power-law index (n) were between 0.525 and 0.536, indicating that the fluids follow a pseudoplastic model. ([[1990-ali|Ali, 1990, p. 86]])
- Temperature effects on shear stress-shear rate are very small after 392°F. ([[1990-ali|Ali, 1990, p. 77]])

## Limitations

The study notes that the roller oven, while useful for aging, may not perfectly simulate bottom hole conditions compared to a dynamic flow loop. Also, only limited aging times (up to 30 days) were studied.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Incorporate measuring techniques for filtration and corrosion rates as they are critical properties of drilling fluid.
- Use an HTHP dynamic flow loop for circulation instead of a Baroid Roller Oven for better simulation of bottom hole conditions.
- Extend the study to different pressures to evaluate pressure effects on the drilling fluid.
