# POTENTIAL SOURCES AND DISTRIBUTION OF NITRATE IN THE GROUNDWATER OF THE WASIA AQUIFER, AL-KHARJ AREA, CENTRAL SAUDI ARABIA

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the sources and distribution of nitrate in the Wasia aquifer within the Al-Kharj area, central Saudi Arabia. Using dual nitrate isotopes (δ15N–NO3 and δ18O–NO3) and tritium analysis, it identifies atmospheric deposition and fertilizers as primary nitrate sources in the north/east, while manure and wastewater dominate the west.

---

## Research Problem

The presence of high nitrate concentrations in the Wasia aquifer groundwater in Al-Kharj area was known but the specific sources (natural vs. anthropogenic) were not clearly identified despite some wells being far from human activity.

## Objectives

- To investigate nitrate sources in the study area by analyzing the isotopes (15N-NO3 and 18O-NO3).
- To assess nitrate concentrations and distribution in the study area.
- To estimate recent recharge date, based Tritium 3H.
- Assess variations of major ion and trace element concentrations in the groundwater of the Wasia aquifer.
- Evaluate water quality for domestic and agricultural uses.
- Identify and differentiate between potential sources of nitrate contamination (atmospheric deposition, synthetic fertilizers, and manure/animal wastes).

## Methods

The study employed stable isotope analysis of nitrogen (δ15N) and oxygen (δ18O) to identify nitrate sources. Tritium (3H) was measured using Liquid Scintillation Counting (LSC) to determine recharge history. X-ray Fluorescence (XRF) was used to analyze rock samples for inherent nitrogen content. Statistical analysis, including T-tests and F-tests, was performed to compare the means and variances of different nitrate source groups.

## Data and Materials

34 groundwater wells sampled for four parameters each; 17 outcrop rock samples collected from the Wasia formation; chemical data including 20+ ions (e.g., Na+, Cl-, NO3-) and trace metals (B, Li, Fe).

## Key Findings

- Nitrate concentrations reached up to 395.2 mg/l in some wells with an average of 68.2 mg/l. ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 41]])
- TDS values averaged 3032 mg/l, with 100% of samples exceeding the permissible limit for drinking water (500 mg/l). ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 41]])
- Total hardness was high on average (1165.2 mg/l), and 100% of samples were classified as very hard. ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 41]])
- Nitrate isotopes revealed that atmospheric deposition and fertilizers are the potential sources in the north and eastern sections, while manure and wastewater are primary contributors in the western section. ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 13]])
- Only two samples showed tritium rates above the detection limit of 0.8 TU, suggesting a mixture of recent and connate waters. ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 13]])
- All groundwater samples were considered appropriate for irrigation based on SAR (all <26), MR (<50%), and KR (>1). ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 47]])
- 100% of the sampled groundwater is considered corrosive (CR > 1) and requires attention regarding transport pipe selection. ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 50]])
- 82.4% of analyzed waters were classified as unsuitable for irrigation based on the Permeability Index (PI > 25%). ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 51]])
- A strong correlation (r > 0.9) was found between TDS and several ions including Na+, Cl-, Ca2+, Mg2+, NO3-, and SO42-. ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 53]])
- PCA showed that Factor 1 (explaining 57.46% of variance) had high loadings for TDS, EC, TH, Na+, Ca2+, Mg2+, Cl-, F-, Br-, SO42-, and NO3-. ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 56]])
- 63% of the studied samples fell within the isotopic range of synthetic nitrate fertilizers (δ15N: -5.3 to +7.99‰, δ18O: +16.74 to +25.8‰). ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 46]])
- 58% of the nitrate concentrations are related to atmospheric deposition based on stable isotope analysis. ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 48]])
- A strong correlation between Cl- and NO3- was found, suggesting that evaporation played a major role in elevating nitrate levels. ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 48]])
- Approximately 35% of the samples (WB05, WB06, WB12, WB13, WB14, WB17, WB18) fell within the manure area based on isotopic composition. ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 52]])
- X-ray fluorescence (XRF) of outcrop rock samples showed no or undetectable nitrogen content. ([[2019-ahmed|Ahmed, 2019, p. 53]])

## Limitations

The LSC technique used for tritium measurement is limited to a detection limit of 0.8 TU; more advanced techniques like helium (3He) ingrowth are required for higher precision.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Wasia aquifer groundwater should be treated before human consumption.
- Frequent monitoring of groundwater quality in Al-Kharj area is needed.
- Investigate the nearby waste-water treatment plant for potential leaks.
- Monitor or close private wells to prevent them from becoming pathways for contamination.
