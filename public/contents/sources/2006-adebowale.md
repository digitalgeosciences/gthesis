# INTERFERENCE TESTING USING HORIZONTAL WELLS IN ANISOTROPIC RESERVOIRS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of varying horizontal well lengths on flux distribution and pressure responses in a pair of parallel horizontal wells within an anisotropic reservoir. It utilizes a modified semi-analytical model to evaluate how these changes affect flow regimes and interpretation of interference tests.

---

## Research Problem

Determining how different well lengths (active and observation) influence the pressure response and flux distribution in multi-well systems, which complicates the interpretation of interference tests in horizontal wells within anisotropic reservoirs.

## Objectives

- Extend the semi-analytic model developed by Al-Khamis, Ozkan and Raghavan (2001) to handle cases in which both horizontal wells are of unequal lengths.
- Study the effect of varying well lengths on pressure response in both wells.
- Investigate the impact of well length on flux distribution and the appearance of flow regimes.
- Extend a semi-analytical solution for interference testing to account for two parallel horizontal wells of unequal lengths.
- Study the effect of varying well lengths on flux distribution in both active and observation wells.
- Evaluate the impact of changing well lengths on pressure responses at the heels of both wells.

## Methods

The study utilizes a semi-analytical approach by coupling reservoir and wellbore flow models. A steady-state momentum equation for single-phase isothermal flow is used to model wellbore hydraulics. The model incorporates non-uniform flux distributions in both wells, which are determined using the Newton-Raphson iterative technique. Dimensional analysis is applied to create a dimensionless model that accounts for unequal lengths by introducing a ratio (RL) between the two wells.

## Data and Materials

The study uses a set of mathematical models with varied parameters: active well lengths (1000, 2000, 4000 ft), observation well lengths (500, 1000, 1500, 2000, 3000, 4000, 6000 ft), and various spatial configurations (offset in x-direction, y-direction, and symmetrically aligned). Reservoir parameters include thickness (h=100 ft), permeability (k_x=200/300 md), porosity (phi=0.25), and fluid properties like viscosity (mu=1.5 cp) and formation volume factor (B=1.1 rb/stb).

## Key Findings

- The modified model successfully accounts for unequal lengths by introducing the ratio RL, allowing for the analysis of varying well lengths. ([[2006-adebowale|ADEBOWALE, 2006, p. 24]])
- The study confirms that the assumption of a vertical interference well is not valid for interference tests between two horizontal wells. ([[2006-adebowale|ADEBOWALE, 2006, p. 20]])
- The model was validated against Al-Khamis et al.'s model and showed excellent agreement when both wells were assumed to be of equal length. ([[2006-adebowale|ADEBOWALE, 2006, p. 41]])
- The model successfully matched the Ozkan et al. results for a horizontal well with a vertical observation well (where the horizontal well's length was reduced to its radius). ([[2006-adebowale|ADEBOWALE, 2006, p. 56]])
- Flux distribution in the observation well is only significant at late times, as there is no flux distribution at early times. ([[2006-adebowale|ADEBOWALE, 2006, p. 46]])
- Increasing the active well length for a fixed observation well length decreases the active well pressure response and increases the observation well pressure response. ([[2006-adebowale|ADEBOWALE, 2006, p. 90]])
- For a fixed active well length, increasing the observation well length has no significant influence on active well pressure response but will decrease the observation well pressure response. ([[2006-adebowale|ADEBOWALE, 2006, p. 90]])
- Increasing both well lengths simultaneously causes a decline in the active well pressure response, while the observation well pressure response varies based on the ratio of the well lengths and geometry. ([[2006-adebowale|ADEBOWALE, 2006, p. 90]])
- For a fixed active well length, increasing the observation well length has no significant influence on flux distribution in the active well, but the magnitude of flux in the observation well increases. ([[2006-adebowale|ADEBOWALE, 2006, p. 90]])
- Increasing active well length for a given observation well length neither delays nor hastens early radial flow or intermediate linear flow in the active well, but lengthens the intermediate linear flow period and delays the late pseudo-radial flow period. ([[2006-adebowale|ADEBOWALE, 2006, p. 103]])
- Increasing the observation well length for a given active well length has no significant effect on any of the three flow regimes in the active well, but it delays the outset of the late pseudo-radial flow regime in the observation well. ([[2006-adebowale|ADEBOWALE, 2006, p. 103]])
- At late times, flux into and out of an observation well increases as its length increases; however, if the length is equal to its radius, no flux is observed. ([[2006-adebowale|ADEBOWALE, 2006, p. 60]])
- The pressure response at the heel of a horizontal observation well decreases as its length increases. ([[2006-adebowale|ADEBOWALE, 2006, p. 72]])
- When the distance between centers is constant, varying the observation well length results in minimal variation in observation well pressure. ([[2006-adebowale|ADEBOWALE, 2006, p. 75]])

## Limitations

The model assumes an anisotropic but homogeneous reservoir with single-phase isothermal flow and a fluid of constant compressibility and viscosity.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Modify the model to incorporate the effects of wellbore storage.
- Generalize the model to handle cases where active and observation wells are not parallel.
