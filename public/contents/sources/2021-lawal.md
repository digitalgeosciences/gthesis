# Surface Relaxivity of Conventional and Unconventional Reservoirs from Rock Cuttings

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the brittleness of Qusaiba shale in the Rub' Al-Khali Basin by developing a new mathematical framework based on energy partitioning between microcracks and the rock matrix. It compares dynamic and static indices of brittleness derived from ultrasonic velocities and uniaxial compression tests, respectively, to evaluate the impact of crack orientation on geomechanical properties.

---

## Research Problem

The lack of standardized, unambiguous methods for quantifying brittleness in shale reservoirs (specifically the Qusaiba formation) makes it difficult to identify optimal zones for hydraulic fracturing. Existing methods based on mineralogy or elastic constants often fail to account for the role of pre-existing microcracks.

## Objectives

- Investigate the physics and chemistry of source rock to better understand reservoir qualities including maturation to assess hydrocarbon bearing capacity.
- Evaluate sorption for storage and gas-in-place estimation.
- Determine brittleness as an index of hydraulic fracturing potential.
- Develop a new quantitative relationship between brittleness and rock properties for the Qusaiba shale.
- Measure the chemical interaction of source rocks with fracturing fluids.
- Characterize the mineralogy and organic content of a Jurassic carbonate source rock from the Jafurah basin.

## Methods

The study utilizes a multi-step workflow: 1) Micro-CT scanning (Versa XRM-500) to visualize and quantify internal crack distribution. 2) Physical measurements of sample dimensions and mass to determine bulk density and porosity via pycnometry. 3) Nondestructive ultrasonic velocity measurements at three orientations (0, 45, and 90 degrees) under varying confining pressures (5-35 MPa) using an AUTOLAB 1500 system. 4) Destructive uniaxial compression tests to determine ultimate strength and static elastic constants. 5) Mathematical modeling of the 'dynamic' and 'static' indices of brittleness ($\gamma_d$ and $\gamma_s$) based on energy partitioning equations derived from Eshelby’s theory.

## Data and Materials

The study used Qusaiba shale samples in their native state, collected from the Rub' Al-Khali Basin. Samples were prepared as cylindrical plugs (oriented horizontally and vertically) for core analysis. Data included ultrasonic velocities at 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, and 35 MPa confining pressures; uniaxial compressive strengths; and micro-CT scan data.

## Key Findings

- The activation energy distribution of carbonate source rock was found to be bimodal in the range of 99-325 kJ/mol, indicating a shift in reaction mechanisms during thermal decomposition. ([[2021-lawal|LAWAL, 2021, p. 49]])
- Gas analysis showed that the volume content of carbon dioxide, methane, and water were 2%, 46%, and 52% respectively during pyrolysis. ([[2021-lawal|LAWAL, 2021, p. 51]])
- Methane production increased with temperature while water production decreased, indicating high natural gas generative potential for the carbonate source rock. ([[2021-lawal|LAWAL, 2021, p. 51]])
- The presence of catalytic minerals such as gypsum, pyrite, kaolinite, and iron was confirmed by EDX and XRD analysis. ([[2021-lawal|LAWAL, 2021, p. 53]])
- The source rock is highly carbonate-rich (91.9% calcite) with a TOC content of 12%. ([[2021-lawal|LAWAL, 2021, p. 41]])
- The source rock is predominantly composed of calcite (91.9 wt.%), with other major minerals including quartz (3.5%), kaolinite (1.8%), gypsum (1.7%), and pyrite (1.1%). ([[2021-lawal|LAWAL, 2021, p. 78]])
- TOC content of the source rock was determined to be high at approximately 12 wt.%. ([[2021-lawal|LAWAL, 2021, p. 78]])
- Pre-treatment temperature significantly affects nitrogen sorption; samples pre-heated to 110°C showed higher adsorption (0.05-0.30 mmol/g) compared to those at 50°C (0.0099-0.125 mmol/g). ([[2021-lawal|LAWAL, 2021, p. 80]])
- Non-isothermal methane sorption capacity generally increases with temperature from 40°C to 300°C, followed by a plateau and a slight decrease at 400°C. ([[2021-lawal|LAWAL, 2021, p. 84]])
- The increase in methane sorption between 100-300°C is attributed to the interplay of maturation (pore creation) and sorption, where maturation dominates over sorption as temperature increases. ([[2021-lawal|LAWAL, 2021, p. 84]])
- A drop in methane sorption at 400°C compared to 300°C suggests that the gas generated from maturation supersedes the amount of gas held by sorption at very high temperatures. ([[2021-lawal|LAWAL, 2021, p. 84]])
- TGA analysis confirmed significant mass loss and gas evolution (water, methane, CO2) during non-isothermal heating, confirming the dynamic coupling between maturation and sorption. ([[2021-lawal|LAWAL, 2021, p. 87]])
- The orientation of crack distribution is a critical factor for brittleness; horizontal samples (2H, 3H, 4H) were found to be more brittle than the vertical sample (2V). ([[2021-lawal|LAWAL, 2021, p. 109]])
- The dynamic index of brittleness ($\gamma_d$) was calculated using ultrasonic velocities ($V_p$, $V_s$), porosity, and crack aspect ratio. ([[2021-lawal|LAWAL, 2021, p. 113]])
- The static index of brittleness ($\gamma_s$) was derived from uniaxial compressive strength (UCS) and static elastic constants. ([[2021-lawal|LAWAL, 2021, p. 115]])

## Limitations

The range of confining pressure (5x10^6 to 35x10^6 Pa) may not be sufficient for full crack closure. Additionally, the use of uniaxial compressive strength as a proxy for crack closure pressure in the static index calculation provides an upper limit for brittleness.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future work will investigate the transformation of apparent surface relaxity ($\rho_{app}$) to intrinsic surface relaxity ($\rho_{int}$) using a formula involving micro-porosity and electrical formation factor.
