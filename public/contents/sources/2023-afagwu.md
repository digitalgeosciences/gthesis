# MULTISCALE ADSORPTION AND DIFFUSION STUDIES IN UNCONVENTIONAL SHALE GAS RESERVOIRS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the multiscale adsorption and diffusion of gas in unconventional shale reservoirs, specifically focusing on the role of clay minerals (illite, kaolinite, montmollionite, and chlorite) on pore structure and gas transport. It combines experimental characterization of clay-rich samples with molecular simulation to develop improved analytical models for predicting gas flow in complex kerogen and mineral systems.

---

## Research Problem

Existing shale gas transport models often fail to accurately represent the multiscale physics (diffusion, adsorption, and transition regimes) occurring in microporous kerogen and clay-rich minerals due to simplified assumptions about pore geometry and lack of consideration for specific clay types' influence on porosity and surface area.

## Objectives

- Develop a new self-diffusion model for realistic kerogen structures considering adsorption effects.
- Establish an apparent permeability model for shale containing both circular and square geometry pores.
- Formulate a novel simulation framework based on Gaussian Pressure Transients to couple multiscale physics in hydraulically fractured reservoirs.
- Evaluate the impact of organic matter heterogeneity (tortuosity, sorbed gas interaction) on transport.
- Perform experimental study of the pore volume and adsorption characteristics of Illite, Kaolinite, Montmorillonite, and Chlorite rich shale.
- Employ molecular simulation to study pore structure and adsorption behavior, and gas self-diffusivity of a realistic kerogen micropores present in shale.

## Methods

The research employs a multi-scale approach: 1) Experimental characterization of four clay types (illite, kaolinite, montmorillonite, chlorite) using XRD for mineralogy, $μ$XRF for elemental analysis, SEM-EDX (QEMSCAN 650F) for mapping, and TOC analysis to determine organic content. 2) Nitrogen adsorption/desorption experiments at 77K using a surface area and porosity analyzer (Micromeritics ASAP 2020) to calculate SSA via BET, pore distribution via BJH, and micropore volume via t-plot. 3) Molecular simulation including Grand Canonical Monte Carlo (GCMC) for adsorption and Molecular Dynamics (MD) for diffusion in realistic kerogen structures. 4) Analytical modeling of apparent permeability ($k_{app}$) incorporating transition diffusion, Knudsen diffusion, and surface diffusion terms to account for complex pore geometries.

## Data and Materials

The study utilizes a set of 8 distinct cases for sensitivity analysis involving varying kerogen tortuosity (1.5 to 3.19), average pore sizes (0.26 nm to 1.0 nm), and sorption capacities (50 to 150 scf/ton). It also incorporates gas properties for methane, including a range of pressures from 500 psi (BHP) to 4073 psi (initial reservoir pressure).

## Key Findings

- The impact of organic matter heterogeneity (diffusion tortuosity, sorbed gas interaction) is significantly higher in micropores when reservoir pressure drops below 2,000 psi. ([[2023-afagwu|AFAGWU, 2023, p. 26]])
- Montmorillonite shows the highest affinity for methane among tested clay minerals, followed by illite/smectite, kaolinite, and chlorite. ([[2023-afagwu|AFAGWU, 2023, p. 49]])
- The transition boundary condition is applicable in the kerogen micropore system for pressure ranges up to 80 MPa. ([[2023-afagwu|AFAGWU, 2023, p. 29]])
- A new self-diffusion model was validated against both experimental and computational datasets, showing that sorption effects significantly alter diffusion coefficients in nanopores. ([[2023-afagwu|AFAGWU, 2023, p. 32]])
- The inclusion of the full definition of apparent permeability (including molecular diffusion parameters) provides a more robust range of applicability at high and low pressures compared to standard models. ([[2023-afagwu|AFAGWU, 2023, p. 232]])
- Kaolinite showed the highest capacity to host $N_2$ gas at standard temperature and pressure (74.43 cm$^3$/g) compared to montmorillonite (65.08 cm$^3$/g), chlorite (21.01 cm$^3$/g), and illite (16.59 cm$^3$/g). ([[2023-afagwu|AFAGWU, 2023, p. 59]])
- Clay samples with purities of 60-98% had a combined 80-96 wt.% of Aluminum (Al), Silicon (Si), and Iron (Fe). ([[2023-afagwu|AFAGWU, 2023, p. 59]])
- Adsorption-desorption curves at $p/p_o < 0.04$ and carbon content analysis indicated that clays accommodate negligible micropores. ([[2023-afagwu|AFAGWU, 2023, p. 59]])
- The transition diffusion regime is the predominant diffusion regime in the kerogen microporous system, occurring when there is an equal contribution of wall-gas molecules and intermolecular collision. ([[2023-afagwu|AFAGWU, 2023, p. 55]])
- Methane transport in kerogen micropores is primarily within the transition domain for pressure ranges up to 80 MPa. ([[2023-afagwu|AFAGWU, 2023, p. 57]])
- The study confirmed that montmorillonite with the largest specific surface area in non-micropores and silicon content of 63.9 wt.% promotes reactivity and cation exchange interaction. ([[2023-afagwu|AFAGWU, 2023, p. 60]])
- Clay minerals in the samples showed high concentrations of aluminum and silicon (80-96% wt.%), with kaolinite showing the highest aluminum content (39.5 wt.%). ([[2023-afagwu|AFAGWU, 2023, p. 100]])
- TOC analysis for all clay samples was low (<0.76%), indicating negligible organic matter and minimal associated microporosity. ([[2023-afagwu|AFAGWU, 2023, p. 100]])
- Kaolinite-rich samples exhibited the highest nitrogen adsorption capacity (74.43 cm3/g) and largest macropore volumes compared to other clay types. ([[2023-afagwu|AFAGWU, 2023, p. 108]])
- Montmorillonite showed the highest specific surface area in non-micropores (due to high silicon content of 63.9 wt.%). ([[2023-afagwu|AFAGWU, 2023, p. 113]])

## Limitations

Analytical models for apparent permeability often lack corrections for pore geometry factors in non-ideal structures and may not fully capture the impact of adsorbed gas layers on effective pore diffusion. Existing numerical methods like MD are accurate but computationally expensive for large-scale field applications.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Study the effect of phase behavior changes (critical pressure and temperature shifts) in shale gas nanopores on Gaussian production rate forecast.
- Extend the study to a multicomponent system (methane/ethane/CO2/H2O) to investigate diffusion in kerogen.
- Integrate complex fracture geometry and multiphase fluid systems into the multiphysics forward simulation algorithm.
- Model hydrogen storage in depleted organic-rich shale gas reservoirs.
