# Realtime Rate Allocation for multilaterals wells equipped with downhole valves a

**Summary**: The thesis develops a methodology to allocate continuous production rates for individual laterals in multilateral wells equipped with downhole multi-position valves and a single permanent pressure and temperature gauge (PDHMS). The study utilizes actual field data from bi-lateral, tri-lateral, and quad-lateral wells to validate models and determine optimal valve settings.

---

## Research Problem

The difficulty of accurately estimating or back-allocating production rates for individual laterals in multilateral wells that share a single downhole gauge (PDHMS) and are controlled by multiple downhole valves. This is critical for real-time reservoir management, accurate well performance analysis, and optimizing production from complex well architectures.

## Objectives

- Provide a methodology based on field data to allocate continuous production rates for individual laterals in multilateral wells equipped with downhole multi-position valves and a single PDHMS.
- Determine the best downhole valve positions to meet specific production targets for each lateral.
- Establish a workflow to facilitate real-time rate allocation even when wells are not being actively tested.
- Construct and validate well models for bi-lateral, tri-lateral, and quad-lateral configurations.
- Perform sensitivity runs to simulate various downhole valve settings.
- Determine accurate allocation factors to estimate individual lateral production rates during commingled production.

## Methods

The study uses actual field data from three types of multilateral wells (bi-lateral, tri-lateral, and quad-lateral). A mathematical modeling approach is used to simulate well performance. The methodology involves: 1) Collecting data on completion details, deviation surveys, surface flow rates (via MPFMs), surface pressures, downhole valve settings, PDHMS readings, and fluid properties. 2) Constructing models using a nodal analysis package to model reservoir inflow and well outflow. 3) Validating these models against actual test data. 4) Determining discharge coefficients (Cd) for downhole valves through sensitivity runs. 5) Developing an allocation factor to calculate individual lateral rates based on real-time measurements.

## Data and Materials

Data from three distinct wells: one bi-lateral, one tri-lateral, and one quad-lateral. Data includes surface flow rate measurements (MPFM), surface pressure readings, downhole valve settings (10 positions), PDHMS pressure/temperature data, and fluid properties (API 30, GOR 450 cfb).

## Key Findings

- A discharge coefficient (Cd) of 0.8 was identified as the best value for the downhole valves in both Well A and Well B based on sensitivity analysis. ([[2011-almubarak|ALMUBARAK, 2011, p. 42]])
- The study successfully modeled three types of complex well architectures: bi-lateral, tri-lateral, and quad-lateral wells. ([[2011-almubarak|ALMUBARAK, 2011, p. 21]])
- A methodology was established to calculate the productivity index (PI) for each lateral using reservoir pressure (Pr), total rate (Qt), and flowing bottom-hole pressure (Pf). ([[2011-almubarak|ALMUBARAK, 2011, p. 40]])
- The study provides a method to estimate flow rates across different valve settings based on delta pressures, as shown in the extensive data table for various positions. ([[2011-almubarak|ALMUBARAK, 2011, p. 48]])
- The model results for the bi-lateral well showed acceptable variances in rates ranging from 4% to 15%. ([[2011-almubarak|ALMUBARAK, 2011, p. 55]])
- For the quad-lateral well (a wet producer), variance in total rate reached 22%, but the variance in oil rate did not exceed 12.1%. ([[2011-almubarak|ALMUBARAK, 2011, p. 55]])
- The absolute allocation factors determined for the bi-lateral, tri-lateral, and quad-lateral wells were 8%, 4%, and 9% respectively. ([[2011-almubarak|ALMUBARAK, 2011, p. 55]])
- The calculated allocation factors (8%, 4%, and 9%) outperformed standard industry practice of using a flat 10% factor to account for uncertainty. ([[2011-almubarak|ALMUBARAK, 2011, p. 56]])
- Model results were validated against actual test data with high accuracy, as shown in the comparison tables (Tables 5.1 through 5.9). ([[2011-almubarak|ALMUBARAK, 2011, p. 58]])

## Limitations

Downhole valves are subject to erosion or may develop leaks over time, requiring periodic re-estimation of discharge coefficients (Cd).

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Integrate the findings into a broader automated system for real--time monitoring.
- Consider installing downhole flow meters for each lateral or multiple gauges to provide upstream/downstream pressure values at each valve in future designs.
