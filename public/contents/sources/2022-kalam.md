# AN INVESTIGATION OF GEMINI SURFACTANT ADSORPTION ON CARBONATE ROCKS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the adsorption mechanisms and reduction strategies for a novel, in-house synthesized cationic Gemini surfactant (GS12) on carbonate rocks and various minerals. It concludes that while sandstone shows high adsorption due to negative charges, carbonates show low adsorption, and specific additives like methylene blue or pH adjustments can further reduce adsorption.

---

## Research Problem

The primary challenge in surfactant-based chemical enhanced oil recovery (cEOR) is the loss of surfactants due to high adsorption on rock surfaces, which can lead to inefficient and uneconomical processes. This study specifically addresses how different mineral types (carbonate vs. sandstone) and impurities (clay/silicate) affect the adsorption of a novel cationic Gemini surfactant.

## Objectives

- Investigation of the adsorption mechanisms of a cationic Gemini surfactant in different types of rocks.
- Analyze the influence of individual minerals (clay vs. silicate vs. carbonate) on surfactant adsorption.
- Inspect different chemicals to reduce surfactant adsorption.
- Characterize the mineralogical and morphological properties of carbonate and silicate minerals using XRD, XRF, and SEM.
- Determine the surface charge (zeta potential) of various rock samples in deionized water.
- Evaluate the static adsorption of GS12 on different rocks as a function of concentration.

## Methods

The study utilized a variety-specified cationic Gemini surfactant (GS12). Rock characterization included XRD for mineralogy, XRF for elemental analysis, FTIR for functional group identification, and SEM for surface morphology. Zeta potential was measured using an Anton Paar Litesizer 500 to determine surface charge. Static adsorption tests were conducted by mixing powdered rocks with surfactant solutions of varying concentrations (100-1500 ppm) and analyzing the supernatant via HPLC (Agilent 1290 Infinity II). Adsorption isotherms were modeled using non-linear least-squares fitting. Soft computing models, including XGBoost and Random Forest, were implemented for prediction, utilizing K-fold cross-validation and grid search for hyperparameter tuning.

## Data and Materials

The study used several rock types: Gray Berea, Scioto, and Buff Berea (sandstones); Calcite and Indiana Limestone (carbonates). Specific minerals included white quartz, dolomite, K-feldspar, and clay minerals (montmorillonite, kaolinite, and illite). Experimental conditions included various salt concentrations (NaCl, CaCl2, MgCl2) and methylene blue as an inhibitor.

## Key Findings

- The adsorption of GS12 on sandstone was significantly higher than in carbonates due to the high density of negative charges on silicate surfaces. ([[2022-kalam|Kalam, 2022, p. 56]])
- It is proposed that an interdigitated bilayer forms on carbonate rocks, whereas a non-interdigitated bilayer forms on sandstone rock samples. ([[2022-kalam|Kalam, 2022, p. 56]])
- Adding 500ppm methylene blue (MB) to the GS12 solution caused a reduction of about eight times to the surfactant's adsorption on the Saudi dolomite samples. ([[2022-kalam|Kalam, 2022, p. 27]])
- Adding 1% formic acid lowered the surfactant solution's pH below the point of zero charge (PZC), reducing GS12 adsorption due to electrostatic repulsion. ([[2022-kalam|Kalam, 2022, p. 27]])
- The addition of 1% formic acid reduced GS12 static adsorption values on Guelph dolomite, Gulf dolomite, and Indiana limestone to 0.16, 0.14, and 0.13 mg/g-rock, respectively. ([[2022-kalam|Kalam, 2022, p. 27]])
- The dynamic adsorption of GS12 on Indiana limestone was reduced to 0.041 mg/g-rock when the pH was lowered below PZC. ([[2022-kalam|Kalam, 2022, p. 27]])
- Using ten times diluted Arabian Gulf water (low salinity) reduced the static and dynamic adsorption of GS12 on Indiana limestone to 0.18 and 0.11 mg/g-rock, respectively. ([[2022-kalam|Kalam, 2022, p. 27]])
- The maximum and minimum adsorption values were found around 9 mg/g-rock and 1 mg/g-rock in sandstone and carbonates, respectively. ([[2022-kalam|Kalam, 2022, p. 56]])
- Sandstone rocks exhibited significantly higher adsorption of GS12 compared to carbonate rocks due to a higher density of negative charges from silica and clay minerals. ([[2022-kalam|Kalam, 2022, p. 68]])
- The maximum adsorption values for carbonates (calcite and Indiana limestone) were low, approximately 0.89-1.11 mg/g-rock, suggesting potential for cEOR in these rocks. ([[2022-kalam|Kalam, 2022, p. 75]])
- The highest adsorption of GS12 was observed in the Scioto sandstone (up to 9.48 mg/g-rock) due to high quartz (59.5%) and illite (11.3%) content. ([[2022-kalam|Kalam, 2022, p. 75]])
- The Hill adsorption model provided the best fit for all rock types, with R2 values reaching as high as 0.9918 for Indiana limestone. ([[2022-kalam|Kalam, 2022, p. 79]])
- A non-interdigitated bilayer was proposed for sandstone, while an interdigitated bilayer was proposed for carbonate rocks based on the Redlich-Peterson and Langmuir models. ([[2022-kalam|Kalam, 2022, p. 77]])
- Clay minerals showed much higher adsorption than non-clay minerals; montmorillonite exhibited the highest adsorption at 22.39 mg/g-rock. ([[2022-kalam|Kalam, 2022, p. 100]])
- Divalent cations (Ca2+ and Mg2+) significantly reduced GS12 adsorption on carbonate rocks compared to monovalent ions (Na+). ([[2022-kalam|Kalam, 2022, p. 103]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study suggests that GS12 can be utilized in carbonate reservoirs containing silica and clay impurities, especially when combined with inhibitors like methylene blue to reduce adsorption.
