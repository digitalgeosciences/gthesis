# Analysis of the Factors Affecting the Gelation Time for Polyacrylamide-Redox System

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the factors affecting gelation time in a polyacrylamide-redox system, specifically focusing on how polymer type, concentration, redox concentration, and temperature influence the formation of a stable gel. It employs mathematical modeling, including factorial analysis and multiple regression, to establish empirical relationships for optimizing treatment parameters.

---

## Research Problem

The difficulty in determining the impact of various chemical and physical parameters on the gelation process in polyacrylamide-chromium systems makes it challenging to ensure accurate placement of gel treatments in reservoir zones. Specifically, a mathematical approach is needed to quantify the influence of polymer type, initial concentration, and redox conditions.

## Objectives

- Conduct a statistical analysis to identify the most affecting parameters on gelation time.
- Introduce a dimensional analysis to develop dimensionless groups using the influencing parameters.
- Develop an empirical formulation between the dimensionless groups and the gelation time using a multiple regression technique.
- Identify the primary factors affecting gelation time in polyacrylamide-redox systems.
- Evaluate the impact of different polymer types (e.g., Calgon 800, 815, 825, 340, and Betz 1160) on gelation kinetics.
- Develop mathematical models to predict gelation time based on concentration and temperature.

## Methods

The study utilizes a series of experimental tests involving five different polymer systems (Calgon 800, 815, 825, 340, and Betz 1160) combined with sodium dichromate and sodium bisulfite. Data collection included measuring gelation time across varying concentrations (up to 3103 ppm for redox) and temperatures (30-70°C). Analytical methods included factorial analysis to determine the significance of parameters, dimensional analysis using the Buckingham method to create dimensionless groups (CRN and CIN), and multiple regression analysis (using STATGRAPH software) to derive polynomial equations for predicting gelation time.

## Data and Materials

Data extracted from existing literature (references 3, 12, and 13) regarding five different polymer systems (System 1-5). Parameters measured include gelation time, initial polymer concentration, redox concentration (sum of reducing agent and chromium source), and temperature.

## Key Findings

- The study identifies four primary parameters affecting the gelation process: polymer type, initial polymer concentration, redox concentration, and temperature. ([[1994-muhammad|Muhammad, 1994, p. 23]])
- Redox concentration is defined as the sum of the concentration of the reducing agent and the source of the chrome (+6). ([[1994-muhammad|Muhammad, 1994, p. 23]])
- The reduction of chromium(+6) to chromium(+3) provides a method for controlling the rate of gelation or polymer crosslinking. ([[1994-muhammad|Muhammad, 1994, p. 45]])
- Sodium bisulfite (NaHSO3) provides a relatively fast redox rate of the order of minutes to hours, while thiourea (NH2CSNHz) provides a much slower reduction rate of days to weeks. ([[1994-muhammad|Muhammad, 1994, p. 46]])
- Note: The thesis establishes that polyacrylamide-based gels are more stable at high temperatures compared to other systems. ([[1994-muhammad|Muhammad, 1994]])
- The gel point in viscosity measurement is defined as the point of maximum rate of increase of viscosity, corresponding to an infinitely large viscosity. ([[1994-muhammad|Muhammad, 1994, p. 42]])
- Gelation time is significantly influenced by polymer type, initial polymer concentration, redox concentration, and temperature. ([[1994-muhammad|Muhammad, 1994, p. 59]])
- Increasing the initial polymer concentration results in a decrease in gelation time, showing a non-linear relationship. ([[1994-muhammad|Muhammad, 1994, p. 60]])
- Increasing redox concentration leads to a decrease in gelation time; this is consistent with kinetic theory where higher reactant concentrations reduce reaction time. ([[1994-muhammad|Muhammad, 1994, p. 61]])
- Gelation time decreases as temperature increases, following an Arrhenius-type relationship (log of gelation time vs. reciprocal of absolute temperature). ([[1994-muhammad|Muhammad, 1994, p. 62]])
- Salinity affects gelation time in a parabolic form; it decreases slightly up to 5,000-10,000 ppm and then increases as concentration rises. ([[1994-muhammad|Muhammad, 1994, p. 62]])
- Statistical analysis confirmed that redox concentration, initial polymer concentration, and polymer type have significant effects on gelation time, with first-order interactions between these parameters being highly significant. ([[1994-muhammad|Muhammad, 1994, p. 126]])
- Polynomial models (e.g., Equation 5.4-5.8) provided a better fit for predicting gelation time compared to exponential models, which showed high relative errors. ([[1994-muhammad|Muhammad, 1994, p. 112]])
- The average gelation times for Calgon 800 (System 1) were recorded at concentrations of 1930, 2150, 2350, 2560, and 2990 ppm. ([[1994-muhammad|Muhammad, 1994, p. 102]])
- The average gelation times for Calgon 815 (System 2) were less reproducible at high concentrations due to the viscosity of the polymer solution. ([[1994-muhammad|Muhammad, 1994, p. 75]])

## Limitations

The study is limited by a small sample size of only five polymer systems, making it difficult to generalize empirical equations across all possible polymer types. Additionally, more data on variables such as pH and salinity are required for a fully comprehensive formulation.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Expand the dataset to include more polymer systems and additional environmental variables like pH and salinity.
- Extend experiments to include lower values of initial polymer concentration (below those listed in Table 4.11).
- Apply the current investigation methodology to other types of gelling systems.
