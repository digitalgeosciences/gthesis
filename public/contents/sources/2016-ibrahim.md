# Isolation and characterization of microalgae for biofuel production

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the isolation and characterization of microalgae strains (Chlorella kessleri, Chlorella vulgaris, and Nannochloropsis oculata) from coastal areas in Saudi Arabia for biofuel production. It evaluates their growth kinetics, nutrient removal capacities (Nitrogen and Phosphorus), and fatty acid profiles under varying temperature and nutrient conditions.

---

## Research Problem

The need for sustainable biofuel sources to replace depleting fossil fuels, specifically utilizing microalgae which are carbon neutral and can be grown on marginal land, while simultaneously addressing the challenge of removing nutrients (nitrogen and phosphorus) from wastewater.

## Objectives

- Isolation of indigenous microalgae strains from the Arabian Gulf.
- Morphological identification of the isolated strains.
- Check the growth performance of the isolates by varying nutrient (NO3 and PO4) and temperature (30°C and 35°C).
- Biofuel analysis of the strains by gas chromatography.
- Sampling and isolation of indigenous microalgae strains from diverse sites in the Arabian Gulf (Al-Khobar and Jubail).
- Isolation of pure microalgae strains using streaking and single-cell isolation methods.
- Identification of microalgae strains morphologically through light microscopy.
- Analysis of growth kinetics under varying temperatures (30°C, 35°C) and three different nitrate/phosphate levels.
- Lipid analysis of selected strains using Gas Chromatography (GC).
- Isolate and identify microalgae strains from coastal samples in Al-Khobar and Jubail.
- Evaluate the growth kinetics of isolated strains under different nutrient levels (N1, N2, N3) and temperatures (30°C, 35°C).
- Assess the removal efficiency of nitrate and phosphate by the selected microalgae strains.
- Quantify lipid content and characterize the fatty acid profiles of the isolated strains for biofuel potential.
- Isolate microalgae strains from the coast of Jubail and Al-Khobar.
- Identify strains through morphological behavior and 18S rRNA analysis.
- Measure growth parameters (Optical Density and Dry Weight) under varying nutrient levels and temperatures.
- Evaluate nitrate and phosphate removal efficiency.
- Quantify lipid content and fatty acid composition in the isolated strains.
- Isolate and characterize microalgae for biofuel production
- Determine growth parameters and biomass concentration
- Evaluate lipid extraction methods for biodiesel production

## Methods

Microalgae were sampled from Al-Khobar (C1-C7) and Jubail (A1-A7). Isolation was performed through five subsequent streaking to obtain axenic cultures. Morphological identification was conducted using an optical microscope at 40x, 60x, and 100x magnification. Growth kinetics were monitored via optical density (OD) and dry weight (DW) measurements over a 24-day period. Nutrient concentrations (Nitrate/Phosphate) were measured using Hach Company kits with DR 6000 Spectrophotometer and DRB200 Reactor. Lipid extraction was performed using the direct esterification method involving acid (H2SO4) and base (NaOH) hydrolysis, followed by solvent extraction (Hexane). Quantification of lipids and fatty acids was conducted via GC-FID (Agilent 7890a) with an hp-88 column.

## Data and Materials

Samples collected from 2 sites: Al-Khobar coast (7 samples) and Jubail industrial city coast (14 samples). Three strains (S1, S2, S3) were characterized. Growth was tested at two temperatures (30°C, 35°C) across three nutrient levels (N1=61.5 mg/L N; 1.45 mg/L P; N2=123 mg/L N; 2.9 mg/L P; N3=246 mg/L N; 5.8 mg/L P).

## Key Findings

- Three strains were successfully isolated and identified as Chlorella kessleri (S1), Chlorella vulgaris (S2), and Nanochloropsis oculata (S3). ([[2016-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2016, p. 11]])
- All three isolated strains showed greater than 90% nitrate and phosphate removal at both tested temperatures. ([[2016-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2016, p. 11]])
- Lipid content was found to be 19%, 27%, and 14% in S1, S2, and S3 respectively. ([[2016-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2016, p. 12]])
- GC-FID analysis showed palmitic acid concentrations of 18%, 22%, and 16% for strains S1, S2, and S3, respectively. ([[2016-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2016, p. 12]])
- GC-FID analysis showed stearic acid concentrations of 6%, 4%, and 5% for strains S1, S2, and S3, respectively. ([[2016-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2016, p. 12]])
- Strain S2 exhibited the highest optical density and dry weight among the three isolates at both temperatures. ([[2016-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2016, p. 11]])
- Strain S3 showed the lowest values for both optical density and dry weight at both tested temperatures. ([[2016-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2016, p. 11]])
- Microalgae can remove significant amounts of nutrients from wastewater, with some studies showing removal rates up to 10 mg/l/day for nitrogen and 4 mg/l/day for phosphorus using membrane photoreactors. ([[2016-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2016, p. 32]])
- Nitrogen limitation leads to enhanced lipid accumulation (up to 53% increase) and triglyceride formation, while also triggering carbon fixation into carbohydrate compounds. ([[2016-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2016, p. 28]])
- Phosphorus is a primary limiting factor for growth; reducing phosphorus from 2 mg/L to 0.1 mg/L can result in more than double the lipid content (23-53%). ([[2016-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2016, p. 28]])
- The optimal temperature range for microalgae growth and biomass accumulation is generally between 20-30°C. ([[2016-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2016]])

- Strain S2 (Chlorella vulgaris) showed the highest optical density of 1.81 at T1 and the highest dry weight of 1.71 mg/L at N3. ([[2016-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2016, p. 53]])
- All three strains (S1, S2, S3) showed over 90% removal of phosphate under both temperature conditions. ([[2016-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2016, p. 48]])
- Nitrate removal exceeded 99% for all strains at the end of the experiment at both T1 and T2. ([[2016-ibrahim|Ibrahim, 2016, p. 50]])
