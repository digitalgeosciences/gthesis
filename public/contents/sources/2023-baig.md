# INVESTIGATION OF MODIFIED ENZYME-INDUCED CARBONATE PRECIPITATION FOR SAND PRODUCTION CONTROL APPLICATIONS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the development of a modified Enzyme-Induced Carbonate Precipitation (EICP) solution using dual salts (CaCl2 and MgCl2), biopolymers, and synthetic copolymers to consolidate loose sand. The study concludes that the AN 125 copolymer provides the highest strength and best performance for preventing sand production in oil and gas wells.

---

## Research Problem

Sand production causes significant issues in wellbores, such as equipment damage and blockage. Existing EICP methods often fail to provide sufficient strength at high temperatures or lack consistent results due to poor control over carbonate precipitation types (calcite vs. dolomite).

## Objectives

- Develop a modified EICP formulation that provides effective sand consolidation at high temperatures (up to 120°C) while maintaining permeability.
- Evaluate the impact of different additives (xanthan gum, cellulose, and synthetic copolymers) on carbonate precipitation and strength.
- Assess the effect of salt combinations (CaCl2 and MgCl2) on mineralogy and consolidation strength.
- Determine the influence of curing temperature on the resulting precipitate's physical properties.
- Develop a modified EICP solution using two types of salts (CaCl2 and MgCl2) with urea, urease, biopolymers, and copolymers.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of different additives (Xanthan gum, Cellulose, AM-AMPS copolymers) on sand consolidation strength.

## Methods

The study involved preparing various EICP formulations with combinations of CaCl2, MgCl2, urea, urease, and additives (Xanthan gum, Cellulose, or AM-AMPS copolymers). Scratch tests were performed on consolidated samples to measure ISE strength at 25°C, 70°C, and 120°C. CT scanning using a Toshiba Alexion TSX-032A Medical CT scanner was used to determine the volume of precipitated minerals (calcite, dolomite, carnallite) by comparing sample CT numbers against a sand-only baseline. Sand pack flooding experiments were conducted with different injection rates (4, 8, and 12 cc/min) to measure permeability changes before and after treatment.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized various EICP formulations (Solutions 1-19), including specific concentrations of salts (0.67 M CaCl2, 0.33 M MgCl2), enzymes (3 g/L Urease), and polymers (Xanthan Gum, Cellulose, AN 113, AN 125). Sand samples were subjected to curing at 70°C for 72 hours.

## Key Findings

- Samples cured at 70°C demonstrated the highest strength compared to those at 25°C or 90°C, highlighting the importance of temperature control. ([[2023-baig|Baig, 2023, p. 12]])
- A combination of CaCl2 and MgCl2 salts resulted in greater strength than solutions containing only one type of salt. ([[2023-baig|Baig, 2023, p. 12]])
- Cellulose (a bio-polymer) exhibited the highest strength at both 70°C and 120°C, while Xanthan Gum showed no strength at 120°C due to degradation. ([[2023-baig|Baig, 2023, p. 12]])
- The synthetic copolymer AN 125 consistently demonstrated the highest strength among the tested copolymers. ([[2023-baig|Baig, 2023, p. 12]])
- TGA analysis showed that all copolymers degraded at temperatures exceeding 300°C, but bio-polymers like Xanthan Gum began losing mass significantly around 250°C. ([[2023-baig|Baig, 2023]])

- Solution 10 (cellulose) yielded a high strength of 1100 Psi at 70°C and 918 Psi at 120°C. ([[2023-baig|Baig, 2023, p. 58]])
- The addition of MgCl2 to the EICP solution increased the precipitation ratio by promoting aragonite growth and smaller calcite crystals. ([[2023-baig|Baig, 2023, p. 24]])
- Solution 13 (AN 125) showed a permeability reduction of approximately 30% when used in sand pack flooding, making it a promising candidate for field application. ([[2023-baig|Baig, 2023, p. 14]])
- XRD analysis revealed that the addition of Mg ions led to a 40% increase in UCS when using Ca(CH3COO)2 and urea. ([[2023-baig|Baig, 2023, p. 25]])
- The highest ISE strength of 2175 psi was achieved using the AN 125 copolymer at both 70°C and 120°C. ([[2023-baig|Baig, 2023, p. 60]])
- EICP solutions showed maximum strength at 70°C and weakest results at 25°C, indicating that curing at 70°C is preferred. ([[2023-baig|Baig, 2023, p. 74]])
- A combination of CaCl2 and MgCl2 salts resulted in higher strength compared to single-salt systems. ([[2023-baig|Baig, 2023, p. 74]])
- Xanthan gum enhanced strength up to 70°C but degraded and lost effectiveness at 120°C. ([[2023-baig|Baig, 2023, p. 74]])

- Applying MICP at very high temperatures (200°C).
