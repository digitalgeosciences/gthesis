# COATING AND LINER FOR HYDROGEN AND NATURAL GAS (H2-NG) BLENDED GAS NETWORK

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the development of a polyurethane (PU) based coating modified with Metal-Organic Frameworks (MOF) to protect pipelines carrying hydrogen and natural gas blends. It combines a simulation study of the Great Gordonville Pipeline (GGP) in Australia to assess the impact of H2 on pressure profiles and a laboratory study to develop and characterize PU/PP-MOF coatings for improved barrier properties and fire resistance.

---

## Research Problem

The integration of hydrogen into existing natural gas networks poses significant risks due to hydrogen's high flammability, low ignition threshold, and its ability to cause hydrogen embrittlement (HE) in steel pipelines. Existing coatings and liners must be evaluated for their durability under these specific conditions.

## Objectives

- Evaluate the performance of natural gas pipelines with blended hydrogen using PIPESIM software for blending ratios from 10% to 80%.
- Develop an external fire-retardant coating to protect the pipeline from degradation and slow down heat transfer.
- Utilize a commercial liner combined with the developed coating to enhance protection against high-pressure H2-rich conditions.
- Simulate the impact of varying hydrogen concentrations on the pressure profile and gas properties within a transmission network using PIPESIM.
- Synthesize and characterize polyurethane (PU) based coatings modified with metal-organic frameworks (MOF).
- Evaluate the protection properties, fire resistance, and durability of the proposed coating in high-pressure hydrogen-rich environments.

## Methods

The study is divided into two parts: simulation and experimental. The simulation part uses PIPESIM 19.4 to model the Great Gordonville Pipeline (GGP) under various H2 concentrations (0% to 80%). Experimental work involves synthesizing PU-based coatings with different MOF contents (up to 1.0 wt.%), preparing polymer films, and applying them to steel and HDPE liner substrates. Characterization includes FT-IR for functional group identification, TGA/LOI for thermal properties, Shore A hardness testing, tensile strength measurement, and SEM/XRD for surface analysis before and after exposure to high-pressure H2-CH4 blends (1480 psia) in a closed cell.

## Data and Materials

Data includes the 1380 km long GGP pipeline network with 7 compression stations. Experimental materials include Sigma Aldrich chemicals, S-36 mild steel Q-panels, and high-density polyethylene (HDPE) liners. Samples were aged in a closed cell for 30 days at 1480 psia.

## Key Findings

- Introduction of hydrogen into the network lowers pressure losses and reduces power needs at compression stations. ([[2023-khalid|Khalid, 2023, p. 17]])
- Higher hydrogen content shifts the phase envelope towards infinity and increases the compressibility factor (Z-factor) while lowering viscosity and density. ([[2023-khalid|Khalid, 2023, p. 17]])
- The in-house formulated polyurethane (PU) coating demonstrated promising results in terms of fire retardance and a slower gas diffusion rate. ([[2023-khalid|Khalid, 2023, p. 17]])
- Coating protective properties remained similar after 1 year of natural weather exposure and 1 month of aging in high-pressure hydrogen environment. ([[2023-khalid|Khalid, 2023, p. 17]])
- Hydrogen embrittlement (HE) is more severe when both H2 concentration and pressure are increased, particularly in high-pressure blends (≥ 1450 psi). ([[2023-khalid|Khalid, 2023, p. 21]])
- A 30% H2 blend is deemed secure for the network in some studies, while higher concentrations risk pipeline integrity. ([[2023-khalid|Khalid, 2023, p. 27]])
- Hydrogen-induced cracking (HIC) and stress corrosion cracking (SCC) are significant risks in high-pressure hydrogen environments. ([[2023-khalid|Khalid, 2023, p. 46]])
- Increasing H2 concentration from 0% to 80% resulted in significant reductions in gas density and viscosity, with the highest reduction of 69% in density and 14% in viscosity observed at 80% H2. ([[2023-khalid|Khalid, 2023, p. 58]])
- The addition of MOF to the PU coating significantly improved barrier properties, reducing water swelling from 8.2% (PU4/PP4) to 5.1% (PU4/PP4-MOF4). ([[2023-khalid|Khalid, 2023, p. 68]])
- The inclusion of MOF increased the coating's hardness from 70 Shore A to 83 Shore A, transitioning it from a medium hard to a hard material. ([[2023-khalid|Khalid, 2023, p. 69]])
- Tensile strength increased significantly with MOF addition, reaching 27 MPa for the PU4/PP4-MOF4 formulation. ([[2023-khalid|Khalid, 2023, p. 69]])
- The water contact angle (WCA) increased from 74° to 91° as MOF content increased, indicating improved hydrophobicity. ([[2023-khalid|Khalid, 2023, p. 68]])
- At hydrogen concentrations of 50% and higher, the pipeline pressure exceeded the Maximum Allowable Operating Pressure (MAOP) at station CS7, reaching over 1500 psia. ([[2023-khalid|Khalid, 2023, p. 62]])
- Hydrogen addition to the GGP resulted in reduced pressure drops, with some delivery points reaching an increase of up to 233 psia at 80 mol% hydrogen content. ([[2023-khalid|Khalid, 2023, p. 99]])
- At 50 mol% and above hydrogen fraction, the final compression station (CS7) exceeded allowable operating pressure, reaching 1563 psia and 1562 psia for 70% and 80% H2 respectively. ([[2023-khalid|Khalid, 2023, p. 99]])

## Limitations

The study notes that while MOF-based coatings show promise, achieving a perfectly homogeneous dispersion of MOF nanoparticles in polymer matrices remains a technical challenge due to their tendency to agglomerate.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Evaluate pipeline materials at various hydrogen fractions to determine optimal mixing ratios; conduct gas permeation/diffusion tests for further evidence of coating performance; explore longer aging times for hydrogen exposure.
