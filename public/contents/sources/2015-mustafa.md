# INTEGRATION OF LITHOFACIES AND GEOMECHANICAL CHARACTERISTICS OF LOWER SILURIAN QUSAIBA SHALE, SAUDI ARABIA

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the Qusaiba Shale in the Rub’ Al-Khali Basin by integrating geological characteristics (lithofacies, mineralogy, and elemental composition) with geomechanical properties (Young's modulus, Poisson's ratio, and anisotropy). The study identifies three distinct lithofacies within the shale and evaluates their suitability for hydraulic fracturing based on brittleness and mechanical stability.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the need for a comprehensive characterization of the Qusaiba Shale (Lower Silurian) in the Rub\u2019 Al-Khali Basin, specifically integrating lithofacies, mineralogy, and geomechanical properties to evaluate its potential as an unconventional shale gas reservoir.

## Methods

The study utilized core analysis of 60 feet of continuous subsurface samples. Lithofacies were identified using core images, thin section petrography, mineralogy (SEM-EDS), and elemental composition. Fracture systems were analyzed from both core images and well logs. Geomechanical properties were derived from acoustic wave velocity measurements (P-wave and S-wave) under varying confining pressures, followed by the construction of a 1-D Mechanical Earth Model (1-D MEM). Brittleness was determined using both elastic parameters (Young's modulus and Poisson's ratio) and mineralogical approaches.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized core samples from the Qusaiba Shale and Mid-Qusaiba Sandstone, thin sections for petrographic analysis, XRD mineralogical data, SEM-EDS elemental data, and well log data (including sonic logs) to derive geomechanical properties across various lithofacies.

## Key Findings

- Identified three distinct lithofacies in the Qusaiba Shale: Lithofacies-I (micaceous laminated organic-rich mudstone), Lithofacies-II (lamined clay-rich mudstone), and Lithofacies-III (massive siliceous mudstone). ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 19]])
- Identified two lithofacies in the Mid-Qusaiba Sandstone: Lithofacies-IV (Quartz Arenite) and Lithofacies-V (Quartz Wacke). ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 26]])
- Fractures were observed in both Qusaiba Shale and Mid-Qusaiba Sandstone, with many fractures being located specifically in Lithofacies-II and III. ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 37]])
- Established correlations between mechanical parameters (Young's modulus, Poisson's ratio) and physical properties like clay content and mineralogy (QFM). ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 65]])
- Developed a 1-D Mechanical Earth Model (1-D MEM) to determine stress profiles including total/effective vertical and horizontal stresses. ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 77]])
- Evaluated brittleness using two methods: an elastic parameter approach (Young's modulus and Poisson's ratio) and a mineralogy approach. ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 90]])
- Qusaiba Shale is categorized into three lithofacies: (1) Micaceous laminated organic-rich mudstone (Lithofacies-I), (2) Laminate clay-rich mudstone (Lithofacies-II), and (3) Massive siliceous mudstone (Lithofacies-III). ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 21]])
- Lithofacies-I covers approximately 18% (4.84 ft) of the examined core, characterized by high organic matter content (12-15%) and presence of pyrite indicating anoxic conditions. ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 39]])
- Lithofacies-I is composed mainly of detrital mineral grains (quartz, micas) with clay minerals forming the groundmass. ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 39]])
- XRD analysis for Lithofacies-I shows it consists of quartz, muscovite, kaolinite, and albite. ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 40]])
- Interparticle porosity in Lithofacies-I is very low (<1%), but fractures and lamination planes can increase visual porosity to 5-6%. ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 40]])
- Lithofacies-II (Laminated clay-rich mudstone) is the dominant lithofacies in Qusaiba Shale, occurring in 55.87% of the formation with a thickness of 5-15 mm. ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 43]])
- Lithofacies-III (Massive siliceous mudstone) contains high quartz content (78.1%) and is interpreted as being deposited in a low energy environment from suspension settling. ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 46]])
- Lithofacies-IV (Quartz Arenite) shows porosity ranging from 5-15% with an average of approximately 9%. ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 50]])
- Lithofacies-V (Quartz Wacke) exhibits a visual porosity of 2 to 8% with an average of about 5%. ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 53]])

## Limitations

Some correlations between mechanical parameters and physical properties were found to be poor (e.g., Figures 4.9, 4.12, 4.13, 4.14, 4.16, 4.23, and 4.26), potentially due to small-scale heterogeneity in shale or measurement errors.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Reservoir simulations should be run for optimum design of the hydraulic fracture treatment to assess fracture compatibility, containment, and complexity.
- Implement Real Time Geomechanics monitoring to update the MEM (Mechanical Earth Model) in real time.
- Use more data from scattered wells to get 3-D MEM for better delineation of mechanical properties variation.
- Field test data such as extended leak-off tests would enhance the reliability of the mechanical earth model.
- Study borehole images for better interpretation of natural fractures.
- Include Tri-axial compression tests in geomechanical testing to obtain more accurate values for failure parameters (c and Φ).
