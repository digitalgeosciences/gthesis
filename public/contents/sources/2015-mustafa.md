# INTEGRATION OF LITHOFACIES AND GEOMECHANICAL CHARACTERISTICS OF LOWER SILURIAN QUSAIBA SHALE, SAUDI ARABIA

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the geological and geomechanical properties of the Qusaiba Shale in the Rub’ Al-Khali Basin. It integrates lithofacies analysis, mineralogy, and mechanical parameters (Young's modulus, Poisson's ratio) to evaluate brittleness and optimize hydraulic fracturing strategies.

---

## Research Problem

Shale gas reservoirs have low permeability (micro- to nanodarcy range), making production dependent on fracture networks. There is a need to understand the relationship between geological features (lithofacies, mineralogy) and geomechanical properties (Young's modulus, Poisson's ratio, brittleness) to optimize hydraulic fracturing in the Qusaiba Shale.

## Objectives

- Evaluate the geological characteristics of the Qusaiba Shale (lithofacies, mineralogy, and elemental composition).
- Determine the geomechanical characteristics (Young’s modulus, Poisson’s ratio, cohesion, and friction) to understand heterogeneity.
- Identify brittle zones within the shale interval to optimize hydraulic fracture designs.
- Identify lithofacies within the Qusaiba Shale and Mid-Qusaiba Sandstone using core analysis, thin sections, and mineralogy.
- Determine mechanical parameters (Young's modulus, Poisson's ratio) from both laboratory tests and well log data.
- Correlate physical properties (porosity, density, mineral content) with geomechanical parameters.

## Methods

The study utilized core analysis, thin-section petrography, SEM imaging, and XRD for lithofacies and mineralogical characterization. Geomechanical properties were determined using ultrasonic velocity measurements (Autolab 500) to find dynamic elastic moduli and uniaxial compression tests to determine static parameters. Well log data (sonic logs, density logs) were used to calibrate laboratory results and calculate in-situ stresses via the Eaton approach for pore pressure and integration of density logs for overburden stress. A 1-D MEM was constructed to evaluate mud weight windows and drilling stability.

## Data and Materials

60 feet of continuous subsurface core samples from two wells in the Rub’ Al-Khali Basin; 59 thin sections; XRD mineralogical data for 10 representative samples; well log data (Gamma Ray, MSFL, Density, Porosity, Caliper) for Qusaiba Shale and Mid-Qusaiba Sandstone.

## Key Findings

- The Qusaiba Shale was categorized into three lithofacies: (1) Micaceous laminated organic-rich mudstone (18.2%), (2) Laminate clay-rich mudstone (55.87%), and (3) Massive siliceous mudstone (26.1%). ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 21]])
- Lithofacies-III is characterized by high quartz content (78.1%) and was identified as the most brittle lithofacies. ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 48]])
- Mid-Qusaiba Sandstone was categorized into two lithofacies: Quartz Arenite (65.8%) and Quartz Wacke (26.8%). ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 51]])
- Lithofacies-I contains high organic matter (12-15% by volume) and was interpreted as a low energy, anoxic environment. ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 40]])
- Quartz Arenite lithofacies shows porosity ranging from 5-15% with an average of approximately 9%. ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 50]])
- Quartz Wacke lithofacies exhibits a visual porosity of 2 to 8% (average of 5%). ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 53]])
- Fracture intensity is higher in Lithofacies-III due to its higher silica content. ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 59]])
- MSFL logs showed lower values at fracture locations due to conductive formation fluids, while density logs showed spikes and variations indicating high fracture intensity. ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 68]])
- Lithofacies-III (Massive siliceous mudstone) showed the highest brittleness due to high quartz content (60-78%) and higher Young's Modulus (32 GPa). ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 127]])
- The Qusaiba Shale exhibits a reverse stress regime with a maximum horizontal stress gradient of 1.18 psi/ft and minimum horizontal stress gradient of 1.08 psi/ft. ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 131]])
- Brittleness for the Qusaiba Shale is found to be greater than or equal to 43% when Young's Modulus > 25 GPa and Poisson's ratio < 0.25. ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 130]])
- The study identified a narrow mud weight window for safe drilling operations ranging from 14,476 psi to 24,840 psi at 17,403 ft depth. ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 131]])
- P-wave and S-wave velocities are higher along the lamination (horizontal) than perpendicular to it (vertical), indicating transversely isotropic behavior. ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 130]])
- Lithofacies-III showed a Young's Modulus of 32 GPa and Poisson's ratio of 0.25, while Lithofacies-I had an average of 26 GPa and 0.34. ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 125]])
- The Qusaiba Shale contains significant organic matter (12-15% in Lithofacies-I) and shows potential for hydrocarbon maturity as evidenced by SEM images of nano-pores. ([[2015-mustafa|Mustafa, 2015, p. 130]])

## Limitations

Some correlations between mechanical parameters and physical properties were found to be poor (e.g., Figures 4.9, 4.12, 4.13, 4.14, 4.16, 4.23, and 4.26), likely due to small-scale heterogeneity in shale or measurement errors.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Conduct geochemical analysis to supplement lithofacies and geomechanical characterization.
- Run reservoir simulations for optimal hydraulic fracture design.
- Implement real-time geomechanics monitoring to update the MEM.
- Use more data from scattered wells to develop a 3D MEM.
- Perform triaxial compression tests to improve accuracy of failure parameters (c and phi).
