# Compositional gradient calculations for a Saudi Arabian gas condensate reservoir.

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of gravity-induced compositional gradients on reservoir continuity and gas-oil contact (GOC) identification in a Saudi Arabian gas condensate reservoir. By comparing observed PVT data against calculated models using various equations of state, the study concludes that the reservoir is non-continuous and lacks a distinct GOC.

---

## Research Problem

The significant variation in dew point pressure and condensate yield across different wells in a Saudi gas-condensate reservoir (ranging from 40 to 280 bbl/MMscf) suggests either gravity-induced compositional gradients or reservoir discontinuity. The study aims to determine if these variations are due to natural gradient effects or structural barriers.

## Objectives

- Perform compositional gradient calculations induced by gravity forces for the reservoir to verify if observed variations are a result of gravity-induced gradients.
- Use the gradient calculation technique to address and evaluate the continuity of the reservoir.
- Determine the presence or absence of a gas-oil contact (GOC) in the reservoir.
- Validate laboratory-measured PVT data using non-linear regression techniques.
- Calculate expected compositional gradients based on gravity forces and thermal equilibrium.
- Identify potential reservoir barriers or compartments by comparing measured vs. calculated compositions.

## Methods

The study utilized a multi-step workflow: 1. Collection of PVT and flow test data from five wellhead samples (Well-A to Well-E). 2. Validation of data consistency using Hoffman Plots (log(Ki*P) vs. Hoffman Factor). 3. Fluid characterization and tuning of the Peng-Robinson (PR) cubic equation of state (EOS) for each sample using a commercial PVT package from Petroleum Expert. 4. Calculation of compositional gradients based on gravity/chemical equilibrium (GCE) models to predict expected changes in composition with depth. 5. Comparison of observed data against calculated results to identify anomalies indicating reservoir discontinuity.

## Data and Materials

Data from five wellhead samples (Well-A, Well-B, Well-C, Well-D, and Well-E) covering a depth range of over 2000 feet. Included datasets: gas composition (Nitrogen, CO2, H2S, Methane, Ethane, Propane, etc.), flow test data (Gas Rate, Condensate Rate, CGR), and pressure data (Dew Point Pressure, Flowing Bottom-hole Pressure).

## Key Findings

- Condensate Yield (CGR) values in the field vary significantly from 40 to 280 bbl/MMscf. ([[2001-al-subai|Al-Subai, 2001, p. 27]])
- Dew point pressures range between 4500 psig and 5970 psig across the five wells. ([[2001-al-subai|Al-Subai, 2001, p. 28]])
- Well-E was identified as a suspicious sample due to its deviation from the trend in Hoffman Plots, likely caused by missing upper reservoir sections; it was excluded from final analysis. ([[2001-al-subai|Al-Subai, 2001, p. 45]])
- The study concluded that the reservoir is not continuous and there is no gas-oil contact (GOC) present. ([[2001-al-subai|Al-Subai, 2001, p. 12]])
- 3-D seismic data supported the findings by showing faults in the area of interest, confirming lack of continuity. ([[2001-al-subai|Al-Subai, 2001, p. 12]])
- Well-A, Well-B, Well-C, and Well-D were identified as high-quality samples, while Well-E was determined to be non-representative due to potential communication with the reservoir above. ([[2001-al-subai|Al-Subai, 2001, p. 61]])
- Calculated composition for C1 and C7+ components showed a smooth and gradual trend with depth, indicating the reservoir is at thermal equilibrium. ([[2001-al-subai|Al-Subai, 2001, p. 73]])
- Well-B exhibited a deviation of more than 15% from calculated values for C1, suggesting a possible sealing fault barrier between it and other wells. ([[2001-al-subai|Al-Subai, 2001, p. 74]])
- 3-D seismic data confirmed the presence of multiple faults in all directions, supporting the identification of reservoir compartments. ([[2001-al-subai|Al-Subai, 2001, p. 75]])
- Well-C and Well-D were found to be in fluid communication as their compositions fell within a +/- 5% range of the expected gravity-driven trend. ([[2001-al-subai|Al-Subai, 2001, p. 74]])
- No Gas-Oil Contact (GOC) was identified, as calculated saturation pressures remained significantly higher than critical pressure throughout the reservoir. ([[2001-al-subai|Al-Subai, 2001, p. 78]])

## Limitations

The study notes that while some models (like those by Faissat et al.) suggest including capillary forces and thermal gradients for higher accuracy, these were not fully integrated into the primary calculation method due to the assumption of a stationary state.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Consider reservoir discontinuity and compartmentalization during development planning.
- Update study with new samples to further validate reservoir connectivity.
- Utilize compositional numerical simulation to evaluate the impact of composition variation on hydrocarbon volume estimations.
