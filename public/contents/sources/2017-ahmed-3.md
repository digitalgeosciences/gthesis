# OPTIMIZATION OF CORE-FLOODING DESIGN FOR SURFACTANT-POLYMER EOR

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the optimization of surfactant-polymer (SP) flooding in carbonate rocks under harsh conditions (high salinity and high temperature). It evaluates different chemical combinations, slug sizes, and injection sequences using core-flooding experiments supported by micro-CT imaging and rheology measurements.

---

## Research Problem

Chemical EOR (CEOR) methods often fail in harsh reservoir conditions (high salinity/temperature) due to chemical instability or precipitation. There is a need for optimized SP systems that maintain effectiveness while minimizing costs in carbonate reservoirs.

## Objectives

- To identify the best chemical combinations.
- To determine the optimum slug size.
- To optimize the injection sequence.
- To investigate the recovery mechanism and wettability at the pore-scale level.
- Evaluate the effect of different chemical combinations on oil recovery and contact angle.
- Investigate the impact of slug size on the recovery of residual oil saturation (ROS).

## Methods

The study utilized a VINCI core-flooding machine to perform experiments on calcite and Indiana limestone rocks. Rheology was measured for ATBS/AM and TVP polymers, both alone and in combination with surfactants (SS-880 and SS-885). Contact angle measurements were performed on outcrop rock and Indiana limestone discs. Ion chromatography (using a Metrohm 850 Professional IC) analyzed effluent samples to determine ion concentration and retention. Micro-CT scanning (Versa XRM-500) and image processing (PerGeos software) were used for pore-scale imaging of core plugs before and after flooding.

## Data and Materials

Two types of polymers (P1: ATBS/AM; P2: TVP), two surfactants (S1: SS-880; S2: SS-885). Crude oil with 24.6 API and 0.89987 g/cc density. Two types of brine (Formation brine at 229,870 ppm TDS and Sea water at 57,612 ppm TDS). Core samples from carbonate outcrops in Riyadh and Indiana limestone.

## Key Findings

- The optimum chemical combination was found to be carboxybetaine (0.5% wt.) and ATBS/AM (0.25% wt.). ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 18]])
- Recoveries were directly proportional to the slug-size. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 18]])
- The injection sequence SW–SP–SW showed higher recovery than other sequences (SW-P-S-SW, SW-S-SW-P-SW and SW-P-SW-S-SW). ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 18]])
- In the low shear range (0.001 to 3) s-1, the dynamic viscosity of mixtures containing ATBS/AM exhibited small variation with or without surfactant. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 18]])
- Two weeks of thermal aging was sufficient to restore original wettability for calcite rock discs, while one week was insufficient. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 110]])
- Micro-CT scan results indicated a water-wet in-situ wettability for the two weeks aged rock. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 18]])
- Calcium dissolution was noticed in most cases except during polymer injection, where no dissolution occurred. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 18]])
- In the presence of polymer, there was an apparent adsorption of sodium and magnesium ions; surfactant had no effect on these ions. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 18]])
- The combination S1P1 (SS-880 surfactant and ATBS/AM polymer) showed the highest incremental recovery of 11.63% of OIIP compared to other combinations. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 73]])
- S1P2 produced an exceptionally high water-flooding recovery of 80.3% of OIIP, though this was attributed to a longer aging period in previous literature. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 83]])
- The optimal slug size for S1P1 was determined to be 3 PV, where the incremental recovery from ROS stabilized. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 86]])
- The SW-S1P1-SW sequence provided the highest ROS recovery (31.29%) compared to other sequences like SW-P-S-SW or SW-S-SW-P-SW. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 89]])
- Ion chromatography showed that sulfate and chloride ions exhibited more significant retention than magnesium and sodium in both core-flooding experiments. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 94]])
- Micro-CT analysis of the S1P2 sample (CF3-1) showed an initial oil saturation of 0.6065 and a final saturation of 0.156, with some rock dissolution observed. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 98]])
- The addition of surfactant had no effect on the rheology of the mixture in the shear rate range of 3 to 100 s-1. ([[2017-ahmed|Ahmed, 2017, p. 110]])

## Limitations

The study noted that the ability to restore original conditions after chemical introduction must be investigated. Additionally, it was noted that some results (like high water-flood recovery) might be due to specific aging durations in previous studies.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Use a core-flooding system coupled with micro-CT for real-time imaging to avoid sample placement issues.
- Study dynamic adsorption of surfactants and polymers using methods that remove the effect of hydrocarbons from the solution.
- Utilize light oil for micro-CT imaging to improve differentiation between phases due to higher density contrast.
