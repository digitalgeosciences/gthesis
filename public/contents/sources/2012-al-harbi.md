# Evaluation of Organic-Hydrofluoric Acid Mixtures for Sandstone Acidizing

**Summary**: The thesis evaluates the use of organic-hydrofluoric (HF) acid mixtures as an alternative to standard mud acids for stimulating sandstone formations. It investigates chemical interactions between specific minerals (silica, kaolinite, illite, and chlorite) and three types of organic-HF acids to identify potential precipitation issues and determine optimal conditions for improving reservoir permeability.

---

## Research Problem

Standard mud acid (HF) has limitations in sandstone stimulation, including rapid spending, high corrosion rates, and incompatibility with sensitive clays. There is a lack of deep understanding regarding the chemical interactions between organic-HF mixtures and specific minerals like silica and various clay types (kaolinite, illite, chlorite), which can lead to damaging precipitates.

## Objectives

- Provide a systematic investigation of the chemical interaction between various sandstone minerals (silica, kaolinite, Illite, and chlorite) and three different organic-HF acids over a wide range of parameters.
- Perform chelating tests to identify types of precipitate and determine critical pH levels where precipitation begins.
- Assess the chelating power of each acid used in the study.
- Conduct coreflood experiments to evaluate the effectiveness of organic-HF acid mixtures for sandstone stimulation.
- Evaluate the ability of organic acids (acetic, formic, citric) to prevent aluminum-based precipitation in HF mixtures.
- Determine the impact of F/Al ratio and pH on the precipitation of aluminum fluoride.

## Methods

The research involved three main experimental stages: 1. Chelating Tests: Performed using non-glass equipment to avoid HF reactions with glass; six acid mixtures (varying in concentration and type) were tested with aluminum chloride to determine precipitation pH and identify precipitate types via XRD/XRF analysis. 2. Solubility Tests: Analyzed the reactivity of silica and three clays (kaolinite, chlorite, illite) with organic-HF systems at 50°C and 75°C over durations of 1, 2, and 4 hours; liquid samples were analyzed via ICP for Al and Si content while solids were analyzed by XRD or XRF. 3. Coreflood Tests: Simulated reservoir conditions using core plugs (1.5 in x 3 in) in a core holder under pressure/temperature matching the target well; included pre-flushing with organic acid to remove calcite, followed by injection of organic-HF and ammonium chloride to evaluate permeability changes.

## Data and Materials

6 types of organic-HF acid mixtures (e.g., 9% Formic & 1% HF, 10% Acetic & 1% HF, 10% Citric & 1% HF); 3 distinct mineral samples (silica, kaolinite, chlorite, and illite); core plugs measuring 1.5 inches in diameter and 3 inches in length; aluminum chloride salt for chelating tests.

## Key Findings

- The study identifies that organic-HF acids are used as alternatives to mud acid to overcome rapid spending, high corrosion rates, and incompatibility with sensitive clays. ([[2012-al-harbi|Al-Harbi, 2012, p. 10]])
- Chelating tests were designed to determine the critical pH where precipitation starts to form and assess the chelating power of each acid. ([[2012-al-harbi|Al-Harbi, 2012, p. 23]])
- Solubility tests aim to identify which acid is more susceptible to precipitation and which is capable of leaching more aluminum and silicon (more reactive). ([[2012-al-harbi|Al-Harbi, 2012, p. 24]])
- Coreflood experiments are used to simulate reservoir conditions and evaluate the effectiveness of the acid system by comparing permeability before and after stimulation. ([[2012-al-harbi|Al-Harbi, 2012, p. 25]])
- The study highlights that kaolinite is a tetrahedral sheet of Si linked to an alumina octahedral sheet, while illite is composed of an aluminum sheet sandwiched by two silica layers. ([[2012-al-harbi|Al-Harbi, 2012, p. 27]])
- Chlorites are 2:1:1 phyllosilicate minerals with a structure containing layers of octahedrally-coordinated Si, Al, and Fe in the interlayer space. ([[2012-al-harbi|Al-Harbi, 2012, p. 27]])
- Aluminum fluoride (AlF3) precipitation occurred in acetic-HF systems when the F/Al ratio exceeded 1.9 and pH was high. ([[2012-al-harbi|Al-Harbi, 2012, p. 45]])
- Citric acid successfully prevented aluminum precipitation across all tested pH values because of its higher chelating ability compared to formic and acetic acids. ([[2012-al-harbi|Al-Harbi, 2012, p. 51]])
- In 1.5 wt% HF solutions, the addition of Al concentration up to 6,000 mg/L led to precipitation, but no precipitation occurred when Al exceeded 10,000 mg/L (F/Al ratio below 1.9). ([[2012-al-harbi|Al-Harbi, 2012, p. 47]])
- Citric acid is the strongest of the three organic acids tested, with a formation constant for aluminum of 11.7. ([[2012-al-harbi|Al-Harbi, 2012, p. 38]])
- The precipitation of AlF3 in acetic-HF systems was linked to higher initial pH values compared to formic and citric acid systems. ([[2012-al-harbi|Al-Harbi, 2012, p. 45]])
- The critical F/Al ratio to prevent $AlF_3$ precipitation was found to be approximately 1.9; when the ratio exceeds this value, $AlF_3$ forms regardless of the organic acid type. ([[2012-al-harbi|Al-Harbi, 2012, p. 87]])
- Acetic-HF showed a higher propensity to precipitate aluminum trifluoride compared to formic-HF and citric-HF in kaolinite tests. ([[2012-al-harbi|Al-Harbi, 2012, p. 88]])
- formic-HF and citric-HF were found to be more effective than acetic-HF at preventing $AlF_3$ precipitation due to higher chelation abilities for Al. ([[2012-al-harbi|Al-Harbi, 2012]])
