# RESERVOIR CHARACTERIZATION OF UNCONVENTIONAL CALCAREOUS MUDSTONES: KIMMERIDGIAN JUBAILA FORMATION, JAFURAH SUB-BASIN, SAUDI ARABIA

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the characterization of organic-rich carbonate mudstones in the Jubaila Formation within the Jafurah sub-basin. It specifically develops an artificial neural network (ANN) model to predict Total Organic Carbon (TOC) using a combination of conventional well logs and X-ray fluorescence (XRF) data, resulting in a 42% reduction in estimation error compared to existing methods.

---

## Research Problem

The complexity and heterogeneity of organic-rich carbonate mudstones in the Jubaila Formation (Kimmeridgian) make them challenging to characterize for unconventional resource potential. Specifically, understanding how lithofacies variability, paleoenvironmental conditions, and mineralogical/geochemical data relate to sediment provenance and source-rock quality is necessary.

## Methods

The study utilized five subsurface cores (total thickness ~210m) from the Central Arabian intra-shelf basin. Core description included lithology, color, texture, bedding style, sedimentary features, and bioturbation index (BI). Analytical techniques included: 1) Micro X-ray fluorescence (μ-XRF) for elemental distribution; 2) Thin-section petrography to identify mineralogy and diagenetic overprint; 3) XRD (using Panalytical Empyrean system) for mineral weight percentages; 4) QEMSCAN (650F) for high-resolution mineral maps; 5) Open-system pyrolysis for TOC quantification; 6) Hand-held ED-XRF for rapid, high-vertical resolution elemental analysis of over 800 points. Statistical analyses included Pearson correlation, Principal Component Analysis (PCA), and Hierarchical Clustering on Principal Components (HCPC) performed in R software.

## Data and Materials

Data from five subsurface cores of the late Jurassic age in the western Neo-Tethys region. Analysis included 100 samples for certain geochemical correlations, and data from multiple wells (J1, J2, J3, J4, J5) including well logs (GR, SGR, POTA, THOR, URAN, DTCO, DTSM, ILD, ILM, ILS, RHOB, PEF, TNPH).

## Key Findings

- Twelve distinct lithofacies were identified in the Jubaila Formation based on texture, sedimentary features, and mineralogy. ([[2022-septriandi|Septriandi, 2022, p. 40]])
- Strong negative correlation between Ca and terrigenous-associated elements (Si, Al) indicates a clastic origin for silica and aluminum. ([[2022-septriandi|Septriandi, 2022, p. 56]])
- Six distinct clusters were identified using PCA of major and trace elements associated with carbonate, terrigenous, and redox features. ([[2022-septriandi|Septriandi, 2022, p. 59]])
- page_number_reference_note_for_finding_3_is_actually_on_page_60_in_the_summary_of_clusters_and_mineralogy ([[2022-septriandi|Septriandi, 2022]])
- Twelve lithofacies were identified: (1) anhydrite, (2) massive very fine bioturbation calcareous mudstone, (3) laminated very fine and fine calcareous mudstone, (4) thinly dark laminated fine mudstone, (5) laminated coarse and fine mudstone, (6) massive-bioturbated fine mudstone, (7) laminated fine and coarse mudstone, (8) soft-sediment deformation fine mudstone, (9) massive pyritic fine mudstone, (10) bioturbated thin dark-laminated calcareous mudstone, (11) thick laminated coarse and fine mudstone, and (12) strongly bioturbated siliceous coarse mudstone. ([[2022-septriandi|Septriandi, 2022, p. 23]])
- Samples contained high percentages of Ca/calcite, low to moderate Si/quartz, and medium to low abundance of redox-sensitive elements (Ni, V, Mo). ([[2022-septriandi|Septriandi, 2022, p. 24]])
- Six distinct clusters were identified from PCA analysis (CG 1 – CG 6), representing categories such as carbonate-rich, oxygen-depleted, and high terrigenous input lithofacies. ([[2022-septriandi|Septriandi, 2022, p. 24]])
- Darker-coloured lithofacies (LF 4, LF 7, LF 9) with medium to high organic content and low bioturbation indices correspond to anoxic/dysoxic conditions and high productivity. ([[2022-septriandi|Septriandi, 2022, p. 24]])
- Lithofacies with low organic content, coarse-grained texture, and high bioturbation (LF 2, LF 3, LF 5, LF 6, LF 10, LF 11, and LF 12) reflect higher oxygen levels, high energy, and lower productivity. ([[2022-septriandi|Septriandi, 2022, p. 25]])
- The organic-rich interval is associated with the Transgressive System Tract (TST), particularly in the distal well, and decreases within the Highstand System Tract (HST). ([[2022-septriandi|Septriandi, 2022, p. 25]])
- Strong correlation was found between TOC and Mo, Cu, Ni for all wells. ([[2022-septriandi|Septriandi, 2022, p. 19]])
- Well J2 and J4 (proximal) were identified as more siliceous (G2), while J3, J1, and J5 (distal) were categorized as marl/marly limestone (G1). ([[2022-septriandi|Septriandi, 2022, p. 19]])
- The ANN model provided a continuous and high-resolution vertical and lateral TOC distribution. ([[2022-septriandi|Septriandi, 2022, p. 20]])
- The Jubaila Formation is characterized as a promising unconventional resource with high organic content (TOC) and an appropriate maturity window for generating hydrocarbons. ([[2022-septriandi|Septriandi, 2022, p. 32]])
- Jubaila mudstones contain micropores to nanopores, with permeability typically below 0.01 millidarcy. ([[2022-septriandi|Septriandi, 2022, p. 35]])

## Limitations

The study noted the need for more advanced imaging techniques (micro-CT, FIB-SEM) to understand pore geometry and stable isotope analysis ($\delta^{13}C$ and $\delta^{18}O$) to reconstruct paleogeographic conditions.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future work should include: 1. Mercury porosimetry and nitrogen gas adsorption for porosity/permeability; 2. Advanced imaging (micro-CT, FIB-SEM) to visualize 3D pore networks; 3. Stable isotope analysis to determine geochemical signatures of diagenetic fluids.
