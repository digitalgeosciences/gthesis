# WORMHOLE SUSTAINABILITY UNDER HIGH STRESSES IN CARBONATE ROCKS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the mechanical stability and sustainability of acid-induced wormholes in carbonate rocks (Austin chalk and Indiana limestone) under high confining pressures. Using a combination of coreflooding, CT scanning, triaxial testing, and finite element modeling, it concludes that Austin chalk is more susceptible to wormhole closure and deformation than Indiana limestone due to its lower Young's modulus.

---

## Research Problem

The degradation of carbonate rock mechanical properties during acid treatment (acidizing) can lead to the loss of wormhole stability under high stress. The study specifically addresses how different carbonate types (chalk vs. limestone) and various acid systems affect the structural integrity of these channels in a reservoir context.

## Objectives

- Understand the behavior and sustainability of wormholes under high confining stress.
- Investigate the impact of high confining stresses on the shape of wormholes and wormholing characteristics in various carbonate formations.
- Evaluate the degree of damage to mechanical properties after stimulation by comparing dynamic elastic properties of acidized versus intact samples.
- Evaluate the petrophysical and mechanical properties of Austin chalk and Indiana limestone before and after acid treatment.
- Compare the impact of different acid systems (EPDI, Acetic Acid, Formic Acid, Hydrochloric Acid) on wormhole formation in limestone.
- Assess the sustainability of wormholes under high confining pressure using triaxial testing and CT scanning.
- Model the mechanical behavior and stress distribution around wormholes using Finite Element Modeling (FEM).
- Evaluate the stability of wormhole channels in chalk and limestone samples under high confining pressure using a triaxial cell.
- Compare the petrophysical properties (porosity, permeability) and elastic properties (Young's modulus) of acidized vs. intact samples.
- Visualize the impact of confining stress on wormhole geometry using CT scans.
- Quantify stress and strain concentration within wormholes using finite element simulations.

## Methods

The study utilized two phases: Phase 1 focused on Austin chalk (AC) and Indiana limestone (IL) treated with EPDI acid; Phase 2 focused on IL treated with Acetic, Formic, and Hydrochloric acids. Core samples were prepared to 3x1.5 inches. Petrophysical properties (porosity/permeability) were measured via helium porosity and air permeability. Mineralogy was determined by XRD. Wormholes were created using a coreflooding system. Post-treatment, samples underwent CT scanning to visualize wormhole geometry. Mechanical properties (Young's modulus, Poisson's ratio) were derived from triaxial tests in an AutoLab 1500 system. Finally, COMSOL Multiphysics was used for finite element modeling of the stress distribution around wormholes.

## Data and Materials

8 samples in Phase 1 (4 Austin chalk, 4 Indiana limestone); 6 samples in Phase 2 (all Indiana limestone). Core dimensions: 3 inches length x 1.5 inches diameter. Measurement parameters included helium porosity (Table 3-1), XRD mineralogy (Table 3-2), and triaxial testing at up to 88 MPa.

## Key Findings

- Acid treatment significantly reduces the stiffness and elastic modulus of carbonate rocks, with the greatest impact observed on chalk samples. ([[2023-al-garni|Al-Garni, 2023, p. 2]])
- Wormholes in chalk samples were found to be less stable than those in limestone; specifically, wormhole branches were more susceptible to deformation and closure in the softer chalk. ([[2023-al-garni|Al-Garni, 2023, p. 13]])
- No fractures were observed in the Indiana limestone samples during high-pressure testing, whereas fractures were noted in one of the chalk samples. ([[2023-al-garni|Al-Garni, 2023, p. 13]])
- The study confirmed that wormholes are more likely to close under confining pressure in porous/weak carbonate matrices like chalk compared to rigid limestone. ([[2023-al-garni|Al-Garni, 2023, p. 15]])
- Finite element simulations successfully replicated experimental results, confirming the higher susceptibility of wormhole branches to deformation and closure. ([[2023-al-garni|Al-Garni, 2023, p. 13]])
- Austin chalk (AC) exhibited higher porosity (27.0–28.6%) and permeability (14.3–32.9 mD) compared to Indiana limestone (IL) (16.8–18.0% and 4.1–15.7 mD). ([[2023-al-garni|Al-Garni, 2023, p. 22]])
- XRD analysis confirmed both Austin chalk and Indiana limestone are composed of nearly pure calcite (>99%). ([[2023-al-garni|Al-Garni, 2023, p. 23]])
- Austin chalk (AC) had a lower initial Young's modulus (17.8 GPa) compared to Indiana limestone (IL) (24.8 GPa). ([[2023-al-garni|Al-Garni, 2023, p. 23]])
- Increasing acid concentration from 10% to 15% resulted in a 60% drop in core strength and poorer conductivity. ([[2023-al-garni|Al-Garni, 2023, p. 21]])
- Analysis of the chalk models revealed that smaller (branched) wormholes were more vulnerable to strain than larger ones. ([[2023-al-garni|Al-Garni, 2023, p. 47]])
- Tilted and horizontal wormholes experienced more strain than vertical ones in the finite element model. ([[2023-al-garni|Al-Garni, 2023, p. 47]])
- Indiana limestone samples were almost immune to high-stress conditions, showing only slight changes in wormhole geometry under stress. ([[2023-al-garni|Al-Garni, 2023, p. 52]])
- Austin chalk was significantly more affected by high stress (88 MPa), leading to the partial closure of main and subsidiary channels. ([[2023-al-garni|Al-Garni, 2023, p. 53]])
- Wormhole stability is a strong function of the rock elastic modulus; Indiana limestone, having a higher Young's modulus, did not suffer significant reduction in wormhole size compared to Austin chalk. ([[2023-al-garni|Al-Garni, 2023, p. 53]])
- Austin rock samples were more susceptible to damage than their Indiana counterparts under high confining pressure. ([[2023-al-garni|Al-Garni, 2023, p. 54]])
