# WORMHOLE SUSTAINABILITY UNDER HIGH STRESSES IN CARBONATE ROCKS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the mechanical stability and sustainability of acid-induced wormholes in carbonate rocks (Austin chalk and Indiana limestone) under high confining pressures up to 88 MPa. Using a combination of coreflooding, triaxial testing, CT scanning, and finite element modeling, it concludes that wormholes in softer chalk are more susceptible to deformation and closure than those in rigid limestone.

---

## Research Problem

The degradation of carbonate rock mechanical properties and the subsequent closure of acid-induced wormhole channels under high confining stresses (up to 88 MPa) during production or injection processes.

## Objectives

- Understand the behavior and sustainability of wormholes in carbonate rocks.
- Investigate, via controlled experimentation, the impact of high confining stresses on the shape of wormholes and the wormholing characteristics of various carbonate formations.
- Evaluate the damage degree on mechanical properties after stimulation by comparing dynamic elastic properties of outcrop carbonate samples.

## Methods

The study utilized two types of rock: Austin chalk (AC) and Indiana limestone (IL). Core samples were prepared (1.5x3 inches), characterized for porosity, permeability, and mineralogy (XRD). Wormholes were created using coreflooding with various acids (EPDI, Acetic, Formic, Hydrochloric). Stability was assessed via triaxial testing in an AutoLab 1500 system to measure Young's modulus and Poisson's ratio under confining pressures up to 88 MPa. Medical-type CT scans were performed before and after stress tests to visualize wormhole geometry. ImageJ was used for segmenting and quantifying wormhole volumes. Finally, COMSOL Multiphysics was used for finite element modeling (FEM) to simulate deformation and identify high strain concentration areas.

## Data and Materials

8 core samples in Phase 1 (4 Austin chalk, 4 Indiana limestone); 6 additional Indiana limestone samples in Phase 2. Core dimensions: 1.5 inches x 3 inches. Measurement parameters included confining pressures up to 88 MPa and permeability measurements at multiple pressure stages.

## Key Findings

- Austin chalk (AC) showed higher porosity (27.0-28.6%) and permeability (14.3-32.9 mD) compared to Indiana limestone (IL) (16.8-18.0% and 4.1-15.7 mD). ([[2023-al-garni|Al-Garni, 2023, p. 22]])
- Both Austin chalk and Indiana limestone were confirmed as nearly pure calcite (>99%) via XRD analysis. ([[2023-al-garni|Al-Garni, 2023, p. 23]])
- Wormholes in the Austin chalk samples showed significant closure of both main channels and branches under high confining pressure, while Indiana limestone wormholes remained largely stable. ([[2023-al-garni|Al-Garni, 2023, p. 48]])
- The permeability of Austin chalk samples declined sharply during confining, specifically linked to the closing of wormhole structures. ([[2023-al-garni|Al-Garni, 2023, p. 56]])
- Young's modulus in Austin chalk (AC-1) showed a significant drop at approximately 40 MPa, indicating potential damage or closure of the wormhole. ([[2023-al-garni|Al-Garni, 2023, p. 55]])
- Finite element modeling revealed that branched and horizontal/tilted wormholes were more susceptible to strain than larger, vertical ones. ([[2023-al-garni|Al-Garni, 2023, p. 47]])
- The study found that different acids (Acetic, Formic, Hydrochloric) produced similar mechanical behavior in Indiana limestone samples. ([[2023-al-garni|Al-Garni, 2023, p. 63]])
- Wormholes in Austin chalk were more susceptible to deformation because of the lower Young's Modulus compared to Indiana limestone. ([[2023-al-garni|Al-Garni, 2023, p. 53]])
