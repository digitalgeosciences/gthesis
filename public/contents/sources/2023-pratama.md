# SPONTANEOUS IMBIBITION OF ACIDIC FLUIDS IN SANDSTONES AND CARBONATES: A CORE- AND MICRO-SCALE PERSPECTIVE

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of acidic fluids on the petrophysical properties (porosity, permeability, and wettability) of sandstone and carbonate core samples during spontaneous imbibition. It utilizes a combination of laboratory experiments, NMR pore size distribution analysis, and coupled hydro-chemical simulations to model calcite dissolution and its effects on fluid dynamics.

---

## Research Problem

The coupling of capillary interactions with fluid reactivity is often neglected in fluid transport studies, despite its importance in processes like acidizing, pollutant transport, and CO2 sequestration. Specifically, the impact of acidic fluids on petrophysical properties during spontaneous imbibition (where no external pressure is applied) remains under-explored.

## Objectives

- Study the impact of reactive transport during spontaneous imbibition on petrophysical properties at two different acidic conditions (pH 2 and 4).
- Evaluate these effects using both core-scale experiments and micro-scale experiments.
- Compare petrophysical property changes (porosity, permeability, pore size distribution, contact angle) between sandstone and carbonate samples.
- Investigate the role of mineral dissolution and precipitation in altering rock properties.
- Determine how reactivity influences the spontaneous imbibition process in capillary systems.
- Identify the underlying mechanisms governing the interaction between reactive fluids and porous media during spontaneous imbibition.

## Methods

The study utilizes a multi-scale experimental approach. Core-scale experiments involve six rock samples (3 sandstones, 3 carbonates) where capillary rise is measured using deionized water and acidic solutions (pH 2, 4). Image analysis of video recordings tracks the capillary rise profile. Micro-scale experiments use capillary tubes of various sizes with in-situ calcite crystallization to simulate mineral growth/dissolution. Numerical modeling and simulation are used to model coupled imbibition and reaction, specifically evaluating parameters like the van-Genuchten constants (alpha, M), liquid entry pressure, and reaction rates on petrophysical properties.

## Data and Materials

6 core samples (3 sandstone, 3 carbonate) for core-scale experiments; multiple capillary tubes of various sizes for micro-scale experiments; fluids used include deionized water and acidic solutions at pH 2 and 4; multi-scale data includes porosity, permeability, pore size distribution (from NMR), and contact angle measurements.

## Key Findings

- Calcite dissolution is identified as the primary reaction resulting in the opening of pore space in carbonate samples. ([[2023-pratama|PRATAMA, 2023, p. 20]])
- Carbonate samples showed higher capillary rise, increased porosity, and larger mean pore sizes when exposed to acidic fluids compared to neutral fluids. ([[2023-pratama|PRATAMA, 2023, p. 20]])
- Acidic interaction caused the carbonate matrix to transition from oil-wet to neutral-wet by altering the contact angle. ([[2023-pratama|PRATAMA, 2023, p. 20]])
- The study identifies four distinct flow regimes during capillary rise: purely inertial, IT (inertial-to-viscous) transition, purely viscous, and VG (viscous-to-gravitational) transition. ([[2023-pratama|PRATAMA, 2023, p. 37]])
- Petrophysical properties in carbonate samples showed an increase in porosity and permeability after acid treatment due to mineral dissolution. ([[2023-pratama|PRATAMA, 2023, p. 12]])
- The study evaluates the impact of various parameters (van-Genuchten constants, entry pressure, reaction rate) on petrophysical property evolution through simulation. ([[2023-pratama|PRATAMA, 2023, p. 117]])
- Austin Chalk (AU) showed the highest capillary height (20 mm), while IL100 had the lowest (6 mm). ([[2023-pratama|PRATAMA, 2023, p. 70]])
- Acid-based capillary height was generally higher than initial capillary height, with a significant increase in IL100 of approximately 50% post-reaction. ([[2023-pratama|PRATAMA, 2023, p. 71]])
- Tsunazawa's model provided the best fit for water imbibition data (R2 values up to 0.996) compared to Lucas-Washburn and Quéré equations. ([[2023-pratama|PRATAMA, 2023, p. 72]])
- Porosity increased by less than 1% in all samples after acid treatment, with the highest increase in AU (0.73%). ([[2023-pratama|PRATAMA, 2023, p. 74]])
- Permeability showed significant changes: IL100 increased by 24.4%, while IL2 experienced a notable drop of 13.3% (4.6 mD). ([[2023-pratama|PRATAMA, 2023, p. 75]])
- Contact angle in the AU sample significantly reduced, transitioning from neutral-wet to water-wet. ([[2023-pratama|PRATAMA, 2023, p. 75]])
- ICP-OES analysis of fluid after reaction with AU core showed Ca2+ levels reaching ~1000 ppm, indicating significant calcite dissolution. ([[2023-pratama|PRATAMA, 2023, p. 84]])
- The study identified that the reduction in contact angle and increase in tortuosity (in some cases) were primary drivers for higher capillary heights in acid-treated samples. ([[2023-pratama|PRATAMA, 2023, p. 83]])
- In carbonate samples, acid-based capillary rise was consistently higher than the pre-reaction water capillary rise at lower pH (pH 2). ([[2023-pratama|PRATAMA, 2023, p. 97]])

## Limitations

Micro-scale experiments had limited camera resolution to capture subtle changes in calcite crystal size/angles. Simulation models used simplified inputs (e.g., a single-value permeability) and lacked the ability to account for complex core heterogeneity or varying reaction rates based on specific mineral compositions.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Expand research to larger scales and longer durations; explore other acids like carbonic and hydrofluoric acid; implement real-time observation of pore-scale interactions; conduct thermo-hydro-chemo-mechanical (THCM) studies including temperature variations and geomechanical assessments.
