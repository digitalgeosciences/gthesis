# MODELING CLIMATE CHANGE IMPACT ON SEAWATER INTRUSION IN THE COASTAL AQUIFER OF THE EASTERN PROVINCE OF SAUDI ARABIA

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of climate change, specifically sea level rise and varying precipitation patterns, on seawater intrusion in coastal aquifers of the Eastern Province of Saudi Arabia. Using a density-dependent flow model (SEAWAT), it simulates three climate scenarios to predict saltwater encroachment and proposes management strategies like injection wells to mitigate these impacts.

---

## Research Problem

The lack of research on hydrogeological conditions and the specific impact of sea level rise and climate change on seawater intrusion in the coastal aquifers of the Eastern Province, Saudi Arabia.

## Objectives

- Interpret the coastal aquifers of the Eastern Province of Saudi Arabia through developing the conceptual, and numerical groundwater flow models.
- Study the movement of saltwater encroachment into the coastal aquifers in the study area by building a seawater intrusion model for the Easter Province aquifer.
- Determine the climate change impacts (sea level rise and recharge rates) on seawater intrusion in the aquifer, using a density-dependent flow model.
- Testing various scenarios of climate change impact using the developed model to produce actionable recommendations for the Eastern Province aquifer.

## Methods

The study utilizes a multi-step methodology: 1. Data collection from literature and reports; 2. Development of a conceptual model incorporating geology and boundary conditions; 3. Numerical modeling using MODFLOW (for flow) and SEAWAT (for density-dependent flow and solute transport); 4. Simulation of three climate scenarios (SC1, SC2, SC3) based on different sea level rise (0.58m, 0.70m, 0.91m) and recharge rates; 5. Analysis of results to provide management recommendations.

## Data and Materials

Hydrogeological data for Neogene, Alat, and Khobar aquifers; historical rainfall records from 2010-2022; sea level rise projections; model grid dimensions (5000m x 50m x 50m) with a resolution of 100m x 5m x 50m.

## Key Findings

- The current saltwater-freshwater interface in the study area is approximately 980 meters inland from the shoreline. ([[2024-ahmad|Ahmad, 2024, p. 42]])
- Scenario SC1 (minimal impact) results in a horizontal increase of 27% in the extent of saltwater reaching the bottom bed of the aquifer compared to current conditions. ([[2024-ahmad|Ahmad, 2024, p. 42]])
- Scenario SC2 (intermediate impact) shows an increase of 32% in the horizontal distance of salt intrusion compared to current conditions. ([[2024-ahmad|Ahmad, 2024, p. 42]])
- Scenario SC3 (maximum impact) results in a 37% increase in the extent of saltwater reaching the bottom bed of the aquifer compared to current conditions. ([[2024-ahmad|Ahmad, 2024, p. 42]])
- The maximum horizontal distance of salt intrusion under scenario SC3 is projected to reach 1,500 meters inland. ([[2024-ahmad|Ahmad, 2024, p. 42]])
- Implementation of injection wells (S1) reduced the impact of the saline area in the aquifer by 12.64% compared to current conditions. ([[2024-ahmad|Ahmad, 2024, p. 52]])
- The average annual precipitation in the Eastern Province was approximately 90 mm/year, with a recharge rate used for modeling at 5% of this value (4.5 mm/yr). ([[2024-ahmad|Ahmad, 2024, p. 36]])

## Limitations

Assumptions include: constant pumping rates; constant lateral flow; current sea level as zero baseline; linear variation of sea level with time; and a homogeneous aquifer with a horizontal to vertical anisotropy factor of 1:10.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Proposed measures include building seawalls, managing groundwater effectively (reducing extraction), developing salt-tolerant crops, and implementing injection wells (S1 or S2) to push back the saltwater-freshwater interface.
