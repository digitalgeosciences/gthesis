# MODELING GEOCHEMICAL AND GEOMECHANICAL EFFECTS OF CO2 REACTIVE TRANSPORT IN DEEP SALINE LIMESTONE AQUIFER

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the geochemical and geomechanical impacts of CO2 injection into a hypothetical saline aquifer. Using the GEM simulator, it evaluates how variables like temperature, salinity, and injection rates affect CO2 solubility, mineral dissolution/precipitation (specifically calcite), and subsequent changes in rock porosity and permeability.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the challenge of predicting and managing the geochemical and geomechanical risks associated with CO2 sequestration in deep saline aquifers, specifically focusing on how reactive transport affects reservoir integrity and storage capacity.

## Objectives

- Examine how aquifer salinity, injection temperature, and injection rates affect the storativity of limestone saline aquifers during the process of CO2 sequestration.
- Assess the geomechanical consequences of CO2 injection to guarantee the secure and effective containment of the stored CO2.
- Evaluate the impact of varying injection temperatures on CO2 solubility and storage capacity.
- Assess the effect of aquifer salinity on CO2 solubility and subsequent mineral interactions.
- Analyze the influence of different injection rates and durations on pressure buildup and total CO2 volume.
- Quantify geomechanical changes (porosity, permeability, stress) resulting from chemical reactions like calcite dissolution/precipitation.

## Methods

The study utilizes the GEM simulator (CMG) to model multiphase fluid flow, geochemical interactions, and thermal dynamics. The simulation incorporates the Peng-Robinson Equation of State (PR EOS) for fluid properties, the B-dot model for mineral dissolution/precipitation, and geomechanical modules to calculate stress and strain. A 10,946-cell grid was used to represent a 11.2 km² aquifer with varying porosity (0.05-0.38) and permeability (7-314 mD). Geostatistical kriging was applied for property distribution.

## Data and Materials

A hypothetical saline aquifer model of 11.2 km² area, 1,000 ft thickness, at a depth of 2,640 ft and temperature of 125°F. The model includes three lithologies (sandstone caprock, limestone aquifer, shale seal). Brine chemistry was based on specific ion concentrations (e.g., Na+ 59,491 ppm, Cl- 132,060 ppm).

## Key Findings

- Injection of CO2 into brine leads to a decrease in pH, causing dissolution of minerals like calcite and feldspar, which can lead to the formation of secondary carbonate minerals. ([[2024-badhafere|BADHAFERE, 2024, p. 31]])
- Exposure to CO2-saturated brine for 12 months resulted in reductions of up to 30% in elastic moduli (Young's modulus and shear modulus) and 20-30% in compressive and tensile strength in sandstone. ([[2024-badhafere|BADHAFERE, 2024, p. 35]])
- Higher temperatures (70°C and 90°C) led to more rapid and extensive formation of wormholes compared to lower temperatures (50°C), increasing porosity and permeability but decreasing mechanical strength. ([[2024-badhafere|BADHAFERE, 2024, p. 38]])
- Lower salinity levels (50,000 ppm) resulted in more extensive and rapid wormhole formation compared to higher salinity levels (100,000 ppm and 200,000 ppm). ([[2024-badhafere|BADHAFERE, 2024, p. 39]])
- The Sleipner project has successfully stored over 20 million tonnes of CO2 in the Utsira sandstone formation. ([[2024-badhafere|BADHAFERE, 2024, p. 52]])
- The Illinois Basin Decatur Project (IBDP) has successfully injected and stored over 1 million tonnes of CO2 in the Mt. Simon Sandstone since 2011. ([[2024-badhafere|BADHAFERE, 2024, p. 53]])
- Lower injection temperatures (90°C-110°C) significantly increased CO2 solubility in brine compared to higher temperatures, allowing for longer injection periods before reaching pressure limits. ([[2024-badhafere|BADHAFERE, 2024, p. 99]])
- Lower salinity levels (e.g., 50,000 ppm vs 200,000 ppm) resulted in a 47.5% increase in storage capacity due to reduced ionic strength promoting CO2 solubility. ([[2024-badhafere|BADHAFERE, 2024, p. 100]])
- Calcite dissolution near the wellbore (due to acidic conditions) increased porosity, while precipitation further from the wellbore led to a reduction in porosity. ([[2024-badhafere|BADHAFERE, 2024, p. 72]])
- Porosity variations resulting from chemical reactions near the wellbore were measured between 0.6% and 2.0%. ([[2024-badhafere|BADHAFERE, 2024, p. 98]])

- Vertical displacements were consistently small, ranging from 0.17 to 0.18 feet across all scenarios, indicating high geomechanical stability. ([[2024-badhafere|BADHAFERE, 2024, p. 100]])
- The study identified three primary trapping mechanisms: structural (initial), residual (post-injection), and solubility (long-term). ([[2024-badhafere|BADHAFERE, 2024, p. 75]])
- Higher injection rates led to larger cumulative CO2 volumes in shorter periods due to rapid pressure build-up, while lower rates allowed for longer durations but less total volume. ([[2024-badhafere|BADHAFERE, 2024, p. 77]])

## Limitations

The study uses a hypothetical aquifer model; it does not account for complex wellbore stability issues or advanced wormhole geometries in the primary simulation results.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Study the effects of CO2 sequestration on wellbore stability and stress dynamics around the wellbore.
- Improve simulation tools to accurately predict the formation, dimensions, and properties of wormholes.
- Expand model scope to include mixed lithology aquifers, high temperature/pressure conditions, and variations in CO2 purity.
