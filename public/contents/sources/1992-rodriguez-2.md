# Surface Charge Behavior and Adsorption of Surfactants on Carbonate Rocks

**Summary**: The thesis investigated the surface charge behavior (zeta potential) and adsorption characteristics of various surfactants (Triton X-102, B1083, Sapogenat T-100) on carbonate rocks under different salinities and temperatures. It concluded that Triton X-102 is a promising candidate for surfactant flooding in carbonate reservoirs due to its low adsorption and high desorption levels.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the challenge of surfactant adsorption on carbonate rock surfaces, which can hinder the effectiveness of enhanced oil recovery (EOR) processes. Specifically, it evaluates how surface charge (zeta potential) correlates with adsorption behavior for different surfactant types in varying reservoir conditions.

## Objectives

- Study the effect of surfactants on the surface charge behavior of carbonate rocks.
- Conduct static and dynamic adsorption tests to determine surfactant loss at the solid-liquid interface.
- Determine which surfactant has the lowest adsorption capacity on carbonate rocks.
- Determine the zeta potential of carbonate rocks in deionized water and high brine salinities.
- Evaluate the static adsorption isotherms of Triton X-102, B1083, and Sapogenat T-100 on carbonate rock.
- Compare dynamic adsorption results with static adsorption experiments.

## Methods

Zeta potential was measured using a Zeta-meter 3.0 unit with a microscope module to evaluate surface charge as a function of pH and concentration in deionized water and 25,000 ppm brine. Static adsorption tests were conducted by contacting crushed carbonate rock (2g) with surfactant solutions at 25°C and 90°C, followed by analysis via UV-260 spectrophotometry. Dynamic adsorption was performed using a core flooding setup involving core plugs (1 inch diameter), high pressure (800 psi), and temperature control (107°C). Surface area was determined using the BET method with nitrogen gas.

## Data and Materials

Laboratory samples of carbonate rock; three types of surfactants (Triton X-102, B1083, Sapogenat T-100); various brine concentrations (up to 57,000 ppm TDS); temperature conditions including 107 °C.

## Key Findings

- The addition of anionic surfactant (B1083) decreased the isoelectric point of the carbonate rock when tested in deionized water. ([[1992-rodriguez|Rodriguez, 1992, p. 52]])
- An increase in salinity resulted in an increase in the negativity of the zeta potential of the carbonate rock, making it more suitable for anionic and nonionic surfactants. ([[1992-rodriguez|Rodriguez, 1992, p. 51]])
- Sapogenat T-100 exhibited the highest adsorption level among the surfactants tested. ([[1992-rodriguez|Rodriguez, 1992, p. 79]])
- Triton X-102 showed the lowest adsorption level and the highest desorption level (over 30% recovery) at 107°C and 57,000 ppm salinity. ([[1992-rodriguez|Rodriguez, 1992, p. 81]])
- The specific surface area of the rock chips was determined to be 0.317 m²/g using the BET method. ([[1992-rodriguez|Rodriguez, 1992, p. 43]])
- At high brine salinities, the rock surface remained negatively charged over the entire range of pH studied. ([[1992-rodriguez|Rodriguez, 1992, p. 90]])
- The time required for adsorption equilibrium in dynamic tests varied inversely with the surfactant concentration. ([[1992-rodriguez|Rodriguez, 1992, p. 90]])
- Dynamic adsorption results correlated well with static adsorption experiments. ([[1992-rodriguez|Rodriguez, 1992, p. 90]])
- For B1083, the adsorption density increased with surfactant concentration while zeta potential increased negatively until a certain value and then remained constant. ([[1992-rodriguez|Rodriguez, 1992, p. 82]])

## Limitations

Zeta potential measurements were limited by the equipment's maximum salinity capacity (25,000 ppm) and required room temperature conditions, whereas adsorption tests were conducted at higher temperatures and salinities.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Conduct interfacial tension studies for Triton X-102.
- Modify the Zeta meter system to obtain measurements at higher ionic strengths and temperatures.
- Use zeta potential as a screening method for adsorption of ionic surfactants, pending further research.
