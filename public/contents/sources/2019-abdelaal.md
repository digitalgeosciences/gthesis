# OPTIMIZATION OF CO2/N2 FOAM PARAMETERS FOR EOR IN SANDSTONE RESERVOIRS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the optimization of CO2/N2 mixed foam parameters (injection rate, gas ratio, and foam quality) for enhanced oil recovery in sandstone reservoirs. Using Berea sandstone cores, it concludes that adding N2 to CO2 improves foam stability at supercritical conditions and that specific foam qualities (up to 80%) optimize oil recovery.

---

## Research Problem

The primary challenge of using supercritical CO2 for EOR is the formation of weak and unstable foam in high-pressure/high-temperature (HPHT) environments, leading to poor sweep efficiency. This study addresses this by evaluating a mixture of CO2 and N2 with an AOS surfactant.

## Objectives

- Evaluate the effect of changing CO2/N2 ratio on the performance of mixed foam.
- Evaluate the effect of different injection rates on foam stability and oil recovery.
- Identify the optimal foam quality for maximizing recovery and maintaining stable pressure drops.
- Determine the optimal N2 percentage within a CO2-N2 mixture to improve foam stability at supercritical conditions.

## Methods

The study utilized a core flooding system to conduct experiments on Berea sandstone cores. The workflow included: 1) Preparation of synthetic formation brine (253.88 TDS) and seawater (67.71 TDS). 2) Core drying, firing, and saturation with brine. 3) Crude oil injection to establish irreducible water saturation (Swi). 4) Aging under reservoir conditions. 5) Water flooding as a secondary recovery method. 6) Mixed CO2/N2 foam flooding using an AOS surfactant at 88°C and 1800 psi, monitored via pressure drop, oil recovery factor, and breakthrough time.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized three different sandstone cores with permeabilities of approximately 97 md (Experiment 1), 72 md (Experiment 2), and 65 md (Experiment 3). Additionally, two other cores were used for varying N2 percentages (Experiments 4 and 5) and foam qualities (Experiments 6 and 7).

## Key Findings

- Adding N2 to CO2 improves foam stability at supercritical conditions, where pure CO2 foam is typically weak. ([[2019-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2019, p. 18]])
- Increasing the percentage of N2 enhances oil recovery and produces more stable foam up to 20% by volume; beyond 20%, oil recovery is adversely affected. ([[2019-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2019, p. 18]])
- Increasing foam quality up to 80% produces a foam with finer texture that gives more stability and recovery, while 90% results in coarser, unstable 'dry' foam. ([[2019-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2019, p. 18]])
- High injection rates produce higher shear rates that may lead to foam collapse. ([[2019-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2019, p. 20]])
- Initial water saturation (Swi) in all experiments ranged from 31.6% to 37.53%. ([[2019-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2019, p. 36]])
- Average porosity of the tested Berea sandstone cores was approximately 20%. ([[2019-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2019, p. 36]])
- Absolute permeability values for the seven core samples ranged from 65 md to 97.78 md. ([[2019-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2019, p. 36]])
- Water flooding alone achieved recovery factors between 45% and 60% in the initial stages. ([[2019-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2019, p. 58]])
- The addition of N2 to CO2 in foam mixtures improved both foam stability (indicated by stable pressure drop) and oil recovery compared to pure CO2 foams. ([[2019-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2019, p. 108]])
- An injection rate of 0.5 cc/min provided the most stable pressure drop and higher oil recovery compared to 0.75 or 1 cc/min rates. ([[2019-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2019, p. 102]])
- A 20% N2 concentration was identified as the optimal balance, providing high stability without significantly sacrificing the recovery benefits of CO2. ([[2019-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2019, p. 103]])
- Foam quality at 0.8 provided the best results; values below 0.8 led to lower recovery, while values above 0.8 (e.g., 0.9) produced 'dry foam' which reduced stability and recovery. ([[2019-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2019, p. 105]])
- Experiment 2 (Base Case: 20% N2, 0.8 Foam Quality) achieved the highest ultimate recovery of 90.7% with a breakthrough at 0.31 PV. ([[2019-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2019, p. 69]])
- The core permeability for Experiment 1 was approximately 97 md, while Experiment 2 was 72 md and Experiment 3 was 65 md. ([[2019-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2019, p. 60]])
- AOS surfactant proved capable of generating stable foam in high-salinity environments. ([[2019-abdelaal|Abdelaal, 2019, p. 108]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Change the flooding strategy from co-injection to surfactant alternating gas (SAG).
- Introduce visualized testing during core flooding to trace flood fronts and contacted regions.
- Investigate phase behavior of injected gases and surfactants when contacting oil in place.
- Use foam generators with cameras to observe foam texture and quality before flooding.
