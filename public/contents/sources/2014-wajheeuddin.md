# DEVELOPMENT OF AN ENVIRONMENTALLY-FRIENDLY DRILLING FLUID USING DATE SEEDS AND GRASS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the development of an environmentally friendly drilling fluid by incorporating natural additives—date seeds, grass, and grass ash—into a water-based mud system. The study evaluates these materials' suitability as alternatives to toxic chemical additives by analyzing their particle size distribution, rheological properties (plastic viscosity, yield point), and filtration characteristics under various conditions.

---

## Research Problem

The use of toxic chemicals in conventional drilling fluids poses significant risks to human health and the environment, necessitating the development of sustainable, low-cost, and environmentally friendly alternatives that maintain essential drilling functions like lubrication, cooling, and hole stability.

## Objectives

- To investigate the particle size distribution of the proposed additives which are date seeds, grass and grass ash.
- To formulate an environmentally friendly mud system using the above natural substitutes as additives.
- To investigate the characteristics and behavior of the newly formulated drilling mud system using the proposed additives.
- Conduct XRF analysis to determine the elemental composition of date seeds, grass, and grass ash.
- Perform particle size distribution (PSD) analysis for the three proposed natural additives.
- Evaluate the impact of different particle sizes on mud rheology (yield point, plastic viscosity, gel strength).

## Methods

The study involved a series of laboratory experiments to evaluate three types of natural additives: date seeds, grass, and grass ash. Particle size distribution tests were conducted to determine the sizing of these materials. A variety of concentrations and particle sizes were tested in water-based muds containing bentonite. Rheological properties (plastic viscosity, yield point, and gel strength) were measured using a Fann Model 35 rotational viscometer. Filtration characteristics were assessed using a filtration unit. The performance of the new formulations was compared against a commercially available additive, modified starch.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized three types of natural additives: date seeds (sourced from palm trees), grass, and grass ash (produced by burning dried grass). These were tested in combination with bentonite and water to form water-based muds. The research included testing multiple concentrations and particle sizes for each additive.

## Key Findings

- Date seeds are a byproduct of date processing plants, containing high amounts of protein, crude fat, and fibers; they were found to be an effective source of phenolic compounds and antioxidants. ([[2014-wajheeuddin|Wajheeuddin, 2014, p. 47]])
- Grass ash is primarily composed of silicon and can serve as a substitute for cement in civil engineering applications due to its simple production process. ([[2014-wajheeuddin|Wajheeuddin, 2014, p. 47]])
- All three materials (date seeds, grass, and grass ash) with varying particle sizes and concentrations exhibited improved rheology, filtration, and pH compared to standard formulations. ([[2014-wajheeuddin|Wajheeuddin, 2014, p. 18]])
- The use of date seeds, grass, and grass ash is suitable for the formulation of a low-cost, environment-friendly, and sustainable drilling fluid system. ([[2014-wajheeuddin|Wajheeuddin, 2014, p. 18]])
- Modified starch was used as a benchmark to validate the results obtained from the natural additives. ([[2014-wajheeuddin|Wajheeuddin, 2014, p. 18]])
- Date seeds were found to contain potassium (37.34%) and calcium (29.69%) as the major elemental contributors. ([[2014-wajheeuddin|Wajheeuddin, 2014, p. 53]])
- Grass ash was primarily composed of silicon (27.56%), calcium (23.46%), and potassium (21.82%). ([[2014-wajheeuddin|Wajheeuddin, 2014, p. 56]])
- Grass samples showed high concentrations of calcium (53.80%) and potassium (19.83%). ([[2014-wajheeuddin|Wajheeuddin, 2014, p. 69]])
- The majority of date seed material was retained on the 300 micron sieve, classifying them as intermediate particles. ([[2014-wajheeuddin|Wajheeuddin, 2014, p. 64]])
- Grass ash showed a high percentage of fine particles, with the highest amount accumulating on the no-sieve pan. ([[2014-wajheeuddin|Wajheeuddin, 2014, p. 67]])
- The average particle size of the finer fraction of grass at 50% net weight was determined to be 35 microns. ([[2014-wajheeuddin|Wajheeuddin, 2014, p. 70]])
- Mud formulated with 1.0 ppb date seeds (600 micron) exhibited the highest plastic viscosity (PV). ([[2014-wajheeuddin|Wajheeuddin, 2014, p. 78]])
- The highest yield point (YP) for 600 micron date seeds was observed at concentrations of 0.25 ppb and 0.5 ppb. ([[2014-wajheeuddin|Wajheeuddin, 2014, p. 78]])
- For 125 micron date seeds, the filtration characteristics improved with addition, obtaining a value of 13.2 ml filtrate loss (a 12% reduction) at an optimum concentration of 2.0 ppb. ([[2014-wajheeuddin|Wajheeuddin, 2014, p. 83]])
- The addition of date seeds lowers the pH of both the mud and the mud filtrate, making it suitable for treating contaminated muds with high pH levels. ([[2014-wajheeuddin|Wajheeuddin, 2014, p. 84]])

## Limitations

High temperature tests were limited to 200°F because no high-temperature additives were used in the formulation.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Conduct tests using these additives with a salt water-based system.
- Formulate an oil-based mud system with date seeds, grass, or grass ash.
- Perform detailed filter cake characterization.
- Conduct cost analysis to establish economic viability.
- Develop a low-cost commercial mud system by incorporating additional additives.
