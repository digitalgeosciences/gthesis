# THE EVALUATION OF USING MICRONIZED BARITE AS A COMPLETION FLUID WEIGHTING MATERIAL IN HPHT WELLS

**Summary**: The thesis evaluates the use of micronized barite as a weighting material for completion fluids in high-pressure, high-temperature (HPHT) wells. It investigates how reducing barite particle size affects fluid stability, sag tendency, and removal efficiency through various laboratory tests including zeta potential, sag tests, and filtration tests.

---

## Research Problem

The complexity of HPHT well conditions requires stable completion fluids that provide sufficient hydrostatic pressure without causing formation damage or settling (barite sag). Conventional barite often poses issues with stability and settlement in these environments.

## Objectives

- Evaluate micronized barite as a weighting material to resolve problems associated with conventional drill-in fluids (formation damage and fluid stability).
- Prepare different sizes of barite (106-75 μm, 75-40 μm, and <40μm) using sieve analysis.
- Produce micronized barite by reducing particle size to a few microns using a ball milling machine.
- Assess the effectiveness of reducing barite particle size on the solubility of barite in removal fluids (DTPA with potassium carbonate).
- Investigate the effect of barite particle size on rheological properties at 120 and 250F.
- Evaluate the impact of barite particle size on fluid stability using zeta potential measurements and static sag tests.

## Methods

The study utilized several experimental methods: 1) Ball milling (TENCAN planetary ball milling machine) was used to reduce the average particle size of raw barite (35 microns) to various sizes. 2) Particle size analysis was conducted using a TURBOTRAC analyzer. 3) Solubility tests were performed using DTPA, potassium carbonate, and heat to measure barite removal. 4) Zeta potential measurements were taken over a range of pH values to assess colloid stability. 5) Sag tests were conducted in aging cells under pressure (500 psi) and temperature (up to 250°F). 6) HPHT filtration tests used ceramic discs to measure fluid loss and filter cake thickness/removal efficiency.

## Data and Materials

The study used a sample of commercial barite with an average particle size of 35 microns. Samples were milled for durations of 0, 0.5, 1, 3, 5, and 8 hours. Filtration tests utilized ceramic discs (2.5-inch diameter, 0.25-inch thickness). Completion fluid formulations included additives like soda ash, bentonite, xanthan polymer, potassium chloride, potassium hydroxide, calcium carbonate, and sodium sulfite.

## Key Findings

- Solubility test results showed a good enhancement in barite removal due to the increase of chemical reaction surface area from micronized particles. ([[2017-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2017, p. 14]])
- HPHT filtration tests confirmed improved removal, showing an improvement of around 5% in filter cake removal efficiency for micronized barite. ([[2017-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2017, p. 14]])
- Reducing barite particle size resulted in a thickening behavior in the rheology of completion fluids, which became more noticeable as temperature increased. ([[2017-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2017, p. 14]])
- Zeta potential measurements showed improved stability with micronized barite for pH > 8, while other sizes were in the unstable range (-30 to +30 mV). ([[2017-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2017, p. 15]])
- Sag tests showed insignificant enhancement in fluid stability as barite was reduced to micronized size (sag factor of 0.546 vs. 0.563 for normal size at 230F). ([[2017-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2017, p. 15]])
- Barite samples milled for 1 hour showed a significant reduction in particle size to approximately 18.85 µm (compared to 34.86 µm for raw barite). ([[2017-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2017, p. 60]])
- XRD analysis confirmed that no iron contamination occurred during the ball milling process as no new peaks were observed. ([[2017-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2017, p. 59]])
- SEM observations showed that barite particles became more clustered and agglomerated after milling due to increased surface energy from breakage. ([[2017-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2017, p. 59]])
- Particle size analysis showed that particle sizes initially decreased but began to increase again after 1 hour due to the agglomeration of smaller particles. ([[2017-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2017, p. 59]])
- Raw barite, 75-106 µm, and 40-75 µm samples were found to be in the unstable range (±30 mV) across all pH levels, indicating potential for solids settlement. ([[2017-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2017, p. 62]])
- Micronized barite (~1.5 µm) showed moderate stability at pH values greater than 8, which corresponds to the optimal range (9-11) for conventional drilling fluids. ([[2017-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2017, p. 62]])
- A sag factor between 0.5 and 0.53 is considered to have good sag performance; values above 0.53 indicate a high risk of solid particle settlement. ([[2017-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2017, p. 55]])
- Sag performance was acceptable (sag factor between 0.5 and 0.53) up to approximately 212°F, but deteriorated significantly beyond that due to Xanthan polymer degradation. ([[2017-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2017, p. 64]])
- Reducing barite particle size to a few microns improved fluid stability in the zeta potential test for pH > 8 (the normal range for completion fluids). ([[2017-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2017, p. 79]])
- Reducing barite particle size led to a thickening behavior in rheology, which became more pronounced at higher temperatures (250°F). ([[2017-mohamed|MOHAMED, 2017, p. 68]])

## Limitations

Zeta potential measurements were not conducted under actual field conditions (real fluid composition, solids percentage, temperature, and pressure).

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Study the effect of reducing barite particle size to Nano size on fluid stability.
- Study the effect of adding ilmenite to barite as a weighting material on enhancing fluid stability.
- Add new additives, polymers, or other chemicals to help in barite dispersion to enhance completion fluids stability and mitigate barite sag tendency.
