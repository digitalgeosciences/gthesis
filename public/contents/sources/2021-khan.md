# APPLICATION OF IONIC LIQUIDS IN CLAY SWELLING REDUCTION: MECHANISM AND PERFORMANCE

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the use of imidazolium-based ionic liquids (ILs) as additives in water-based drilling and completion fluids to mitigate clay swelling and formation damage. The study evaluates several ILs with different anions (bromide, chloride, dicyanamide) against standard inhibitors like sodium silicate and various salts (CaCl2, MgCl2, NH4Cl) using coreflooding, rheology, and mineralogical analysis.

---

## Research Problem

Clay minerals in petroleum reservoirs, particularly in shale formations, cause swelling and disintegration when in contact with water during drilling, completion, workover, and stimulation. Existing inhibitors like salts or polymers have limitations such as high dosage requirements, poor temperature stability, or potential for environmental impact, necessitating the study of ionic liquids (unconventional salts) as more effective alternatives.

## Objectives

- To use ILs as clay swelling inhibitor in drilling fluid application
- To understand the mechanism of the clay swelling process with the interaction of ILs
- To investigate the porosity and permeability reduction due to interaction of ILs based completion fluid
- To prevent clay swelling during hydraulic fracturing operations
- To understand the application of ILs in rock physics application
- To study mechanism of interaction of ILs with clay is important for designing drilling, completion, and fracturing fluids

## Methods

The study utilized several experimental techniques: 1. Linear swelling tests were conducted on Na-Mt samples to measure expansion over 48 hours at various IL concentrations (0.1%, 0.3%, 0.5%). 2. Free swelling (immersion) tests were performed to observe physical disintegration of clay. 3. Capillary Suction Timer (CST) was used to characterize the dispersion and filter cake properties. 4. Particle size distribution was analyzed via laser diffraction or similar methods. 5. Wettability was measured using contact angle measurements on coated glass surfaces. 6. XRD was used to determine mineralogy and d-spacing of clay minerals. 7. SEM was used to observe morphology and surface texture. 8. Rheological properties (PV, YP, viscosity) were measured for drilling fluids. 9. Coreflooding experiments were performed on Scioto sandstone core plugs (16.9% porosity, 0.72 mD permeability) using various completion fluids (ILs and salts). 10. NMR T2 response was used to evaluate changes in pore structure and size distribution.

## Data and Materials

The study utilized sodium bentonite (Na-Mt), primarily composed of montmorillonite (54%), illite (12%), and cristobalite (15%). Various ionic liquids were tested based on different cation types (Imidazolium, Pyridinium, Sulfonium, Phosphonium, and Ammonium) and anion types (Bromide, Chloride, Dicyanamide, Iodide, Phosphate, Sulphonate).

## Key Findings

- Ionic liquids were found to reduce the clay swelling capacity by interacting with the clay surface and reducing its hydrophilicity, which restricts water exchange. ([[2021-khan|KHAN, 2021, p. 20]])
- Completion fluids prepared using ionic liquids resulted in complete stabilization of clays and nearly maintained the in-situ permeability of tight sandstone. ([[2021-khan|KHAN, 2021, p. 21]])
- Ionic liquids were found to be potentially useful as clay swelling additives in fracturing fluids, as evidenced by geomechanical testing including breakdown pressure and scratch tests. ([[2021-khan|KHAN, 2021, p. 21]])
- The amount of ionic liquids adsorbed on the clay surface depends on the concentration of the ionic liquids, as determined by FTIR and XRD analysis. ([[2021-khan|KHAN, 2021, p. 23]])
- Sodium bentonite (Na-Mt) used in the study had a cation exchange capacity (CEC) of 81 meq/100g and a surface area of 710 m2/g. ([[2021-khan|KHAN, 2021, p. 50]])
- A lower Capillary Suction Time (CST) is considered an indicator of a better solution to avoid the hydration of clay minerals and prevent swelling. ([[2021-khan|KHAN, 2021, p. 54]])
- Large contact angles indicate a hydrophobic surface, which is an indicator to inhibit shale swelling. ([[2021-khan|KHAN, 2021, p. 58]])
- XRD analysis of Scioto sandstone core samples showed a mineral composition of 70% Quartz, 18% Illite, 5% Plagioclase, 4% Chlorite, 2% Potassium Feldspar, and 1% Kaolinite. ([[2021-khan|KHAN, 2021, p. 61]])
- The average core plug porosity was 16.9%, with an average absolute liquid-corrected permeability of 0.72 mD and a dry bulk density of .265 g/cc. ([[2021-khan|KHAN, 2021, p. 61]])
- A concentration of 0.1 wt.% Tpb-P showed the best inhibition in linear swelling tests, with a swelling rate of 98.1% compared to 125% for deionized water. ([[2021-khan|KHAN, 2021, p. 79]])
- Contact angle measurements for base fluid were 40.6°, while the addition of 0.1 wt.% Tpb-P increased the contact angle to 46.3°, indicating reduced hydrophilicity. ([[2021-khan|KHAN, 2021, p. 85]])
- The inclusion of 0.1% Tpb-P in drilling fluid resulted in a lower filtrate loss compared to higher concentrations (0.3% and 0.5%), while remaining within API limits. ([[2021-khan|KHAN, 2021, p. 89]])
- A combination of 0.1% Tpb-P and 0.5% GB resulted in the highest contact angle (36.6°) and best inhibition performance among tested combinations. ([[2021-khan|KHAN, 2021, p. 101]])
- Imidolium-based ionic liquids with dicyanamide anions showed slightly better swelling inhibition compared to bromide, iodide, or chloride variants. ([[2021-khan|KHAN, 2021, p. 103]])
- The addition of ionic liquids to sodium bentonite resulted in fewer cavities and a smoother, denser surface compared to untreated samples. ([[2021-khan|KHAN, 2021, p. 101]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

The study suggests that ionic liquids have significant potential as clay stabilizing additives in completion fluids and can be utilized as potential clay swelling additives in fracturing fluids.
