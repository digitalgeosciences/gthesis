# CARBONATE SURFACE CHEMISTRY: EFFECT OF MONOVALENT IONS

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the surface chemistry of carbonate rocks (calcite and carbonate outcrop) modified with model oils and treated with various brines containing monovalent ions. The study utilizes zeta potential measurements, SEM-EDS, and Raman spectroscopy to characterize the mineral surfaces and evaluate the effectiveness of different salts in desorbing oil.

---

## Research Problem

The primary challenge is understanding why low salinity or 'smart' water flooding improves oil recovery in carbonate reservoirs, which are typically oil-wet. While multi-valent ions (Ca2+, Mg2+, SO42-) are known to play a role, the specific contribution of monovalent anions in high-salinity environments remains under-explored.

## Objectives

- Investigate the role of individual monovalent ions such as Chloride, Bromide and Iodide on the surface charges of calcite and carbonate outcrop rock samples.
- Identify the ions adsorbed on the rock surface and desorbed from the rock surface when exposed to the different investigated brines used.
- Quantify the changes in the composition of the rock surface
- Identify the mechanism leading to the changes of the rock surface properties
- To measure zeta potential for calcite and carbonate outcrop samples in various brine systems.
- To evaluate the effect of model oils (containing stearic acid and/1or asphaltene) on rock surface chemistry.

## Methods

The study used two types of rock: calcite crystals and carbonate outcrop samples from the Arab-D formation. Samples were crushed to 10-30 $\mu$m for zeta potential analysis. Zeta potential was measured using a ZetaPALS instrument (electrophoretic light scattering). SEM-EDS was used for mineralogical and elemental characterization, while Raman spectroscopy was employed to analyze brine samples after filtration to detect the release of stearic acid. A series of synthetic brines (low salinity and high salinity/sea water variants) were prepared to test the influence of specific ions.

## Data and Materials

The study used calcite rock and carbonate outcrop samples modified with model oils (TS, TA, TSA). Samples were treated with various brines: SW0.5NaCl, SW0.5NaBr, and SW0.5NaI. SEM-EDS analysis was performed on multiple spots (Spectra 1-14) to determine the weight percentages of Carbon (C), Oxygen (O), and Calcium (Ca). Raman spectroscopy was used to analyze five different samples of evaporated sea water.

## Key Findings

- Zeta potential of calcite and carbonate outcrop is negative in DI water due to preferential leaching of Ca2+ ions from the calcium carbonate lattice. ([[2017-sadeed|SADEED, 2017, p. 18]])
- The magnitude of the negative zeta potential values is higher in carbonate outcrop as compared to calcite, potentially due to silica content in the carbonate outcrop. ([[2017-sadeed|SADEED, 2017, p. 129]])
- Adding 5000 ppm NaI or NaBr to sea water was found to be a good alternative for releasing polar oil components from the rock surface, with NaI showing more promising results than NaBr. ([[2017-sadeed|SADEED, 2017, p. 19]])
- The addition of 5000 ppm NaBr showed the least negative charge on carbonate outcrop rocks modified with stearic acid compared to those in DI water, due to the desorption of adsorbed model oil components (stearic acid). ([[2017-sadeed|SADEED, 2017, p. 19]])
- The study found that the release of adsorbed oil from the rock surface alters the rock surface from strong oil-wet to less oil-wet, leading to incremental oil recovery. ([[2017-sadeed|SADEED, 2017, p. 19]])
- Unmodified carbonate outcrop samples showed a more negative zeta potential (-8.05 mV at pH 7.5) compared to calcite (-4.70 mV), attributed to the presence of quartz (SiO2). ([[2017-sadeed|SADEED, 2017, p. 73]])
- Modification with model oil (TSA) resulted in a higher negative charge than either TS or TA alone, indicating that both stearic acid and asphaltene contribute to surface modification. ([[2017-sadeed|SADEED, 2017, p. 77]])
- The addition of asphaltenes to carbonate rock led to a lower increase in zeta potential compared to the addition of stearic acid, suggesting less adsorption on carbonate surfaces. ([[2017-sadeed|SADEED, 2017, p. 81]])
- Zeta potential values for modified rocks (TSA) were higher than those for modified rocks (TA), confirming that asphaltene and stearic acid compete for limited sites on the carbonate surface. ([[2017-sadeed|SADEED, 2017, p. 78]])
- The presence of magnesium ions (Mg2+) was shown in literature to shift zeta potential toward more positive values, potentially promoting a more water-wet surface compared to sulfate ions. ([[2017-sadeed|SADEED, 2017, p. 50]])
- Zeta potential for unmodified calcite in 5000 ppm NaCl was -6.85±0.23 mV at 7.5 pH, while carbonate outcrop was -8.05±0.48 mV. ([[2017-sadeed|SADEED, 2017, p. 83]])
- Calcium ion concentration in filtrates followed the sequence: DI water (65.78 ppm) < NaI (1721.2 ppm) < NaBr (2367.08 ppm) < NaCl (3981.25 ppm). ([[2017-sadeed|SADEED, 2017, p. 84]])
- The higher zeta potential values for NaI and NaBr compared to NaCl in some cases were attributed to the adsorption of ions rather than just dissolution. ([[2017-sadeed|SADEED, 2017, p. 84]])
- 5000 ppm NaI was identified as the most effective brine for modifying rock wettability toward less oil-wet for both calcite and carbonate outcrop rocks. ([[2017-sadeed|SADEED, 2017, p. 89]])
- The zeta potential of carbonate outcrop in sea water (SW) was -6.83±1.08 mV, which is higher than the magnitude of calcite for all used brines. ([[2017-sadeed|SADEED, 2017, p. 100]])

## Recommendations and Future Work

Core flooding experiments should be performed using sea water with NaI or NaBr brines to confirm effectiveness. Future studies should investigate different carboxylic groups (Naphthenic acid, resins), different carbonate minerals (magnesite, gypsum, anhydrite), dead crude oil, and other ions like borate or phosphate.
