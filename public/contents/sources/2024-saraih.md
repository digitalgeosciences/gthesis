# An Integrated Study to Characterize and Model Strata with Biogenically Influenced Flow Media in the Aruma Formation, Central Saudi Arabia

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the impact of bioturbation and burrow systems on petrophysical properties in the Arum Formation, a carbonate reservoir in central Saudi Arabia. It utilizes Ground Penetrating Radar (GPR) to characterize burrow attributes like morphology, diameter, and percentage, comparing these results with CT scans and field observations to validate GPR as a non-invasive tool for reservoir characterization.

---

## Research Problem

The study addresses the challenge of characterizing complex, bioturbated carbonate reservoirs where large burrows (up to 8 cm) significantly influence fluid flow. Traditional methods like coring often fail due to core breakage or limited volume, making it difficult to accurately model and evaluate reservoir quality in these strata.

## Objectives

- Describe the diversity of burrows found within the Aruma Formation and how they vary stratigraphically.
- Discuss in a broad context, the sedimentological and stratigraphic controls on the biogenically influenced flow media.
- To investigate the influence of bioturbation on fracture development and propagation within the geological formations.
- To employ cutting-edge techniques, specifically Ground Penetrating Radar (GPR), to thoroughly analyze the attributes of burrows in the strata.
- Characterize the petrophysical properties of three distinct biogenically influenced fluid flow media (BIFMs) within the Khanasir Member.
- Evaluate the impact of burrow morphology and fill material on porosity and permeability.

## Methods

The study employed a multi-scale approach: 1) Field investigation including photography and manual measurement of burrow attributes (percentage, diameter). 2) Image analysis using ImageJ and JMicrovision to process field photos. 3) Medical CT scanning (Toshiba Alexion TSX-032A) of rock samples from Subunit 1A and 2A to analyze internal morphology and connectivity. 4) Ground Penetrating Radar (GPR) survey using a 2.7 GHz antenna over a 1m x 1m area, followed by data processing in ReflexW (de-wow, bandpass filter, background removal, gain filters). 5) 3D block construction of GPR data in MATLAB with resampling and normalization. 6. Visualization and analysis of 3D volumes using Voxler and Petrel.

## Data and Materials

The study utilizes field photographs from the vicinity of the GPR survey site, 48 2D slices of CT-scan hand specimens, and GPR survey data covering a volume over 144 times larger than that captured by CT scans. The research focuses on the Aruma Formation in central Saudi Arabia.

## Key Findings

- The study identified three distinct biogenically influenced flow media (BIFM): BIFM 1 consists of solution-enlarged open Thalassinoides (avg. diameter 5 cm) in a mud-dominated matrix; BIFM 2 consists of Thalassinoides with mud-dominated infills (avg. diameter 3 cm) in a dolomite matrix; BIFM 3 exhibits compacted open Thalassinoides. ([[2024-saraih|Saraih, 2024, p. 34]])
- Burrow percentages (BP) in the studied units ranged from 10% to 50%, with interconnected networks of burrows providing potential permeability pathways. ([[2024-saraih|Saraih, 2024, p. 34]])
- Unit 1 burrow fillings were found to consist of 51.8% calcite, 33.4% quartz, and 14.7% palygorskite (confirmed by XRD/XRF), while the host rock matrix was 100% calcite. ([[2024-saraih|Saraih, 2024, p. 42]])
- GPR data provided information on burrow morphology (size, shape, and percentage) that is difficult to capture in 2D views or limited 3D volumes from CT scans alone. ([[2024-saraih|Saraih, 2024, p. 19]])
- Fracture characteristics were influenced by factors including burrow fillings, matrix mineralogy, and burrow percentage, highlighting the impact of bioturbation on fracture networks. ([[2024-saraih|Saraih, 2024, p. 20]])
- Burrow diameters in sub-unit 1A ranged from 3.0 to 6.5 cm (mean 4.7 cm, SD 1.43). ([[2024-saraih|Saraih, 2024, p. 43]])
- Identified three distinct BIFMs: BIFM 1 (solution-enlarged, open/partially filled burrows), BIFM 2 (filled burrows in a dolomite matrix), and BIFM 3 (compacted open/partially filled burrows). ([[2024-saraih|Saraih, 2024, p. 53]])
- BIFM 1 host rock matrix porosity ranges from 3.4% to 14.4% (mean 7.7%), while its burrow-related permeability is expected to be in the multiple Darcy range due to large, interconnected pores. ([[2024-saraih|Saraih, 2024, p. 53]])
- BIFM 2 acts as a flow baffle because of tight burrow fillings; it has an average porosity of 12.62% and permeability of 0.74 mD. ([[2024-saraih|Saraih, 2024, p. 60]])
- BIFM 3 features open burrows with average pore diameters of 3.3 cm, contributing significantly to total porosity (mean 12.62% for the unit). ([[2024-saraih|Saraih, 2024, p. 54]])
- Unit 1 fracture density varies from 20 to 150 fractures/m² with an average of 77 fractures/m², and a mean spacing of 3 m. ([[2024-saraih|Saraih, 2024, p. 81]])
- In unit 1, higher fracture concentration was observed in areas where burrow shapes deviated from perfect circles (e.g., elongated or irregular forms). ([[2024-saraih|Saraih, 2024, p. 81]])
- Subunit 2A shows a significant increase in fracture density from 4.5 fractures/m² to 25 fractures/m² as burrow percentage increases from 25% to 35%. ([[2024-saraih|Saraih, 2024, p. 85]])
- Very-small scale fractures (length < 5 cm) are more prevalent within the burrow fillings than in the host rock matrix in subunit 2A. ([[2024-saraih|Saraih, 2024, p. 86]])
- Unit 3 shows a significant increase in permeability (up to 134 mD) in certain zones, potentially serving as high-quality aquifers or reservoirs. ([[2024-saraih|Saraih, 2024, p. 60]])

## Limitations

The study notes that while GPR is superior for large volumes, it may be subject to differences in results if the survey points are not representative of the broader area; however, the focus remained on specific surveyed locations.

## Recommendations and Future Work

Future work should include: 1) investigating why burrows with open shafts remain open, 2) determining the origin of dolomite in unit 2's host rock matrix, 3) conducting high-resolution GPR surveys on sub-unit tops, and 4) performing acoustic property measurements across various scales.
