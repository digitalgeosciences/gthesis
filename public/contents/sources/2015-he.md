# Destabilization and Treatment of Produced Water-Oil Emulsions from EOR Application using Dissolved Air Flotation Technique

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the destabilization and treatment of water-oil emulsions produced during enhanced oil recovery (EOR) using different types of anionic polyacrylamide (PAM). The study evaluates several parameters, including volume of separated water, turbidity, zeta potential, COD, FTIR, and viscosity, to determine the optimal demulsifier concentration for both formation brine and seawater systems.

---

## Research Problem

Produced water-oil emulsions from EOR operations are difficult to separate due to the presence of chemicals like surfactants and polymers. The study addresses the need for effective demulsifiers to treat these emulsions before discharge or reuse, specifically focusing on the impact of different concentrations and types of PAM and HEDTA in high-salinity environments.

## Objectives

- Investigate flocculation and destabilization of oil droplets using different polyelectrolytes and varying charge densities.
- Evaluate the impact of electrolytes (aluminum sulphate and ferrous sulphate) on turbidity reduction and water separation volume.
- Determine the optimal concentration and pH for chelating agent HEDTA as a system to enhance demulsification efficiency.
- Prepare stable water-in-oil (W/O) emulsions using formation brine and seawater.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of different anionic polyacrylamide (PAM) types with varying charge densities (AN 913, AN 934, AN 945) as demulsifiers.
- Determine the optimal concentration of PAM for reducing turbidity and increasing the volume of separated water.

## Methods

The study utilized a series of laboratory tests: 1. Jar Test: Used to observe the physical separation of oil and water using a Stuart Flocculator SW6, with specific stirring speeds (250 rpm for 10 min, then 30 rpm for 15 min). 2. Turbidity Measurement: Conducted using a HACH 2100N Turbidimeter to assess the clarity of separated water. 3. Density Measurement: Performed using an Anton Paar density meter. 4. Viscosity Measurement: Measured via Ostwald viscometer. 5. Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD): Analyzed in a HACH COD reactor at 150°C for 120 minutes, followed by titration with ferrous ammonium sulfate (FAS). 6. FTIR Spectroscopy: Performed using a Bruker Tensor 27 to identify chemical components like surfactants or polymers. 7. Zeta Potential Analysis: Conducted via Brookhaven ZetaPALS to study the effect of double layers on separation mechanisms.

## Data and Materials

The study used two types of water for emulsion preparation: formation brine (200,000 ppm) and seawater (57,000 ppm). The oil phase consisted of commercial diesel. Three types of anionic polyacrylamides (PAM) with different charge densities (AN 913 - low, AN 934 - medium, AN 945 - high) were tested at concentrations ranging from 600 mg/L to 1000 mg/L.

## Key Findings

- Anionic PAM (specifically AN 934) at its optimum concentration was the most effective for reducing residual turbidity and increasing separated water volume compared to other tested PAMs. ([[2015-he|He, 2015, p. 13]])
- The addition of electrolytes (aluminum sulphate and ferrous sulphate) increased the volume of separated water by more than 25% compared to using only PAMs, while significantly improving turbidity and COD reduction. ([[2015-he|He, 2015, p. 17]])
- Chelating agent HEDTA was found effective as a demulsifier at high pH (specifically 12.2) for efficient separation and turbidity reduction. ([[2015-he|He, 2015, p. 13]])
- W/O emulsion stability is directly related to its salinity, while the optimal concentration of demulsifiers remained consistent across both high and low salinity conditions. ([[2015-he|He, 2015, p. 13]])
- Zeta potential values decreased as PAM concentration increased but did not vary significantly with different molecular weights. ([[2015-he|He, 2015, p. 28]])
- The optimal concentration of demulsifier (PAM) for both formation brine and seawater was found to be 800 mg/L, where the volume of separated water reached its peak before plateauing at higher concentrations. ([[2015-he|He, 2015, p. 69]])
- Anionic polyacrylamide AN 934 (medium charge density) provided the best results in reducing residual turbidity compared to other PAMs tested. ([[2015-he|He, 2015, p. 61]])
- Emulsions prepared with formation brine were more stable than those with seawater due to higher salinity, which increased viscosity and created larger, stronger oil drops. ([[2015-he|He, 2015, p. 69]])
- The volume of separated water (WS) increased from 48.21% to 73.21% for brine and 53.57% to 77.14% for seawater when PAM concentration was increased from 600 mg/L to 800 mg/L. ([[2015-he|He, 2015, p. 69]])
- Higher charge density in PAM (AN 945) resulted in higher zeta potential values, leading to greater electrostatic repulsion and making it more difficult for oil droplets to aggregate compared to lower charge density variants. ([[2015-he|He, 2015, p. 70]])
- The concentration of 800 mg/L showed the lowest turbidity (14 NTU for brine; 10.9 NTU for seawater) among all tested concentrations. ([[2015-he|He, 2015, p. 70]])
- Medium charge density PAM (AN 934) showed lower turbidity for both formation brine and seawater compared to high or very high charge density variants. ([[2015-he|He, 2015, p. 73]])
- The addition of aluminum sulphate significantly reduced the viscosity of separated water from 3.162 cp to 1.385 cp when used with PAM. ([[2015-he|He, 2015, p. 98]])
- HEDTA as a demulsifier was found to be most effective at a concentration of 10% (wt) or approximately 46.67 g/L, where it yielded the lowest viscosity and best turbidity reduction. ([[2015-he|He, 2015, p. 105]])
- The addition of aluminum sulphate resulted in a higher increase in the volume of separated water compared to ferrous sulphate (up to 22% increase). ([[2015-he|He, 2015, p. 97]])

## Limitations

The study used a synthetic emulsion rather than real field crude/waste oil and formation brine to match actual field conditions.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Investigate the effect of pH on emulsion stability in more varied scenarios.
- Repeat experiments using crude/waste oil from fields and actual formation brine.
- Conduct a pilot test of optimal Polyacrylamide concentration with aluminum sulfate and ferrous sulfate on a flotation system.
- Perform a pilot test on real field emulsions from SP flooding in EOR applications.
