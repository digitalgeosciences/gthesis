# Digital Outcrop Modeling of The Early Triassic Upper Khartam Member of Khuff Formation; Implications for Reservoir Heterogeneity and Quality, Central Saudi Arabia

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the reservoir heterogeneity and quality of the Upper Khartam Member of the Khuff Formation in Central Saudi Arabia using a combination of traditional field methods and modern digital technologies. It integrates sedimentological, stratigraphical, and petrophysical data with 3D models generated from terrestrial laser scanning (LiDAR) to characterize lithofacies and stratigraphic units.

---

## Research Problem

The Khuff Formation is a major gas reservoir in the Middle East, but its high heterogeneity at various scales makes it difficult to characterize accurately using only subsurface data. The Upper Khartam Member serves as an excellent outcrop analog for understanding these complexities.

## Objectives

- Establish high resolution digital outcrop analog model for Upper Khartam Member using LiDAR, field and laboratory data.
- Characterize the reservoir heterogeneity at outcrop scale of lithofacies, cyclicity and facies continuity; and at micro scale by examining microfacies and petrophysical properties.
- Characterize the lithofacies and stratigraphic framework of the Upper Khartam Member.
- Evaluate the impact of sedimentological features on reservoir heterogeneity and quality.
- Construct a composite section for the Upper Khartam Member based on sedimentological and stratigraphical investigations.
- Apply LiDAR scanning to create digital models of the outcrop to integrate geological data.

## Methods

The study utilized a multi-step workflow: 1. Field investigation of four vertical stratigraphic outcrop sections to construct a composite section. 2. LiDAR scanning using a Riegl VZ-4000 instrument to create high-resolution digital models. 3. Integration of sedimentology and stratigraphy into the digital model using software including RiScan, Polyworks, VRGS, Draping software, and ArcGIS. 4. Selection of three reservoir units (A, B, C) for detailed petrophysical analysis. 5. Core-plug sampling at 2.5m to 5m intervals followed by porosity measurement via the saturation method and permeability measurement using a TKA-209 Permeameter (air/helium). 6. Microscopic and mineralogical characterization of selected samples using thin-section-petrography, SEM, and XRD.

## Data and Materials

4 vertical stratigraphic outcrop sections; 3 reservoir units (A: 135m long, 28 samples; B: 155m long, 32 samples; C: 65m long, 27 samples); Riegl VZ-4000 LiDAR data; thin section and XRD analysis for 12 selected samples (4 per unit).

## Key Findings

- The Upper Khartam Member comprises six lithofacies associations deposited in three depositional settings: marine transgression, fore shoal, and shoal complex. ([[2014-sami|Sami, 2014, p. 17]])
- High resolution stratigraphy identifies three high-frequency sequences (HFSs) within the Upper Khartam Member: HFS-1, HFS-2, and HFS-3. ([[2014-sami|Sami, 2014, p. 18]])
- Reservoir heterogeneity varies from extremely heterogeneous in unit C to very heterogeneous in units A and B. ([[2014-sami|Sami, 2014, p. 18]])
- The main controls on reservoir heterogeneity are depositional and post-depositional diagenesis at outcrop scale. ([[2014-sami|Sami, 2014, p. 18]])
- The study area in Al-Tarafiyah town shows the Khartam Member well exposed as a road cut with a dip to the east. ([[2014-sami|Sami, 2014, p. 27]])
- Lithofacies include oolitic and peloidal limestone, bioclastic dolomite, and dolomitic claystone in the upper unit (Early Triassic). ([[2014-sami|Sami, 2014, p. 44]])
- The Upper Khartam Member is bounded by the Permian-Triassic boundary at the bottom and the Sudair Formation at the top. ([[2014-sami|Sami, 2014, p. 75]])
- SGR logs show a significant decrease in uranium, thorium, and potassium contents across the Upper Khartam Member compared to the Lower Khartam Member. ([[2014-sami|Sami, 2014, p. 76]])
- The study identified 6 distinct lithofacies ranging from offshore to foreshoal to shoal complex environments. ([[2014-sami|Sami, 2014, p. 90]])
- Grainstone units in section KM-1 exhibit high porosity reaching 33% and permeability of up to 13 mD. ([[2014-sami|Sami, 2014, p. 79]])
- Grainy units in section KM-2 show high porosity (up to 34%) but poor permeability, not exceeding 1 mD. ([[2014-sami|Sami, 2014, p. 80]])
- In the dolomitized oolitic grainstone of section KM-4, the highest porosity reached 32% and permeability was 1.5 mD. ([[2014-sami|Sami, 2014, p. 85]])
- ArcMap thickness measurements for various lithofacies showed high correlation with field data (e.g., Graded mud- to wackestone: Field 3m, ArcMap 3.1079m). ([[2014-sami|Sami, 2014, p. 151]])
- The Upper Khartam Member is composed of three High Frequency Sequences (HFSs) showing a general shallowing upward pattern. ([[2014-sami|Sami, 2014, p. 180]])
- LiDAR-based models allowed for more accurate determination of bed thicknesses and structural measurements (strike, dip, plunge) compared to standard digital photography. ([[2014-sami|Sami, 2014, p. 153]])

## Limitations

The digital model did not cover the entire outcrop of the Upper Khartam Member in the area, but only one specific road cut was selected for modeling.

## Recommendations and Future Work

The author suggests creating a digital outcrop model library for Saudi Arabian reservoir analogs and integrating these models with traditional field investigations and subsurface data to increase confidence in results.
