# Iron Sulfide Scale Inhibition in Water Injection Wells

**Summary**: The thesis investigates the inhibition of iron sulfide scale in carbonate rock samples using various chelating agents (EDTA, DTPA) and other chemical formulations. It evaluates the effectiveness of these inhibitors based on ferric ion concentration in effluent and resulting permeability changes across different temperatures and concentrations.

---

## Research Problem

Iron sulfide scale poses significant challenges in sour oil/gas fields and water injection wells due to its mineral heterogeneity and potential for causing formation damage, pressure drops, and equipment failure. Existing acids like HCl are limited by high corrosion rates at elevated temperatures.

## Objectives

- Evaluate inhibition efficiency of different concentrations of EDTA and DTPA against scale crystal (Fe3+) on Indiana limestone rock samples through dynamic adsorption tests employing coreflooding setup and ICP-OES under certain temperature conditions
- Investigate iron precipitation at the wide range of pH environments and analyze solid/solid interaction with carbonate rocks
- Analyze the effectiveness of the best formulation obtained from dynamic adsorption experiments on carbonate rock samples to study formation damage due to iron sulfide scale precipitation
- Determine the optimal chelating agent concentration for inhibiting iron sulfide scale formation.
- Evaluate the impact of different EDTA concentrations (10%, 15%, 20%) on ferric ion adsorption and permeability preservation.
- Assess the effect of cations (Ca2+, Mg2+) and soaking time on the inhibition efficiency of chelating agents.

## Methods

The study utilized three phases: 1) Dynamic adsorption experiments using coreflooding to determine optimal chelator concentrations; 2) Zeta-potential measurements to analyze surface charges and the impact of salts/chelators on iron precipitation; 3) Coreflooding experiments to simulate iron sulfide scale formation and inhibition. Analytical techniques included XRD for mineralogy, Particle Size Analysis (PSA) for grain size, ICP-OES for quantifying ferric ions in effluent, and Zeta Potential measurements using a Malvern Zetasizer Nano Z.

## Data and Materials

The study used core samples of Indiana limestone (IL) with initial permeabilities ranging from 9 mD to 12.5 mD. Zeta potential measurements were conducted on calcite minerals using various concentrations of Fe3+ (100, 500, 1500, 10000 ppm), Fe2+ (100, 500, 1500, 3000, 5000, 10000 ppm), and salts (NaCl, MgCl2, CaCl2). Inhibitors tested included EDTA and DTPA at 10% and 20% concentrations.

## Key Findings

- Iron sulfide scales are common in the Ghawar field, accounting for approximately 43% of the scales identified. ([[2021-gasimli|Gasimli, 2021, p. 51]])
- The average particle size of iron sulfide minerals ranges from 75-150 μm (mackinawite) to 296 μm (pyrite), which can lead to pore throat blockage in rocks with mean pore throat diameters of 19-44 μm. ([[2021-gasimli|Gasimli, 2021, p. 54]])
- EDTA is more effective at pH values between 1 and 10, while DTPA shows an optimum range from 2 to 11 for Iron (III). ([[2021-gasimli|Gasimli, 2021, p. 50]])
- The study identifies three main types of scale formation: incompatible mixing, autoscaling, and evaporation-caused scaling. ([[2021-gasimli|Gasimli, 2021, p. 28]])
- Hydrochloric acid (HCl) is effective for removing iron sulfide but is limited by high corrosion rates at temperatures above 100°C. ([[2021-gasimli|Gasimli, 2021, p. 40]])
- Iron sulfide minerals like pyrite have high thermal stability, making them difficult to remove with acids; however, as the iron-to-sulfur ratio approaches 1:1, solubility increases. ([[2021-gasimli|Gasimli, 2021, p. 60]])
- The addition of 10 wt% EDTA resulted in a significant decrease in pressure drop after FeCl3 injection compared to no inhibitor at 77°F. ([[2021-gasimli|Gasimli, 2021, p. 104]])
- At 77°F, 10 wt% EDTA complexed 80% of ferric ions, while its efficiency dropped to 69% and 59% at 120°F and 200°F respectively. ([[2021-gasimli|Gasimli, 2021, p. 107]])
- 15 wt% EDTA showed better results than 10 wt% EDTA at elevated temperatures (120°F and 200°F), although it complexed ~300 ppm less ferric ions at ambient temperature. ([[2021-gasimli|Gasimli, 2021, p. 111]])
- 20 wt% EDTA showed the highest inhibition efficiency at all tested temperatures: 84% at 77°F, 86% at 120°F, and 78% at 200°F. ([[2021-gasimli|Gasimli, 2021, p. 115]])
- The addition of Ca2+ ions to EDTA resulted in a 56% inhibition efficiency, while Mg2+ resulted in 52% efficacy at 77°F. ([[2021-gasimli|Gasimli, 2021, p. 119]])
- A soaking time of two hours for 10 wt% EDTA allowed it to complex an additional 61 ppm more ferric ions compared to no soaking time. ([[2021-gasimli|Gasimli, 2021, p. 120]])
- 20 wt% EDTA was identified as the best-adsorbed chelating agent for inhibiting iron(III) precipitation across ambient, medium, and high temperature conditions. ([[2021-gasimli|Gasimli, 2021, p. 124]])
- Ferric ions (Fe3+) show a high tendency to precipitate on calcite surfaces across the entire pH range of 1-13, regardless of concentration. ([[2021-gasimli|Gasimli, 2021, p. 128]])
- Ferrous ions (Fe2+) are more stable in acidic environments but show significant instability and precipitation starting from neutral pH toward high alkaline regions. ([[2021-gasimli|Gasimli, 2021, p. 129]])

## Limitations

The study notes that dynamic filter block tests are limited by filter size, and static experiments are limited by low-temperature conditions where natural dynamics cannot be fully investigated.

## Recommendations and Future Work

- Perform more zeta potential measurements across a wider range of EDTA concentrations.
- Extend the study of aminocarboxylic acid adsorption on calcite using Molecular Dynamics Simulation (MDS).
- Validate the impact of cations on inhibitor adsorption through additional coreflooding experiments.
- Use oxygen scavengers during iron sulfide scale formation to avoid simultaneous formation of iron oxides.
